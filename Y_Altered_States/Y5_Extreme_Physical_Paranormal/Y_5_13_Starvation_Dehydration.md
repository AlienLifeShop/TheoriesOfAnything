## QUICK SUMMARY

**Starvation and dehydration** — states of severe and prolonged nutritional and fluid deprivation — produce a characteristic and well-documented progression of **cognitive, perceptual, and emotional alterations** that constitute some of the most dramatic involuntary altered states of consciousness experienced by humans. The most detailed scientific study of starvation's psychological effects remains the **Minnesota Starvation Experiment** (1944–1945), conducted by physiologist Ancel **Keys** at the University of Minnesota: 36 conscientious objectors voluntarily underwent semi-starvation (1,570 calories/day for 24 weeks — approximately half their normal intake) followed by controlled refeeding. The results documented a devastating progression of psychological effects: obsessive preoccupation with food (subjects dreamed of food, collected cookbooks, could not concentrate on anything else), irritability, depression, social withdrawal, decreased libido, apathy, cognitive impairment (reduced concentration, impaired decision-making), and in some cases, self-mutilation and psychotic episodes. More extreme **total starvation** (as experienced by shipwreck survivors, prisoners of war, and famine victims) produces delirium, hallucinations, and states of dissociation that blend waking and dreaming. **Dehydration** produces cognitive impairment at a faster rate than food deprivation — even mild dehydration (1–2% body mass loss) measurably impairs attention, working memory, and mood; severe dehydration produces confusion, hallucinations, and delirium within 2–5 days (total water deprivation is typically lethal within 3–7 days depending on conditions).

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 The Minnesota Starvation Experiment
- **Ancel Keys et al.** (1945): *The Biology of Human Starvation* (1950, 2 volumes) — the definitive scientific study of semi-starvation; 36 male conscientious objectors volunteered; 3-month control period (normal diet) → 6-month semi-starvation (1,570 cal/day) → 3-month rehabilitation
- **Physical effects**: dramatic weight loss (~25% body weight), reduced metabolic rate, cardiac changes (bradycardia, reduced cardiac output), edema, cold intolerance, decreased strength
- **Psychological effects** — extensively documented and profoundly disturbing:
  - **Obsessive preoccupation with food**: subjects could think of almost nothing else; collected cookbooks, watched others eat, fantasized about food constantly
  - **Depression and irritability**: emotional instability, crying spells, temper outbursts, social withdrawal
  - **Cognitive impairment**: difficulty concentrating, reduced comprehension, impaired decision-making
  - **Personality changes**: formerly sociable subjects became withdrawn, apathetic, and isolated
  - **Psychotic episodes**: one subject (Harold Blickenstaff) experienced severe psychological breakdown; another subject deliberately cut off three fingers with an axe (unclear whether this was intentional or dissociative)
  - **Binge eating** during rehabilitation: compulsive overconsumption, inability to feel satiated

### 1.2 Physiology of Starvation
- **Metabolic progression**: glycogen depletion (12–24 hours) → lipolysis and ketogenesis (fatty acid oxidation, ketone body production — 24–72 hours) → protein catabolism (muscle breakdown for gluconeogenesis — begins after several days of fasting; becomes dominant energy source in prolonged starvation)
- **Brain adaptation**: the brain normally requires ~120g glucose/day; during prolonged starvation, the brain adapts to use ketone bodies for up to 60–70% of energy needs — this shifts happens over 1–3 weeks; cognitive function may actually improve transiently during early ketosis before declining with prolonged inadequate nutrition

### 1.3 Dehydration and Cognition
- **Mild dehydration** (1–2% body mass loss — typical of inadequate fluid intake or moderate exercise without replacement): produces measurable impairments in **attention**, **psychomotor function**, **working memory**, and **mood** (increased fatigue, anxiety, tension); effects are more pronounced in women and in heat
- **Moderate dehydration** (3–5%): headache, dizziness, reduced cognitive performance, difficulty with complex tasks, emotional volatility
- **Severe dehydration** (>5%): confusion, disorientation, hallucinations, delirium, cardiovascular compromise; total water deprivation is typically lethal within **3–7 days** (highly variable depending on environmental temperature, humidity, and physical activity)

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Hallucinations in Extreme Deprivation
- Extreme starvation and dehydration (as experienced by castaway survivors, desert lost-person incidents, concentration camp prisoners, and famine victims) produce **hallucinations** — typically visual (mirages in the desert — real atmospheric phenomena but also perceptual projections), auditory (voices), and tactile; these hallucinations likely result from a combination of hypoglycemia, dehydration, electrolyte imbalance, sleep deprivation, and physiological stress
- **Concentration camp survivors** (Frankl, Levi, Wiesel) described altered states under extreme starvation — dissociation from the body, dreamlike states, visions, and a dissolution of the normal boundary between waking and dreaming

### 2.2 Relevance to Eating Disorders
- The Minnesota Starvation Experiment's psychological findings have been extensively applied to understanding **anorexia nervosa** — the obsessive food preoccupation, cognitive rigidity, social withdrawal, depression, and body image distortion seen in anorexia closely parallel the semi-starvation syndrome described by Keys; this supports the understanding that many "symptoms" of anorexia are direct consequences of starvation rather than primary psychopathology
