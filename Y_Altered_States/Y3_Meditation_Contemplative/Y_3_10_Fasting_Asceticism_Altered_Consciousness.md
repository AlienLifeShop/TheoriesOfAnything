## QUICK SUMMARY

**Fasting** and **ascetic practices** — deliberate deprivation of food, sleep, comfort, or sensory input — have been used across virtually all religious and spiritual traditions to induce **altered states of consciousness**, **visions**, and experiences interpreted as divine encounter. The neurobiological basis is increasingly understood: prolonged fasting shifts the brain's fuel source from glucose to **ketone bodies** (beta-hydroxybutyrate, acetoacetate), which cross the blood-brain barrier and alter neurotransmitter balance; extended fasting also increases **brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF)**, modulates serotonin and dopamine systems, and may promote mild euphoria and perceptual changes. The **Desert Fathers** (3rd–5th century CE Egyptian monastics — Anthony, Pachomius, Evagrius) practiced extreme fasting combined with sleep deprivation and isolation to achieve spiritual transformation — their reported visions (demons, angels, divine light) are consistent with the neurological effects of caloric restriction and sensory deprivation. The **vision quest** traditions of North American Indigenous peoples (Lakota *hanblečeya*, Ojibwe *niimi'idiwin*) involve multi-day fasting alone in wilderness as a rite of passage, seeking visionary contact with spirit beings. Hindu **tapas** (literally "heat" — intense ascetic discipline including fasting, breath control, physical austerity) is understood as generating spiritual power; the most extreme practitioners historically included prolonged standing, holding one arm raised for years, or staring at the sun. Medieval Christian **fasting saints** (Catherine of Siena, Lidwina of Schiedam) reported surviving on virtually no food for extended periods — a phenomenon termed *anorexia mirabilis* ("miraculous loss of appetite"), now understood as likely involving hidden eating, medical complications, and hagiographic exaggeration, though the neurological effects of extreme caloric restriction on consciousness are documented. **Physiologically**, fasting beyond ~24–72 hours triggers significant metabolic shifts: hepatic glycogen depletion → increased fatty acid oxidation → ketogenesis → elevated circulating ketone bodies; these changes are associated with increased mental clarity by some practitioners but also with cognitive impairment, irritability, and eventually delirium in extreme cases. The relationship between fasting and altered consciousness is genuine and neurobiologically grounded, but the **interpretation** of fasting-induced experiences as supernatural is a cultural overlay on physiological events.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Scholarly Consensus)

### 1.1 Metabolic Neuroscience of Fasting
- **Ketogenic metabolism**: after ~12–16 hours of fasting, liver glycogen stores are depleted; the liver begins converting fatty acids to ketone bodies (beta-hydroxybutyrate [BHB], acetoacetate, acetone); BHB crosses the blood-brain barrier and serves as an alternative fuel source for neurons (Cahill, 2006, *Annual Review of Nutrition*)
- **Neurological effects of ketosis**:
  - BHB modulates GABA/glutamate balance, potentially reducing neural excitability (Yudkoff et al., 2007)
  - Fasting increases **BDNF** (brain-derived neurotrophic factor), which supports neuronal survival and may enhance mood (Mattson et al., 2018, *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*)
  - Mild ketosis is associated with subjective reports of mental clarity and mild euphoria in some individuals — though individual responses vary considerably
  - The ketogenic diet (medically supervised) has been used since the 1920s to treat epilepsy, confirming that ketone metabolism significantly affects brain function
- **Prolonged fasting effects**: beyond 3–5 days, protein catabolism increases, electrolyte imbalances develop, and cognitive function may decline; extreme starvation produces confusion, hallucinations, and delirium — consistent with reports from historical fasting practitioners

### 1.2 Desert Fathers and Early Christian Asceticism
- The **Desert Fathers** (3rd–5th century CE):
  - Anthony of Egypt (c. 251–356) retreated to the desert for ~20 years of solitary ascetic practice — his biographer Athanasius (*Life of Antony*, c. 360) reports vivid battles with demons and visions of angels, occurring under conditions of fasting, sleep deprivation, and extreme isolation
  - **Evagrius Ponticus** (345–399) developed a systematic psychology of ascetic experience, identifying eight *logismoi* (disturbing thoughts: gluttony, lust, avarice, sadness, anger, *acedia* [spiritual torpor], vainglory, pride) — a precursor to the seven deadly sins and a sophisticated phenomenology of the effects of deprivation on mental states
  - The *Apophthegmata Patrum* (Sayings of the Desert Fathers) record hundreds of accounts of altered states, visions, and extraordinary psychological experiences under ascetic conditions
  - Peter Brown (*The Body and Society*, 1988) documented how early Christian asceticism functioned as a technology of self-transformation, not merely self-punishment

### 1.3 Cross-Cultural Fasting Traditions
- **Islamic Ramadan**: month-long dawn-to-dusk fasting; neuroimaging published findings demonstrate metabolic and cognitive effects during Ramadan fasting (Chamari & Briki, 2017); spiritual practitioners report enhanced concentration and devotional experiences during late Ramadan
- **Jewish Yom Kippur**: 25-hour complete fast; serves as collective purification and atonement
- **Buddhist dhutanga** (austere practices): regulated but significant food restriction among forest monks; the Buddha himself reportedly practiced extreme fasting before his enlightenment, then rejected it as counterproductive — the "Middle Way" was explicitly a rejection of extreme asceticism
- **Hindu tapas**: the *Mahabharata* and Puranic literature describe ascetics generating such heat (*tapas*) through fasting and austerity that even the gods are threatened — reflecting the belief that self-denial generates spiritual power

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Vision Quest and Indigenous Fasting Practices
- **Lakota vision quest** (*hanblečeya*, "crying for a vision"):
  - Involves 1–4 days of fasting alone on a hilltop, without food or water, seeking visionary encounter with spirit beings who provide guidance, power, or a personal song
  - The physiological conditions (dehydration, caloric deprivation, isolation, sleep deprivation, exposure to elements) are precisely those known to alter consciousness neurobiologically
  - Joseph Epes Brown (*The Sacred Pipe*, 1953) documented Black Elk's account; the practice continues among contemporary Lakota spiritual practitioners
- **Sun Dance** (Lakota, Cheyenne, and other Plains peoples): involves fasting, dancing, and in some forms piercing of the chest skin with skewers — the combination of pain, fasting, and exhaustive dancing reliably produces altered states and visionary experience
- **Sweat lodge** (*inipi*): intense heat combined with fasting, prayer, and darkness creates conditions for altered consciousness — heat alone can produce lightheadedness and perceptual changes

### 2.2 Medieval Fasting Saints and Anorexia Mirabilis
- **Anorexia mirabilis** ("miraculous loss of appetite"):
  - Catherine of Siena (1347–1380) reportedly survived on almost no food for extended periods, consuming only the Eucharist; died at age 33, likely from the effects of extreme self-starvation
  - Lidwina of Schiedam (1380–1433) reportedly ate nothing for the last 19 years of her life — modern medical assessment suggests this is impossible without hidden food intake, though extreme caloric restriction can be sustained much longer than commonly assumed
  - Rudolph Bell (*Holy Anorexia*, 1985) analyzed these cases as a medieval form of anorexia nervosa, driven by religious rather than body-image motives; Caroline Walker Bynum (*Holy Feast and Holy Fast*, 1987) argued this interpretation was reductive, emphasizing the positive religious meanings of food refusal in women's spiritual authority
  - The altered states experienced by fasting saints (visions, ecstasies, stigmata) are consistent with the neurological effects of extreme caloric restriction — but this does not exhaust their cultural and phenomenological significance

### 2.3 Modern Fasting Research
- **Intermittent fasting** research (Mattson, de Cabo, Longo):
  - Time-restricted eating and periodic fasting show metabolic benefits (improved insulin sensitivity, reduced inflammation, enhanced autophagy) in controlled trials
  - Some studies report cognitive benefits during mild fasting states, though evidence is mixed and largely based on animal models (Mattson et al., 2018)
  - The relationship between fasting and mood is complex: short-term fasting may enhance mood through ketone-mediated effects; prolonged fasting typically produces irritability, difficulty concentrating, and eventually cognitive decline

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Fasting as Technology of Consciousness
- The cross-cultural ubiquity of fasting as a spiritual practice (appearing independently in virtually every major tradition) may reflect an empirical discovery: that controlled food deprivation reliably shifts brain chemistry in ways that facilitate perception beyond ordinary waking consciousness
- Whether the visions and insights produced under fasting conditions reflect genuine spiritual realities or are neurochemical artifacts is an irreducible question — the neurobiological mechanism does not determine the ontological status of the experience (see the general problem discussed in Y_3_04 — Mystical Experience Neuroscience)

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Inedia / Breatharianism
- **[DEBUNKED]** The claim that humans can survive indefinitely without food (inedia) or without food and water (breatharianism, promoted by Jasmuheen/Ellen Greve and others) has been repeatedly debunked — controlled tests have ended when subjects developed dangerous dehydration within days; several followers have died; the human body requires caloric and hydric intake to sustain life; no verified case of long-term survival without food exists under controlled observation (Randi, 2001; multiple journalistic investigations)
