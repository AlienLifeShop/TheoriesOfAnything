## QUICK SUMMARY

**Psychedelic research** has undergone a dramatic revival since ~2006, with major studies at Johns Hopkins, Imperial College London, NYU, and MAPS demonstrating the therapeutic potential of psilocybin, MDMA, and ayahuasca/DMT for treatment-resistant depression, PTSD, end-of-life anxiety, and addiction. Neuroimaging (fMRI, MEG) has revealed that psychedelics produce their consciousness-altering effects primarily through **serotonin 5-HT2A receptor agonism** and **disruption of the Default Mode Network (DMN)** — the brain network associated with the sense of self and habitual thought patterns. This document surveys the modern clinical evidence, the neurological mechanisms, and critically evaluates the connection between modern psychedelic experiences and ancient entheogen use documented in traditions from Vedic Soma to Eleusinian Mysteries to Mesoamerican mushroom cults. The ancient practices are real and well-attested; claims that psychedelics were the *sole origin* of religion or consciousness itself (Stoned Ape theory) remain speculative.

## §1 — MODERN CLINICAL EVIDENCE

### Key Clinical Trials

| Study | Substance | Condition | Results | Publication |
|-------|-----------|-----------|---------|-------------|
| **Griffiths et al. (2006)** | Psilocybin | Healthy volunteers — mystical experience | 67% rated the experience among the top 5 most meaningful in their lives; 14-month follow-up showed persistent positive changes | *Psychopharmacology* |
| **Griffiths et al. (2016)** | Psilocybin | Cancer-related existential distress | 80% showed clinically significant decreases in depressed mood and anxiety at 6 months | *Journal of Psychopharmacology* |
| **Carhart-Harris et al. (2016, 2021)** | Psilocybin | Treatment-resistant depression | Significant reduction in depression scores; 2021 trial showed non-inferiority to escitalopram (SSRI) | *Lancet Psychiatry*; *NEJM* |
| **Mitchell et al. (2021)** | MDMA-assisted therapy | PTSD | 67% no longer met PTSD diagnostic criteria after 3 sessions (vs. 32% placebo) | *Nature Medicine* |
| **Davis et al. (2021)** | Psilocybin | Major depressive disorder | 71% responded; 54% achieved remission at 4 weeks | *JAMA Psychiatry* |

- **FDA Breakthrough Therapy Designation:** Psilocybin for treatment-resistant depression (Compass Pathways, 2018) and for major depressive disorder (Usona Institute, 2019). MDMA for PTSD (MAPS, 2017). Failed FDA advisory committee vote in 2024 due to trial design concerns, not efficacy doubts.
- **Tier 1** — Published in New England Journal of Medicine, JAMA, Nature Medicine, Lancet

### Neurological Mechanism

- **5-HT2A receptor agonism:** All classical psychedelics (psilocybin/psilocin, LSD, DMT, mescaline) are **serotonin 5-HT2A receptor agonists**. The profound subjective effects are abolished by 5-HT2A antagonists (ketanserin), proving this receptor is the primary mechanism
- **Default Mode Network (DMN) disruption:** fMRI studies (Carhart-Harris et al., 2012) show psychedelics **reduce activity and connectivity** within the DMN — the brain network active during self-referential thought, mind-wandering, and autobiographical memory. This correlates with reported "ego dissolution"
- **Increased global connectivity:** Simultaneously, psychedelics increase connectivity between brain regions that do not normally communicate — producing a more "entropic" brain state (Carhart-Harris, "Entropic Brain Hypothesis," 2014)
- **The connectome under psilocybin:** Petri et al. (2014, *Journal of the Royal Society Interface*) showed that psilocybin creates novel network connections that disappear after the drug wears off — visualized as a "hyperconnected" network topology

## §3 — DMT AND THE "SPIRIT MOLECULE" DEBATE

### Rick Strassman's Studies

- **Strassman (2001):** Conducted DEA-approved clinical trial with intravenous DMT at University of New Mexico (1990–1995) — the first US psychedelic study in 20+ years
- **Findings:** Participants reported vivid encounters with "entities," "beings," geometric spaces, and experiences described as "more real than real." Many experiences resembled near-death and mystical experience reports
- **The "spirit molecule" claim:** Strassman hypothesized that endogenous DMT (produced in the human body) may be released during birth, death, and mystical experiences. This remains **unproven**:
  - DMT has been detected in rat brain tissue (Barker et al., 2013, *Biomedical Chromatography*) — but at trace levels
  - No study has demonstrated DMT release during death, birth, or NDEs in humans
  - The pineal gland origin claim is unsupported — DMT synthesis enzymes are found in the lungs, liver, and other tissues, not specifically concentrated in the pineal
- **Tier 2** — The clinical phenomenology is documented; the endogenous release theory is speculative (Tier 3)

## §4 — COUNTER-ARGUMENTS AND CRITICAL ASSESSMENT

### The "Stoned Ape" Hypothesis

- **Terence McKenna (1992, *Food of the Gods*):** Proposed that psilocybin mushroom consumption by *Homo erectus* on the African savanna catalyzed the evolution of human language, consciousness, and culture
- **Problems:**
  - No archaeological evidence of Paleolithic psilocybin use
  - The mechanism by which a temporary drug experience would produce heritable genetic change is not explained (this would require Lamarckian inheritance, which is discredited)
  - Evolutionary psychology already provides mechanisms for language/consciousness evolution without psychedelics
  - The timeline is vague — "some time in East Africa" — with no testable predictions
- **Tier 3–4** — Interesting speculation; not a scientific theory (no testable predictions, no evidence)

### Pharmacological Reductionism

- **The claim:** That all religious/mystical experience is "just" neurochemistry — psychedelics prove that visions of God are serotonin events
- **Counter:** This commits the **genetic fallacy** — knowing the mechanism of an experience does not determine its truth value or meaning. The fact that psilocybin has a known receptor mechanism does not prove or disprove the ontological status of what is experienced
- **Scholarly position:** Griffiths et al. note that participants' mystical experiences under psilocybin were *indistinguishable* from "naturally occurring" mystical experiences on validated measures (Hood's Mysticism Scale) — this cuts both ways

### The "Soma Was Everything" Problem

- Multiple substances have been proposed as Soma/Haoma — fly agaric, ephedra, cannabis, Syrian rue, magic mushrooms. The lack of consensus after 50+ years of scholarship suggests the identification may be unresolvable with current evidence
- Generalizing from "some ancient rituals involved psychedelics" to "all ancient religion was entheogenic" is an overextension not supported by evidence

## Counter-Arguments & Criticisms

Modern psychedelic research has generated both enthusiasm and methodological criticism. Muthukumaraswamy et al. (2021) argue that inadequate blinding—participants can easily identify whether they received a psychedelic or placebo—undermines many clinical trial results. The "mystical experience" hypothesis has been challenged by Olson (2021), whose research on non-hallucinogenic psychoplastogens suggests neuroplasticity rather than subjective experience may drive therapeutic effects. MAPS MDMA therapy trials have faced FDA scrutiny over methodology and adverse event reporting. Breeksema (2022) warns against "psychedelic exceptionalism" that overpromises therapeutic benefits. Historical parallels to the 1960s, when premature adoption contributed to regulatory prohibition, inform cautionary perspectives. The field acknowledges the need for larger, better-controlled trials before psychedelic therapies can be recommended as standard treatments.
