## QUICK SUMMARY

Several **animal toxins** and **plant poisons** produce dramatic altered states of consciousness, and their use in ritual, medicine, and folklore constitutes a significant chapter in the relationship between humans and psychoactive substances. Unlike classical psychedelics (which act primarily on serotonin 5-HT2A receptors), toxin-induced altered states operate through diverse mechanisms — sodium channel blockade, cholinergic disruption, glutamate modulation, and others — and often carry greater physical danger. **Bufo alvarius** (the Sonoran Desert toad, now classified *Incilius alvarius*) produces a venom containing **5-MeO-DMT** (5-methoxy-N,N-dimethyltryptamine), one of the most potent psychoactive substances known — when vaporized and inhaled, it induces within seconds a brief (~15–20 minute) but overwhelmingly intense experience often described as "ego dissolution," "white light," "cosmic unity," and "the most profound experience of my life"; it has attracted growing ceremonial use and clinical research interest for treatment-resistant depression and end-of-life anxiety (Uthaug et al., 2019). **Tetrodotoxin (TTX)** — found in pufferfish (*fugu*), blue-ringed octopus, and certain newts — blocks voltage-gated sodium channels, producing progressive paralysis while potentially preserving consciousness; **Wade Davis** (*The Serpent and the Rainbow*, 1985) proposed that Haitian *vodou* "zombie" creation involved TTX administration to simulate death followed by revival with datura — an ethnobotanically plausible but scientifically controversial hypothesis. **Tropane alkaloids** (atropine, scopolamine, hyoscyamine) from **Solanaceae** plants (henbane/*Hyoscyamus niger*, belladonna/*Atropa belladonna*, datura/*Datura stramonium*, mandrake/*Mandragora officinarum*) produce true hallucinations (indistinguishable from reality, unlike psychedelic visual distortions), delirium, memory blackout, and the sensation of flying — these plants are strongly implicated in European **witchcraft "flying ointments"** (Harner, 1973; Müller, 1998), which were reportedly applied to the skin (often to mucous membranes via broomstick — the literal origin of the witch-on-broomstick image). **Kambo** (secretion of the giant monkey frog, *Phyllomedusa bicolor*) is used by Amazonian peoples (Matsés, Mayoruna) as a hunting aid and purification ritual — applied to small burns on the skin, it produces intense vomiting, diarrhea, tachycardia, facial swelling, and an altered state described as heightened sensory perception; the active components include dermorphin (a potent μ-opioid agonist) and phyllocaerulein (a cholecystokinin analog). **Ibogaine** (from *Tabernanthe iboga*, Central African) produces an extended (~24–36 hour) visionary state used in Bwiti initiation ceremonies (Gabon); it has generated clinical interest for treating opioid addiction, with observational studies showing significant reduction in withdrawal symptoms and craving, though it carries cardiac risk (QT prolongation) and deaths have occurred.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Scholarly Consensus)

### 1.1 Bufo alvarius / 5-MeO-DMT
- **5-MeO-DMT**: a tryptamine psychedelic that acts as a potent agonist at serotonin 5-HT2A and 5-HT1A receptors; also found in certain Anadenanthera and Virola plant preparations used by South American Indigenous peoples
- **Bufo alvarius** venom: contains both 5-MeO-DMT and bufotenin (5-HO-DMT); when dried and vaporized, the onset is within seconds and the peak experience lasts 15–20 minutes
- **Phenomenology** (Uthaug et al., 2019, *Psychopharmacology*): participants reported intense mystical experiences, ego dissolution, and unity; a survey of 362 users found improvements in satisfaction with life, mindfulness, and reductions in depression and anxiety
- **Safety concerns**: 5-MeO-DMT can produce severe hypertensive crisis, serotonin syndrome (especially with concurrent SSRI use), and intense psychological distress; deaths have been reported in ceremonial contexts; the Sonoran Desert toad population is threatened by overharvesting

### 1.2 Tetrodotoxin and Sodium Channel Blockade
- **Tetrodotoxin (TTX)**: one of the most potent non-protein toxins known; blocks voltage-gated sodium channels (Nav1.1–1.7) on the external surface, preventing action potential propagation → progressive muscular paralysis, respiratory failure, and death at sufficient doses
- Found in pufferfish (*Takifugu* spp.), blue-ringed octopus (*Hapalochlaena* spp.), certain newts (*Taricha*), and some crabs — the toxin is actually produced by symbiotic bacteria (e.g., *Vibrio*, *Pseudomonas*)
- **Fugu**: a Japanese delicacy prepared by licensed chefs who remove the most toxic organs (liver, ovaries, skin); fatalities still occur (~20–40 deaths/year in Japan historically, declining with regulation); the sensation of trace TTX on the lips and tongue is described as a tingling numbness
- TTX is used as a neuroscience research tool for sodium channel studies; clinical trials for chronic pain (Nav1.7 blockade) are ongoing

### 1.3 Tropane Alkaloids: Solanaceae
- **Tropane alkaloids** (atropine, scopolamine [hyoscine], hyoscyamine):
  - Mechanism: competitive antagonism at muscarinic acetylcholine receptors → anticholinergic effects (dry mouth, dilated pupils, tachycardia, urinary retention, hyperthermia, delirium)
  - Differ fundamentally from serotonergic psychedelics: produce **true hallucinations** (visual and auditory experiences indistinguishable from reality — patients interact with hallucinated people and objects as if real), **complete amnesia** for the experience, and **delirium** (confusion, disorientation, loss of insight)
  - **Datura** (*Datura stramonium*, jimsonweed): used ritually by some Native American groups (Chumash *toloache*, southwestern vision quest practices) under careful elder guidance; unsupervised use is extremely dangerous — hyperthermia, rhabdomyolysis, and death occur
  - **Scopolamine**: used criminally in South America ("burundanga" — administered to victims to produce compliant delirium and amnesia, facilitating robbery or assault)

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Witchcraft Flying Ointments
- **European witchcraft literature** (15th–17th century) describes ointments (*unguentum sabbati*) applied to the body (particularly armpits, genitals — areas with high percutaneous absorption) that induced the sensation of **flying** and attendance at the witches' sabbath
- **Michael Harner** (1973) and other ethnobotanists identified the likely ingredients as tropane-alkaloid-containing Solanaceae: henbane (*Hyoscyamus niger*), belladonna (*Atropa belladonna*), mandrake (*Mandragora officinarum*), and sometimes aconite (*Aconitum* — a sodium channel activator producing tingling and numbness)
- The "flying" sensation is pharmacologically plausible: tropane alkaloid delirium produces vivid hallucinations including the sensation of movement and flight; application to mucous membranes (vaginal — via the broomstick) would provide good absorption while reducing gastrointestinal toxicity
- Johann Weyer (1563, *De Praestigiis Daemonum*) — a physician — proposed that accused witches experienced pharmacologically induced delirium rather than actual supernatural transport; this was one of the earliest rationalist critiques of witch persecution

### 2.2 Wade Davis and the Zombie Hypothesis
- **Wade Davis** (*The Serpent and the Rainbow*, 1985; *Passage of Darkness*, 1988):
  - Proposed that Haitian *vodou* "zombie creation" involved: (1) administration of **TTX** (from pufferfish) to simulate death (paralysis with possible preserved consciousness), (2) burial and retrieval, (3) subsequent administration of **datura** (*Datura stramonium*, called *concombre zombi* in Haiti) to produce compliant delirium and amnesia
  - The social context — a community that believes in zombification, with *bokors* (sorcerers) wielding social power — would maintain the zombie's subjugation through fear and ongoing drug administration
- **Critique**: Kao and Yasumoto (1986) analyzed Davis's powder samples and found extremely variable TTX concentrations — some samples contained no detectable TTX; the pharmacological plausibility is debated (TTX doses sufficient to simulate death are very close to lethal doses); most toxicologists consider the hypothesis intriguing but insufficiently supported by chemical evidence

### 2.3 Ibogaine and Addiction Treatment
- **Ibogaine** (principal alkaloid of *Tabernanthe iboga*):
  - Produces an extended altered state (24–36 hours) with three phases: acute visionary (vivid imagery, life review), evaluative (reflection, insight), and residual stimulation
  - Used in **Bwiti** initiation ceremonies (Gabon, Cameroon): large doses produce the visionary state, interpreted as communication with ancestors; initiates report "seeing" their life story and receiving ancestral guidance
  - **Anti-addiction potential**: Lotsof (1985, patent) reported that a single ibogaine dose eliminated opioid withdrawal symptoms and craving; observational studies (Mash et al., 2000; Brown & Alper, 2018) support significant reductions in opioid withdrawal severity and craving
  - **Risks**: ibogaine prolongs the QT interval (cardiac electrical conduction), producing risk of fatal ventricular arrhythmia; at least 30+ deaths have been reported in unregulated settings; clinical development requires cardiac monitoring and patient screening

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Kambo and Hunting Enhancement
- **Kambo** (*sapo* — secretion of *Phyllomedusa bicolor*):
  - Used by Matsés, Mayoruna, and other Amazonian peoples: applied to small burns on the skin, producing intense vomiting, tachycardia, edema, and a subsequent state described as heightened sensory awareness, reduced need for food and water, and improved hunting success
  - Active peptides include: **dermorphin** (40× more potent than morphine at μ-opioid receptors), **deltorphin** (potent δ-opioid agonist), **phyllomedusin** (tachykinin — vasodilation, secretion), **phyllocaerulein** (cholecystokinin analog — gallbladder contraction, satiety)
  - Whether kambo genuinely enhances hunting ability (beyond placebo/motivational effects and purging of parasites/toxins) is unverified — claimed mechanisms include heightened visual acuity and reduced scent emission (from vomiting/purging), but no controlled studies exist
  - Kambo has spread to Western "neo-shamanic" contexts with safety concerns — deaths from hyponatremia (excessive water consumption during kambo sessions) have been reported

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Toad Licking as Safe Recreation
- **[DEBUNKED]** The popular-culture notion that "licking toads" produces a safe psychedelic experience is misleading and dangerous — Bufo alvarius venom contains both 5-MeO-DMT (psychoactive) and cardiac glycosides (bufadienolides — toxic to the heart); oral ingestion can cause fatal cardiac arrhythmia; the psychoactive compound is typically accessed by drying and vaporizing the venom, not oral consumption; other toad species (non-Bufo alvarius) do not contain 5-MeO-DMT and their venom is simply toxic without psychoactive effects
