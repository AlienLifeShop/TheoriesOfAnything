## QUICK SUMMARY

**Déjà vu** — from the French "already seen" — is the subjective, compelling sensation that a present experience has been previously encountered, despite the experiencer's knowledge that the situation is objectively novel. First named by **Émile Boirac** (1876) and systematically investigated by psychical researcher **Frederic Myers** (1895) and neurologist **Hughlings Jackson** (1888), déjà vu occurs in approximately **60–70% of healthy adults** at least once in their lives and is particularly common in young adults (ages 15–25). The phenomenon represents one of a family of **anomalous memory experiences** — including **jamais vu** (the uncanny feeling that a familiar situation is entirely new), **déjà entendu** (already heard), **déjà visité** (already visited), and **presque vu** (the "tip-of-the-tongue" state). The dominant modern neuroscientific explanation follows a **dual-process recognition model**: normal recognition memory involves two distinct components — **familiarity** (a rapid, automatic sense of having encountered something before) and **recollection** (the slower, contextual retrieval of where, when, and how — source memory). Déjà vu may occur when the **familiarity signal fires without corresponding recollection** — producing the anomalous experience of something feeling intensely familiar while simultaneously being recognized as new. The phenomenon has an important clinical dimension: **temporal lobe epilepsy (TLE)** frequently produces déjà vu as part of the aura preceding seizures — the temporal lobe (particularly the **hippocampus**, **entorhinal cortex**, and **perirhinal cortex**) is the brain region most intimately involved in both recognition memory and the generation of déjà vu experiences.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 Prevalence and Demographics
- **Déjà vu prevalence**: approximately 60–70% of healthy adults report having experienced it at least once; 30–40% report experiencing it several times per year
- **Age pattern**: most common between ages 15–25, declining in frequency after age 25; this is counterintuitive (older adults have more memories to match against), suggesting the mechanism is related to neural maturation/connectivity rather than memory storage
- **Correlates**: more frequent in people who are more educated, who travel more, who remember their dreams, who hold more liberal political views, and who experience more fatigue and stress — probably reflecting greater attentional and metacognitive self-monitoring
- **No psychopathological association** in non-epileptic populations: occasional déjà vu in healthy adults is entirely normal and does not indicate neurological disease

### 1.2 Temporal Lobe Epilepsy Connection
- **Hughlings Jackson** (1888): first clinical description of déjà vu associated with temporal lobe seizures — described it as part of the "dreamy state" aura preceding temporal lobe seizures
- In **temporal lobe epilepsy (TLE)**, déjà vu is reported by approximately **60–80%** of patients as a component of the ictal aura — it is so characteristic that frequent, prolonged, or intensifying déjà vu episodes are considered a potential indicator for neurological evaluation
- **Electrical stimulation studies** (Penfield, 1958; Bancaud et al., 1994): direct electrical stimulation of the **hippocampus, entorhinal cortex**, and **perirhinal cortex** during neurosurgery reliably elicits déjà vu experiences — confirming the mesial temporal lobe as the neural substrate

### 1.3 Dual-Process Recognition Memory Theory
- **Brown (2003, 2004)**: comprehensive reviews proposing that déjà vu results from a **mismatch between familiarity and recollection** — two separable components of recognition memory
  - **Familiarity**: fast, automatic, context-free sense of having encountered a stimulus before (mediated by perirhinal cortex)
  - **Recollection**: slower, deliberate retrieval of contextual and associative details — when, where, and how the stimulus was encountered (mediated by hippocampus)
- Déjà vu occurs when familiarity fires **without recollection** — the experience is familiar but cannot be "placed," generating the characteristic paradoxical sensation
- **Cleary (2008)**: experimental demonstration that déjà vu can be reliably triggered in the laboratory using scenes that have the same spatial layout as previously studied scenes (the Global Matching model) — showing that partial overlap between new and stored configurations can produce the familiarity signal without source memory

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Related Anomalous Memory Phenomena
- **Jamais vu**: the opposite of déjà vu — a situation that should be familiar feels entirely novel, strange, and unfamiliar; less common but also associated with temporal lobe pathology; can be induced experimentally through word satiation (repeating a common word until it loses meaning — "semantic satiation")
- **Déjà vécu** ("already lived"): a more immersive variant in which the entire ongoing experience (not just visual recognition) feels as though it has been previously lived through; may occur in older adults with confabulation and is clinically distinct from simple déjà vu
- **Presque vu** ("almost seen"): the tip-of-the-tongue state — the intense, frustrating sense of almost being able to recall something; distinct from déjà vu but similarly involves recognition/retrieval mismatch

### 2.2 Neural Network Models
- **O'Connor and Moulin** (2010, 2013): proposed that déjà vu involves not just a false familiarity signal but also a **metacognitive monitoring process** — the prefrontal cortex detects the conflict between the familiarity signal and the absence of recollection, generating the characteristic subjective awareness that the familiarity is erroneous; this explains why déjà vu is rare in older adults (who may have reduced metacognitive monitoring) and in children (who have not yet developed robust metacognitive capacities)

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Déjà Vu as Evidence of Past Lives or Precognition
- Reports of "prophetic déjà vu" (the sense that one has foreseen the current situation in a dream) and interpretations of déjà vu as evidence of reincarnation or premonition have a long history in popular culture and some parapsychological research — there is no scientific evidence supporting a paranormal explanation; the phenomenon is well-explained by recognition memory mechanisms and source monitoring errors
