### 1.4 The 19-Year Cycle and Intercalation

The **Metonic cycle** â€” the discovery that 235 synodic months â‰ˆ 19 tropical years (to within ~2 hours) â€” is conventionally attributed to the Athenian astronomer **Meton** in **432 BCE**. ### 2.2 The Five Epagomenal Days

### 2.4 Sirius, Agriculture, and the Nile

The heliacal rising of Sirius was not merely an astronomical curiosity â€” it was a matter of **life and death**. The Nile flood (*Akhet*) deposited the fertile silt on which Egyptian agriculture depended. The connection between Sirius and the Nile flood likely arose from the coincidence that at the latitude of Memphis (~30Â° N), the heliacal rising of Sirius occurred in mid-July â€” close to the onset of the annual inundation. The Egyptians did not necessarily believe Sirius *caused* the flood, but they recognized it as a reliable harbinger.

This connection resonates with the Dogon people of Mali (see **C24**), who attribute extraordinary significance to Sirius and reportedly possess knowledge of Sirius B (a white dwarf invisible to the naked eye). Whether the Dogon Sirius traditions derive from ancient Egyptian contact, independent observation, or 20th-century contamination remains one of the most contested debates in ethnography (Marcel Griaule and Germaine Dieterlen, *Le Renard PÃ¢le*, 1965; skeptic: Walter van Beek, "Dogon Restudied," *Current Anthropology*, 1991).

### 2.5 Decans and Star Clocks

The Egyptians divided the night sky into **36 decans** â€” groups of stars whose successive heliacal risings marked 10-day intervals (the Egyptian "week," or *tp tr*). Each decan ruled over one 10-day period, and the full set of 36 decans Ã— 10 days = 360 days + 5 epagomenal days = 365.

**Star clocks** (also called **diagonal star tables** or **decanal clocks**) appear on the lids of Middle Kingdom coffins (~2055â€“1650 BCE), most famously on the coffin of **Meshet** (Cairo Museum, CG 28118) and the coffin of **Idy** from Asyut. Later, during the New Kingdom (~1550â€“1070 BCE), decanal clocks evolved into **transit star clocks** (Ramesside period), which tracked stars crossing the meridian rather than rising, and eventually into the **Ramesside star maps** painted on the ceilings of royal tombs (e.g., the tomb of Senmut, KV 17 / Seti I).

The decan system is the direct ancestor of the modern division of the sky into 360Â° â€” further evidence of Egyptian influence on Hellenistic and modern astronomy.

### 2.6 The Canopus Decree

The **Canopus Decree** (238 BCE), issued under **Ptolemy III Euergetes**, is a trilingual inscription (hieroglyphic, Demotic, Greek) discovered at Tanis in 1866. Among its provisions, it explicitly acknowledges the Â¼-day discrepancy of the civil calendar and mandates the addition of a **6th epagomenal day every 4 years** â€” essentially proposing a leap year.

### 4.6 Megalithic Calendars

Some of the oldest known astronomical alignments predate writing entirely, raising the possibility that calendar-keeping is far older than previously assumed.

**Key sites:**

**Nabta Playa (Egyptian Western Desert, ~7000â€“6500 BCE):**

**Newgrange (Ireland, ~3200 BCE):**
- Passage tomb with a **roof box** above the entrance engineered so that sunlight penetrates the 19-meter passage and illuminates the chamber floor for ~17 minutes at **winter solstice** sunrise
- This is not disputed â€” it has been observed repeatedly in modern times and is one of the most precisely documented archaeoastronomical alignments in the world

- The **Aubrey Holes** (56 post-holes in a circle): Gerald Hawkins (*Stonehenge Decoded*, 1965) proposed these were used to predict eclipses â€” controversial but not definitively disproven

### 5.2 "Hamlet's Mill" and Calendar Mathematics

Calendar systems, in this framework, served as the **operational interface** for precessional knowledge. **Critical assessment:** *Hamlet's Mill* is methodologically problematic â€” the authors cherry-pick data, ignore counter-examples, and make claims that often exceed the evidence. The book is best understood as a provocative hypothesis-generator rather than a proven thesis.
