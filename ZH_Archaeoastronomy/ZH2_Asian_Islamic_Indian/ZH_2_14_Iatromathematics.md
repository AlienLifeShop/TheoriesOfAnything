## QUICK SUMMARY

**Iatromathematics** (Greek: *iatros* = healer + *mathēmatikos* = astrologer/mathematician) was the systematic integration of astrology with medical diagnosis and treatment — a dominant medical paradigm in the Western world from antiquity through the 17th century. Its central concept was the **macrocosm–microcosm** correspondence: the human body mirrors the cosmos, each body part governed by a zodiacal sign (the **Zodiac Man** or *homo signorum*), each organ influenced by a planet, and each disease conditioned by celestial configurations at the time of its onset. In this system, **Aries** rules the head, **Taurus** the throat, descending through the zodiac to **Pisces** governing the feet — the *melothesia* (body-mapping). Physicians used planetary positions to determine the **critical days** of illness (drawing on Hippocratic/Galenic theory), selected herbs based on **planet-herb correspondences** (as systematized by Nicholas Culpeper in the 1650s), and cast **decumbiture charts** (horoscopes for the moment a patient took to bed) to diagnose and prognosticate. Far from marginal, astrological medicine was taught at major European universities (Bologna, Paris, Montpellier, Padua) from the 13th to 17th centuries, and physicians were expected to consult ephemerides before prescribing treatments. The system persisted until the rise of mechanistic anatomy (Vesalius), experimental physiology (Harvey), and the Enlightenment rejection of celestial causation — though its imagery survives in calendar illustrations, the term "influenza" (from Italian *influenza celeste* — celestial influence), and folk herbalism traditions.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Experimentally Confirmed)

### 1.1 The Zodiac Man (Homo Signorum) and Melothesia
- The **Zodiac Man** — an illustration showing a human body with each zodiacal sign assigned to a body region — is one of the most widely reproduced images in medieval and Renaissance manuscripts:
  - The canonical scheme of **zodiacal melothesia** (from Greek *melos* = limb + *thesis* = placement):
    - **Aries** → head and face
    - **Taurus** → throat and neck
    - **Gemini** → arms, shoulders, lungs
    - **Cancer** → chest, stomach, breasts
    - **Leo** → heart, upper back
    - **Virgo** → intestines, abdomen
    - **Libra** → kidneys, lower back
    - **Scorpio** → reproductive organs, bladder
    - **Sagittarius** → hips, thighs
    - **Capricorn** → knees
    - **Aquarius** → shins, ankles
    - **Pisces** → feet
  - This correspondence appears as early as **Manilius** (*Astronomica*, ~1st century CE, Book 2) and is traceable to earlier Greco-Egyptian sources
  - The Zodiac Man image appeared in virtually every medieval *Book of Hours*, almanac, and medical manual — the *Très Riches Heures du Duc de Berry* (1413–1416) contains a famous example

### 1.2 Planetary Melothesia and the Seven Planets
- A parallel scheme assigned the seven classical planets to body organs, metals, and temperaments:
  - **Sun** → heart, vitality
  - **Moon** → brain, bodily fluids
  - **Mercury** → nervous system, lungs
  - **Venus** → kidneys, reproductive system
  - **Mars** → muscles, blood, bile
  - **Jupiter** → liver, growth
  - **Saturn** → bones, spleen, melancholy
- Both zodiacal and planetary melothesiae were synthesized with **humoral theory** (the four humors: blood, phlegm, yellow bile, black bile — each associated with a temperament and an element/quality pair)

### 1.3 University Medical Astrology (13th–17th Centuries)
- Astrological medicine was not folk practice — it was **taught at major European universities**:
  - The **University of Bologna** (founded 1088) had a chair of astrology from the 13th century on — physicians were expected to have astrological competency
  - The **University of Paris** medical faculty issued an official astrological explanation for the Black Death (1348), attributing the plague to a conjunction of Saturn, Jupiter, and Mars in Aquarius on March 20, 1345
  - **Montpellier**, **Padua**, and **Kraków** similarly included astrology in the medical curriculum
  - Physicians were expected to consult **ephemerides** (planetary position tables) and could not in good conscience prescribe treatments when the Moon was in the sign governing the affected body part — e.g., no surgery to the head when the Moon is in Aries

### 1.4 The Decumbiture Chart
- A **decumbiture** was a horoscope cast for the moment a patient took to bed (Latin *decumbere* = to lie down):
  - Used to diagnose the disease, predict its course, and determine favorable times for treatment
  - The English physician **Richard Saunders** (*Astrological Judgment and Practice of Physick*, 1677) and **Nicholas Culpeper** (*Astrological Judgement of Diseases*, 1651) provided detailed instructions
  - The decumbiture was considered essential information — like a modern patient intake form

### 1.5 Critical Days
- The Hippocratic/Galenic concept of **critical days** — specific days in an illness when a crisis or turning point was expected (days 7, 14, 20, 21, 27, etc.) — was explained astrologically:
  - The Moon's aspects to its position at the onset of illness (squares on days 7/21, opposition on day 14, etc.) were held to determine critical days
  - Galen himself hinted at lunar influences — later commentators developed the theory fully

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Supported by Multiple Scholars / Strong Circumstantial Evidence)

### 2.1 Planet-Herb Correspondences (Culpeper)
- **Nicholas Culpeper** (1616–1654) systematized the correspondence between planets, herbs, and diseases in his hugely influential *The English Physician* (1652) and *Complete Herbal*:
  - Each herb was assigned a planetary ruler — e.g., rosemary = Sun, mugwort = Venus, comfrey = Saturn
  - Treatment followed the principle of **sympathy** (using herbs of the same planetary ruler as the disease's planet) or **antipathy** (using herbs of the opposing planet)
  - Culpeper democratized medical knowledge by publishing in English rather than Latin — his works were bestsellers for over a century and remain in print
  - Modern scholarship (Tobyn, 2011) treats Culpeper's system as a coherent framework within its historical context — it is incorrect to label it simply "superstition" without understanding its theoretical basis

### 2.2 Islamic Iatromathematics
- The Islamic medical tradition inherited and developed Greco-Roman astrological medicine:
  - **Abū Ma'shar** (787–886 CE) — his *Great Introduction to Astrology* influenced both Islamic and Latin medical astrology
  - Medical astrology was practiced alongside Galenic humoral medicine — the two systems were considered complementary
  - Some Islamic scholars (e.g., **Ibn Sīnā** / Avicenna) were skeptical of detailed astrological prediction but accepted general celestial influences on health

### 2.3 Paracelsus and Astrological Medicine
- **Paracelsus** (1493–1541) rejected humoral theory but retained astrology within his reformed medical system:
  - His doctrine of **correspondences** linked specific diseases to specific celestial bodies and their corresponding minerals/metals
  - "A physician who does not know astrology cannot properly call himself a physician" — attributed to Paracelsus
  - The Paracelsian tradition merged astrology with alchemy and iatrochemistry — influencing medical practice into the 17th century

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Limited Evidence / Emerging Hypotheses)

### 3.1 Ancient Egyptian and Mesopotamian Origins
- Whether the melothesia system originated in Mesopotamia or Egypt is debated:
  - The Babylonian tradition associated body parts with deities (not zodiacal signs — the zodiac postdates most Babylonian medical texts)
  - Egyptian *Papyrus Ebers* (c. 1550 BCE) contains medical prescriptions linked to decanal stars — a possible proto-iatromathematics
  - The fully developed zodiacal melothesia appears to be a Hellenistic synthesis (~3rd–1st centuries BCE)

### 3.2 Survival in Folk Medicine
- Planet-herb correspondences and zodiacal body-part associations persist in various folk and alternative medicine traditions:
  - Biodynamic agriculture (Rudolf Steiner) assigns planting schedules based on zodiacal/planetary positions — a modern derivative of the same conceptual framework
  - Some modern herbalists still use planetary correspondences — though detached from systematic astrological diagnosis

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — Fringe / Not Supported by Evidence)

### 4.1 Celestial Bodies Directly Influence Health
- No physical mechanism exists by which planetary or zodiacal positions could affect human health:
  - Gravitational and electromagnetic effects of distant planets on the human body are negligibly small compared to local environmental factors
  - Controlled studies have found no correlation between birth sign and susceptibility to specific diseases (Shulman and Alpert, 1973; Mayo, White, and Eysenck, 1978 — initial apparent effects disappeared under proper controls)

### 4.2 Revival of Medical Astrology as Evidence-Based Practice
- Attempts to validate astrological medicine through statistical studies have consistently failed — the system has historical and anthropological significance but no demonstrated diagnostic or therapeutic value
