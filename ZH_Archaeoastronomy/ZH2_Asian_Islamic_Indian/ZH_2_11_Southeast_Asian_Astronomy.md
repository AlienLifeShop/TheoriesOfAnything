## QUICK SUMMARY

The astronomical traditions of **Southeast Asia** — Thailand (Siam), Myanmar (Burma), Cambodia (Khmer), Java, Bali, and the wider Malay-Indonesian archipelago — represent a distinctive synthesis of **Indian**, indigenous, and (in later periods) **Chinese** astronomical knowledge. The dominant influence is **Indian**: the transmission of Indian astronomical texts and methods (particularly the *Sūryasiddhānta* tradition) beginning in the early centuries CE brought to Southeast Asia the concepts of the **navagraha** (nine celestial bodies — Sun, Moon, five visible planets, plus the lunar nodes Rahu and Ketu), Indian planetary computational methods, the zodiac, and the framework of ages (*yugas*). The monumental architecture of Southeast Asia encodes astronomical knowledge: **Angkor Wat** (12th century, Cambodia) incorporates solar alignments, numerical symbolism related to astronomical cycles, and a bas-relief alignment that may encode cosmological time; **Borobudur** (9th century, Java) has been analyzed for possible astronomical orientations; and Burmese pagodas often incorporate navagraha shrines. Each region developed distinctive calendar systems — the **Thai**, **Burmese**, and **Khmer** calendars are all lunisolar systems derived from Indian prototypes but diverging over centuries. **Javanese** and **Balinese** calendar systems uniquely combine a 210-day *pawukon* cycle (indigenous) with a lunisolar *saka* year (Indian-derived). Southeast Asian astronomy is understudied compared to its Indian, Chinese, and Islamic counterparts — but represents a crucial zone of cultural transmission and local innovation.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Experimentally Confirmed)

### 1.1 Indian Astronomical Transmission
- Indian astronomical knowledge arrived in Southeast Asia through **cultural Indianization** (not military conquest) beginning ~1st–5th centuries CE:
  - Key transmitted elements:
    - **Sūryasiddhānta** computational methods: mean and true planetary longitudes, eclipse prediction, sine tables
    - **Navagraha** concept: nine celestial influences — Sun (*Sūrya/Āditya*), Moon (*Candra*), Mars (*Maṅgala*), Mercury (*Budha*), Jupiter (*Bṛhaspati*), Venus (*Śukra*), Saturn (*Śani*), plus the ascending and descending **lunar nodes** (Rahu and Ketu — conceived as shadow planets responsible for eclipses)
    - **Zodiacal system**: the 12 rāśi (zodiacal signs) and 27 nakṣatras (lunar mansions)
  - These elements were adopted and adapted throughout Southeast Asia — but each region modified the received system over time:
    - Different parameter values for planetary mean motions
    - Different epoch dates for calendar computation
    - Different intercalation schedules for lunisolar calendars

### 1.2 Angkor Wat Astronomical Dimensions
- **Angkor Wat** (built under Suryavarman II, ~1113–1145/1150 CE):
  - The temple faces **west** — unusual for Hindu temples (which typically face east) — interpreted as either funerary symbolism or as an alignment toward the setting Sun
  - **Equinoctial alignment**: at the spring equinox, the Sun rises directly over the central tower when viewed from a specific point on the western causeway (confirmed by Stencel, Gifford, and Morón, 1976)
  - **Numerical astronomy**: Mannikka (1996) proposed that the temple's dimensions encode astronomical periods — the main causeway length (~1,500 *hat*) corresponding to the number of years in certain yuga subdivisions; the total number of bas-relief *apsaras* (celestial dancers) encoding calendrical constants:
    - Some of these numerical correspondences are compelling; others are criticized as post hoc selection from a large building with many measurable dimensions
  - The **churning of the ocean of milk** bas-relief has been interpreted as an astronomical allegory (Milky Way, eclipse mythology involving Rahu/Ketu)

### 1.3 Southeast Asian Lunisolar Calendars
- The major Southeast Asian calendars share an Indian-derived lunisolar structure:
  - **Burmese calendar** (*Thingyan*): lunisolar, with the year beginning in Tagu (March/April), months alternating 29/30 days, intercalary months and days added to maintain synchronization; era based on the **Kali Yuga** or Burmese Sakkaraj era (beginning 638 CE)
  - **Thai calendar**: historically lunisolar — the Chula Sakarat era (638 CE) and later the Buddhist Era (543 BCE); reformed to a solar Gregorian-adjacent system in 1888–1941, but traditional lunisolar reckoning continues for religious festivals (Songkran, Loy Krathong)
  - **Khmer calendar**: lunisolar system closely related to the Indian siddhānta tradition — used in Angkorian inscriptions and continuing in modified form today
  - All three calendars diverge from each other (and from the Indian originals) due to independent intercalation histories and different epoch corrections

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Supported by Multiple Scholars / Strong Circumstantial Evidence)

### 2.1 Javanese and Balinese Calendar Systems
- The **Javanese calendar** combines two independent systems:
  - **Pawukon**: an indigenous 210-day cycle — the product of 10 concurrent week-cycles of lengths 1 through 10 (though weeks of 4 and 8 are sometimes omitted or modified):
    - The 5-day (*pasaran*) and 7-day (*padinan*) weeks are the most important — their intersection produces a 35-day cycle central to market schedules, ceremonies, and omens
    - The 210-day cycle does not correspond to any obvious astronomical period — it may have agricultural origins
  - **Saka year**: an Indian-derived lunisolar calendar (era beginning 78 CE) — 12 months with intercalary adjustments
  - Balinese culture uses the pawukon system even more intensively — the 210-day ceremonial calendar governs temple festivals (odalan)

### 2.2 Navagraha in Southeast Asian Art and Architecture
- **Navagraha** shrines, reliefs, and sculptures appear throughout Southeast Asian religious architecture:
  - Burmese pagodas (e.g., Shwedagon Pagoda): eight planetary posts (*geiza*) at the base of the stupa, each associated with a day of the week and a celestial body — devotees worship at the post corresponding to their birthday's day of the week
  - Khmer temples: navagraha sculptures appear as doorway guardians and temple decoration
  - The navagraha system also determines auspicious timing for ceremonies, construction, coronations, and warfare

### 2.3 Eclipse Traditions
- Eclipses in Southeast Asian cultures are universally interpreted through the **Rahu myth** (Indian origin):
  - **Rahu** — the demon who swallows the Sun or Moon — is the standard explanation for eclipses across Thai, Burmese, Khmer, Javanese, and Balinese traditions
  - During eclipses, communities traditionally make noise (banging pots, firing guns, setting off firecrackers) to frighten Rahu into releasing the celestial body
  - Court astronomers in historical kingdoms (Ayutthaya, Pagan, Angkor) were expected to predict eclipses — failure could result in loss of position or worse

### 2.4 Borobudur and Astronomical Orientation
- **Borobudur** (Central Java, ~9th century CE): the world's largest Buddhist monument:
  - Oriented to the cardinal directions — the main entrance faces east
  - Scholars (Atmadi, 1988; Barnard, 2012) have proposed that the monument's multiple terraces encode cosmological schemes related to Buddhist cosmic geography, including possible astronomical references
  - The evidence for specific astronomical alignments (beyond the general east-west axis) is limited and debated

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Limited Evidence / Emerging Hypotheses)

### 3.1 Pre-Indian Indigenous Astronomy
- Before Indianization, Southeast Asian peoples presumably had their own astronomical traditions — but these are largely unrecoverable:
  - The pawukon cycle may preserve a pre-Indian calendrical system
  - Austronesian maritime peoples (ancestors of the Malay, Javanese, and other populations) were expert navigators — implying star knowledge similar to Polynesian traditions
  - Ethnographic work on marginal groups (Dayak, Manggarai, Baduy) occasionally preserves non-Indianized star lore — but systematic documentation is limited

### 3.2 Chinese Astronomical Influence
- Later Chinese influence (especially in Vietnam, but also in Thailand and Indonesia through Chinese merchants and immigrants) brought Chinese calendar elements:
  - The Chinese sexagenary cycle (60-year system) was adopted alongside Indian-derived eras in some Southeast Asian court calendars
  - The extent to which Chinese astronomical methods (as opposed to calendrical labels) were adopted in Southeast Asia is unclear

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — Fringe / Not Supported by Evidence)

### 4.1 Angkor as a Scale Model of the Universe
- The claim that Angkor Wat's dimensions precisely encode all astronomical constants (planetary periods, precessional cycle, etc.) — while some numerical correspondences are plausible, the more elaborate claims (Mannikka) have been criticized for selecting post hoc from a large dataset of possible measurements

### 4.2 Lost Advanced Southeast Asian Astronomy
- The claim that Southeast Asian kingdoms possessed astronomical knowledge superior to their Indian sources — no evidence supports this; the transmitted sūryasiddhānta-tradition astronomy was generally maintained without significant theoretical advancement
