## QUICK SUMMARY

The **Moon** — the most visible and rhythmically changing celestial body — has been a central object of mythology, worship, and symbolic elaboration in virtually every human culture. The **29.5-day synodic cycle** (new moon to new moon) provided humanity's first widely accessible **natural calendar**, preceding solar calendrical systems and giving rise to "month" (from the same Indo-European root as "moon," *mēnsis/mēnōn*). The Moon's waxing and waning became the master metaphor for **cyclical processes**: birth-growth-death-rebirth, fertility and barrenness, the menstrual cycle (approximately lunar in period), the tides, plant growth, and the rhythm of agricultural and ceremonial life. Across cultures, lunar mythology organizes around several recurrent themes: the Moon as **deity** (Selene/Artemis/Hecate in Greek tradition, Chandra/Soma in Vedic-Hindu, Tsukuyomi in Japanese, Ixchel in Maya, Khonsu/Thoth in Egyptian, Sin/Nanna in Mesopotamian); the Moon as **gendered being** (typically feminine in European, East Asian, and Mesoamerican traditions; masculine in Mesopotamian, Hindu, and Germanic traditions — demonstrating that the association of Moon with femininity is cultural, not universal); the Moon as **source of madness** (Latin *lunaticus*, from *luna*); the Moon as **measurer of time** (the original purpose of monuments like Stonehenge may include lunar tracking); and the Moon as **landscape of myth** (the "Man in the Moon," the Jade Rabbit of East Asian tradition, the woman banished to the Moon in various traditions).

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Lunar Calendrical Systems
- The **synodic month** (29.53 days) was among the earliest time-reckoning units used by humans — the Islamic calendar remains purely lunar (12 months of 29–30 days, 354-day year), while Jewish, Hindu, Chinese, and many other traditional calendars are **lunisolar** (lunar months adjusted by intercalation to align with the solar year)
- The **Ishango bone** (c. 20,000 BCE, Congo) and the **Lebombo bone** (c. 42,000 BCE, Eswatini) have been interpreted as possible early lunar tally sticks — if correct, lunar timekeeping would be among the oldest systematic intellectual activities of *Homo sapiens*
- **Mesopotamian lunar calendar**: the month began with the first visible crescent after new moon — professional astronomers tracked lunar phases, eclipses, and the 18.6-year lunar nodal cycle in the *MUL.APIN* series and later *Astronomical Diaries* (from 747 BCE onward, the longest continuous astronomical record in history)

### 1.2 Lunar Deities (Cross-Cultural Survey)
- **Mesopotamian Sin/Nanna**: Among the most important deities in the Sumerian-Akkadian pantheon — the Moon-god, lord of Ur, father of Shamash (Sun) and Ishtar (Venus); his temple at Ur was the economic and spiritual center of the Third Dynasty of Ur; the Moon was **masculine** and senior to the Sun
- **Egyptian Khonsu and Thoth**: Khonsu ("the Traveler") — youthful lunar god associated with healing and time; Thoth — the ibis-headed god associated with the Moon, wisdom, writing, mathematics, and the adjudication of the dead; Egyptian astronomical ceiling paintings depict the lunar cycle in detail
- **Greek triple lunar goddess**: Selene (full moon), Artemis (crescent/hunting/wilderness), and Hecate (dark moon/crossroads/witchcraft) — representing the Moon's distinct phases as distinct divine aspects; the later Hellenistic-Roman period synthesized these into a powerful triadic symbol
- **Hindu Chandra/Soma**: Chandra rides a chariot across the night sky; Soma is both the Moon and the intoxicating ritual drink of the Vedas — the waxing and waning Moon was explained mythologically as the gods gradually consuming Soma (which Chandra is obligated to refill)
- **Maya Ixchel/Ix Chel**: lunar goddess associated with weaving, medicine, childbirth, and water — depicted as both a young woman and an aged crone; the Island of Cozumel was a major center of her worship

### 1.3 Eclipse Mythology
- Lunar eclipses are almost universally interpreted as **cosmic attack or crisis**: a dragon, demon, or monster swallowing the Moon and requiring human intervention (noise-making, prayer, arrow-shooting) to rescue it:
  - Chinese: the celestial dog (*tiangou*) devouring the Moon
  - Hindu: the demon Rahu swallowing Chandra (Rahu was decapitated for stealing soma, so the Moon passes through his severed head)
  - Norse: the wolf Hati pursuing and periodically catching Máni (the Moon)
  - Mesopotamian: eclipses were omens of royal peril — substitute kings sometimes enthroned during eclipses to absorb the curse

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Moon and Gender
- The association of Moon with femininity is **culturally variable**, not biologically determined:
  - Feminine: Greek (Selene/Artemis), Roman (Luna/Diana), Chinese (Chang'e), Japanese (Tsukuyomi is male but the Moon is culturally feminine in practice), most European folk traditions
  - Masculine: Mesopotamian (Sin/Nanna — senior male deity), Hindu (Chandra — male), Germanic (Máni — male; the Sun, Sól, is female in proto-Germanic), many Australian Aboriginal traditions
  - The widespread (but not universal) association of Moon with femininity may relate to the approximate correspondence between the synodic month (~29.5 days) and the human menstrual cycle (~28 days) — but this correlation is not exact, and the causal mechanism is unexplained

### 2.2 Lunar Influence on Behavior
- The folk belief in lunar influence on human behavior ("lunacy") is ancient and persistent but **unsubstantiated** by rigorous modern research:
  - Meta-analyses (Rotton and Kelly 1985; Raison et al. 1999) found no significant correlation between Moon phase and psychiatric admissions, emergency room visits, violent crime, or birth rates
  - The persistence of the belief despite negative evidence illustrates powerful cognitive biases: selective attention, confirmation bias, and the appeal of celestial causation

### 2.3 Neolithic Lunar Monuments
- Alexander Thom's surveys (1967, 1971) proposed that many British megalithic sites were aligned to extreme positions of the Moon (the **lunar standstill** cycle of 18.6 years) — particularly at Callanish and Carnac
  - This interpretation remains debated: the statistical significance of the alleged alignments is contested, but the 18.6-year cycle was demonstrably tracked by Mesopotamian astronomers and could in principle have been noticed by Neolithic observers of the horizon

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Paleolithic Moon Worship
- Some Upper Paleolithic figurines (e.g., the Venus of Laussel holding a crescent-shaped horn with 13 notches — possibly representing 13 lunar months) have been interpreted as evidence of Paleolithic lunar symbolism, but the interpretation remains speculative

### 3.2 Lunar Tidal Knowledge in Antiquity
- While the Moon-tide connection was proposed by ancient authors (Posidonius, Pliny), the extent to which coastal cultures worldwide understood and mythologized this relationship before the modern scientific explanation remains uncertain

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Moon Planting Efficacy
- **[UNSUBSTANTIATED]** Claims that planting crops according to Moon phases (biodynamic agriculture) produces measurably superior results are not supported by controlled agricultural experiments — systematic reviews (e.g., Kollerstrom 2004) find no consistent evidence of phase-dependent crop performance
