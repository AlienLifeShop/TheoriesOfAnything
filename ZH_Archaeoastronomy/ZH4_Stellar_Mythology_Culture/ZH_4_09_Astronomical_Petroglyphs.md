## QUICK SUMMARY

Humans have carved, painted, and pecked celestial imagery into rock surfaces for at least **10,000 years** — and possibly far longer. **Astronomical petroglyphs** and **pictographs** are found on every inhabited continent: images of the sun, moon, stars, constellations, comets, and eclipses scratched into cliff faces, boulders, and cave walls. Some of the most studied examples include the **Chaco Canyon Sun Dagger** (New Mexico), the possible **SN 1054 supernova pictograph** at Peñasco Blanco, **cup-and-ring marks** across Atlantic Europe, Aboriginal Australian rock art depicting celestial events, and **Scandinavian Bronze Age carvings** showing solar imagery. Interpreting whether a rock art element is genuinely astronomical — rather than decorative, religious, or symbolic of something else entirely — is the central methodological challenge. Scholars such as **E. C. Krupp**, **John Eddy**, **Anthony Aveni**, and **Ray Norris** have developed criteria for evaluating astronomical rock art claims, emphasizing the need for cultural context, ethnographic analogy, statistical rigor, and an awareness of selection bias. Not every circle is a sun, not every cluster of dots is the Pleiades — but in contexts where ethnographic, cultural, and positional evidence converge, rock art provides invaluable windows into ancient astronomical knowledge.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Experimentally Confirmed)

### 1.1 Types of Celestial Rock Art
- **Solar imagery**: sun symbols (circles with rays, spirals, concentric rings) are among the most common motifs in rock art worldwide — but not all circles represent the sun:
  - Context is critical: associated imagery, site orientation, ethnographic testimony, and interaction with solar light and shadow phenomena can support astronomical interpretation
  - **Sun Dagger at Chaco Canyon** (Fajada Butte, New Mexico): discovered by Anna Sofaer (1977) — a carefully positioned arrangement of three stone slabs creates a "dagger" of sunlight that bisects a spiral petroglyph at the summer solstice; at the winter solstice and equinoxes, light patterns interact with a secondary spiral
  - The site has been confirmed as intentionally designed for solar marking by multiple independent analyses (Sofaer et al., 1979; Sinclair, 1987)

- **Lunar imagery**: crescent forms are common but ambiguous — crescents can represent the moon, horns, boats, or other symbols
  - The **Knowth kerbstones** (Ireland, ~3200 BCE) have been interpreted by researchers (Brennan, 1983) as lunar phase sequences — debated but plausible given Knowth's known astronomical alignments

- **Star and constellation depictions**: clusters of dots, often interpreted as the **Pleiades** (the most distinctive naked-eye star cluster):
  - **Lascaux cave** (France, ~17,000 BCE): a group of dots near the painted bull has been interpreted as the Pleiades and Taurus (Rappenglueck, 1999) — provocative but unconfirmed
  - Aboriginal Australian rock art from multiple sites explicitly depicts constellations and celestial objects, with associated living oral traditions explaining their meaning (Norris & Hamacher, 2009)

### 1.2 The SN 1054 Supernova Pictographs
- The **Crab Nebula supernova** (SN 1054, July 4, 1054 CE) was visible in daylight for 23 days and at night for ~2 years:
  - Documented in Chinese and Japanese records, and possibly in Arabic sources
  - Several rock art panels in the American Southwest — notably at **Peñasco Blanco** (Chaco Canyon) and **White Mesa** — show a crescent (moon) next to a bright star, consistent with the configuration of the Moon and SN 1054 on the morning of July 5, 1054 CE
  - **Miller (1955)** first proposed this interpretation; **Brandt & Williamson (1979)** provided detailed astronomical analysis
  - *Caveat*: the association remains circumstantial — no independent dating of the rock art confirms a 1054 CE date, and the star-and-crescent motif appears in other contexts. The hypothesis is plausible but not proven

### 1.3 Scandinavian Bronze Age Rock Art
- **Tanum** (Sweden, UNESCO World Heritage), **Alta** (Norway, UNESCO), and other Scandinavian sites feature elaborate carved panels (hällristningar) with solar imagery:
  - **Solar ships/boats**: frequent motif — boats carrying a circle or disc, interpreted as the sun being carried across the sky (paralleling Egyptian/Near Eastern solar barque mythology)
  - **Wheel crosses** and **concentric circles**: interpreted as solar symbols by Burenhult (1999) and Kaul (1998), who linked them to Bronze Age solar cosmology and the **Trundholm sun chariot** (Denmark, ~1400 BCE)
  - **Dated by context**: approximately 1800–500 BCE based on associated Bronze Age artifacts and superposition studies

### 1.4 Cup-and-Ring Marks
- **Cup marks** (hemispherical hollows pecked into rock) and **cup-and-ring marks** (cup marks surrounded by concentric circles) are found across Atlantic Europe, the Mediterranean, India, and parts of the Americas:
  - Most abundant in **Scotland**, **Ireland**, **Northern England**, and **Galicia** (Spain) — dated to the **Neolithic and Bronze Age** (~4000–1500 BCE)
  - Astronomical interpretations proposed by various authors (e.g., Morris, 1979; Heath, 2010) — but no consensus exists on their meaning:
  - Some appear at sites with known astronomical alignments (e.g., Kilmartin Glen, Scotland)
  - Others may be territorial markers, route markers, or ritual symbols — the astronomical hypothesis is one of several

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Supported by Multiple Scholars / Strong Circumstantial Evidence)

### 2.1 Aboriginal Australian Astronomical Rock Art
- Aboriginal Australian knowledge of the sky is encoded in rock art, paintings, and oral traditions extending over thousands of years:
  - **Emu in the Sky**: the dark nebulae in the Milky Way forming an emu shape — recognized across multiple Aboriginal groups and depicted in rock engravings at sites such as **Ku-ring-gai Chase** (near Sydney), where ground engravings show an emu aligned with the celestial dark-cloud emu (Norris & Hamacher, 2009)
  - Solar halos, eclipses, and meteor events are recorded in oral traditions with associated painted or engraved depictions
  - **Significance**: Aboriginal astronomical traditions represent some of the world's oldest continuously transmitted astronomical knowledge

### 2.2 Mesoamerican Astronomical Carvings
- **Aztec Sun Stone** (Piedra del Sol, ~1502–1521 CE): massive carved basalt disc (3.6m diameter, 24 tons) — not strictly a "calendar" but a cosmological monument depicting the five suns (world ages), calendrical signs, and solar deity Tonatiuh:
  - Integrates the 260-day tonalpohualli and the 365-day xiuhpohualli cycles
- **Monte Albán Building J** (Oaxaca, ~100 BCE): arrow-shaped building with zenith tubes and carved glyphs — Aveni (1980) demonstrated alignments to stars including **Capella** and to the **zenith passage of the sun** (critical at Mesoamerican latitudes near the tropics)
- **Maya stella and lintels**: carved inscriptions recording precise planetary positions, eclipse predictions, and Long Count dates — these go beyond rock art into systematic astronomical record-keeping

### 2.3 North American Calendar Stones
- Several carved stones from North American sites have been interpreted as calendrical:
  - **Calendar Stone 1** at Cahokia (Illinois, ~1000 CE): debated interpretation
  - **Pawnee star chart** (hide, not rock — included here for comparison): a painted star map on bison hide showing celestial configurations used in Pawnee cosmology and ceremony (Chamberlain, 1982)
  - **Medicine wheels** (e.g., Bighorn Medicine Wheel, Wyoming, ~1200–1700 CE): cairn-and-spoke arrangements with apparent solstice and stellar alignments — Eddy (1974) proposed alignments to the summer solstice sunrise and the heliacal risings of Aldebaran, Rigel, and Sirius. Some alignments are statistically plausible; others debated

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Limited Evidence / Emerging Hypotheses)

### 3.1 Paleolithic Star Maps
- **Rappenglueck (1999)**: proposed that the Lascaux bull painting includes a map of the Hyades and Pleiades star clusters, and that a figure in the "Shaft Scene" represents the Summer Triangle (Vega, Deneb, Altair):
  - Provocative but the evidence is circumstantial — pattern-matching between dot clusters and star configurations can produce false positives
  - Similar claims have been made for other Paleolithic sites (e.g., El Castillo, Chauvet) — none are confirmed

### 3.2 Saharan Astronomical Petroglyphs
- Rock art in the **Sahara** (Tassili n'Ajjer, Algeria; Fezzan, Libya) includes circular motifs and potential solar imagery from the Holocene wet period (~8000–3000 BCE):
  - Limited study from an astronomical perspective — the art is often interpreted in terms of pastoral economy, ritual, and contact

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — Fringe / Not Supported by Evidence)

### 4.1 All Spirals Are Solar Calendars
- Claims that every spiral petroglyph — worldwide — represents the Sun or a solar calendar marker. Spirals are among the most universal motifs in human art and likely had many different meanings in different cultures. Astronomical interpretation requires context-specific evidence, not iconographic assumption

### 4.2 Rock Art as Evidence of Alien Contact
- Fringe interpretations of celestial rock art imagery as depictions of spacecraft or alien visitors (von Däniken, *Chariots of the Gods?*, 1968) — archaeologists and rock art specialists unanimously reject these interpretations, which ignore cultural context and the rich astronomical knowledge of indigenous peoples

## COUNTER-ARGUMENTS

- **Supernova recording claims**: The identification of specific petroglyphs as records of the 1054 CE supernova (Crab Nebula) — including a famous Chaco Canyon example showing a crescent-and-star motif — has been questioned. **E.C. Krupp** noted that crescent-star combinations are extremely common in rock art worldwide and predate 1054, making the supernova identification plausible but far from certain
- **Lascaux astronomical interpretation**: **Michael Rappenglueck's** (1999) identification of Lascaux cave paintings as depicting the Pleiades, Taurus, and the summer triangle was described even by sympathizers as "provocative but unconfirmed." The identification depends on matching a few dots to a star pattern — a procedure vulnerable to confirmation bias given the thousands of dots in Paleolithic cave art
- **Methodological challenge**: Astronomical petroglyphs face a fundamental interpretive problem: without associated text, distinguishing intentional astronomical representations from decorative circles, hunting tallies, or unrelated symbols requires assumptions that cannot be independently verified
