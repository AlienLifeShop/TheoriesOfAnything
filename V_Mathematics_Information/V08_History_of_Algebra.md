## QUICK SUMMARY

Algebra — the generalization of arithmetic to unknown quantities and their relationships — has a 4,000-year documented history, from **Babylonian** equation-solving tablets (c. 1800 BCE) through **Brahmagupta's** Indian treatise (628 CE, first rules for zero and negative numbers in equations) to **Muhammad ibn Musa al-Khwarizmi's** foundational *Al-Kitāb al-mukhtaṣar fī ḥisāb al-jabr wal-muqābala* (c. 820 CE, Baghdad) — the work whose title gave us the word "algebra" (*al-jabr* = "restoration/completion") and whose author's name gave us "algorithm."
The Renaissance breakthrough of solving **cubic** (Cardano/Tartaglia, 1545) and **quartic** equations, followed by the proof that **quintic** equations have no general algebraic solution (**Abel, 1824; Galois, 1832**), transformed algebra from the art of solving equations into the study of abstract structures — **groups**, **rings**, and **fields**.
**Emmy Noether** (1882–1935) revolutionized both algebra and physics: her theorem connecting symmetries to conservation laws (Noether's theorem, 1918) is one of the most important results in all of mathematical physics, and her work in abstract algebra (ideal theory, ring theory) shaped 20th-century mathematics.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Babylonian algebraic problem-solving (c. 2000–1600 BCE)

Babylonian clay tablets contain sophisticated equation-solving:
- **YBC 6967** and other tablets (Yale Babylonian Collection): contain problems equivalent to solving quadratic equations — "find two numbers whose sum is S and product is P."
- Babylonian method: algorithmic, step-by-step procedures using specific numerical examples — no symbolic notation, but the procedures work for any numbers substituted.
- **Plimpton 322** (c. 1800 BCE): a table of Pythagorean triples generated by a systematic algebraic procedure — demonstrating that Babylonians understood the relationship $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$ over 1,000 years before Pythagoras.
- Babylonian algebra was rhetorical (expressed in words) rather than symbolic, and procedural rather than general — solving individual problems rather than proving universal theorems.

### 1.2 Indian algebra — Brahmagupta and beyond

Indian mathematicians made fundamental algebraic contributions:
- **Brahmagupta** (598–668 CE), *Brāhmasphuṭasiddhānta* (628 CE): established rules for arithmetic with zero and negative numbers (including the problematic $0 ÷ 0 = 0$), solved general linear and quadratic equations, and provided the first systematic treatment of **Pell's equation** ($x^2 - Ny^2 = 1$).
- **Bhāskara II** (1114–1185 CE), *Bījaganita* (Seed Arithmetic): systematic algebraic treatise using abbreviations for unknowns — approached symbolic algebra.
- **Indian syncopated algebra**: a transitional stage between fully rhetorical and fully symbolic — abbreviations and specialized terms reduced verbal complexity.

### 1.3 Al-Khwarizmi and the birth of algebra (c. 820 CE)

**Muhammad ibn Musa al-Khwarizmi** (c. 780–850 CE), working at the House of Wisdom (Baghdad, Abbasid Caliphate):
- *Al-Kitāb al-mukhtaṣar fī ḥisāb al-jabr wal-muqābala* (The Compendious Book on Calculation by Completion and Balancing, c. 820 CE): systematically classified and solved **six types of quadratic equations** (since negative numbers and zero were not yet accepted as coefficients or solutions in this tradition).
- **Al-jabr** ("restoration"): moving subtracted terms to the other side of an equation. **Al-muqābala** ("balancing"): cancelling equal terms on both sides. These are the operations that define equation-solving.
- Al-Khwarizmi provided **geometric proofs** for his algebraic procedures — completing the square visually.
- The Latin translation (Robert of Chester, 1145; Gerard of Cremona, c. 1170) transmitted Arabic algebra to medieval Europe — "algebra" entered European languages directly from *al-jabr*.
- **"Algorithm"** derives from the Latinized form of al-Khwarizmi's name (*Algoritmi*) — initially referring to arithmetic using Hindu-Arabic numerals, later generalized.

### 1.4 Renaissance equation-solving and the cubic (16th century)

The solution of cubic and quartic equations:
- **Scipione del Ferro** (c. 1515): first solved a specific form of the cubic equation — kept the solution secret.
- **Niccolò Tartaglia** (1535): independently solved cubic equations and shared his method (as a poem) with **Gerolamo Cardano** under oath of secrecy.
- **Cardano** (1545): published the solution in *Ars Magna* after learning that del Ferro had priority — the **Cardano formula** for cubics (general solution involving cube roots).
- **Lodovico Ferrari** (Cardano's student): solved the **quartic** (degree 4) equation by reducing it to a cubic.
- **Casus irreducibilis**: for some cubics with three real roots, Cardano's formula produces expressions involving square roots of negative numbers — this was the historical origin of **complex numbers** (not $\sqrt{-1}$ as an abstract concept, but as a necessary intermediate step in a real calculation).

### 1.5 Abel and Galois — the unsolvability of the quintic

The deepest result in classical algebra:
- **Niels Henrik Abel** (1802–1829, Norway): proved (1824) that there is no general algebraic formula (using radicals — roots, powers, and arithmetic) for solving polynomial equations of degree ≥ 5. The **Abel-Ruffini theorem**.
- **Évariste Galois** (1811–1832, France): created **group theory** to explain *why* — the solvability of a polynomial equation by radicals corresponds to a property of its symmetry group (the Galois group). The quintic's Galois group ($S_5$) is not solvable.
- Galois wrote his theory in a letter the night before his death in a duel (May 30, 1832, age 20) — "I have not time" (*je n'ai pas le temps*) — one of mathematics' most dramatic episodes. The theory was eventually published and understood by the 1850s.
- **Group theory** — the study of symmetry operations that form algebraic structures (closure, associativity, identity, inverse) — became the organizing concept of modern algebra.

### 1.6 Emmy Noether and abstract algebra

**Amalie Emmy Noether** (1882–1935):
- **Noether's theorem** (1918): every continuous symmetry of a physical system corresponds to a conserved quantity — time symmetry → energy conservation, spatial symmetry → momentum conservation, rotational symmetry → angular momentum conservation. Einstein called it one of the most important results in mathematical physics.
- **Abstract algebra** (1920s–1930s): Noether transformed ring theory and ideal theory — introducing the abstract, axiomatic approach that defines modern algebra. Her students (van der Waerden, whose *Moderne Algebra* [1930] was based on her lectures) spread her methods worldwide.
- Denied a proper faculty position at Göttingen due to gender — "I do not see that the sex of the candidate is an argument against her admission as *Privatdozent*" (David Hilbert, 1915).
- Dismissed from Göttingen under Nazi racial laws (1933); emigrated to Bryn Mawr College, Pennsylvania; died in 1935.

## 2. CREDIBLE BUT DEBATED CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated)

### 2.1 Priority questions between civilizations

Who "invented" algebra?
- Babylonians solved quadratic equations ~2,000 years before al-Khwarizmi — but without systematic classification or general theory.
- Indian mathematicians (Brahmagupta, Bhāskara) developed algebraic techniques independent of both Babylonian and Arabic predecessors.
- Al-Khwarizmi's originality lies in *systematization* — treating algebra as a self-contained discipline rather than a collection of problem-solving tricks.
- The question "who invented algebra?" oversimplifies — multiple civilizations contributed to a cumulative development, and the word itself is Arabic.

### 2.2 The role of Islamic algebra in transmitting to Europe

- The standard narrative: Babylonian → Greek → Arabic → Latin Europe. This is partly true but oversimplifies — Islamic mathematicians (al-Karajī, Omar Khayyam, al-Tūsī) made original contributions, not just translations.
- **Omar Khayyam** (1048–1131): classified and solved cubic equations geometrically (intersection of conic sections) — anticipated Cardano's algebraic solution by 400+ years.

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Egyptian algebraic methods beyond what manuscripts show

The Rhind Papyrus and Moscow Papyrus contain algebraic-like problems (false-position method), but whether Egyptian mathematicians had more sophisticated algebraic techniques that were not preserved is unknowable — the surviving corpus is small and possibly unrepresentative.
