## QUICK SUMMARY

The concept of zero — both as a placeholder in positional notation and as a number in its own right — is one of humanity's most consequential intellectual achievements, yet it emerged independently in multiple civilizations and was resisted by others for centuries. Babylonians developed a placeholder symbol (~3rd century BCE) but never used zero as a number. The Maya independently invented a true zero for their Long Count calendar (~4th century CE). Indian mathematicians, particularly Brahmagupta (628 CE), first defined zero as a number with arithmetic operations, connected philosophically to the Buddhist concept of śūnyatā (emptiness). Transmitted through Arabic mathematics (al-Khwarizmi) and eventually to Europe via Fibonacci (1202), zero transformed mathematics, enabled modern algebra, and raised profound philosophical questions about the nature of nothing.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Mathematical History)

### 1.1 Babylonian Placeholder
- Babylonian sexagesimal (base-60) system used positional notation by ~2000 BCE
- By ~300 BCE, a double-wedge placeholder symbol appeared in astronomical texts to indicate an empty position
- This was a placeholder only — never used at the end of a number, never treated as a quantity
- The absence of a true zero contributed to ambiguity in Babylonian numerical records

### 1.2 Maya Zero
- The Maya Long Count calendar required a zero symbol for positional notation in vigesimal (base-20)
- A shell-shaped glyph (or variants) represented zero, appearing on stelae dating to the 4th century CE
- The earliest securely dated Long Count inscription with zero: Stela 1 at La Mojarra (291 CE) — though its Mayan origin is debated
- Epi-Olmec origins possible: the concept may predate Classic Maya
- Maya zero was both placeholder and a true number concept within calendrical mathematics

### 1.3 Indian Zero — Brahmagupta's Arithmetic
- The Bakhshali manuscript (redated to 3rd-4th century CE by Oxford radiocarbon study, 2017 — dating disputed) contains dot-placeholder zeros
- Brahmagupta (*Brahmasphutasiddhanta*, 628 CE): first known rules for zero arithmetic — a + 0 = a, a × 0 = 0, a - a = 0
- He also attempted 0 ÷ 0 = 0 (incorrect) and discussed division by zero (left problematic)
- Bhaskara II (12th century) stated that n/0 = infinity (khahara), approaching modern limit concepts
- The Sanskrit word *śūnya* (void/empty) gave zero its identity — connected to Buddhist philosophical concept of śūnyatā

### 1.4 Transmission to the Islamic World and Europe
- Al-Khwarizmi (*Kitab al-Hisab al-Hindi*, ~825 CE) introduced Indian numerals and zero to Arabic mathematics
- The word "algorithm" derives from al-Khwarizmi's name; "algebra" from his *al-jabr*
- Fibonacci (*Liber Abaci*, 1202) brought Hindu-Arabic numerals to Europe, calling them "modus Indorum"
- European resistance: Florence banned Hindu-Arabic numerals in official documents (1299) — merchants were suspected of fraud because zero could alter values
- The transition from Roman to Hindu-Arabic numerals took ~400 years in Europe

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Philosophical Significance of Zero
- Greek mathematicians largely avoided zero — Aristotle's rejection of the void (*horror vacui*) created a conceptual barrier
- The Pythagorean commitment to positive whole numbers as reality's foundation left no room for "nothing as a number"
- Indian philosophical traditions were more receptive: Nagarjuna's *Mulamadhyamakakarika* (~2nd century CE) explored śūnyatā (emptiness) as foundational
- Robert Kaplan (*The Nothing That Is*, 2000) argues that Indian philosophy made zero conceptually possible in a way Greek philosophy did not
- The Jain concept of mathematical infinity and infinitesimal also provided receptive ground

### 2.2 Zero and the Development of Algebra
- Without zero, solving equations like x + 5 = 5 is conceptually awkward
- The concept of negative numbers (first formalized by Brahmagupta alongside zero) depends on zero as a reference point
- Cartesian coordinates (Descartes, 1637) require a zero origin point
- Calculus (Newton, Leibniz) fundamentally depends on approaching zero as a limit
- Zero enabled the modern number line and complex numbers (0 + 0i as origin)

### 2.3 Independent Invention vs Diffusion
- Babylonian, Maya, and Indian zeros appear to be independent inventions — no evidence of transmission
- The philosophical context differs dramatically: Babylonian (practical accounting), Maya (calendrical-cosmological), Indian (mathematical-philosophical)
- Chinese counting rod system used blanks for zero by the 1st century CE — another potentially independent development
- The Cambodian Sambor inscription (683 CE) contains the oldest known use of the zero symbol as we know it (a small circle)

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Zero and Consciousness
- Hypothesis that the capacity to conceptualize "nothing" represents a cognitive breakthrough comparable to language
- Some theorists connect zero to the awareness of absence — a capacity rare or absent in non-human animals
- Andreas Nieder's research (2016) shows that neurons in monkey prefrontal cortex can respond to "empty set" — but this is not equivalent to the mathematical concept of zero

### 3.2 The Bakhshali Manuscript Dating Controversy
- Oxford radiocarbon dating (2017) produced three dates from different folios: 3rd-4th, 7th, and 10th centuries CE
- If the 3rd-century date is correct, it pushes Indian zero back ~300 years before Brahmagupta
- Critics (Kim Plofker et al.) argue the manuscript may have been repaired with older materials, making the oldest date unreliable
- The debate remains unresolved and has significant implications for the history of zero

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source)

- Claims that ancient Sumerians or Egyptians had a fully developed zero concept — no evidence supports this
- The idea that zero was "suppressed" by religious authorities as heretical — the resistance was practical and philosophical, not theological persecution
- Assertions that zero was transmitted from Atlantis or an advanced pre-flood civilization
