## QUICK SUMMARY

Ethnomathematics — a term coined by Ubiratan D'Ambrosio in 1985 — studies mathematical practices embedded in cultural activities worldwide, challenging the narrative that mathematics is exclusively a product of Greek-European civilization. From the Ishango bone's 20,000-year-old tally marks in central Africa to the Inca quipu's sophisticated decimal recording system, from Yoruba base-20 numeration to the fractal layouts of African villages, non-Western peoples developed complex mathematical systems independently. These traditions encompass number theory, geometry, logic, combinatorics, and navigation — often encoded in forms (textiles, sand drawings, oral poetry, kinship systems) that Western scholars initially failed to recognize as mathematical. Recovering and studying these systems enriches our understanding of mathematical universality and human cognitive diversity.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Archaeological and Ethnographic Evidence)

### 1.1 The Ishango Bone (~20,000 BCE)
- Discovered at Ishango (Lake Edward, Democratic Republic of Congo) by Jean de Heinzelin in 1960
- A bone tool with three columns of grouped notches that some scholars interpret as a tally system, lunar calendar, or prime-number recognition
- Column markings include groups of 11, 13, 17, 19 (all primes between 10 and 20)
- Other columns show doubling sequences (3, 6; 4, 8) and sums that equal 48 and 60
- Debated interpretation: Alexander Marshack proposed a lunar phase calendar; others see systematic mathematical thinking
- Now housed at the Royal Belgian Institute of Natural Sciences, Brussels

### 1.2 Maya Vigesimal System (Base-20)
- The ancient Maya (~300 BCE onward) developed a complete positional numeral system using base 20
- Three symbols: dot (1), bar (5), shell-shaped glyph (0) — one of the earliest independent inventions of zero as a placeholder
- Could represent very large numbers efficiently — Long Count dates reach millions of days
- Maya astronomers computed Venus cycles with errors of only ~2 hours per 481 years
- The mathematical system was embedded in calendrical, astronomical, and ritual practices

### 1.3 Inca Quipu — Knotted String Mathematics
- Quipus (khipu): knotted cords used by the Inca Empire (~1400-1532 CE) for recording numerical and possibly narrative information
- Employed a decimal (base-10) positional system encoded through knot types and positions
- Single knots = tens/hundreds/thousands; long knots = units; figure-eight knots = "1" in the units position
- Some 600+ quipus survive; Gary Urton has proposed that some encode binary-like information, potentially recording names, places, or narratives
- Served as the accounting and administrative backbone of an empire of 10+ million without a written script

### 1.4 Yoruba Base-20 Numeration (Nigeria)
- The Yoruba number system is vigesimal (base-20) and uses both additive and subtractive principles
- 15 = "5 from 20" (ẹẹ́dógún); 35 = "5 from 40" (àrún dín lógójì)
- Claudia Zaslavsky (1973) documented the system's logical sophistication: it requires mental multiplication, subtraction, and addition for everyday number naming
- Functions as an oral system — mastery requires fluency in complex mental arithmetic
- Still in use in traditional Yoruba contexts; demonstrates that elaborate mathematical reasoning can exist without written notation

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Well-Documented Ethnographic Research)

### 2.1 African Fractals (Ron Eglash)
- Ron Eglash (1999) documented fractal patterns in African village layouts, hairstyles, textiles, and art across the continent
- Ba-ila village (Zambia): aerial view shows a self-similar pattern — large oval enclosures containing smaller oval enclosures
- Ethiopian crosses display recursive geometric complexity
- Eglash used fractal dimension analysis to confirm the patterns are genuinely fractal, not coincidental
- He argues these represent intentional design principles, though not necessarily formalized in the Western sense

### 2.2 Pacific Navigation — Marshall Islands Stick Charts
- Micronesian and Polynesian navigators developed sophisticated wayfinding methods encoding wave refraction, swell patterns, and island positions
- Marshall Islanders created mattang, meddo, and rebbelib charts: frameworks of palm ribs and shells representing swell interactions and island locations
- These charts encoded information about wave diffraction around islands — a phenomenon requiring implicit understanding of fluid dynamics
- Ben Finney and others have demonstrated the practical efficacy of these navigation systems over thousands of miles of open ocean
- Not easily translatable into Western cartographic conventions — they represent dynamic interactions, not static positions

### 2.3 Vanuatu Sand Drawings (Lusona)
- In Vanuatu (and similarly, the Chokwe of Angola), elaborate continuous-line sand drawings called "sand stories" are traced in a single path without lifting the finger
- These are topologically equivalent to Euler paths — traversing every edge exactly once
- Marcia Ascher (1991) analyzed these as combinatorial and graph-theoretic practices
- The tradition requires memorization of hundreds of patterns and the ability to plan complex graph traversals mentally
- UNESCO recognized Vanuatu sand drawing as Intangible Cultural Heritage (2008)

### 2.4 Aboriginal Australian Kinship Systems
- Aboriginal kinship systems (e.g., the eight-subsection system of the Warlpiri) encode marriage rules and social relations with mathematical precision
- These systems are isomorphic to finite groups from abstract algebra (particularly dihedral groups)
- Anthropologists and mathematicians (including Marika and Ascher) have shown the kinship rules constitute well-defined algebraic structures
- The systems manage genetic diversity across small populations through mathematically optimal partner selection
- Demonstrate sophisticated logical reasoning without formal mathematical notation

### 2.5 Indian Mathematics Beyond Zero
- Indian mathematics contributed far more than zero: Brahmagupta (628 CE) formulated rules for negative numbers and arithmetic with zero
- The Kerala school (14th-16th century): Madhava and successors developed infinite series for trigonometric functions 200+ years before Newton and Leibniz
- Aryabhata (499 CE): calculated π ≈ 3.1416 and solved linear Diophantine equations
- Panini's grammar (4th century BCE) is a formal system that anticipated aspects of modern computer science (Backus-Naur form)
- Indian mathematical contributions were transmitted to the Islamic world and then to Europe

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Plausible but Debated Interpretations)

### 3.1 Ishango Bone as Deliberate Prime Recognition
- The strongest interpretation of the Ishango bone — that its creators understood primality — remains controversial
- Tally marks might represent lunar months or game-counting rather than number-theoretic insight
- The presence of primes may be coincidental given the small sample of notches
- The truth likely lies in sophisticated pattern recognition without formal theory

### 3.2 Quipu as Full Writing System
- Gary Urton's research suggests some quipus may encode narrative or administrative information beyond numbers
- If confirmed, this would constitute a unique non-visual writing system — information encoded in knot topology and string color
- Most scholars accept the numerical interpretation but remain cautious about claims of full literacy

### 3.3 Universal Mathematical Intuition
- Ethnomathematics research supports the hypothesis that mathematical reasoning is a universal human capacity
- All documented cultures count, classify, measure, design, and navigate — core mathematical activities
- Whether this implies a shared cognitive architecture for mathematics (as Chomsky proposed for language) or emerges from convergent cultural evolution remains debated

---

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — Unsupported)

- Claims that any culture lacked mathematics entirely — all documented human societies perform mathematical reasoning
- Pop-culture narratives that non-Western mathematics is "inferior" or merely "practical" — the dichotomy between "pure" and "applied" is a recent Western construct
- Conversely, over-romanticized claims that indigenous peoples had advanced mathematical theories equivalent to modern graduate-level mathematics — respecting actual practices means describing them accurately, not inflating them
- Afrocentric or other ideological claims that all mathematics originated in a single culture
