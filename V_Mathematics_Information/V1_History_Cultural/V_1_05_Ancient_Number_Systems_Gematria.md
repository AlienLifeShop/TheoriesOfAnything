## QUICK SUMMARY

Every literate civilization developed a number system, and the diversity of these systems reveals both universal mathematical needs and culturally specific solutions.
**Babylonian base-60** (sexagesimal, c. 3000 BCE onward) used positional notation with only two symbols — a system so effective that we still divide hours into 60 minutes, minutes into 60 seconds, and circles into 360 degrees.
**Egyptian mathematics** (Rhind Papyrus, c. 1650 BCE; Moscow Papyrus, c. 1800 BCE) employed **unit fractions** (fractions with numerator 1, plus the special fraction 2/3) and a base-10 non-positional system.
The **Maya vigesimal** (base-20) system independently invented **zero** as a placeholder (before 36 BCE), creating a full positional notation for calendrical and astronomical calculations.
**Hebrew gematria** and **Greek isopsephy** assigned numerical values to letters, enabling a practice where words with equal numerical sums were considered mystically connected — a tradition that profoundly influenced Kabbalistic, Pythagorean, and early Christian numerological thought.
These systems demonstrate that mathematical notation is not neutral — the structure of a number system shapes which calculations are easy, which are hard, and what patterns are noticed.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Babylonian sexagesimal system (base-60)

The most sophisticated number system of the ancient world:
- **Positional notation**: place value — the same symbol means different values depending on its position (like our 1 in 1, 10, 100). Babylonians achieved this by c. 2000 BCE, ~3,000 years before Hindu-Arabic numerals reached Europe.
- Only **two cuneiform symbols**: a vertical wedge (1) and a corner wedge (10) — combined additively within each position, then read positionally in base 60.
- **No zero placeholder** initially — ambiguity resolved by context. A placeholder character (two angled wedges) appeared in later periods (Seleucid, c. 300 BCE) but was never used as a number in its own right.
- **Legacy**: 60 minutes in an hour, 60 seconds in a minute, 360 degrees in a circle — all derive from Babylonian sexagesimal arithmetic transmitted through Greek astronomy.
- **Mathematical sophistication**: Babylonians computed square roots, solved quadratic equations, and generated Pythagorean triples (Plimpton 322, c. 1800 BCE) using sexagesimal arithmetic.

### 1.2 Egyptian number system and fractions

Ancient Egyptian mathematics used a base-10 additive system:
- **Hieroglyphic numerals**: distinct symbols for 1, 10, 100, 1,000, 10,000, 100,000, and 1,000,000 — repeated and combined additively (no positional value).
- **Hieratic numerals**: cursive shorthand with distinct symbols for 1–9, 10–90, 100–900 — more compact but still non-positional.
- **Unit fraction system**: Egyptians expressed all fractions (except 2/3) as sums of distinct unit fractions: $\frac{2}{5} = \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{15}$. The **Rhind Papyrus** (BM 10057/10058, c. 1650 BCE, copied from c. 1850 BCE original) opens with a table decomposing $\frac{2}{n}$ for all odd $n$ from 3 to 101 into unit fractions.
- **Why unit fractions?** Debated — possibly connected to bread/beer distribution practices (dividing loaves equally), possibly to the eye of Horus fraction system, possibly an aesthetic/traditional preference.
- The **Moscow Papyrus** (c. 1800 BCE): contains the formula for the volume of a truncated pyramid (frustum) — a genuinely advanced geometric result.

### 1.3 Maya vigesimal system and zero

The Maya developed a complete positional number system:
- **Base-20** (vigesimal): likely related to counting on fingers and toes.
- **Three symbols only**: a dot (1), a bar (5), and a shell glyph (0) — zero as a true placeholder in positional notation.
- **Earliest known zero**: the Maya zero appears on Stela C at Tres Zapotes (Long Count date, 36 BCE or earlier, Olmec/epi-Olmec context) — an independent invention of zero, not derived from Indian or Babylonian precedents.
- **Modified vigesimal for calendrics**: the third position represents 360 (18 × 20) instead of 400 (20 × 20) — adapted to fit the 360-day *tun* of the calendar.
- Maya astronomical calculations required handling numbers in the millions — the positional system made this feasible.

### 1.4 Chinese and Indian number systems

Independent numerical traditions:
- **Chinese rod numerals** (from c. 500 BCE): positional base-10 system using counting rods — vertical and horizontal arrangements alternated by position to avoid ambiguity. A blank space represented zero until a circular symbol (○) appeared by the 13th century.
- **Indian numerals** (Brahmi numerals, c. 3rd century BCE → Gupta numerals → Devanagari): the system that eventually became "Hindu-Arabic numerals." Key innovations: unique symbols for 1–9 (not repeated counting marks), positional base-10 notation, and zero as both placeholder and number (*shūnya*, c. 5th–7th century CE).
- **Transmission**: Indian → Arabic (al-Khwarizmi, c. 825 CE) → Latin Europe (Fibonacci, *Liber Abaci*, 1202) — the modern decimal system.

### 1.5 Greek and Hebrew alphabetic numerals

Alphabetic numeral systems assign number values to letters:
- **Greek**: α = 1, β = 2, ... ι = 10, κ = 20, ... ρ = 100, σ = 200, ... using 27 letters (including three archaic letters: digamma, qoppa, sampi).
- **Hebrew**: aleph = 1, bet = 2, ... yod = 10, kof = 100, ... — 22 letters covering integers to 400 (combinations for higher values).
- **Isopsephy** (Greek) and **gematria** (Hebrew): the practice of calculating the numerical value of a word by summing its letter values. Words or phrases with equal sums are considered mystically connected.

### 1.6 Gematria — practices and attestations

Gematria is documented in multiple ancient contexts:
- **Sargon II of Assyria** (c. 722–705 BCE): built the walls of Khorsabad to measure 16,283 cubits, the numerical value of his name — the earliest known instance of architectural gematria.
- **Hebrew Bible**: gematria appears in rabbinic literature (Talmud, Mishnah) as a hermeneutic method — e.g., Genesis 14:14 (Abraham's 318 servants = gematria value of "Eliezer," his chief servant, suggesting he went alone).
- **Greek New Testament**: Revelation 13:18 — "the number of the beast is 666" — almost certainly a gematria reference to "Nero Caesar" (נרון קסר in Hebrew = 50+200+6+50+100+60+200 = 666).
- **Kabbalah**: gematria became a central interpretive tool — elaborate systems (ordinal, reduced, filled) connecting Torah passages through numerical equivalences.

## 2. CREDIBLE BUT DEBATED CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated)

### 2.1 The purpose of Egyptian unit fractions

Why Egyptians restricted themselves to unit fractions:
- **Practical distribution** (Gillings, 1972): dividing 2 loaves among 5 workers requires unit fraction decomposition for equal physical division.
- **Computational efficiency** (Knorr, 1982): unit fractions enable comparison without common denominators.
- **Cultural tradition** (Ritter, 2000): the system persisted because of scribal training conventions, not because alternatives were unknown.
- No scholarly consensus — all three explanations likely contribute.

### 2.2 Whether Plimpton 322 is a trigonometric table

- **Neugebauer & Sachs (1945)**: interpreted Plimpton 322 as a table of Pythagorean triples — the standard view for decades.
- **Robson (2002)**: argued it is a pedagogical exercise — a teacher's aid for generating reciprocal pairs — not a systematic trigonometric table.
- **Mansfield & Wildberger (2017)**: re-argued that it represents "exact sexagesimal trigonometry" superior to modern approximations — a controversial claim most historians regard as overstated.

### 2.3 Gematria as a valid interpretive method

Whether gematria reveals genuine textual meaning:
- **Supportive view** (Kabbalistic tradition, scholars): gematria reflects intentional design by the text's authors — numerical structures embedded in sacred texts.
- **Skeptical view**: with a flexible enough gematria system, any two words can be connected — the method is unfalsifiable and amenable to confirmation bias.
- **Historical view**: gematria was a real cultural practice — the question is whether it reveals authorial intent or reader-imposed patterns.

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Undiscovered number systems in undeciphered scripts

The Indus Valley script (c. 2600–1900 BCE) remains undeciphered — any number system it encodes is currently unknown. The Elamite Linear script and Proto-Elamite may contain numerical systems not yet fully understood.

## 4. DUBIOUS OR FRINGE CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Gematria proves Bible codes containing modern predictions

"Bible code" claims (Drosnin, 1997) — that equidistant letter sequences in the Torah predict modern events — were statistically debunked (McKay et al., 1999, *Statistical Science*). The method finds "predictions" in any sufficiently long text, including *War and Peace*.

### 4.2 Babylonian base-60 was transmitted by advanced beings who understood base-60's mathematical superiority

While base-60 has excellent divisibility properties (60 = 2² × 3 × 5, divisible by 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 10, 12, 15, 20, 30, 60), its development is well-explained by the merger of base-10 and base-6 counting systems in early Mesopotamia.
