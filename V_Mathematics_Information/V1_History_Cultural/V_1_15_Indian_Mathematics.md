## QUICK SUMMARY

**Indian mathematics** represents one of the most profound and consequential mathematical traditions in human history — contributing foundational innovations that shaped the course of global mathematics, most notably the **decimal place-value numeral system** (the "Hindu-Arabic numerals" now used worldwide), the concept of **zero** as both a placeholder and a number in its own right, and the algebraic treatment of **negative numbers**, **infinity**, and **indeterminate forms**. The tradition spans over 3,000 years: from the **Sulbasutras** (c. 800–500 BCE — geometric rules for constructing fire altars, containing early approximations to √2 and the relationship later known as the Pythagorean theorem), through the classical era of **Aryabhata** (476–550 CE — the first systematic Indian astronomical-mathematical treatise, *Aryabhatiya*, including the sine function, spherical astronomy, and a remarkably accurate value of π ≈ 3.1416), **Brahmagupta** (598–668 CE — *Brahmasphutasiddhanta*, 628 — the first text to treat zero as a number with defined arithmetic operations, the first systematic rules for negative numbers, and solutions to the Pell equation), and **Bhaskara II** (1114–1185 CE — *Lilavati* and *Bijaganita*, comprehensive algebra and arithmetic including solutions to indeterminate equations). The **Kerala school** of mathematics and astronomy (c. 1350–1600 CE — founded by **Madhava of Sangamagrama**, c. 1340–1425) independently developed **infinite series expansions** for trigonometric functions — the power series for sine, cosine, and arctangent — approximately 250 years before Newton, Leibniz, and Gregory achieved similar results in Europe. In the modern era, **Srinivasa Ramanujan** (1887–1920) — a largely self-taught genius from Tamil Nadu — produced results of astonishing originality in number theory, infinite series, continued fractions, and partition theory, contributing ~3,900 identities and equations, many still yielding new mathematical insights a century later.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 The Decimal Place-Value System and Zero
- **Positional decimal notation**: the Indian system of representing numbers using ten digits (0–9) in a place-value scheme where each position represents a power of 10 — developed gradually from the early centuries CE; the Bakhshali manuscript (dated controversially — portions possibly 3rd–4th century CE) contains early examples
- **Zero (śūnya)**: Brahmagupta (628 CE) was the first mathematician to systematically define zero as a number and specify arithmetic rules: $a + 0 = a$, $a \times 0 = 0$, $a - a = 0$; he attempted (incorrectly) to define $a/0$ — a problem that would not be properly resolved until the development of limits and calculus
- **Transmission to the Islamic world**: Indian numerals and methods were transmitted to the Arab world from roughly the 8th century — al-Khwarizmi's *Kitab al-Hisab al-Hindi* (c. 825) and later translations brought the system to Europe; the Italian mathematician Fibonacci (*Liber Abaci*, 1202) popularized "Hindu-Arabic numerals" in Europe, eventually displacing Roman numerals for computation

### 1.2 Aryabhata and Classical Indian Mathematics
- **Aryabhata** (476–550 CE, Kusumapura/Pataliputra): *Aryabhatiya* (499 CE) — a 121-verse astronomical-mathematical treatise covering:
  - **Trigonometry**: the first known table of **sine values** (jyā — literally "bowstring") at 3.75° intervals; the Indian sine function (half-chord) replaced the Greek chord function and became the basis of all subsequent trigonometry (transmitted to the Islamic world, then to Europe)
  - **π ≈ 3.1416**: "Add four to one hundred, multiply by eight, and then add sixty-two thousand. The result is approximately the circumference of a circle of diameter twenty thousand" — yielding π ≈ 62832/20000 = 3.1416
  - **Earth's rotation**: Aryabhata proposed that the apparent rotation of the stars is due to the Earth's rotation on its axis — a correct heliocentric insight, though he placed Earth at the center of the solar system
  - **Algebra**: methods for solving linear and quadratic indeterminate equations (kuttaka — "pulverizer" method for linear Diophantine equations)

### 1.3 Brahmagupta
- **Brahmagupta** (598–668 CE): *Brahmasphutasiddhanta* (628 CE):
  - First systematic treatment of **zero** as a number with arithmetic operations
  - First systematic rules for **negative numbers** (interpreted as debts) — correct rules for addition, subtraction, and multiplication of positive and negative numbers
  - **Brahmagupta's formula**: area of a cyclic quadrilateral $A = \sqrt{(s-a)(s-b)(s-c)(s-d)}$ where $s$ is the semiperimeter — a generalization of Heron's formula for triangles
  - **Pell equation** ($x^2 - Ny^2 = 1$): Brahmagupta described the *bhavana* (composition) method for generating infinite solutions from an initial solution — a technique later rediscovered by Euler and Lagrange in the 18th century

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 The Kerala School and Infinite Series
- **Madhava of Sangamagrama** (c. 1340–1425, Kerala): founder of the Kerala school of astronomy and mathematics; credited (through attributions by later Kerala mathematicians) with discovering:
  - **Power series for sine**: $\sin x = x - x^3/3! + x^5/5! - x^7/7! + \cdots$ (the Maclaurin series for sine)
  - **Power series for cosine**: $\cos x = 1 - x^2/2! + x^4/4! - x^6/6! + \cdots$
  - **Arctangent series** (Madhava-Leibniz series): $\arctan x = x - x^3/3 + x^5/5 - x^7/7 + \cdots$
  - **π series**: $\pi/4 = 1 - 1/3 + 1/5 - 1/7 + \cdots$ (the Madhava-Leibniz formula for π)
  - These results predate Newton (1665–1666), Leibniz (1675), and Gregory (1671) by approximately 250 years
- **Nilakantha Somayaji** (*Tantrasangraha*, 1501): rigorous expositions of Kerala school results; improved series convergence through error terms
- **Question of transmission**: whether Kerala school results influenced European mathematicians (through Jesuit missionaries present in Kerala from the 16th century) is debated — plausible routes of transmission exist but no documentary proof of direct influence has been established; it is possible that the discoveries were independent

### 2.2 Bhaskara II and Medieval Indian Mathematics
- **Bhaskara II** (Bhaskaracharya, 1114–1185 CE): the most celebrated medieval Indian mathematician:
  - *Lilavati* (arithmetic) and *Bijaganita* (algebra): comprehensive treatments including permutations, combinations, surds, indeterminate equations (second-degree), and the beginnings of differential calculus concepts (he computed the derivative of the sine function: $d(\sin\theta)/d\theta = \cos\theta$, expressed in his own notation, c. 1150 CE)
  - Solved certain cases of the Pell equation using the *chakravala* (cyclic) method — an efficient algorithm that was independently developed and is arguably more elegant than the European continued fraction approach

### 2.3 Ramanujan
- **Srinivasa Ramanujan** (1887–1920, Erode and Kumbakonam, Tamil Nadu): largely self-taught; produced ~3,900 identities, equations, and results — many highly original, many initially without proof:
  - Invited to Cambridge by G.H. Hardy (1914) — their collaboration is one of the most famous in mathematical history
  - Contributions: partition function (the Hardy-Ramanujan asymptotic formula, 1918), highly composite numbers, Ramanujan's tau function (1916 — connected to modular forms and the Langlands program), mock theta functions (defined in his last letter to Hardy, 1920 — their deep significance for number theory and physics was not understood until the 2000s)
  - The "man who knew infinity" — Ramanujan's notebooks continue to yield new mathematical results and connections

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Kerala-to-Europe Transmission
- The question of whether Kerala school mathematical results (infinite series, proto-calculus) reached Europe through Jesuit missionaries in Kerala (active from 1540s) and influenced the development of calculus by Newton and Leibniz is one of the most debated questions in the history of mathematics. Possible channels exist (Jesuit correspondence, Portuguese trade), and the timing is suggestive, but no direct documentary evidence of transmission has been found. Most historians consider independent discovery more likely, while acknowledging the possibility remains open

### 4.1 India Invented Everything First
- **[EXAGGERATED]** Nationalistic claims attributing virtually all mathematical discoveries to ancient India — including advanced algebra, calculus, and modern physics — are not supported by evidence. While Indian mathematical achievements are genuinely profound and historically underappreciated in Western accounts, exaggerated claims (that the full infinitesimal calculus existed in India millennia ago, or that specific modern theorems were known to ancient Vedic scholars) lack textual support and distort the actual historical record, which is remarkable enough without embellishment

## COUNTER-ARGUMENTS

- **Priority disputes and the transmission question**: Whether the Kerala school's infinite series results (Madhava's series for π and trigonometric functions, c. 1350–1500 CE) influenced European mathematics remains contentious. **Kim Plofker** (*Mathematics in India*, 2009) reviewed the evidence and concluded that while the mathematical priority of Indian mathematicians on series expansions is clear, direct transmission to Europe has not been demonstrated — circumstantial evidence (Jesuit missionaries in Kerala, the timing of comparable European discoveries by Newton and Leibniz in the 1660s–1680s) is suggestive but not conclusive
- **Nationalist overclaiming**: **George Gheverghese Joseph** (*The Crest of the Peacock*, 2011), while championing recognition of Indian mathematical achievements, cautioned against the tendency — especially in popular and political discourse — to attribute inventions to India that either were independent parallel developments or are not supported by textual evidence. Genuine Indian mathematical achievements (zero, decimal place-value, infinite series) are remarkable enough without embellishment
- **Dating uncertainties**: Many key Indian mathematical texts survive only in later copies, making precise dating difficult. **David Pingree** ("The Logic of Non-Western Science," *Daedalus*, 2003) noted that attributions to specific mathematicians (Aryabhata, Brahmagupta, Madhava) sometimes depend on later commentaries rather than original manuscripts, introducing uncertainty about who discovered what and when
