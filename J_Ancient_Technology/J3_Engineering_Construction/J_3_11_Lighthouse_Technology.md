## QUICK SUMMARY

The **Pharos of Alexandria** — the lighthouse built on the island of Pharos at the entrance to Alexandria's harbor around **280 BCE** under the Ptolemaic dynasty — was one of the **Seven Wonders of the Ancient World** and the archetype for all subsequent lighthouses (the word *pharos* or *faro* became the generic term for "lighthouse" in many languages). Standing an estimated **100-130 meters** tall (roughly 40 stories), the Pharos was among the tallest human-made structures of the ancient world and remained operational for over **1,500 years** until earthquakes (particularly in 956 and 1303 CE) progressively destroyed it. Ancient descriptions and later Arab accounts indicate a three-tiered structure: a square base, an octagonal middle section, and a cylindrical top — crowned by a fire and possibly a reflective mirror system visible up to **50 km** at sea. Beyond this iconic structure, the Romans built numerous lighthouses (*pharos*) across their empire — the **Tower of Hercules** at A Coruña (Spain), operational since the 1st century CE and still functioning today (the oldest extant working lighthouse in the world), the lighthouses at **Ostia** and **Ravenna**, at **Dover** (the Roman *pharos* within Dover Castle survives), and at **Boulogne-sur-Mer** (destroyed in the 17th century). The engineering of these structures — fire management at height, structural stability, wind resistance, reflector or lens technology, and fuel logistics — represents a sophisticated integration of architecture, materials science, and optical design.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 The Pharos of Alexandria
- Commissioned by **Ptolemy I Soter** or **Ptolemy II Philadelphus** and designed by the architect **Sostratus of Cnidus**, completed approximately **280 BCE**
- Height estimates range from **100-130 meters** (based on ancient descriptions and Arab-period accounts) — making it the second or third tallest structure of the ancient world after the Great Pyramid (146 m)
- **Three-tiered design** (confirmed by multiple sources including the Arab traveler **Ibn Battuta** and **al-Idrisi**):
  - **Lower tier**: square base, approximately 55-60 m tall, containing rooms and a ramp for fuel transport
  - **Middle tier**: octagonal, approximately 30 m tall
  - **Upper tier**: cylindrical, topped by a statue (variously described as Poseidon, Zeus, or Alexander the Great)
- The fire at the summit was reportedly visible at distances of **30-50 km** — ancient sources mention a mirror or reflective system that intensified and directed the light
- **Destruction**: progressively damaged by earthquakes:
  - 956 CE: upper sections collapsed
  - 1303/1323 CE: major earthquakes destroyed the remaining structure
  - 1477 CE: the Mamluk Sultan **Qaitbay** built a fort (Citadel of Qaitbay) on the Pharos foundations, incorporating surviving stone

### 1.2 Underwater Archaeological Evidence
- **Jean-Yves Empereur** (Centre d'Études Alexandrines) conducted underwater excavations beginning in **1994** in the harbor near the Qaitbay Citadel:
  - Recovered **massive granite and limestone blocks** (some weighing over 50 tons), columns, statues (including sphinxes and a colossal statue attributed to Ptolemy II as pharaoh), and architectural fragments
  - The recovered materials confirm the Pharos site and provide evidence of the structure's massive scale and materials
  - Over **2,500 architectural elements** were catalogued on the seafloor

### 1.3 Roman Lighthouses
- The Romans built lighthouses across the empire:
  - **Tower of Hercules** (A Coruña, Spain): built in the 1st century CE under the emperor Trajan, renovated in the 18th century — the **oldest extant working lighthouse in the world** (UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2009)
  - **Dover Roman Pharos** (England): octagonal tower, approximately 24 m original height (12 m survive), built c. 1st century CE within Dover Castle — one of the best-preserved Roman lighthouses
  - **Ostia Pharos**: the lighthouse at Rome's port of Ostia, depicted on numerous Roman coins, mosaics, and reliefs — no longer extant but well-documented iconographically
  - **Boulogne-sur-Mer** (France): Tour d'Ordre, a Roman lighthouse that survived until the 17th century

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Reflective Technology
- Multiple ancient and medieval sources describe a **mirror** at the Pharos summit that could direct and amplify the fire's light:
  - Arab accounts describe a mirror (possibly polished bronze or copper) that could direct light to ships at great distance and — in some accounts — could be used to detect and even ignite enemy ships (a claim that parallels Archimedes' mirror stories and is similarly dubious in its more extreme forms)
  - A polished bronze parabolic reflector is physically feasible with ancient technology and would significantly increase the visibility of a fire beacon — modern reconstructions suggest plausibility for a directional light beam

### 2.2 Fuel Logistics
- Maintaining a fire at 100+ meters height required sophisticated logistics:
  - Fuel (wood, possibly oil) had to be transported up the tower — ancient sources describe an internal ramp or spiral corridor wide enough for pack animals
  - The fire had to burn continuously through the night, requiring a permanent staff of fuel handlers and fire tenders

### 2.3 Network of Mediterranean Lighthouses
- Scholars have proposed that Roman lighthouses functioned as an integrated **network** — positioned at key harbors, straits, and headlands to guide maritime commerce across the Mediterranean and beyond (including the English Channel and Atlantic coast)

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Lens Technology
- Researchers have speculated that ancient lighthouses may have used glass or crystal lenses (rather than mirrors) to focus light — but no archaeological evidence of such lenses in lighthouse contexts has been found

### 3.2 Sound Beacons
- Some accounts suggest the Pharos may have incorporated a **sound signaling device** (horn or trumpet) for fog conditions — plausible but unconfirmed
