## QUICK SUMMARY

The Inquisition—spanning the Medieval (1184), Spanish (1478–1834),
Portuguese (1536–1821), and Roman (1542–1965) iterations—represents
one of the most sustained institutional campaigns of knowledge
suppression in Western history. Through mechanisms including the
Index Librorum Prohibitorum, heresy trials, auto-da-fé public
burnings, and the destruction of indigenous manuscripts, the
Inquisition shaped what information survived into the modern era.
Landmark cases such as the execution of Giordano Bruno (1600) and
the trial of Galileo Galilei (1633) illustrate the collision between
ecclesiastical authority and emerging scientific paradigms. The
destruction of Maya codices by Diego de Landa and the eradication
of Cathar texts represent irreplaceable losses to the historical record.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 The Spanish Inquisition (1478–1834)

Pope Sixtus IV issued the papal bull *Exigit Sincerae Devotionis
Affectus* on November 1, 1478, at the request of Ferdinand II of
Aragon and Isabella I of Castile. The Spanish Inquisition was unique
in being controlled primarily by the crown rather than the papacy.
Tomás de Torquemada served as the first Grand Inquisitor (1483–1498),
establishing procedural norms that persisted for centuries. The
tribunal formally operated until its abolition on July 15, 1834,
by Royal Decree under Regent María Cristina (Kamen, 2014).

### 1.2 The Roman Inquisition (1542–1965)

Pope Paul III established the Supreme Sacred Congregation of the
Roman and Universal Inquisition via the bull *Licet ab initio* in
1542 to combat Protestantism and doctrinal deviation. It was
reorganized as the Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith in
1965 under Pope Paul VI. Its archives were partially opened to
scholars in 1998, revealing thousands of trial records
(Bethencourt, 2009).

### 1.3 Index Librorum Prohibitorum

First published in 1559 under Pope Paul IV, the Index went through
twenty editions over four centuries. At its peak, it listed over
4,000 titles including works by Copernicus (*De Revolutionibus*,
listed 1616–1758), Kepler, Descartes, Montesquieu, Voltaire, Kant,
and Hugo. The Index was formally abolished only in 1966 by Pope
Paul VI. Its chilling effect on intellectual production across
Catholic Europe is well documented (Godman, 2000).

### 1.4 The Galileo Trial (1633)

Galileo was tried by the Roman Inquisition for "vehement suspicion
of heresy" after publishing *Dialogue Concerning the Two Chief World
Systems*. He was sentenced to formal abjuration and house arrest at
his villa in Arcetri, where he remained until his death in 1642.
The Vatican formally acknowledged the error in 1992 under Pope
John Paul II, after a 13-year papal commission review
(Finocchiaro, 2005).

### 1.5 Giordano Bruno's Execution (1600)

The Dominican friar was burned at the stake in Rome's Campo de'
Fiori on February 17, 1600, after an eight-year trial. His offenses
included heliocentric cosmology, belief in infinite worlds with
intelligent inhabitants, hermeticism, and denial of core Catholic
doctrines. Bruno's case is distinguished from Galileo's by its
fatal outcome and by Bruno's refusal to recant (Rowland, 2008).

### 1.6 Auto-da-fé Ceremonies

Public rituals of penance and punishment were performed across Spain,
Portugal, and their colonies from the 15th through the 18th
centuries. These elaborate ceremonies served both juridical and
theatrical functions, demonstrating institutional power. Records
from the Spanish Inquisition archives document approximately
150,000 trials, with an estimated 3,000–5,000 executions by burning
(Pérez, 2005).

### 1.7 Diego de Landa's Codex Burning (1562)

Bishop Diego de Landa ordered the destruction of thousands of Maya
manuscripts, idols, and sacred objects in Maní, Yucatán, on July 12,
1562. De Landa himself later wrote: "We found a large number of
books...and as they contained nothing in which were not to be seen
as superstition and lies of the devil, we burned them all." Only
three or four Maya codices survive today—the Dresden, Madrid, Paris,
and possibly the Grolier (Clendinnen, 1987).

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Malleus Maleficarum and Witch Persecution

Published in 1487 by Heinrich Kramer, *Malleus Maleficarum* was not
an official Inquisition document but was widely used by both Catholic
and Protestant authorities for witch trials. The attached papal bull
*Summis desiderantes affectibus* (1484) authorized witch persecution
generally but did not specifically endorse the text. The book went
through 28 editions by 1600 (Broedel, 2003).

### 2.2 Converso Persecution and Knowledge Networks

The targeting of *conversos* (Jewish converts) and *moriscos* (Muslim
converts) disrupted significant intellectual networks. Converso
scholars had maintained Arabic-to-Latin translation traditions vital
for transmitting Aristotelian philosophy, medical knowledge, and
astronomical data. The 1492 Edict of Expulsion and subsequent
persecution scattered these communities (Netanyahu, 1995).

### 2.3 Cathar Text Destruction

The Albigensian Crusade (1209–1229) and subsequent Languedoc
Inquisition resulted in near-total eradication of Cathar writings.
Almost all surviving knowledge of Cathar theology comes from
Inquisition records and hostile polemical sources. The *Liber de
duobus principiis* and the Cathar *Ritual* in Occitan are rare
exceptions (Barber, 2000).

### 2.4 Mesoamerican Manuscript Destruction

Beyond de Landa, Franciscan missionaries in central Mexico
systematically destroyed Aztec *amoxtli* (pictographic codices).
Bishop Juan de Zumárraga's campaigns in the 1530s destroyed
thousands. Only about fifteen pre-Columbian Mesoamerican codices
survive worldwide (Gruzinski, 1993).

### 2.5 Chilling Effect on Scientific Inquiry

Historians argue the Galileo affair produced a measurable chilling
effect on Italian science for decades. René Descartes, upon learning
of Galileo's condemnation, reportedly suppressed his own *Le Monde*
(1633). The center of European scientific gravity shifted northward
to Protestant Holland, England, and the German states (Heilbron, 2010).

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

- **Lost alchemical and hermetic traditions:** The Inquisition may
  have destroyed substantial hermetic corpora revealing continuous
  transmission from late-antique Egyptian and Neoplatonic sources.
  Inventory records of seized libraries were often themselves
  destroyed (Hanegraaff, 2012).

- **Suppressed astronomical observations:** Pre-Copernican church
  astronomers may have made heliocentric-supporting observations
  that were self-censored or suppressed.

- **Broader knowledge control pattern:** Researchers position
  the Inquisition within a longer pattern from the Library of
  Alexandria's destruction through modern classification systems.

- **Indigenous oral knowledge survival:** Suppressed Mesoamerican
  knowledge may have survived through oral transmission within
  indigenous communities, encoding information in narrative form
  that escaped colonial authorities' notice.

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source)

- **"Millions killed" by the Inquisition:** Popular claims of
  millions of executions are not supported by archival evidence.
  Best scholarship: 3,000–5,000 executions across 356 years
  (Kamen, 2014).

- **Deliberate suppression of advanced ancient technology:** Claims
  that the Inquisition targeted knowledge of lost civilizations or
  ET contact have no archival support.

- **Secret Vatican vault of scientific truths:** While the Vatican
  Archives contain vast uncatalogued material, conspiracy claims
  about hidden revolutionary manuscripts are unsubstantiated.
