## QUICK SUMMARY

**Information warfare** — the strategic use of information (and misinformation) to achieve political, military, or economic objectives — has entered a new and qualitatively different phase in the digital era. While propaganda and historical revisionism are as old as civilization itself, the combination of **social media platforms** (which reward engagement over accuracy), **computational propaganda** (bots, troll farms, algorithmic amplification), **deepfake and synthetic media** (AI-generated video, audio, and text that can fabricate convincing false evidence), and **platform monopoly** (a small number of companies controlling information flows for billions of people) has created an information environment in which **historical revisionism, conspiracy theories, and state-sponsored disinformation** can spread faster, further, and with greater apparent credibility than at any previous point in history. For this project — which deals extensively with contested historical claims, alternative narratives, and suppressed knowledge — understanding modern information warfare is essential: the same digital ecosystem that can amplify genuine suppressed knowledge can equally amplify deliberate falsification. Key threats include: **state-sponsored historical revisionism** (governments rewriting history through digital disinformation campaigns), **deepfake archaeology** (fabricated evidence for false historical claims), **epistemic tribalism** (communities that form around shared conspiracies and become impervious to correction), and **platform-driven radicalization** (algorithmic recommendation of increasingly extreme content).

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 State-Sponsored Disinformation Campaigns
- Multiple state actors operate large-scale digital disinformation operations:
  - **Russia's Internet Research Agency** (IRA): the St. Petersburg-based "troll farm" was documented by the U.S. Senate Intelligence Committee, the Mueller investigation, and academic researchers as conducting systematic disinformation campaigns — creating fake social media accounts, amplifying divisive content, and spreading fabricated narratives about history and current events
  - **Chinese state media** and affiliated accounts have been documented promoting revisionist narratives about Tiananmen Square, Xinjiang, Taiwan, and historical territorial claims through coordinated social media operations
  - The operations involve creating **false consensus** — making fringe narratives appear mainstream through coordinated posting, fake engagement, and simulation of organic social media activity

### 1.2 Computational Propaganda
- The **Oxford Internet Institute's Computational Propaganda Project** has documented organized social media manipulation in 70+ countries:
  - Government, military, or political party operations use bots, fake accounts, and coordinated inauthentic behavior to manipulate public discourse
  - Techniques include: automated posting (bots), paid human operators ("trolls"), coordinated amplification networks, hashtag manipulation, and targeted disinformation
  - Woolley and Howard (*Computational Propaganda*, 2019) provide systematic analysis of how these operations function across platforms and countries

### 1.3 Deepfakes and Synthetic Media
- **Deepfake technology** — AI-generated synthetic video and audio — creates new possibilities for fabricating historical evidence:
  - As of the early 2020s, deepfake technology can produce realistic video of public figures saying things they never said, audio that mimics specific voices, and synthetic photographs indistinguishable from real ones to untrained observers
  - Implications for historical revisionism: fabricated "archival footage," fake "eyewitness testimony," and synthetic "documentary evidence" become technically possible
  - Detection technology exists but is in a continuous arms race with generation technology

### 1.4 Platform-Driven Radicalization
- Social media recommendation algorithms have been documented driving users toward more extreme content:
  - YouTube's recommendation algorithm was found to systematically recommend increasingly extreme content — a user watching mainstream videos about ancient history could be algorithmically guided toward conspiracy theories through chains of recommendation
  - Facebook's internal research (revealed by whistleblower Frances Haugen 2021) acknowledged that the platform's algorithms amplified divisive and sensational content because it generated more engagement

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Historical Revisionism in the Digital Age
- Digital disinformation has been used to promote specific historical revisionist narratives:
  - **Holocaust denial** has been amplified through social media — despite platform policies against it, denial content circulates through coded language, alternative platforms, and ephemeral media
  - **Japanese war crime revisionism**: digital campaigns promoting denial of the Nanjing Massacre, comfort women, and other WWII atrocities — coordinated through nationalist social media networks
  - **Lost Cause mythology**: the narrative that the American Civil War was about "states' rights" rather than slavery has been amplified through digital networks — with coordinated efforts to edit Wikipedia, produce YouTube content, and shape social media discourse

### 2.2 The "Post-Truth" Epistemic Environment
- Several scholars have argued that digital information warfare has produced a qualitatively new epistemic environment:
  - **Kakutani** (*The Death of Truth*, 2018): traces the erosion of shared factual foundations in public discourse
  - **Benkler, Faris and Roberts** (*Network Propaganda*, 2018): document the asymmetric media ecosystem in which partisan media operates with minimal accountability
  - The concept of **"epistemic crisis"** — a breakdown in shared mechanisms for establishing facts — has been applied to contemporary democracies facing organized disinformation

### 2.3 Implications for Alternative History
- The information warfare environment creates specific challenges for evaluating alternative historical claims:
  - Legitimate alternative history (backed by evidence and rigorous analysis) becomes difficult to distinguish from fabricated conspiracy theories in a disinformation-saturated media environment
  - Coordinated campaigns can create false evidence for fake alternative histories — or suppress genuine alternative histories by burying them in noise
  - The "cry wolf" effect: when disinformation is ubiquitous, genuine anomalies and suppressed evidence may be dismissed as "just another conspiracy theory"

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 AI-Generated Fake Archaeological Evidence
- As AI image and video generation improve, the creation of convincing fake archaeological photos, inscriptions, and artifacts becomes increasingly feasible — potentially creating a new category of archaeological fraud that is harder to detect than physical forgery

### 3.2 Long-Term Epistemic Consequences
- The long-term effects of sustained information warfare on societies' ability to maintain accurate historical knowledge are unknown — some theorists warn of a potential "Dark Age" of information in which reliable knowledge becomes increasingly difficult to establish or maintain

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 All Mainstream Media Is Propaganda
- **[OVERSTATED]** While mainstream media has biases, failures, and vulnerabilities to manipulation, the claim that all mainstream media is equivalent to state propaganda collapses important distinctions between institutions with editorial standards, fact-checking, and correction mechanisms and those without

### 4.2 Digital Technology Makes Truth Impossible
- **[OVERSTATED]** While digital disinformation creates genuine epistemic challenges, it also creates new tools for verification — open-source intelligence (OSINT), satellite imagery analysis, digital forensics, collaborative fact-checking, and transparent source databases. The epistemic environment is more challenging, not hopeless
