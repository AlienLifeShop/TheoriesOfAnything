## QUICK SUMMARY

Academic historiography has systematically **privileged written texts over oral sources** — treating written documents as reliable evidence and oral traditions as unreliable, distorted, or "merely" mythological. This **literacy bias** — the assumption that written records are inherently superior to spoken ones — has deep roots in Western intellectual tradition (from Plato's critique of writing in *Phaedrus* through Enlightenment rationalism to modern historical methodology) and has profound consequences: it systematically devalues the knowledge traditions of **non-literate and semi-literate societies** (the vast majority of human cultures throughout history), marginalizes oral sources even when they contain empirically verifiable information, and privileges the perspectives of literate elites while silencing the voices of the majority who did not write. **Jan Vansina** (*Oral Tradition as History*, 1985) established the methodological framework for treating oral traditions as historical sources — subject to critical analysis analogous to (but different from) the criticism applied to written documents. **Paul Thompson** (*The Voice of the Past*, 1978) advocated oral history as a democratic methodology — giving voice to people excluded from the written record (workers, women, minorities, colonized peoples). Yet despite decades of methodological development, oral sources continue to occupy a subordinate position in historical practice — oral evidence is treated as supplementary to written evidence rather than as an independent, equally valid category of historical knowledge. For this project, which draws on both textual and oral traditions, recognizing and compensating for literacy bias is essential.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 The Literacy Bias in History
- **Western historical methodology** since the 19th century has been founded on written documents as the primary evidence base:
  - Leopold von Ranke's principle of history "as it actually happened" (*wie es eigentlich gewesen*) was explicitly tied to archival research — critical analysis of written documents
  - "Prehistory" was defined as the period before writing — implying that history proper begins only with literacy, and that the 95%+ of human existence before writing is not "history"
  - This framework systematically excludes non-literate societies from historical discourse — or relegates them to "anthropology" or "folklore" rather than "history," with corresponding loss of epistemic authority

### 1.2 Vansina's Oral Tradition Methodology
- **Jan Vansina** (1961, revised 1985) established the first rigorous methodology for using oral traditions as historical sources:
  - **Classification**: accounts (testimony about the past), lists (genealogies, king lists), poetry/songs (mnemonic vehicles), and formalized public recitations
  - **Source criticism**: oral traditions, like written sources, must be evaluated for bias, purpose, transmission chain, and reliability — but the criteria differ (oral traditions are shaped by performance context, mnemonic constraints, and social function rather than by scribal accuracy)
  - **Testimonial chains**: just as written documents are evaluated through transmission history (copy chains, scribal errors), oral traditions have transmission chains — each link potentially introducing modification
  - **Key insight**: oral traditions are not simply "garbled history" — they are **structured by their own logic** (mnemonic, poetic, genealogical) that must be understood on its own terms

### 1.3 Empirically Validated Oral Traditions
- Multiple cases demonstrate that oral traditions preserved **historically accurate information** over centuries or millennia:
  - **Aboriginal Australian oral traditions** of coastal inundation events — consistent with post-glacial sea-level rise 7,000-13,000 years ago (Nunn & Reid 2016)
  - **Hawaiian oral traditions** of volcanic eruptions — corresponding to geologically documented eruption dates with remarkable precision (Swanson 2008)
  - **North American indigenous traditions** of the Cascadia subduction zone earthquake and tsunami (~1700 CE) — confirmed by geological and Japanese historical records of the event
  - **Icelandic sagas**: the *Vinland Sagas* described Viking settlement in North America — confirmed by archaeological discovery of L'Anse aux Meadows (Newfoundland) in 1960
  - These cases demonstrate that oral traditions can preserve empirically accurate information for far longer than the literacy-biased model predicted

### 1.4 Thompson and Democratic History
- **Paul Thompson** (*The Voice of the Past*, 1978, 4th ed. 2017) argued that oral history is inherently **democratic**:
  - Written historical records disproportionately represent the literate, the powerful, and the official — creating history from the perspective of administrators, clergy, and elites
  - Oral history provides access to the experiences, perspectives, and knowledge of people who left no written records — workers, women, minorities, immigrants, colonized peoples
  - The "history from below" movement (E.P. Thompson, Raphael Samuel, the History Workshop) relied heavily on oral testimony to reconstruct working-class, subaltern, and marginalized histories

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Oral and Written as Complementary Systems
- **Tonkin** (*Narrating Our Pasts*, 1992) argued that oral and written sources should be treated as **complementary rather than hierarchical**:
  - Each has distinctive strengths: written records excel at preserving verbatim content, dates, and administrative detail; oral traditions excel at preserving meaning, context, emotional significance, and community relevance
  - Combining oral and written evidence produces richer and more balanced history than either source type alone
  - In many historical contexts (medieval Europe, colonial encounters, indigenous-settler interactions), oral and written records coexist — and comparing them reveals the biases of each

### 2.2 Memory Studies and Oral Reliability
- Research on human memory complicates both the dismissal and the celebration of oral sources:
  - **Episodic memory** is reconstructive and unreliable for specific details — eyewitness testimony studies (Loftus) demonstrate this clearly
  - But **social/collective memory** — structured by mnemonic devices (rhyme, rhythm, narrative, song, genealogy), social accountability (public performance, correction by audience), and institutional practice (designated memory-keepers, initiation) — operates differently from individual recall
  - The difference between "an individual trying to remember what happened" and "a trained specialist performing a structured tradition validated by community" is fundamental — the latter can achieve remarkable accuracy over long periods

### 2.3 The "Oral Residue" in Written Texts
- **Walter Ong** (*Orality and Literacy*, 1982) argued that Western writing itself carries "oral residues" — stylistic, structural, and cognitive patterns inherited from oral tradition:
  - Formulaic language (Homeric epithets, Biblical parallelism), repetitive structure, narrative organization, and other features of ancient literature reflect composition in oral or semi-oral contexts
  - Recognizing oral residue in written texts can illuminate their original performance context and meaning — information that is lost when they are treated as purely literary artifacts

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Depth of Oral Memory
- If Aboriginal traditions can preserve accurate information about events 7,000-13,000 years old, this challenges assumptions about the **maximum depth** of oral memory:
  - What other information — geomorphological, astronomical, biological, historical — might be encoded in oral traditions that have not yet been checked against scientific records?
  - The systematic cross-referencing of oral traditions with geological, archaeological, and genetic data is still in its infancy

### 3.2 Recovering Suppressed Oral Knowledge
- In regions where colonial contact disrupted but did not completely destroy oral traditions (Australia, Amazonia, Papua New Guinea, parts of Africa), systematic collection and analysis of surviving traditions could recover knowledge that predates any written record of those regions

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Oral Traditions Are Perfectly Preserved
- **[OVERSTATED]** While oral traditions can preserve accurate core information, they are not immune to distortion — they change through retelling, are influenced by contact with other traditions, and adapt to contemporary social needs. Critical evaluation is essential, just as it is for written sources

### 4.2 Written Sources Are Always More Accurate
- **[CONTRADICTED]** Written sources are subject to their own forms of distortion — scribal error, deliberate falsification, ideological bias, and selective preservation. Medieval chronicles, ancient royal inscriptions, and official histories are frequently propagandistic and must be read critically — writing does not guarantee accuracy
