## QUICK SUMMARY

**Thomas Kuhn's** *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* (1962) fundamentally altered understanding of how science changes by arguing that scientific progress is **not** a smooth, cumulative accumulation of knowledge but rather a punctuated process alternating between periods of **"normal science"** — research conducted within an accepted paradigm (shared assumptions, methods, exemplars) — and revolutionary **"paradigm shifts"** — extraordinary episodes in which the entire framework is overturned and replaced. Kuhn identified a characteristic sequence: **(1)** a dominant paradigm governs normal science; **(2)** anomalies — findings that resist explanation within the paradigm — accumulate; **(3)** crisis develops as anomalies become too numerous or fundamental to ignore; **(4)** revolutionary science proposes a new paradigm that resolves the anomalies; **(5)** the new paradigm wins adherents (often generationally — as Max Planck observed, "science advances one funeral at a time"); **(6)** a new period of normal science begins. Kuhn's framework was supplemented and challenged by **Karl Popper's** falsificationism (science advances through conjecture and refutation, not paradigm-bound puzzle-solving), **Imre Lakatos's** methodology of scientific research programmes (competing programmes with "hard cores" and "protective belts"), **Paul Feyerabend's** epistemological anarchism (there is no universal method — "anything goes"), and **Larry Laudan's** problem-solving approach. For this project's meta-analysis about knowledge suppression, Kuhn's framework is essential: it demonstrates that **resistance to new ideas is not a dysfunction of science but a structural feature** — normal science actively suppresses anomalies, marginalizes heterodox researchers, and defends established paradigms. This pattern explains why even well-supported new evidence can face decades of institutional resistance before acceptance.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Kuhn's Framework
- **Normal science** operates within a paradigm — a constellation of shared beliefs, values, techniques, and exemplars that define legitimate problems and acceptable solutions:
  - Scientists engaged in normal science are "puzzle-solvers" — working on problems that the paradigm defines as meaningful and solvable
  - **Anomalies** (findings inconsistent with the paradigm) are initially dismissed, rationalized, or set aside as "not yet explained" rather than treated as threats to the paradigm
  - The paradigm determines what counts as evidence, what questions are worth asking, and what methods are legitimate — creating a self-reinforcing framework
- **Crisis and revolution**:
  - When anomalies accumulate beyond a tolerable threshold — especially when fundamental predictions fail — the paradigm enters "crisis"
  - During crisis, new candidate paradigms emerge. These are **incommensurable** with the old paradigm — they don't share the same assumptions, vocabulary, or standards of evidence, making direct comparison impossible
  - The transition to a new paradigm involves a "Gestalt switch" — scientists see the same data differently, not just interpret it differently
- **Historical examples Kuhn analyzed**: the Copernican revolution (geocentric → heliocentric), the shift from phlogiston to Lavoisier's oxygen theory, Einstein's relativity replacing Newtonian mechanics, quantum mechanics

### 1.2 Popper's Falsificationism
- **Karl Popper** (*The Logic of Scientific Discovery*, 1934/1959) argued that the defining characteristic of scientific theories is their **falsifiability** — the possibility of being proven wrong:
  - Science advances through **conjecture and refutation** — bold hypotheses subjected to severe tests
  - Theories that survive repeated attempts at falsification are "corroborated" (not "proven") — they remain provisional
  - **Demarcation criterion**: what distinguishes science from pseudoscience is not whether a theory is true but whether it is falsifiable — theories that cannot in principle be disproven (because they explain everything) are not scientific
- **Conflict with Kuhn**: Popper criticized Kuhn's framework as describing an irrational, sociological process rather than a logical one — if paradigm choice is not determined by evidence but by community consensus, how is science different from other social practices?

### 1.3 Lakatos's Research Programmes
- **Imre Lakatos** (*The Methodology of Scientific Research Programmes*, 1978) proposed a synthesis of Kuhn and Popper:
  - Scientific research programmes consist of a **"hard core"** (non-negotiable assumptions) surrounded by a **"protective belt"** of auxiliary hypotheses that can be modified to accommodate anomalies
  - A programme is **"progressive"** when its protective belt modifications generate novel predictions that are confirmed — and **"degenerating"** when modifications are purely ad hoc, saving the theory without producing new knowledge
  - Rational choice between competing programmes is possible — choose the progressive one — but the transition may take time, as initially degenerating programmes can become progressive again
- **Relevance to suppression thesis**: Lakatos's framework explains why anomalous evidence may be legitimately set aside (it belongs in the protective belt) but also why institutions can become irrational (defending a degenerating programme long past its utility)

### 1.4 Documented Historical Resistance
- **Continental drift / plate tectonics**: Wegener proposed continental drift in 1912 — systematically rejected for ~50 years by mainstream geology despite substantial evidence, partly because Wegener's proposed mechanism was wrong (though his observation was correct). Accepted only after sea-floor spreading evidence in the 1960s
- **Helicobacter pylori and ulcers**: Barry Marshall and Robin Warren proposed (1982) that stomach ulcers were caused by bacteria, not stress/acid. The medical establishment resisted for over a decade — Marshall famously drank H. pylori culture to demonstrate infection. Nobel Prize in 2005
- **Semmelweis and handwashing**: Ignaz Semmelweis demonstrated (1847) that handwashing before obstetric examinations dramatically reduced maternal mortality. His findings were ridiculed and rejected by the medical establishment; he died in an asylum in 1865. His work was vindicated by germ theory

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 The Planck Principle
- Max Planck's observation (often paraphrased as "science advances one funeral at a time") has been empirically investigated:
  - Azoulay, Fons-Rosen, and Graff Zivin (2019, *American Economic Review*) found that after prominent scientists die, entry into their field by new researchers increases — and the new entrants' work diverges from the established paradigm, suggesting that intellectual "incumbents" do suppress innovation during their lifetimes
  - This provides empirical support for the idea that resistance to new paradigms is partly generational

### 2.2 Feyerabend's Epistemological Anarchism
- **Paul Feyerabend** (*Against Method*, 1975) argued that no single methodology governs scientific success:
  - Successful scientific revolutions often violate accepted methodological standards — Galileo used rhetoric, propaganda, and selective evidence as much as rigorous observation
  - The implication: methodological rules can function as tools of suppression — enforcing conformity rather than truth-seeking
  - Feyerabend's position is controversial but raises legitimate questions about whether methodological orthodoxy can prevent recognition of valid but unconventional evidence

### 2.3 The Sociology of Scientific Knowledge (SSK)
- **Strong programme** (Bloor 1976, Barnes & Bloor): scientific knowledge is socially constructed — the same sociological explanations should apply to both true and false beliefs
  - Scientific facts are not simply "discovered" but are **produced** through social processes — negotiation, consensus-building, institutional authority, resource allocation
  - **Controversy**: SSK has been criticized for implying that scientific claims have no epistemic privilege over other beliefs — the "science wars" of the 1990s centered on this debate

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Permanent Paradigm Lock-In
- If paradigm resistance is a structural feature of science rather than a failure, it raises the possibility that some paradigm-locked fields may **never** resolve certain anomalies — not because the evidence is insufficient, but because the institutional structures (funding, peer review, career incentives) make paradigm-threatening research too professionally risky to pursue

### 3.2 Archaeology's Pending Paradigm Shifts
- Several domains within archaeology may be accumulating anomalies that could eventually trigger paradigm shifts:
  - The age and complexity of pre-agricultural monumental construction (Göbekli Tepe and successors)
  - Pre-Clovis evidence in the Americas
  - The role of catastrophic events in punctuating human history
  - Whether these represent genuine Kuhnian crisis or normal science accommodating new data remains debated

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Mainstream Science Is a Conspiracy of Suppression
- **[OVERSTATED]** While Kuhn's framework demonstrates that paradigm resistance is real and structural, characterizing mainstream science as a deliberate conspiracy to suppress truth misrepresents the mechanism. Paradigm resistance is largely unconscious, structural, and institutional — not orchestrated by coordinated actors

### 4.2 All Rejected Ideas Are Suppressed Paradigm Shifts
- **[CONTRADICTED]** The vast majority of ideas rejected by scientific consensus are rejected because they are wrong, poorly supported, or unfalsifiable — not because they represent suppressed paradigm shifts. Genuine paradigm shifts, while resisted initially, are rare events; most heterodox claims are heterodox because they lack adequate evidence
