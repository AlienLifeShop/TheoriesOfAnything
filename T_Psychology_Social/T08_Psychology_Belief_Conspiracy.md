## QUICK SUMMARY

The psychology of conspiracy thinking examines why individuals adopt beliefs in secret plots by powerful actors to achieve malevolent goals — beliefs that often resist disconfirmation and form interconnected "monological" networks where endorsing one conspiracy predicts endorsing others.
Research by Jan-Willem van Prooijen, Rob Brotherton, Viren Swami, and others has identified several cognitive and motivational factors underlying conspiracy belief: hyperactive agency detection (Barrett, 2000), pattern perception in random stimuli (apophenia), proportionality bias (major events need major causes), need for cognitive closure (Kruglanski & Webster, 1996), and feelings of powerlessness or threat.
Critically, conspiracy thinking is not pathological in itself — it exists on a spectrum, and some conspiracies are real (Watergate, MKULTRA, COINTELPRO, the Tuskegee experiment).
The challenge for rational inquiry is distinguishing warranted suspicion from unfounded conspiratorial ideation — a distinction that requires institutional transparency, epistemological literacy, and understanding one's own cognitive tendencies.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Conspiracy belief as a spectrum

Conspiracy belief is not a binary trait but a continuous dimension present in all populations:
- Generic Conspiracist Beliefs Scale (Brotherton et al., 2013) measures endorsement on a continuum.
- Epidemiological surveys consistently find that 50–70% of Americans endorse at least one conspiracy theory (Oliver & Wood, 2014).
- Belief levels vary by context, political orientation, and perceived threat — no demographic group is immune.
- The "conspiracy mentality" (general disposition to see conspiracies) predicts endorsement better than specific political ideology.

### 1.2 Cognitive factors in conspiracy thinking

Several cognitive mechanisms contribute to conspiracy belief:
- **Pattern perception (apophenia):** People who detect more patterns in random stimuli are more likely to endorse conspiracies (van Prooijen et al., 2018).
- **Agency detection:** The tendency to attribute events to intentional agents rather than impersonal forces is linked to conspiracy belief (Douglas et al., 2016).
- **Proportionality bias:** A major event (assassination of a president) "deserves" a major cause (conspiracy) rather than a minor one (lone gunman) (Leman & Cinnirella, 2007).
- **Conjunction fallacy:** Conspiracy narratives often include detailed scenarios that feel more plausible because of their specificity, even when probability theory says otherwise.

### 1.3 Motivational underpinnings

Douglas et al. (2017) identified three categories of psychological needs served by conspiracy beliefs:
- **Epistemic needs:** desire for certainty and understanding of complex events.
- **Existential needs:** desire for control and safety when feeling powerless or threatened.
- **Social needs:** desire to maintain a positive image of one's in-group.
Paradoxically, while conspiracy beliefs serve these needs, evidence suggests they do not actually satisfy them — belief in conspiracies correlates with *increased* anxiety, anomie, and political disengagement (Jolley & Douglas, 2014).

### 1.4 Monological belief system

Goertzel (1994) proposed that conspiracy beliefs form a "monological belief system" — endorsing one conspiracy predicts endorsing others, even logically contradictory ones:
- Wood et al. (2012) demonstrated that endorsing the claim that Osama bin Laden was already dead before the raid correlated with endorsing the claim that he was still alive.
- This pattern suggests that a general worldview of powerful-actor deception underlies specific conspiracy endorsements.
- However, Sutton & Douglas (2014) note that not all conspiracy beliefs cluster equally — some are more independent.

### 1.5 Real conspiracies and institutional trust

The dismissal of all conspiracy thinking as irrational is complicated by documented real conspiracies:
- **MKULTRA:** CIA mind-control experiments (documented through FOIA).
- **COINTELPRO:** FBI surveillance and disruption of civil rights organizations.
- **Iran-Contra:** Government arms dealing and covert operations.
- **Tuskegee syphilis study:** 40-year study denying treatment to Black men.
These real cases demonstrate that institutional deception does occur, and that dismissing all conspiracy claims as paranoid can itself be epistemically irresponsible (Keeley, 1999; Dentith, 2014).

## 2. CREDIBLE BUT DEBATED CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated)

### 2.1 Conspiracy belief and political extremism

Research suggests conspiracy beliefs are elevated at *both* political extremes:
- van Prooijen et al. (2015) found a quadratic relationship between political orientation and conspiratorial thinking.
- However, the content differs: left-wing conspiracies focus on corporate/military-industrial collusion; right-wing conspiracies focus on government overreach/cultural subversion.
- Whether this is genuinely symmetric or whether one side currently shows higher levels is contested and politically sensitive.

### 2.2 Social media amplification

Social media's role in spreading conspiracy theories is debated:
- Algorithm-driven recommendation systems can create "rabbit holes" toward increasingly extreme content (Roose, 2019; Ribeiro et al., 2020).
- However, Guess et al. (2020) found that actual exposure to misinformation via social media is concentrated among a small minority of users.
- The relationship between exposure and belief change is weaker than commonly assumed.

### 2.3 Need for cognitive closure

Kruglanski & Webster's (1996) need for closure (NFC) — the desire for definite answers and discomfort with ambiguity — has been linked to conspiracy belief:
- High NFC individuals may latch onto conspiracy theories as simple, all-encompassing explanations.
- However, some studies find no relationship, or find that conspiracy beliefs actually *increase* uncertainty rather than reducing it.
- The relationship likely depends on whether conspiracy beliefs are adopted early (providing closure) or encountered later (creating uncertainty).

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Evolutionary function of conspiracy detection

van Prooijen & van Vugt (2018) proposed an "adaptive conspiracism" hypothesis: in ancestral environments, detecting coalitional threats was a survival advantage — those who failed to detect real conspiracies faced harm or death.
While plausible, the retrospective nature of evolutionary explanations makes this difficult to test, and the gap between ancestral coalition detection and modern conspiracy theories about moon landings is substantial.

---

## 4. DUBIOUS OR FRINGE CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Conspiracy theorists are mentally ill

While clinical paranoia exists, mainstream conspiracy belief is not a psychiatric disorder. Most conspiracy believers are psychologically normal individuals responding to genuine uncertainty with imperfect cognitive tools (Freeman & Bentall, 2017). Pathologizing conspiracy thinking risks dismissing legitimate institutional criticism.

### 4.2 Education eliminates conspiracy thinking

While analytical thinking is modestly correlated with lower conspiracy belief, education does not eliminate it — many conspiracy beliefs are held by educated individuals, and the "smart idiot" effect (Kahan, 2013) shows that more educated partisans can be *more* entrenched in motivated reasoning.
