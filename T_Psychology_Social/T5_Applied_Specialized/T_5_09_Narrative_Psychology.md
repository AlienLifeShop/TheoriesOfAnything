## QUICK SUMMARY

**Narrative psychology** — the study of how humans make sense of their lives, construct identity, and organize experience through **storytelling** — emerged as a distinct field in the 1980s–1990s through the work of **Jerome Bruner** (who distinguished **narrative knowing** from paradigmatic/logical-scientific knowing, 1986), **Dan McAdams** (whose **life story model of identity** proposed that adults construct a coherent self through an evolving, internalized narrative, 1985/1993), and **Theodore Sarbin** (who argued that narrative is the root metaphor for psychology itself, 1986). The core insight is that humans are fundamentally **storytelling animals** (Homo narrans): we do not simply record events but actively **emplot** them — selecting, sequencing, and interpreting experiences into causal-temporal chains with protagonists, conflicts, turning points, and moral themes. **Narrative identity** (McAdams & McLean, 2013) is the internalized, evolving story of the self that integrates the reconstructed past, perceived present, and anticipated future — providing a sense of unity, purpose, and meaning. Key narrative structures include **redemption sequences** (bad events leading to good outcomes — "I suffered but grew from it") — associated with well-being and generativity — and **contamination sequences** (good events ruined by subsequent negatives — "things were going well but then everything fell apart") — associated with depression and stagnation. **Narrative therapy** (Michael White and David Epston, 1990) applies these insights clinically: if identity is a story, then psychological problems are maintained by **dominant narratives** (problem-saturated stories) — and therapy involves **externalizing** the problem, identifying **unique outcomes** (exceptions to the problem narrative), and **re-authoring** a preferred, more agentic life story.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 Bruner's Two Modes of Thought
- **Jerome Bruner** (*Actual Minds, Possible Worlds*, 1986): distinguished two fundamental modes of cognitive functioning:
  1. **Paradigmatic mode**: logical-scientific thinking — categorization, abstraction, deduction, hypothesis testing
  2. **Narrative mode**: storying — organizing experience in terms of characters, intentions, temporal unfolding, and meaning
- Both modes are irreducible — narrative is not a primitive form of logical thought but a distinct, equally valid way of constructing reality
- Narratives achieve **verisimilitude** (lifelikeness) rather than **verifiability** (truth conditions) — stories are judged by coherence and resonance, not by logical proof

### 1.2 McAdams's Life Story Model
- **Dan McAdams** (*The Stories We Live By*, 1993): proposed that beyond dispositional traits (Big Five) and characteristic adaptations (goals, defenses, coping strategies), identity is constituted by an evolving **life narrative** — an internalized story of the self incorporating:
  - **Narrative tone**: overall optimism/pessimism established in early experiences (attachment)
  - **Imagery**: key images, scenes, and metaphors that define the self-story
  - **Theme**: agency (power, achievement, autonomy) and communion (love, intimacy, belonging)
  - **Ideological setting**: beliefs, values, and worldview providing a backdrop
  - **Nuclear episodes**: key life story scenes — high points, low points, turning points
- Longitudinal research: narrative identity consolidates in late adolescence/emerging adulthood and continues evolving into old age
- Cultural variation: Western individualist cultures emphasize autonomous, achievement-oriented narratives; East Asian cultures emphasize relational, communal themes

### 1.3 Redemption and Contamination Sequences
- **Redemption**: narratives in which bad events or suffering are followed by growth, insight, or positive transformation — "I went through a terrible divorce, but it taught me who I really am"
- **Contamination**: narratives in which positive events are undermined or spoiled by subsequent negatives — "I was happy until…"
- McAdams (2006): adults with more redemption sequences in their life narratives score higher on well-being, generativity, and ego development; contamination sequences correlate with depression and lower life satisfaction

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Narrative Therapy
- **Michael White and David Epston** (*Narrative Means to Therapeutic Ends*, 1990): developed narrative therapy based on:
  - **Externalization**: separating the person from the problem ("The problem is the problem, not the person") — e.g., "When did Anxiety first show up in your life?"
  - **Unique outcomes**: identifying times when the problem did not dominate — exceptions that reveal the person's existing competence and agency
  - **Re-authoring**: helping the client construct a preferred narrative that amplifies strengths, values, and hopes over problem-saturated descriptions
- Evidence base: growing but smaller than CBT; RCTs show benefits for depression, PTSD, and family conflict — narrative therapy is particularly valued in cross-cultural and community contexts

### 2.2 Autobiographical Reasoning and Development
- **Autobiographical reasoning** (Habermas & Bluck, 2000): the cognitive process of linking life events to the self — creating causal-motivational connections ("because X happened, I became Y")
- Develops through adolescence: teens progressively construct more causally coherent and thematically organized life narratives — this process is itself a key developmental task contributing to identity formation (Erikson's identity achievement)
- **Narrative coherence** relates to psychological adjustment: people with more coherent, causally connected life narratives tend to show better mental health

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Narrative as the Fundamental Structure of Consciousness
- Some theorists (Sarbin, 1986; Dennett's "narrative self" in *Consciousness Explained*, 1991; Ricoeur's narrative identity theory) propose that the self is *nothing but* a narrative — that there is no core self beneath the story, only the ongoing act of narration. This strong ontological claim remains philosophically debated — critics argue it conflates the *representation* of the self with the *reality* of subjective experience, and that pre-narrative infants and non-verbal experiences suggest self-awareness that precedes narrative capacity

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Everyone Has a Single, Fixed Life Story
- **[INCORRECT]** Life narratives are not static — they are actively revised, reinterpreted, and reconstructed throughout life. The same events can be emplotted differently at different ages or after new experiences. Furthermore, individuals hold multiple, sometimes contradictory narrative threads simultaneously. The idea of a single "true" life story is itself a cultural construct
