## QUICK SUMMARY

Psychology's formal history as an independent discipline spans approximately 150 years — from **Wilhelm Wundt's founding** of the first experimental psychology laboratory in Leipzig (1879) to the present day. The discipline has undergone multiple paradigm shifts, each redefining what psychology studies, how it studies it, and what counts as evidence.

**Major eras:** (1) **Structuralism** (Wundt, Titchener, 1879–1920s) — introspective analysis of conscious experience into elements. (2) **Functionalism** (James, Dewey, 1890s–1920s) — how mental processes help organisms adapt. (3) **Psychoanalysis** (Freud, 1900–1960s) — unconscious drives, defense mechanisms, developmental stages. (4) **Behaviorism** (Watson, Skinner, 1913–1960s) — observable behavior; conditioning; rejection of mentalism. (5) **Humanistic psychology** (Maslow, Rogers, 1950s–1970s) — self-actualization, free will, subjective experience. (6) **Cognitive revolution** (1956–present) — mental representations, information processing, computational models. (7) **Neuroscience integration** (1990s–present) — brain-behavior relationships, fMRI, genetics. (8) **Replication crisis and reform** (2011–present) — open science, pre-registration, and methodological reform.

Each era contributed enduring concepts while also producing ideas that were later abandoned. Modern psychology is methodologically pluralistic, increasingly neuroscience-informed, globally diverse (though still WEIRD-dominated), and undergoing a profound methodological reformation.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Pre-scientific and philosophical roots

- **Ancient contributions:** Aristotle's *De Anima* (~350 BCE) — empiricist approach to the soul/psyche as the form of the body; systematic observation of memory, sensation, emotion. Plato — nativist/rationalist; tripartite soul (reason, spirit, appetite). Hippocrates — biological basis of temperament (four humors; incorrect but foundational for biopsychology).
- **Early modern period:** Descartes (1641) — mind-body dualism, reflex arc concept; Locke (1690) — *tabula rasa*, empiricism, associationism; Kant (1781) — argued psychology could never be a science because mental processes cannot be mathematically quantified (proved wrong).
- **Psychophysics (Weber, 1834; Fechner, 1860):** First quantitative measurement of psychological phenomena — Weber's law (just-noticeable difference is proportional to stimulus magnitude); Fechner's psychophysical methods (method of limits, method of constant stimuli) — demonstrated that mental phenomena could be measured, paving the way for experimental psychology.

### 1.2 Founding and early schools (1879–1920s)

- **Wilhelm Wundt (1879):** Founded the first experimental psychology laboratory at the University of Leipzig — studied reaction time, attention, sensation, perception; trained many students who spread experimental psychology globally; used controlled introspection (Selbstbeobachtung) as a method.
- **Structuralism (Titchener, 1890s–1920s):** Wundt's student at Cornell — aimed to identify the structural elements of consciousness (sensations, images, feelings) through introspection; declined because introspection was unreliable (different labs got different results).
- **Functionalism (James, Dewey, Angell, 1890s–1920s):** Influenced by Darwin — asked *what functions* consciousness serves (adaptation) rather than *what it is made of*; William James's *Principles of Psychology* (1890) — among the most influential psychology texts ever written; pragmatic, applied orientation; preceded behaviorism.
- **Gestalt psychology (Wertheimer, Köhler, Koffka, 1912–1940s):** "The whole is different from the sum of its parts" — studied perceptual organization (laws of grouping: proximity, similarity, closure, good continuation); challenged both structuralism (atomistic) and behaviorism (anti-mentalistic); contributed to cognitive psychology.

### 1.3 The great schools (1900–1960s)

- **Psychoanalysis (Freud, 1900–1960s):** Unconscious processes, psychosexual development (oral, anal, phallic, latency, genital), id/ego/superego, defense mechanisms, dream interpretation, free association, transference; enormously influential in culture, clinical practice, and humanities; largely rejected by academic psychology for poor falsifiability (Popper, 1962), lack of empirical support for many specific claims (repression, psychosexual stages), and retrospective explanatory flexibility.
- **Behaviorism (Watson, 1913; Skinner, 1938):** Watson's manifesto — psychology should study only observable behavior; *Little Albert* experiment (1920 — conditioned fear; methodologically deeply flawed; Harris, 1979); Skinner's radical behaviorism — behavior is fully explained by reinforcement contingencies; dominated American psychology for ~50 years; produced powerful applied methods (ABA, token economies) but could not account for language, cognition, or complex human behavior.
- **Humanistic psychology (Maslow, Rogers, 1950s–1970s):** "Third force" — rejected both psychoanalytic determinism and behaviorist reductionism; emphasized free will, self-actualization (Maslow, 1954), unconditional positive regard and client-centered therapy (Rogers, 1951); influenced counseling and positive psychology; criticized for lack of empirical rigor and vague constructs.

### 1.4 The cognitive revolution (1956–present)

- **Key events of 1956:** Chomsky's review of Skinner's *Verbal Behavior* (published 1959, presented 1956); Miller's "The Magical Number Seven" paper; Bruner, Goodnow, & Austin's *A Study of Thinking*; the Dartmouth Conference (AI founding); the MIT Symposium on Information Theory — collectively signaled that the study of internal mental processes was scientifically legitimate.
- **Information processing model:** Mind as an information processor — input, encoding, storage, retrieval, output; computational metaphor; Atkinson & Shiffrin (1968) multi-store memory model; Broadbent (1958) filter model of attention.
- **Enduring contributions:** Cognitive psychology produced the most empirically validated research programs in psychology — memory research (Ebbinghaus → modern episodic/semantic distinctions), attention, language processing, problem solving, judgment and decision making (Kahneman & Tversky), cognitive development (Piaget → modern theories).

### 1.5 The replication crisis and reform (2011–present)

- **Open Science Collaboration (2015):** Attempted to replicate 100 published psychology findings — only 36–47% replicated successfully (depending on criteria); average effect size dropped by half; triggered widespread concern about the reliability of psychological science.
- **Contributing factors:** Publication bias (positive results published preferentially), p-hacking (flexible data analysis to achieve p < .05), HARKing (hypothesizing after results are known), underpowered studies, lack of pre-registration.
- **Reforms adopted:** Pre-registration of hypotheses and analysis plans; Registered Reports (peer review before data collection); open data and open materials; larger sample sizes; many-lab replication projects (ManyLabs, Psychological Science Accelerator); shift from novel findings to cumulative, replicable science.
- **Casualties:** Ego depletion (Hagger et al., 2016 — null RRR), behavioral priming (Bargh elderly priming), facial feedback (Wagenmakers et al., 2016), power posing hormonal effects, stereotype threat original effect sizes, and many more.

## 2. CREDIBLE BUT DEBATED CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated)

### 2.1 Women and minorities in psychology's history

- **Marginalized contributions:** Mary Whiton Calkins (1863–1930) — invented the paired-associate technique, first woman APA president (1905), denied PhD by Harvard despite completing all requirements; Margaret Floy Washburn (1871–1939) — first woman to earn a psychology PhD (1894); Mamie Phipps Clark (1917–1983) — doll studies on racial identity cited in Brown v. Board of Education (1954); Kenneth Clark — first Black APA president (1970).
- **Francis Cecil Sumner (1895–1954):** First Black American to earn a PhD in psychology (Clark University, 1920); "father of Black psychology."
- **Ongoing underrepresentation:** Despite majority-female student enrollment since 1980s, women and minorities remained underrepresented in leadership, named chairs, and citation networks until recently; WEIRD sampling (Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic — Henrich et al., 2010) limits generalizability.

### 2.2 Ethical controversies that shaped the discipline

- **Little Albert (Watson & Rayner, 1920):** Conditioned fear in a 9-month-old infant — no deconditioned (unethical); infant's post-study fate debated (Beck et al., 2009 — possibly Douglas Merritte, who had neurological impairments; Powell et al., 2014 — possibly William Barger).
- **Milgram obedience studies (1963):** 65% of participants administered maximum "shocks" to a confederate despite apparent distress — revealed obedience to authority; ethical concerns about deception and psychological harm led to formal ethical guidelines.
- **Stanford Prison Experiment (Zimbardo, 1971):** Guard-prisoner simulation — guards became abusive, experiment terminated after 6 days; heavily criticized for methodological problems (demand characteristics, experimenter influence, only 1 run); Le Texier (2019) archival analysis revealed that guards were coached and the narrative was shaped for dramatic effect; scientific value is now considered minimal.
- **Tuskegee syphilis study (1932–1972):** PHS withheld treatment from Black men with syphilis for 40 years — not psychology-specific but catalyzed the Belmont Report (1979) and modern IRB regulations.
- **APA ethics code evolution:** 1952 (first formal code) → Nuremberg Code → Belmont Report → current APA Ethics Code (2017) — informed consent, debriefing, protection from harm, right to withdraw; IRB review of all human subjects research.

### 2.3 Positive psychology

- **Seligman & Csikszentmihalyi (2000):** Launched positive psychology as a formal movement — studying strengths, well-being, flourishing, and optimal functioning rather than only pathology; produced research on gratitude, flow, positive emotions, character strengths (VIA), and subjective well-being.
- **Critique:** Accused of repackaging humanistic psychology with better methods; ignoring structural inequality by focusing on individual positivity; the PERMA model (Positive emotion, Engagement, Relationships, Meaning, Accomplishment) has been criticized as vague and difficult to falsify; some positive psychology interventions show small effects (gratitude journals d ≈ 0.20).

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Post-WEIRD psychology

The push for globally representative psychology (Henrich et al., 2010 — "the WEIRDest people in the world") may fundamentally revise core findings that turn out to be culture-specific; early evidence: Müller-Lyer illusion shows cultural variation; some moral foundations differ across cultures; individualism-collectivism effects on conformity, self-concept, and attribution vary; the extent to which "universal" findings will survive cross-cultural scrutiny remains to be seen.

### 3.2 AI as the next paradigm shift

Large language models and computational cognitive science may constitute the next paradigm shift after the cognitive revolution — artificial intelligence as both model and tool for understanding cognition; the field is in early stages of integrating LLMs, deep neural networks, and computational psychiatry into mainstream psychological theory.

## 4. DUBIOUS OR FRINGE CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Freud's psychosexual stages are empirically valid

Specific psychosexual stages (oral, anal, phallic fixations producing adult personality types) have no empirical support; while Freud's observation that early experience shapes development and that unconscious processes exist is broadly supported, the specific mechanisms he proposed (Oedipus complex, penis envy, castration anxiety) are not.

### 4.2 The Stanford Prison Experiment demonstrates valid science

Le Texier's (2019) archival analysis and Reicher & Haslam's (2006) critique showed that Zimbardo coached guards, manipulated conditions, and over-interpreted results; the experiment was not a demonstration of situational power but a demonstration of demand characteristics and experimenter influence; textbooks are being revised accordingly.

### 4.3 Psychology is just "common sense"

Hindsight bias makes psychological findings *seem* obvious after the fact — but many replicated findings are counterintuitive (bystander effect, cognitive dissonance, false memories, obedience to authority levels); when people are asked to predict results before knowing them, they perform at chance levels (Stanovich, 2012).
