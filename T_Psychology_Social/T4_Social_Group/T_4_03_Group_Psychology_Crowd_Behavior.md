## QUICK SUMMARY

**Group psychology** examines how individuals' thoughts, feelings, and behaviors are influenced by the presence and actions of others — from small groups to mass crowds. Foundational research includes **Gustave Le Bon's** *The Crowd* (1895), which argued that individuals in crowds undergo a psychological transformation, losing rationality and becoming susceptible to **contagion** (rapid spread of emotions and behaviors) and **suggestibility**. Modern research has refined Le Bon's framework through concepts including: **conformity** (Solomon Asch, 1951 — line-length experiments showing ~37% of participants yielded to obviously incorrect group consensus); **obedience to authority** (Stanley Milgram, 1963 — ~65% of participants administered apparent maximum electric shocks when directed by an authority figure); **groupthink** (Irving Janis, 1972 — deterioration of decision quality in cohesive groups due to conformity pressure, self-censorship, and illusion of unanimity); **social facilitation** (Robert Zajonc, 1965 — presence of others enhances dominant responses: improving simple tasks but impairing complex ones); **deindividuation** (Philip Zimbardo, 1969 — anonymity within groups reduces self-awareness and accountability, facilitating behavior that would be inhibited in identified individuals); and the **bystander effect** (Darley & Latané, 1968 — diffusion of responsibility in groups reduces individual helping behavior). The **Stanford Prison Experiment** (Zimbardo, 1971) and **Milgram's obedience studies** became the most famous demonstrations of situational power over behavior, though both have been subject to significant methodological criticism and reinterpretation. Contemporary research emphasizes that crowd behavior is more complex than simple "irrationality" — the **elaborated social identity model (ESIM)** (Reicher, 1996) argues that crowds act based on **shared social identity** rather than losing individual identity, and that crowd behavior is typically **normatively regulated** rather than anarchic.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Scholarly Consensus)

### 1.1 Conformity Research
- **Asch conformity experiments** (1951, 1956): participants judged line lengths in groups with confederates giving unanimously wrong answers — approximately **37% of trials** produced conforming (incorrect) responses; ~75% of participants conformed at least once across multiple trials; when one confederate gave the correct answer, conformity dropped to ~5%
- Conformity is modulated by: group size (increases up to ~4–5 members then plateaus), unanimity (breaking unanimity dramatically reduces conformity), task ambiguity, culture (meta-analyses show higher conformity in collectivist cultures), and public vs. private response

### 1.2 Obedience to Authority
- **Milgram obedience experiments** (1961–1963): using a rigged "shock generator" (15–450 volts), ~65% of participants in the baseline condition administered the maximum 450-volt shock to a protesting "learner" (actually a confederate) when directed by an experimenter in a lab coat
- Key moderating variables: **proximity** (obedience decreased when the learner was in the same room), **legitimacy of authority** (obedience dropped in a non-laboratory setting), **social support for disobedience** (two defiant confederates reduced compliance to ~10%)
- Milgram's studies have been partially replicated in modified forms (Burger, 2009, with ethical constraints stopping at 150V) — compliance rates were comparable to the original

### 1.3 Groupthink
- **Irving Janis** (1972, 1982) analyzed historical foreign policy fiascoes (Bay of Pigs, Pearl Harbor, Vietnam escalation) and identified **eight symptoms of groupthink**: illusion of invulnerability, collective rationalization, belief in inherent morality, stereotyping out-groups, pressure on dissenters, self-censorship, illusion of unanimity, self-appointed "mindguards"
- Groupthink is promoted by: **group cohesion**, **insulation** from outside experts, **directive leadership**, **homogeneity**, and **high stress** with no perceived alternative
- The concept has been influential but **debated** — researchers argue that cohesion alone is insufficient to produce poor decisions, and that structural conditions (lack of impartial leadership, absence of norms encouraging critical evaluation) are more important

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Stanford Prison Experiment
- The **Stanford Prison Experiment (SPE)** (August 1971, 6 days): 24 male college students randomly assigned to "guard" or "prisoner" roles in a simulated prison in Stanford's psychology department basement — guards became increasingly abusive, some prisoners suffered emotional breakdowns, and the experiment was terminated after 6 days (of a planned 14)
- The SPE has been **heavily criticized**: Le Texier (2019) found evidence that guards were coached to be tough; Zimbardo's dual role as principal investigator and "prison superintendent" compromised objectivity; the sample was small and non-representative; and demand characteristics likely influenced behavior — some psychologists consider it more a demonstration than a controlled experiment
- The **BBC Prison Study** (Reicher & Haslam, 2006) failed to replicate key SPE findings — guards did not automatically become abusive, and group identification modulated behavior

### 2.2 Bystander Effect and Kitty Genovese
- The **bystander effect** (Darley & Latané, 1968): across many experiments, the presence of others decreases individual probability of helping — explained by diffusion of responsibility, audience inhibition, and social influence (pluralistic ignorance)
- The founding case — the 1964 murder of **Kitty Genovese** in New York City, reportedly witnessed by 38 passive bystanders — has been **substantially revised**: Manning et al. (2007) and subsequent reporting revealed that many fewer people witnessed the attacks, some did call police, and the "38 witnesses who did nothing" narrative was largely a media construction by the *New York Times*

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Mass Psychogenic Illness
- **Mass psychogenic illness** (formerly "mass hysteria") — the spread of physical symptoms (fainting, nausea, tremors) through groups without organic cause — is well-documented but poorly understood mechanistically; cases include medieval dancing plagues, laughing epidemics (Tanganyika, 1962), and periodic school outbreaks

---

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Le Bon's "Group Mind"
- **[DEBUNKED]** Le Bon's assertion that individuals in crowds regress to a primitive "racial unconscious" and become incapable of rational thought is contradicted by modern research showing that crowd behavior is typically **normatively structured** by shared identity rather than irrational (Reicher, 2001)

### Counter-Arguments
- Situationist explanations (Milgram, Zimbardo) must be balanced against **dispositional** and **cultural** factors — individuals differ in conformity, obedience, and aggression, and situations do not affect all people equally
- The replication crisis has affected group psychology — some classic findings (especially the SPE and the original Genovese narrative) have been revised significantly, requiring more cautious interpretation of older studies
