## QUICK SUMMARY

**Cross-cultural psychology** investigates how culture shapes human thought, emotion, and behavior — and which psychological processes are universal versus culturally specific. The field distinguishes between **etic** approaches (seeking universal laws across cultures) and **emic** approaches (understanding behavior within a specific cultural context — Pike, 1967). The most influential dimensional framework is **Hofstede's cultural dimensions theory** (1980, updated 2010): originally four dimensions derived from IBM employee surveys across 72 countries — **individualism vs. collectivism**, **power distance** (acceptance of hierarchical authority), **uncertainty avoidance**, and **masculinity vs. femininity** — later expanded with **long-term orientation** and **indulgence vs. restraint**. **Triandis (1995)** refined the individualism-collectivism dimension, distinguishing horizontal (egalitarian) from vertical (hierarchical) forms. A transformative critique came from **Henrich, Heine, and Norenzayan (2010)** — their landmark *Behavioral and Brain Sciences* paper demonstrated that the overwhelming majority of psychological research subjects are **WEIRD** (Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic) — comprising ~12% of the world's population but 96% of psychological study samples — and that WEIRD populations are often outliers on measures of visual perception, fairness, cooperation, spatial reasoning, and moral reasoning. **Culture and emotion**: Ekman's basic emotion theory (six universal facial expressions — happiness, sadness, anger, fear, disgust, surprise) has been supported by many cross-cultural studies but challenged by findings that recognition accuracy varies significantly and that some non-Western cultures categorize emotions differently (Russell, 1994; Barrett, 2017). **Acculturation** research (Berry, 1997) identifies four strategies when individuals move between cultures: **integration** (maintaining heritage culture while adopting host culture — associated with best psychological outcomes), **assimilation**, **separation**, and **marginalization**. Cross-cultural studies of **Müller-Lyer illusion susceptibility** (Segall et al., 1966) showed Western populations are more susceptible than non-Western groups — attributed to the "carpentered world hypothesis" (exposure to rectilinear architecture). The field has prompted psychology to reexamine claims of universality in areas including attachment styles, cognitive dissonance, self-concept, and mental health diagnosis.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Scholarly Consensus)

### 1.1 WEIRD Sampling Bias
- **Henrich et al. (2010)** documented that 96% of behavioral science samples came from ~12% of the world's population — demonstrating that many "universal" findings in psychology may reflect WEIRD-specific patterns, not human universals
- Subsequent analyses confirmed that WEIRD populations differ markedly in areas including fairness norms (Ultimatum Game offers), visual perception, analytic vs. holistic reasoning, and self-construal

### 1.2 Individualism-Collectivism
- **Hofstede (1980, 2010)** — individualism-collectivism is among the most replicated cultural dimensions, with substantial variation between nations (U.S., UK, Australia highest in individualism; China, South Korea, Colombia high in collectivism)
- **Markus & Kitayama (1991)** — distinguished **independent self-construal** (common in Western cultures — self defined by internal attributes) from **interdependent self-construal** (common in East Asian cultures — self defined by relationships and social roles) — extensively replicated but with nuances (within-culture variation is substantial)

### 1.3 Müller-Lyer Illusion
- **Segall et al. (1966)** — cross-cultural comparisons of illusion susceptibility across 19 societies found that groups living in "carpentered" environments (rectangular buildings, roads) were more susceptible to the Müller-Lyer illusion, demonstrating that perceptual processes are shaped by environmental experience

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Universal vs. Culture-Specific Emotions
- Ekman's **basic emotion theory** (1992) — six universal facial expressions — is supported by above-chance recognition across cultures but recognition accuracy varies (higher in literate industrialized populations), and isolated communities (e.g., Himba, Trobriand Islanders) show different categorization patterns (Gendron et al., 2014)
- **Barrett's constructionist theory** (2017) challenges the universality of discrete emotion categories, arguing emotions are constructed from more basic affective dimensions (valence, arousal) shaped by cultural concepts

### 2.2 Acculturation Strategies
- **Berry (1997)** — the four-strategy acculturation model (integration, assimilation, separation, marginalization) is well-supported, with integration consistently associated with the best psychological adjustment — but critics note the model may oversimplify complex, context-dependent processes

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Cultural Neuroscience
- Emerging research on whether cultural experience shapes neural architecture beyond perception (e.g., cultural differences in brain activation during self-referential processing, empathy, or moral reasoning) is intriguing but methodologically challenging — small samples, confounds with language, education, and genetic ancestry

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Cultural Determinism
- **[DEBUNKED]** The extreme view that culture entirely determines thought and behavior — leaving no room for biological or universal psychological processes — is contradicted by converging evidence of cross-cultural universals in areas including face perception, basic emotion recognition, attachment formation, language acquisition, and theory of mind development

### Counter-Arguments
- Hofstede's dimensions have been critiqued for **ecological fallacy** (nation-level averages may not apply to individuals within a nation), **sampling limitations** (original data from a single multinational corporation), and for oversimplifying complex cultural variation into a few continuous dimensions
- The individualism-collectivism framework may impose a Western analytical lens on cultures that don't neatly fit the dichotomy
