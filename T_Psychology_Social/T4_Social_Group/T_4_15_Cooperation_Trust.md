## QUICK SUMMARY

**Cooperation** — acting in ways that benefit others at a cost to oneself — is both theoretically puzzling (why would natural selection favor organisms that sacrifice fitness for others?) and practically essential (every human institution from families to governments depends on cooperative behavior). **Game theory**, formalized by von Neumann and Morgenstern (1944) and applied to evolution by Maynard Smith (1982), provides the mathematical framework: the **Prisoner's Dilemma** captures the core tension — mutual cooperation produces the best collective outcome, but individual incentives favor defection (free-riding). **Robert Axelrod's** computer tournaments (1984) demonstrated that **tit-for-tat** — cooperate first, then mirror the other's previous move — is a robust strategy in iterated (repeated) Prisoner's Dilemmas: it is nice (cooperates first), retaliatory (punishes defection), forgiving (returns to cooperation after the other does), and clear (easy for other strategies to interpret). **Reciprocity** comes in two forms: **direct reciprocity** ("I help you, you help me" — requires repeated interactions) and **indirect reciprocity** ("I help you, someone helps me" — requires reputation systems; Nowak & Sigmund, 1998). **Elinor Ostrom** (Nobel Prize, 2009) demonstrated that communities can sustainably manage common-pool resources without either privatization or government regulation — through self-organized institutions with clear boundaries, graduated sanctions, and local conflict resolution. **Trust** — the willingness to accept vulnerability based on positive expectations of another's intentions — is measured experimentally through the **trust game** (Berg et al., 1995): investors send money to trustees (tripled by experimenter), and trustees decide how much to return. Trust varies across cultures, correlates with economic development (Knack & Keefer, 1997), and is both a cause and consequence of well-functioning institutions. **Strong reciprocity** (Fehr & Gächter, 2002): humans cooperate even in one-shot anonymous interactions and willingly pay costs to punish free-riders — suggesting that cooperation is sustained not merely by self-interest but by evolved preferences for fairness and punishment of norm violators.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 Game Theory Foundations
- **Prisoner's Dilemma** (Tucker, 1950; formally attributed): two players each choose to cooperate or defect; mutual cooperation > mutual defection for collective payoff, but individual incentive always favors defection — generating the tension between individual and collective rationality
- **Nash Equilibrium** (Nash, 1950): in a one-shot Prisoner's Dilemma, the Nash equilibrium is mutual defection — both players defect because defection is the dominant strategy regardless of what the other does
- **Iterated Prisoner's Dilemma**: when the game is repeated indefinitely (or with uncertain endpoint), cooperation can be sustained as an equilibrium — the "shadow of the future" makes reciprocity viable

### 1.2 Axelrod's Tournaments (1984)
- Robert Axelrod's computer tournaments invited game theorists to submit strategies for the iterated Prisoner's Dilemma — **tit-for-tat** (submitted by Anatol Rapoport) won both tournaments
- Key features of successful strategies: **nice** (never defect first), **retaliatory** (punish defection immediately), **forgiving** (restore cooperation after defection stops), **clear** (behavior is predictable and interpretable)
- Real-world applications: Axelrod noted that tit-for-tat-like dynamics emerged spontaneously in WWI trench warfare ("live and let live" system) — soldiers on opposing sides developed tacit cooperation until command disrupted it

### 1.3 Trust Game and Trust Measurement
- **Berg, Dickhaut, & McCabe (1995)**: the trust game (investment game) — Player 1 (investor) sends any portion of an endowment to Player 2 (trustee); the amount is tripled; Player 2 returns any portion. Standard finding: investors send ~50% on average; trustees return ~33% — indicating both trust and trustworthiness exceed game-theoretic predictions (which predict zero transfer in one-shot games)

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Strong Reciprocity and Altruistic Punishment
- **Fehr & Gächter (2002)**: in public goods games, people cooperate even in one-shot anonymous interactions — and willingly pay personal costs to punish free-riders (altruistic punishment), even when they will never interact with the punished individual again
- **Interpretation**: humans have evolved preferences for fairness and willingness to enforce norms — cooperation is maintained not just through self-interested reciprocity but through a psychology of punishment, norm enforcement, and moral outrage
- **Cross-cultural variation**: Henrich et al. (2001, 2006) — ultimatum game and public goods experiments across 15 small-scale societies showed significant cross-cultural variation in cooperation and punishment norms, correlated with market integration and community size

### 2.2 Ostrom's Common-Pool Resource Governance
- **Elinor Ostrom** (*Governing the Commons*, 1990): challenged the "tragedy of the commons" narrative (Hardin, 1968) — demonstrated through extensive fieldwork that communities worldwide (Swiss alpine meadows, Philippine irrigation systems, Maine lobster fisheries) successfully manage common-pool resources through self-organized institutional arrangements
- **Eight design principles** for successful commons governance: (1) clearly defined boundaries, (2) proportional equivalence between benefits and costs, (3) collective-choice arrangements, (4) monitoring, (5) graduated sanctions, (6) conflict-resolution mechanisms, (7) minimal recognition of rights to organize, (8) nested enterprises for larger-scale systems

### 2.3 Social Capital and Trust
- **Robert Putnam** (*Bowling Alone*, 2000): documented the decline of social capital (civic participation, social trust, associational membership) in the United States since the 1960s — argued that declining social trust weakens democracy, community, and public health
- **Knack & Keefer (1997)**: cross-national analysis — countries with higher generalized trust have stronger economic growth, better government institutions, and lower corruption — though the direction of causation (trust → institutions vs. institutions → trust) is debated

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Evolutionary Origins of Large-Scale Cooperation
- **Cultural group selection** (Richerson & Boyd, 2005; Henrich, 2016): human large-scale cooperation (far exceeding that of any other primate) may have evolved through competition between culturally defined groups — groups with more cooperative norms outcompeted less cooperative groups, selecting for psychological dispositions (conformity, norm internalization, parochial altruism) that support group-level cooperation. While influential, this framework remains debated — some theorists (West et al.) argue that individual-level selection mechanisms (reciprocity, reputation, partner choice) can explain large-scale cooperation without invoking group selection

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Humans Are Fundamentally Selfish
- **[CONTRADICTED BY EVIDENCE]** The folk economic and folk evolutionary claim that humans are fundamentally selfish — and that cooperation is always reducible to disguised self-interest — is not supported by experimental evidence. Trust games, dictator games, public goods experiments, and altruistic punishment findings consistently show that people cooperate, share, and punish at personal cost in one-shot anonymous interactions where self-interest predicts zero cooperation. Cooperation is not merely strategic; it reflects evolved prosocial preferences
