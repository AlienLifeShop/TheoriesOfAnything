## QUICK SUMMARY

**The psychology of power and authority** examines how social hierarchy, dominance, obedience, and institutional authority shape human behavior. Two landmark experiments defined the field: **Stanley Milgram's obedience studies** (1963, Yale University) — in which 65% of ordinary participants administered what they believed were lethal 450-volt shocks to a screaming "learner" (actually a confederate) simply because an authority figure in a lab coat instructed them to continue — and **Philip Zimbardo's Stanford Prison Experiment (SPE)** (1971) — in which college students assigned as "guards" in a simulated prison became abusive, while "prisoners" became passive and distressed, leading to early termination after 6 days. However, both studies have faced major **methodological and ethical reanalyses**: Milgram's procedures varied across 23 conditions with obedience rates from 0% to 100% depending on proximity, legitimacy, and social support — recent archival analysis (Perry, 2012) revealed many participants did not believe the shocks were real; the SPE has been severely critiqued (Le Texier, 2019) for **experimenter coaching of guard behavior**, selective reporting, and demand characteristics, calling into question whether it demonstrates situational determinism or experimenter manipulation. **French and Raven (1959)** identified five bases of social power: **reward, coercive, legitimate, referent, and expert** — later expanded with informational power. **Dacher Keltner's power paradox** (2016) — individuals gain power through socially valued behaviors (empathy, generosity, fairness) but once empowered tend to exhibit **reduced empathy, greater impulsivity, and more self-serving behavior** — supported by experimental studies showing high-power priming reduces perspective-taking (Galinsky et al., 2006) and moral reasoning. **Right-Wing Authoritarianism (RWA)** (Altemeyer, 1981, 1996) measures submission to authority, aggression toward out-groups, and conventionalism — RWA scores predict prejudice, punitiveness, and support for authoritarian political policies. **Weber's typology** (1922) distinguished three sources of **legitimacy**: traditional, charismatic, and rational-legal authority — still foundational in political psychology. Power's corrupting effects are not inevitable: checks and balances, accountability, and institutional transparency moderate the relationship between power and abuse.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Scholarly Consensus)

### 1.1 Milgram Obedience Studies
- **Milgram (1963, 1974)** — across 23 experimental variations, obedience to destructive authority was powerfully influenced by situational variables: **proximity to victim** (obedience dropped when participants could see/touch the learner), **proximity of authority** (obedience dropped when experimenter gave orders by phone), and **social support** (obedience dropped when confederate "participants" refused)
- Partial replications (Burger, 2009 — stopping at 150V for ethical reasons) confirmed high obedience rates (~70%) even in modern samples

### 1.2 Power and Reduced Empathy
- **Galinsky et al. (2006)** — power priming (recalling a time of having power) reduced participants' tendency to spontaneously adopt others' visual perspectives and reduced accuracy in identifying others' emotions
- **Hogeveen et al. (2014)** — experimentally induced power reduced motor resonance (a neural marker of empathy), providing neurophysiological evidence for the empathy-reducing effect of power

### 1.3 Right-Wing Authoritarianism
- **Altemeyer (1996)** — RWA is among the strongest individual-difference predictors of prejudice, punitiveness, and support for governmental restrictions on civil liberties — replicated across multiple nations and validated against behavioral measures

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Stanford Prison Experiment Reappraisal
- **Zimbardo (1971/2007)** argued the SPE demonstrated the "Lucifer Effect" — how situations turn good people into perpetrators of evil
- **Le Texier (2019)** — archival analysis revealed that Zimbardo's research team actively coached guards to be more aggressive, that some guards resisted the role, and that the study was presented more as a demonstration than a controlled experiment — the SPE is now considered a **cautionary tale about experimenter effects** rather than evidence for pure situational determinism
- The broader point — that situations powerfully influence behavior — remains well-supported by other research (Milgram, Abu Ghraib analyses) even if the SPE itself is compromised

### 2.2 Power Paradox
- **Keltner (2016)** — the observation that empathic, prosocial behavior helps people gain power, but power then erodes the very social sensitivities that enabled its acquisition — supported by converging experimental and field evidence but with acknowledged boundary conditions (individual differences, accountability, cultural variation)

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Pure Situational Determinism
- **[DEBUNKED]** The strong interpretation of the SPE and Milgram studies — that situations entirely override individual character — is an overstatement: significant within-condition variation exists in all obedience and conformity experiments; individual differences in personality (authoritarianism, empathy, conscientiousness) moderate responses to authority; and many participants in both paradigms did resist
- Power does not inevitably corrupt — the relationship is moderated by individual values, accountability structures, institutional design, and cultural context

### Counter-Arguments
- Milgram's studies have been critiqued on **ethical grounds** (deception, distress) and for potentially poor **ecological validity** — the specific conditions of a laboratory authority relationship may not generalize to real-world obedience to authority in political or military contexts
- The "power corrupts" narrative may reflect publication bias — studies showing that power increases prosocial behavior (in individuals with communal values) receive less attention (Chen et al., 2001)
