## QUICK SUMMARY

Forensic psychology applies psychological science to legal and criminal justice systems — encompassing criminal behavior, courtroom processes, investigative methods, risk assessment, and rehabilitation.
**Eyewitness testimony,** long assumed reliable, is now recognized as highly fallible — the **Innocence Project** has documented >375 wrongful convictions overturned by DNA evidence, with ~69% involving eyewitness misidentification (Innocence Project, 2024). The **misinformation effect** (Loftus, 1979), **weapon focus,** cross-race effect, and suggestive identification procedures contribute to high error rates.
**Psychopathy,** assessed via the **PCL-R** (Hare, 1991; 2-factor model — interpersonal/affective callousness + antisocial lifestyle), is the strongest single clinical predictor of violent recidivism; ~1% general population prevalence but ~15–25% of prison populations. **False confessions** occur at alarming rates — an estimated 25–30% of Innocence Project DNA exonerations involved false confessions, typically resulting from psychologically coercive interrogation techniques. **Structured professional judgment** tools (e.g., HCR-20) outperform unstructured clinical judgment for violence risk assessment.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Eyewitness testimony reliability

- **Loftus misinformation effect (1979):** Post-event information systematically distorts eyewitness memory — participants who received misleading information about a car accident ("How fast were the cars going when they *smashed* into each other?") recalled higher speeds and even fabricated broken glass.
- **Eyewitness misidentification:** The single greatest cause of wrongful conviction in the US; cross-race identification is particularly error-prone (own-race bias: d ≈ 1.40 for hits, d ≈ 0.56 for false alarms; Meissner & Brigham, 2001 meta-analysis).
- **National Academy of Sciences (2014):** Recommended reforms: double-blind administration of lineups, standardized instructions ("the suspect may or may not be present"), sequential presentation (one at a time vs. simultaneous), videotaping of identification procedures, expert testimony on eyewitness fallibility.
- **Confidence-accuracy relationship:** Originally considered weak (r ≈ .25); more recent research under pristine testing conditions shows stronger confidence-accuracy calibration (Wixted & Wells, 2017); high-confidence initial identifications are highly accurate (~95%), but low/moderate confidence identifications are unreliable.

### 1.2 Psychopathy and the PCL-R

- **Hare's Psychopathy Checklist-Revised (PCL-R, 1991, 2003):** 20-item clinician-rated instrument; **Factor 1:** interpersonal (glibness, grandiosity, lying, manipulation) + affective (callousness, lack of remorse, shallow affect); **Factor 2:** antisocial lifestyle (impulsivity, irresponsibility, parasitic orientation) + criminality.
- **Prevalence:** ~1% general population, ~15–25% North American male prison populations.
- **Predictive validity:** PCL-R scores predict violent recidivism (AUC ≈ 0.70), general recidivism, institutional misconduct, and treatment failure; the strongest single predictor among personality measures.
- **Neuroscience:** Meta-analyses link psychopathy to reduced amygdala volume and reactivity, prefrontal dysfunction, and impaired fear conditioning; reduced cortisol stress response; attenuated startle potentiation to aversive images (Patrick, 1994).

### 1.3 False confessions

- **Kassin & Wrightsman (1985) taxonomy:** (1) **Voluntary** false confessions (attention-seeking, protecting others, mental illness); (2) **Compliant** false confessions (yielding to interrogation pressure to escape aversive situation); (3) **Internalized** false confessions (suspect actually comes to believe they committed the crime).
- **Central Park Five (1989):** Five teenagers falsely confessed to rape after 14–30 hours of interrogation; convicted; served 6–13 years; DNA exonerated all five (actual perpetrator identified by DNA and confession, 2002).
- **Risk factors:** Youth, intellectual disability, mental illness, sleep deprivation, interrogation duration, Reid Technique maximization tactics (implied evidence, theme development, minimization suggesting leniency).
- **PEACE model (UK):** An information-gathering alternative to confrontational interrogation — Planning, Engage, Account, Closure, Evaluation — produces more reliable information and fewer false confessions than the Reid Technique.

### 1.4 Violence risk assessment

- **Unstructured clinical judgment:** Poor predictive validity (AUC ≈ 0.55–0.60); only slightly better than chance; Monahan (1981): clinicians are wrong about dangerousness about two out of every three times.
- **Actuarial instruments (VRAG-R, Static-99R):** Fixed-item scoring using empirically derived risk factors; AUC ≈ 0.70–0.75 for violent/sexual recidivism; superior to unstructured judgment.
- **Structured professional judgment (HCR-20v3):** Combines actuarial risk factors with clinical judgment within a structured framework — 20 items across Historical (10), Clinical (5), and Risk management (5) domains; AUC ≈ 0.70–0.76; widely used internationally.
- **Key risk factors (validated):** Prior violence (#1 predictor), age at first offense, antisocial personality, substance abuse, psychopathy, active symptoms of major mental illness, treatment noncompliance, lack of social support.

## 2. CREDIBLE BUT DEBATED CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated)

### 2.1 Criminal profiling

- **FBI behavioral analysis (organized/disorganized typology; Douglas & Ressler, 1980s):** Inferring offender characteristics from crime scene evidence — widely popularized in fiction but weak evidence base; Snook et al. (2007 meta-analysis) found profilers did not significantly outperform non-profilers in accuracy.
- **Geographic profiling (Rossmo, 2000):** Mathematical analysis of serial crime locations to estimate offender's residence — better empirical support than behavioral profiling; based on environmental criminology principles (distance decay function).
- **Statistical approaches:** Modern efforts use actuarial methods and machine learning on large databases rather than idiosyncratic clinical inference — improved reliability but limited by base rate problems and overfitting.

### 2.2 Jury decision-making

- **Story model (Pennington & Hastie, 1992):** Jurors construct narrative stories from evidence and select the verdict that best fits their constructed narrative; story coherence and completeness drive decisions more than raw evidence evaluation.
- **Pretrial publicity:** Substantial meta-analytic evidence of biasing effects (d ≈ 0.25–0.45; Steblay et al., 1999); judicial instructions to disregard inadmissible evidence are often ineffective (the "don't think of a white bear" problem).
- **Juror characteristics:** Authoritarianism associated with conviction-proneness; death qualification (excluding anti–death penalty jurors) produces juries more conviction-prone even on guilt determination.

### 2.3 Insanity defense

- **M'Naghten rule (1843):** Defendant did not know the nature/quality of the act or did not know it was wrong — the dominant legal standard in most US states.
- **Frequency:** Raised in <1% of felony cases; successful in ~25% of those raised — about 0.25% of all felony cases result in a finding of not guilty by reason of insanity (NGRI).
- **Public misperception:** The public dramatically overestimates the frequency and success rate of the insanity defense and incorrectly believes NGRI acquittees are quickly released — in reality, NGRI patients are typically hospitalized longer than they would have been imprisoned.

### 2.4 Psychopathy treatment responsiveness

- **Traditional view:** Psychopathy is untreatable; treatment may even make psychopathic individuals more skilled manipulators (Rice, Harris, & Cormier, 1992 — Oak Ridge therapeutic community study).
- **Updated evidence:** More recent available evidence suggests psychopathic traits can be reduced modestly with intensive cognitive-behavioral programs, especially in younger offenders (Caldwell et al., 2006); complete "cure" unlikely but risk reduction is achievable.

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Neuroimaging-based lie detection

fMRI-based deception detection (showing differential prefrontal and anterior cingulate activation during lying vs. truth-telling) — laboratory published findings demonstrate above-chance accuracy (~75–90%), but ecological validity is poor; no court acceptance; countermeasures are easy; National Research Council (2003) found polygraphy similarly lacking in validity.

### 3.2 Predictive policing via psychological models

Using psychological risk factor research to predict individual offending before crimes occur — raises profound ethical concerns about pre-crime intervention, racial bias embedded in training data, false positive rates, and civil liberties.

## 4. DUBIOUS OR FRINGE CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Physiognomy and criminal appearance

The discredited 19th-century claim (Lombroso, 1876) that criminals can be identified by physical features (sloping forehead, prominent jaw, asymmetric skull) — no scientific support; reflects racist and classist pseudoscience. Modern AI "criminal face" detection algorithms reproduce these biases.

### 4.2 Polygraph reliability

Despite widespread use by law enforcement and government agencies, the polygraph (measuring autonomic arousal) has unacceptable error rates — National Academy of Sciences (2003): accuracy in specific-incident testing is "well above chance, though well below perfection" with significant false positive rates; innocent people who are anxious fail; guilty people who are calm pass; countermeasures are effective.
