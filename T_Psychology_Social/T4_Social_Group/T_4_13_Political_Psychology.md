## QUICK SUMMARY

**Political psychology** — the scientific study of the psychological bases of political behavior, beliefs, and ideologies — investigates why people hold the political views they do, how they process political information, and what psychological mechanisms drive partisan conflict, authoritarianism, and democratic participation. A central discovery is that political ideology is not merely a product of rational deliberation about policy — it is deeply rooted in **personality**, **cognitive style**, **emotional processing**, and even **biology**. **Jonathan Haidt's Moral Foundations Theory (MFT**, 2004–present) proposes that political differences reflect differential weighting of five (later six) innate moral foundations: (1) **Care/Harm** — sensitivity to suffering; (2) **Fairness/Cheating** — concern with reciprocity and justice; (3) **Loyalty/Betrayal** — group cohesion and patriotism; (4) **Authority/Subversion** — respect for hierarchy and tradition; (5) **Sanctity/Degradation** — purity, disgust, and the sacred; (6) **Liberty/Oppression** — resistance to domination. Liberals/progressives rely primarily on Care and Fairness; conservatives draw more evenly on all six foundations — explaining why each side finds the other morally incomprehensible. **Jost et al.'s motivated social cognition model** (2003) identified two core psychological factors predicting conservatism: **resistance to change** (preference for tradition, stability, certainty) and **acceptance of inequality** (tolerance for hierarchy) — both linked to higher needs for cognitive closure, lower tolerance of ambiguity, and greater sensitivity to threat. **Right-Wing Authoritarianism (RWA)** (Altemeyer, 1981) and **Social Dominance Orientation (SDO)** (Sidanius & Pratto, 1999) are two personality-level predictors that together account for substantial variance in political attitudes, prejudice, and intergroup behavior. **Motivated reasoning** (Kunda, 1990) — the tendency to process information in ways that confirm pre-existing beliefs — affects both sides of the political spectrum and drives **political polarization**, made worse by social media echo chambers and partisan media ecosystems.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 Moral Foundations Theory
- **Haidt et al. (2004, 2012)**: moral judgment is driven by rapid, automatic emotional responses ("moral intuitions") followed by post-hoc reasoning ("the rider and the elephant" — Haidt, *The Righteous Mind*, 2012)
- **Six foundations** (measured by the Moral Foundations Questionnaire):
  - Care/Harm: evolved from attachment system; sensitivity to cruelty and suffering
  - Fairness/Cheating: evolved from reciprocal altruism; justice, rights, proportionality
  - Loyalty/Betrayal: evolved from tribal cooperation; in-group solidarity, patriotism
  - Authority/Subversion: evolved from hierarchical social structures; respect, deference, leadership
  - Sanctity/Degradation: evolved from pathogen avoidance; purity, contamination, disgust
  - Liberty/Oppression: sensitivity to domination and coercion
- **Political asymmetry**: liberals (in US context) score high on Care and Fairness, low on Loyalty, Authority, Sanctity; conservatives score more evenly across all foundations — replicated in 30+ countries

### 1.2 Personality and Ideology
- **Big Five personality traits** correlate with political orientation:
  - **Openness to experience**: positively correlated with liberalism — tolerance of ambiguity, curiosity, aesthetic sensitivity
  - **Conscientiousness**: positively correlated with conservatism — orderliness, traditionalism, self-discipline
- **Threat sensitivity**: conservatives show greater physiological reactivity to threatening stimuli (startling faces, contamination cues — Oxley et al., 2008; Dodd et al., 2012); the relationship is debated but has been replicated in several studies
- **Need for cognitive closure** (NFCC): desire for definite answers and discomfort with ambiguity — predicts conservatism across multiple cultures (Jost et al., 2003 meta-analysis of 88 samples, N > 22,000)

### 1.3 Motivated Reasoning
- **Kunda (1990)**: people process information in a biased manner when they have a directional motivation (a preferred conclusion) — selectively accessing knowledge and generating justifications that support desired conclusions
- **Political application**: partisans evaluate the *same* evidence differently depending on whether it supports their party/ideology — demonstrated in experimental studies with fabricated research findings, policy evaluations, and candidate assessments (Taber & Lodge, 2006)
- **Both sides**: while some available evidence suggests asymmetries, motivated reasoning is documented across the political spectrum

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Right-Wing Authoritarianism and Social Dominance Orientation
- **RWA** (Altemeyer, 1981, 1996): three attitudinal clusters — authoritarian submission (deference to established authorities), authoritarian aggression (aggressiveness toward out-groups sanctioned by authorities), and conventionalism (adherence to established social norms). Strong predictor of prejudice, punitiveness, and support for strong leaders
- **SDO** (Sidanius & Pratto, 1999): preference for group-based hierarchies and dominance of in-groups over out-groups. Predicts support for policies that maintain social inequality (opposition to affirmative action, immigration, welfare)
- **Dual process model** (Duckitt, 2001): RWA and SDO represent two distinct pathways to prejudice — RWA driven by perceived threat to social order; SDO driven by competitiveness and dominance motivation

### 2.2 Political Polarization
- **Affective polarization**: increasing dislike and distrust of the opposing party — in the US, negative feelings toward the other party have risen dramatically since the 1990s (Iyengar et al., 2019) while policy positions have diverged more modestly
- **Media ecosystems**: partisan media (Fox News, MSNBC, social media filter bubbles) contribute to polarization by providing identity-confirming information and outrage-generating content about the opposition
- **Debate**: whether polarization is primarily elite-driven (politicians and media) or mass-driven (public sentiment driving elite behavior)

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Biological Bases of Political Orientation
- Twin studies (Alford, Funk, & Hibbing, 2005) suggest moderate heritability (~40–60%) of political attitudes — implying biological predispositions. Researchers have identified genetic variants (DRD4 dopamine receptor) and neuroanatomical differences (amygdala volume, anterior cingulate cortex size) that correlate with political orientation. However, these findings are preliminary, effect sizes are small, replication has been inconsistent, and the causal pathways from genes/brain structure to complex political beliefs are unclear — environment, socialization, and historical context remain the strongest determinants of specific political positions

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Only One Political Side Engages in Motivated Reasoning
- **[INCORRECT]** While research has explored asymmetries in cognitive style between liberals and conservatives, motivated reasoning affects both sides of the political spectrum. Meta-analyses show that partisans of all ideologies selectively evaluate evidence, dismiss inconvenient facts, and strengthen their beliefs when challenged. Claims that one side is uniquely rational while the other is uniquely biased are themselves examples of politically motivated reasoning

## Counter-Arguments & Criticisms

Jonathan Haidt’s Moral Foundations Theory has been challenged by Suhler and Churchland (2011), who argue the proposed moral foundations are culturally constructed categories lacking neuroscientific evidence for innate modularity. Gray, Waytz, and Young (2012) proposed that all moral judgments reduce to perceived harm, challenging Haidt’s claim of irreducible moral foundations. The liberal–conservative personality differences described by Jost et al. (2003) have been critiqued for potential ideological bias in psychological research and for reifying political categories that vary culturally. Many priming effects in political psychology—such as flag priming increasing conservative attitudes—have failed to replicate in the broader replication crisis affecting social psychology. The field continues to debate whether political attitudes reflect stable personality traits or are shaped primarily by social context and group identity.
