## QUICK SUMMARY

Attachment theory, developed by John Bowlby (1958, 1969) and empirically validated by Mary Ainsworth (1978), proposes that humans are biologically predisposed to form close emotional bonds with caregivers — and that the quality of these early bonds shapes emotional regulation, relationship patterns, and psychological development across the lifespan.
Bowlby drew on ethology (Lorenz's imprinting), psychoanalysis, and cybernetics to argue that the attachment behavioral system evolved to maintain proximity to protective caregivers during the period of infant vulnerability.
Ainsworth's "Strange Situation" paradigm (1978) identified three primary attachment patterns — secure, insecure-avoidant, and insecure-anxious/ambivalent — based on infant behavior during brief separations from and reunions with the caregiver.
Main & Solomon (1986) later identified a fourth pattern — disorganized/disoriented — associated with frightened or frightening caregiver behavior, often linked to caregiver trauma.
Hazan & Shaver (1987) extended attachment theory to adult romantic relationships, showing that the same three attachment styles characterize adult love relationships and are moderately stable from childhood through adulthood.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Bowlby's attachment behavioral system

John Bowlby proposed that attachment is an evolved behavioral system:
- **Biological function:** keeping infants close to protective caregivers to increase survival.
- **Set goal:** proximity to the attachment figure, especially under threat.
- **Internal working models:** mental representations of self and others, formed through early interactions, that guide expectations about relationships.
- **Monotropy:** initially controversial claim that infants form a primary attachment to one figure; now modified to reflect a hierarchy of attachments with multiple figures (Bowlby, 1969).

Key predictions confirmed:
- Prolonged separation from attachment figures produces a predictable sequence: protest → despair → detachment (Robertson & Bowlby, 1952).
- Early attachment disruption is associated with later emotional and behavioral difficulties (Rutter et al., 2007).

### 1.2 Ainsworth's Strange Situation and attachment classifications

Mary Ainsworth developed the Strange Situation paradigm (1978) — a 20-minute laboratory procedure with 8 episodes of separation and reunion:

| Pattern | Behavior | Caregiver Pattern | Prevalence |
|---------|----------|-------------------|------------|
| **Secure (B)** | Distressed by separation, seeks comfort on reunion, quickly soothed | Sensitive, responsive | ~60–65% |
| **Insecure-Avoidant (A)** | Little distress, ignores caregiver on reunion | Rejecting of contact, emotionally unavailable | ~20–25% |
| **Insecure-Anxious/Ambivalent (C)** | Highly distressed, seeks but resists comfort, difficult to soothe | Inconsistent responsiveness | ~10–15% |

These proportions are remarkably consistent across studies in Western populations (van IJzendoorn & Kroonenberg, 1988).

### 1.3 Disorganized/disoriented attachment

Main & Solomon (1986) identified a fourth category:
- **Disorganized (D):** contradictory behaviors (approaching caregiver while looking away, freezing, stereotypies).
- Associated with frightened or frightening caregiver behavior — the attachment figure is simultaneously the source of safety and the source of threat.
- Meta-analysis shows elevated rates in maltreated infants (80% vs. 15% in normative samples; van IJzendoorn et al., 1999).
- Disorganized attachment is the strongest predictor of later psychopathology, including dissociative symptoms (Lyons-Ruth & Jacobvitz, 2008).

### 1.4 Harlow's experimental foundations

Harry Harlow's (1958) experiments with rhesus monkeys provided powerful evidence for attachment beyond feeding:
- Infant monkeys preferred a cloth-covered "surrogate mother" that provided contact comfort over a wire surrogate that provided food.
- Social isolation during critical periods produced lasting behavioral abnormalities — withdrawal, self-injury, inability to mate or parent normally.
- These experiments were ethically controversial but provided compelling evidence that attachment serves functions beyond nourishment.

### 1.5 Cross-cultural attachment patterns

van IJzendoorn & Kroonenberg's (1988) meta-analysis of Strange Situation studies across 8 countries found:
- Secure attachment is the most common pattern in every culture studied.
- Within-culture variation exceeds between-culture variation.
- Cultural differences exist in the distribution of insecure subtypes: more avoidant (A) in Western European samples, more ambivalent (C) in Japanese and Israeli kibbutz samples.
- These differences reflect cultural variation in parenting practices and daily separation experiences, not differences in attachment system function.

## 2. CREDIBLE BUT DEBATED CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated)

### 2.1 Stability of attachment from infancy to adulthood

The degree to which infant attachment predicts adult attachment is debated:
- **Prototype model:** early attachment creates internal working models that persist unless revised by significant relationship experiences.
- **Revisionist model:** attachment security is influenced throughout life by ongoing relational experiences; early patterns can be overridden.
- Longitudinal studies (Sroufe et al., 2005) show moderate continuity (~0.30–0.40 correlation) but significant change is also common, particularly in response to trauma, loss, or secure adult relationships.

### 2.2 Adult attachment and romantic relationships

Hazan & Shaver (1987) applied attachment theory to adult love:
- Secure adults: comfortable with intimacy and independence; longest relationships.
- Avoidant adults: uncomfortable with closeness; value independence; less committed.
- Anxious adults: preoccupied with relationships; fear abandonment; highly emotional.
The Adult Attachment Interview (AAI; George et al., 1985) assesses attachment representations through narrative coherence. However, self-report measures (ECR; Brennan et al., 1998) and interview measures often correlate only modestly, raising questions about what each measures.

### 2.3 Attachment and psychopathology

While insecure attachment is a risk factor for psychopathology, the specificity of the link is debated:
- Insecure attachment is associated with depression, anxiety, personality disorders, and conduct problems.
- However, most insecurely attached individuals do *not* develop clinical disorders — attachment is a risk factor, not a deterministic cause.
- The pathway from attachment to psychopathology involves multiple mediating factors (temperament, later life events, other relationships).

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Attachment styles and political behavior

Researchers have proposed links between attachment security and political attitudes:
- Weber & Federico (2007) found that avoidant attachment was associated with political conservatism.
- However, these links are weak and likely mediated by personality traits rather than attachment per se.

## 4. DUBIOUS OR FRINGE CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Attachment trauma is irreversible

Claims that early attachment disruption permanently damages individuals ignore evidence for "earned security" — adults who experienced insecure childhoods but achieved secure attachment through therapy, stable relationships, or personal reflection (Roisman et al., 2002).

### 4.2 Attachment style is genetically fixed

While temperament has a genetic component, attachment classification is primarily determined by caregiver behavior. Twin published findings demonstrate that attachment style is not heritable in the traditional behavioral genetics sense — rather, it reflects the unique relationship between each infant and each caregiver.
