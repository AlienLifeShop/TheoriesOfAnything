## QUICK SUMMARY

Moral psychology — the empirical study of how humans make moral judgments and develop moral understanding — has undergone a revolution over the past two decades, shifting from Lawrence Kohlberg's rationalist stage theory (1958) to Jonathan Haidt's intuitionist Moral Foundations Theory (2012).
Kohlberg proposed that moral development progresses through six invariant stages from obedience-based reasoning to universal ethical principles, with moral judgments driven by conscious reasoning.
Haidt's Social Intuitionist Model (2001) challenged this by arguing that moral judgments are primarily driven by rapid, automatic intuitions ("gut feelings"), with moral reasoning serving as post-hoc rationalization — "the emotional dog and its rational tail."
Moral Foundations Theory posits at least five (later six) innate psychological foundations — Care/Harm, Fairness/Cheating, Loyalty/Betrayal, Authority/Subversion, Sanctity/Degradation, and Liberty/Oppression — whose differential weighting explains political and cultural differences in moral judgment.
The trolley problem paradigm (Thomson, 1985; Greene, 2001) has generated extensive neuroscientific data on the tension between utilitarian calculation and deontological intuition in moral cognition.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Kohlberg's stage theory of moral development

Lawrence Kohlberg (1958, 1981) proposed six stages across three levels:
- **Pre-conventional:** Stage 1 (obedience/punishment), Stage 2 (self-interest/exchange).
- **Conventional:** Stage 3 (interpersonal accord), Stage 4 (law and order).
- **Post-conventional:** Stage 5 (social contract), Stage 6 (universal ethical principles).
Key claims validated:
- Stage sequence is invariant — individuals do not skip stages.
- Cross-cultural studies confirm the same sequence across 27 countries (Snarey, 1985).
Criticisms:
- Gilligan (1982) argued Kohlberg's stages were biased toward "justice" reasoning and undervalued "care" reasoning (more common in women).
- Post-conventional stages (5–6) are extremely rare and may reflect Western liberal philosophy rather than a universal endpoint.

### 1.2 Haidt's Social Intuitionist Model

Haidt (2001) proposed that moral judgments are typically:
1. Made by **rapid, automatic intuitions** (System 1).
2. Followed by **post-hoc reasoning** (System 1 generates the judgment; System 2 constructs justifications).
3. Influenced by **social persuasion** — moral judgments can be changed by others' expressed judgments, not just by logical arguments.

Evidence:
- **Moral dumbfounding:** Participants confronted with harmless-but-disgusting scenarios (e.g., consensual adult incest with perfect contraception) judge them wrong but cannot articulate reasons beyond "it's just wrong" (Haidt et al., 2000).
- Neuroimaging shows moral judgments activate emotion-related brain regions (vmPFC, amygdala) before deliberative regions engage (Greene et al., 2001).

### 1.3 Moral Foundations Theory

Haidt & Joseph (2004) and Graham et al. (2013) proposed innate moral foundations:

| Foundation | Triggered by | Evolved from |
|------------|-------------|-------------|
| Care/Harm | Suffering, vulnerability | Mammalian attachment/care systems |
| Fairness/Cheating | Unequal treatment, free riding | Reciprocal altruism |
| Loyalty/Betrayal | Group membership, treason | Tribal coalitional psychology |
| Authority/Subversion | Hierarchy, respect, rebellion | Primate dominance hierarchies |
| Sanctity/Degradation | Waste, contamination, purity | Pathogen avoidance |
| Liberty/Oppression | Domination, tyranny | Egalitarian instincts |

Political relevance: Liberals weight Care and Fairness most heavily; conservatives weight all six foundations more equally (Graham et al., 2009).

### 1.4 Trolley problem and dual-process moral cognition

Philippa Foot (1967) and Judith Thomson (1985) introduced trolley dilemmas:
- **Switch case:** Divert trolley to kill 1 instead of 5 — most say yes (utilitarian).
- **Footbridge case:** Push a large man off a bridge to stop trolley — most say no (deontological).

Joshua Greene (2001) used fMRI to show:
- "Personal" dilemmas (footbridge) activate emotion regions (vmPFC, amygdala).
- "Impersonal" dilemmas (switch) activate cognitive control regions (dlPFC).
- Utilitarian judgment in personal dilemmas correlates with higher cognitive control and lower empathic response.

### 1.5 Cross-cultural moral variation

Richard Shweder (1990) identified three "ethics" across cultures:
- **Autonomy** (individual rights, harm — dominant in Western secular morality).
- **Community** (duty, hierarchy, role obligations — prominent in East Asian, South Asian cultures).
- **Divinity** (purity, sanctity, natural order — prominent in religious communities).

The Moral Foundations Questionnaire has been administered across 30+ countries, confirming cultural variation in foundation weighting while the foundations themselves appear universal (Graham et al., 2011).

## 2. CREDIBLE BUT DEBATED CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated)

### 2.1 Innateness of moral foundations

Whether the five-six foundations are genuinely "innate" (present at birth, requiring only environmental triggering) or constructed through cultural learning is debated:
- Hamlin et al. (2007) showed that 6-month-old infants prefer helpful over hindering agents, suggesting early moral evaluation.
- However, Suhler & Churchland (2011) argue that the foundations may emerge from more general cognitive processes rather than dedicated moral modules.

### 2.2 Moral reasoning as mere rationalization

Haidt's claim that moral reasoning is primarily post-hoc has been challenged:
- Paxton & Greene (2010) showed that deliberate reasoning can sometimes *change* moral judgments, not just rationalize them.
- Moral expertise (professional ethicists, legal scholars) involves genuine reasoning, not just intuition.
- The degree to which reasoning influences vs. rationalizes likely varies by individual, context, and cognitive load.

### 2.3 Political asymmetry in moral psychology

Haidt's claim that conservatives have a broader moral palette (using all foundations equally) while liberals rely primarily on Care/Fairness has been criticized:
- Some argue the Moral Foundations Questionnaire is constructed in ways that advantage conservative framing (Suhler & Churchland, 2011).
- Others argue that liberal moral foundations include additional concerns (epistemic autonomy, social justice) not well captured by the six-foundation model.

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Moral progress as a historical phenomenon

Steven Pinker (*The Better Angels of Our Nature*, 2011) argues for measurable moral progress — expanding circles of moral concern (from kin → tribe → nation → all humanity → animals).
Whether this represents genuine moral development or merely changing cultural norms without underlying progress is a philosophical question not fully resolved by empirical evidence.

---

## 4. DUBIOUS OR FRINGE CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Morality requires religion

Cross-cultural published evidence demonstrates that secular populations demonstrate comparable moral behavior to religious populations. Moral intuitions appear to develop independently of religious instruction (Bloom, 2012).

### 4.2 Moral foundations map onto brain regions 1:1

While neuroimaging shows that moral judgments involve specific brain networks, there is no evidence for dedicated neural modules corresponding to each moral foundation.
