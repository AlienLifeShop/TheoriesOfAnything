## QUICK SUMMARY

Personality psychology seeks to understand individual differences in characteristic patterns of thinking, feeling, and behaving — and why these patterns remain relatively stable across time and situations.
The **Big Five** (Five-Factor Model, FFM) — Openness to Experience, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, and Neuroticism (OCEAN) — emerged from the **lexical hypothesis** (personality-relevant differences are encoded in language) and represents the dominant taxonomic framework, replicated across dozens of cultures and languages (McCrae & Costa, 1997). Factor-analytic studies consistently recover these five dimensions from diverse personality questionnaires, behavioral ratings, and even linguistic analyses of social media text.
The field has evolved from early **type** theories (Galen's four temperaments, Jung's introversion/extraversion) through **trait** theories (Allport's ~4,500 trait terms; Cattell's 16PF; Eysenck's PEN model) to the Big Five consensus. Key findings include moderate **heritability** (~40–60% for all five factors), **rank-order stability** increasing from adolescence through midlife (Roberts & DelVecchio, 2000), and significant predictive validity for life outcomes including health, longevity, occupational performance, relationship quality, and psychopathology.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 History and the lexical approach

- **Allport & Odbert (1936):** Identified ~4,500 personality-describing terms in English — the beginning of the lexical approach to personality taxonomy.
- **Cattell (1943–1957):** Reduced Allport's list via cluster analysis to 16 primary factors measured by the **16PF Questionnaire**; though the 16-factor structure proved difficult to replicate across labs.
- **Norman (1963), Goldberg (1990):** Factor analyses of trait adjective ratings converged on five broad factors — eventually labeled the "Big Five."
- **Costa & McCrae (1992):** Developed the **NEO-PI-R** (240 items, 30 facets under 5 domains) — the gold-standard Big Five instrument; facets include: O — fantasy, aesthetics, feelings, actions, ideas, values; C — competence, order, dutifulness, achievement striving, self-discipline, deliberation; E — warmth, gregariousness, assertiveness, activity, excitement-seeking, positive emotions; A — trust, straightforwardness, altruism, compliance, modesty, tender-mindedness; N — anxiety, hostility, depression, self-consciousness, impulsiveness, vulnerability.

### 1.2 Cross-cultural replication

- **McCrae & Costa (1997):** The five-factor structure replicated in >50 cultures across six continents using translated NEO-PI-R; factor congruence coefficients > 0.90 for all five factors in most cultures.
- **Saucier & Goldberg (2001):** Lexical studies in diverse languages (German, Dutch, Hungarian, Italian, Czech, Filipino, Hebrew, Turkish, Korean, Chinese) generally recover factors resembling the Big Five, though some languages yield 6 or 7 factors.
- **Gender differences:** Women score slightly higher on Agreeableness and Neuroticism than men across nearly all cultures; gender differences are **larger** in more gender-egalitarian societies (Costa et al., 2001) — contradicting social role predictions and suggesting biological contributions.

### 1.3 Heritability and biological bases

- **Twin studies:** All Big Five factors show moderate heritability (40–60%): O ~57%, C ~49%, E ~54%, A ~42%, N ~48% (meta-analysis: Vukasović & Bratko, 2015); shared environment contributes relatively little (~0–11%); the remainder is non-shared environment + measurement error.
- **GWAS:** Personality traits are highly polygenic — thousands of variants each of small effect; Lo et al. (2017) identified genome-wide significant loci for neuroticism, extraversion, and openness; total variance explained by common SNPs remains small (~5–15%).
- **Eysenck's biological theory (1967):** Extraversion linked to cortical arousal (introverts chronically more aroused → seek less stimulation); neuroticism linked to limbic system reactivity; partially supported by fMRI studies showing amygdala reactivity correlates with neuroticism.

### 1.4 Stability and change

- **Rank-order stability (Roberts & DelVecchio, 2000 meta-analysis):** Test-retest correlations increase from ~0.31 in childhood to ~0.74 by ages 50–70 — personality becomes increasingly stable with age but never reaches unity (1.0); the "plaster" metaphor overstates stability.
- **Mean-level changes (Roberts et al., 2006 meta-analysis):** Across the lifespan: Agreeableness and Conscientiousness increase, Neuroticism decreases (most change in young adulthood, 20–40) — consistent with **maturation principle** (people become more socially mature). Openness increases in adolescence, then declines slightly after midlife.
- **Life events and personality change:** Major life events (first job, marriage, divorce, widowhood, unemployment) can produce personality change beyond normative maturation, though effects are typically small (Bleidorn et al., 2018).

## 2. CREDIBLE BUT DEBATED CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated)

### 2.1 Person-situation debate

- **Mischel (1968):** Challenged trait psychology by showing cross-situational consistency of behavior was typically r ≈ .30 (the "personality coefficient") — implying situations are more important than traits.
- **Resolution:** Most researchers now accept an **interactionist** position — behavior is jointly determined by personality traits and situational demands; traits predict behavior aggregated across many situations; **if...then... situation-behavior profiles** (Mischel & Shoda, 1995 CAPS model) show individuals are consistent in their patterns of situation-specific behavior.
- **Funder (2006):** The trait approach and social-cognitive approach are complementary, not competing — traits describe what people typically do; cognitive-affective units explain how they do it.

### 2.2 HEXACO model

Ashton & Lee (2007):
- Adds a sixth factor — **Honesty-Humility** (sincerity, fairness, greed avoidance, modesty) — derived from lexical studies in multiple languages where six factors emerged more naturally than five.
- HEXACO Agreeableness and Emotionality differ from Big Five Agreeableness and Neuroticism in content allocation.
- Honesty-Humility is the strongest predictor of workplace counterproductive behavior (r ≈ −.40) and correlates negatively with Dark Triad traits.
- **Debate:** Whether this represents a genuine improvement over the Big Five or merely a rotation of the factor space; Big Five proponents argue Honesty-Humility content is partially captured by low Agreeableness.

### 2.3 Dark Triad

Paulhus & Williams (2002):
- **Narcissism** (grandiosity, entitlement, need for admiration), **Machiavellianism** (strategic manipulation, cynicism, pragmatic morality), **Psychopathy** (callousness, impulsivity, antisocial behavior) — three conceptually distinct but empirically correlated (r ≈ .25–.50) dark personality traits.
- Predict workplace deviance, unethical behavior, short-term mating strategies, and interpersonal aggression.
- **Debate:** Whether these are truly distinct from each other and from Big Five traits (low Agreeableness + low Conscientiousness captures substantial variance); the **Dark Tetrad** adds everyday **sadism** as a fourth dimension (Buckels et al., 2013); measurement concerns with brief self-report measures.

### 2.4 Personality assessment validity

- **MMPI-2/MMPI-3 (Hathaway & McKinley, 1940s; updated 2020):** The most widely used clinical personality measure (567/335 items); empirically keyed validity scales detect response distortion (faking good/bad); strong evidence for clinical diagnosis but criticized for item content overlap and changing norms.
- **Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI):** Despite enormous popularity in business and career counseling (~2 million administrations/year), the MBTI has poor psychometric properties — low test-retest reliability for type classifications (~50% of people get a different type within 5 weeks), dichotomizing continuous dimensions loses information, and the four-factor structure does not replicate in factor analyses as cleanly as Big Five models (McCrae & Costa, 1989).

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Digital personality assessment

Social media behavior (likes, posts, word use, music preferences) can predict Big Five traits — some published findings demonstrate algorithmic predictions from Facebook Likes rival self-report accuracy and exceed informant accuracy for some traits (Youyou et al., 2015) — but generalizability, privacy concerns, and potential for misuse remain serious issues.

### 3.2 Personality and political ideology

Openness consistently correlates with liberal political orientation (r ≈ .30) and Conscientiousness with conservatism (r ≈ .20) (Carney et al., 2008) — but the causal direction, mediating mechanisms, and whether these reflect genuine personality traits versus politically tinged self-presentation remain debated.

## 4. DUBIOUS OR FRINGE CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Blood type personality theory

Popular in Japan and South Korea — the belief that ABO blood type determines personality (Type A = earnest, Type B = creative, Type O = confident, Type AB = rational). Large-scale studies (>10,000 participants) find no meaningful relationship between blood type and personality (Rogers & Glendon, 2003; Nawata, 2014).

### 4.2 Astrology-based personality

No credible evidence that birth date/zodiac sign predicts personality traits; large studies (N > 15,000) find no significant differences in Big Five scores by sun sign; any self-reported effects likely reflect confirmation bias and Barnum/Forer effects (Hartmann et al., 2006).
