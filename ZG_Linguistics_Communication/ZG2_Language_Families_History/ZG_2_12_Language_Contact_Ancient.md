## QUICK SUMMARY

**Language contact** — the situation in which speakers of different languages interact and their languages influence one another — is one of the most powerful forces shaping linguistic change, and its effects are pervasive throughout the history of ancient civilizations. When two or more languages coexist in a region, the outcomes include **lexical borrowing** (loanwords: the most common and least structurally penetrating form of contact), **calques** (loan translations: translating a foreign expression morpheme by morpheme into the native language), **structural convergence** (phonological, morphological, or syntactic features spreading between unrelated languages in contact), **substrate effects** (features of a conquered or subordinated language persisting in the dominant language that replaces it), **superstrate effects** (features of a politically dominant language penetrating the subordinated language), **pidginization** (emergence of a simplified contact language), and **creolization** (a pidgin becoming a full native language with its own grammar). The ancient Near East provides the richest laboratory for studying language contact over millennia: **Sumerian** and **Akkadian** coexisted in Mesopotamia for over a millennium (~2600–1800 BCE), producing massive mutual influence — Sumerian contributed thousands of loanwords, logographic writing conventions, and literary genres to Akkadian, while Sumerian itself absorbed Akkadian phonological and morphological features during its final centuries as a spoken language. The **Sumerian substrate** persisted in Babylonian and Assyrian cuneiform culture for 2,000 years after Sumerian ceased to be a living language. **Aramaic** gradually replaced Akkadian across the Near East (c. 700 BCE–), becoming the lingua franca of the Persian Empire — leaving substrate effects on later languages across the region. **Koine Greek**, spreading with Alexander's conquests, came into contact with dozens of local languages across the Eastern Mediterranean and Near East, producing characteristic contact phenomena visible in Septuagint Greek, papyri, and inscriptions. **Latin** in the Western Roman Empire replaced Celtic, Iberian, and other local languages — but these substrates left lasting effects visible in Romance languages (e.g., the pre-Roman substrate influence on French phonology, Basque substrate in Gascon). The study of language contact in antiquity draws on epigraphy, philology, historical linguistics, and sociolinguistics to reconstruct the complex multilingual ecologies of the ancient world — societies where bilingualism and multilingualism were the norm, not the exception.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Experimentally Confirmed)

### 1.1 Core Concepts of Language Contact
- **Borrowing**: the transfer of linguistic material (words, morphemes, sounds, structures) from one language (the **donor** or **source language**) to another (the **recipient** or **borrowing language**):
  - **Loanwords** (lexical borrowing): the most common contact phenomenon — words adopted from another language, often with phonological and morphological adaptation (English *beef* from French *boeuf*, *algebra* from Arabic *al-jabr*)
  - **Borrowing hierarchy** (Thomason & Kaufman, 1988): casual contact produces mainly vocabulary borrowing; more intense contact produces structural borrowing (phonology, syntax, morphology) — the more intense and prolonged the contact, the deeper the structural influence
  - **Calque** (loan translation): translating a foreign expression morpheme by morpheme (English *skyscraper* → French *gratte-ciel*, literally "scrape-sky"; Akkadian borrowed Sumerian compound expressions by translating each element)

- **Substrate, superstrate, and adstrate**:
  - **Substrate**: the language of a subordinated population that is abandoned in favor of a dominant language — features of the abandoned language persist in the new language (e.g., pre-Roman Celtic substrate features in French)
  - **Superstrate**: the language of a dominant group that is eventually abandoned in favor of the local language — features persist (e.g., Norman French superstrate in English)
  - **Adstrate**: contact between languages of roughly equal prestige (e.g., Finnish and Swedish in Finland)

- **Sprachbund** (linguistic area): a geographical region where unrelated or distantly related languages converge in features due to prolonged contact:
  - **Balkan Sprachbund**: Romanian, Bulgarian, Albanian, Greek, Macedonian, and Serbian share features (postposed definite article, loss of infinitive, merged dative-genitive case) despite belonging to different branches of Indo-European
  - **South Asian Sprachbund**: Indo-Aryan and Dravidian languages share retroflexion, SOV word order, and converb constructions despite being unrelated
  - **Mesoamerican Sprachbund**: Mayan, Mixe-Zoquean, Oto-Manguean, and Nahuatl languages share vigesimal numeral systems, relational nouns, and inalienable possession patterns

### 1.2 Sumerian–Akkadian Contact
- **Sumerian** (a language isolate) and **Akkadian** (East Semitic) coexisted in southern Mesopotamia from at least the mid-3rd millennium BCE:
  - **Lexical borrowing**: massive in both directions — Sumerian words entered Akkadian for concepts relating to agriculture, administration, religion, and literature; Akkadian loanwords are detectable in late Sumerian texts
  - **Writing system influence**: Akkadian adopted cuneiform script from Sumerian, including Sumerograms (Sumerian logograms used to write Akkadian words — analogous to writing "etc." in English from Latin *et cetera*)
  - **Substrate effects**: after Sumerian ceased to be a spoken language (c. 1800 BCE), it persisted as a learned, literary, and liturgical language — Babylonian scribes continued to study, copy, and compose in Sumerian for nearly 2,000 years, and Sumerian vocabulary, expressions, and literary genres deeply shaped Akkadian literature
  - **Bilingual texts**: thousands of Sumerian-Akkadian bilingual word lists, glossaries, and interlinear translations survive — the earliest systematic bilingual lexicography

### 1.3 Aramaic as Lingua Franca
- **Aramaic** (Northwest Semitic) gradually spread across the Near East from the late 2nd millennium BCE:
  - By the 8th–7th centuries BCE, Aramaic was widely used for international communication alongside Akkadian
  - **Official Aramaic** became the administrative language of the **Achaemenid Persian Empire** (550–330 BCE) — the lingua franca from Egypt to Central Asia
  - Aramaic replaced numerous local languages (Phoenician, Akkadian, and many others) through a process of language shift — but contact effects persisted:
    - Akkadian substrate in Babylonian Aramaic (loanwords, calques)
    - Various local substrates in different Aramaic dialects
  - Aramaic itself fragmented into diverse dialects (Syriac, Mandaic, Jewish Babylonian Aramaic, Samaritan, etc.) — some still spoken today

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Supported by Multiple Scholars / Strong Circumstantial Evidence)

### 2.1 Greek Contact Phenomena
- **Koine Greek** (the common Greek that spread with Alexander's conquests, c. 330 BCE–) came into contact with Aramaic, Egyptian, Iranian, and other languages across the Eastern Mediterranean and Near East:
  - **Septuagint Greek** (3rd–2nd century BCE Greek translation of the Hebrew Bible): shows significant **Semitic influence** — Hebraisms and Aramaisms in syntax (parataxis with *kai* "and" reflecting Hebrew *waw*), calques (*prosōpon* "face" used for "presence" calquing Hebrew *panim*), and loanwords
  - **Egyptian Greek** (papyri from Ptolemaic and Roman Egypt): shows Egyptian substrate influence in phonology and vocabulary
  - Greek itself contributed massively to the languages it contacted — Greek loanwords are abundant in Latin, Aramaic, Coptic, Armenian, and many other ancient languages

### 2.2 Pre-Roman Substrates in Romance Languages
- **Celtic substrate** in Gallo-Romance (especially French):
  - Phonological: the Gaulish u → ü (front rounded vowel) shift may underlie the French *u* [y] vowel (absent in other Romance languages); the lenition (weakening) of intervocalic consonants may reflect Celtic phonological tendencies
  - Lexical: French words of Gaulish origin include *charrue* (plow), *chemin* (path), *bouleau* (birch) — ~150–200 Gaulish-origin words survive in French
  - Debated: the role of Celtic substrate vs. internal development is contested for many features

- **Basque substrate** in Gascon (a variety of Occitan spoken in the Pyrenees region):
  - Loss of Latin initial *f-* (Latin *filium* → Gascon *hilh* "son," parallel to Castilian *hijo* but independently derived) — attributed to Basque substrate influence (Basque lacks /f/)
  - Voicing of initial voiceless stops (contravening Romance norms)

- **Etruscan substrate** in Latin:
  - Etruscan contributed vocabulary to Latin, including personal names, some institutional terms, and possibly certain phonological features — the extent is debated because Etruscan remains poorly understood

### 2.3 Contact-Induced Change vs. Internal Development
- **Thomason & Kaufman** (1988, *Language Contact, Creolization, and Genetic Linguistics*): argued that virtually any linguistic feature can be transferred between languages given sufficient contact intensity — challenging the purely tree-model view of language change
  - Their framework distinguishes **borrowing** (in maintained languages) from **shift-induced interference** (when a population shifts to a new language and transfers substrate features)
  - Intensity scale: casual contact (lexical borrowing only) → moderate (function words, some phonology) → intense (major structural borrowing, phonological restructuring) → language shift (substrate features pervade the new language)

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Limited Evidence / Emerging Hypotheses)

### 3.1 Pre-Sumerian Substrates in Mesopotamia
- Several scholars have proposed a **pre-Sumerian substrate language** in southern Mesopotamia based on:
  - Place names and river names (toponyms/hydronyms) that appear neither Sumerian nor Semitic (e.g., the Tigris and Euphrates names have disputed etymologies)
  - Agricultural and technological vocabulary in Sumerian that does not have clear Sumerian etymologies
  - This is highly speculative — the evidence is indirect, and alternative explanations (internal Sumerian word formation, borrowing from other known languages) exist

### 3.2 Language Contact and the Emergence of Indo-European Features
- Scholars (e.g., Nichols, 1992; Thomason, 2001) suggest that certain features of individual Indo-European branches may reflect substrate/contact effects rather than inheritance:
  - E.g., the retroflex consonants of Indo-Aryan reflecting Dravidian substrate influence (widely accepted)
  - The tonal system of Punjabi and some Scandinavian languages as contact-induced innovations
  - The exact boundary between inherited and contact-induced features remains debatable for many cases

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — Fringe / Not Supported by Evidence)

### 4.1 Substrate Explanations for Everything
- A common tendency in popular linguistics is to attribute every unexplained feature of a language to "substrate influence" from an unattested pre-existing language. While substrate effects are real and well-documented in many cases, positing unattested substrates as explanations for features that may have other causes (internal change, drift, analogy, pragmatic factors) is methodologically weak without independent evidence

### 4.2 All Ancient Civilizations Were Monolingual
- Popular imagination often projects modern monolingual national ideologies onto the ancient world. In reality, multilingualism was the norm in most ancient societies: Mesopotamian scribes were trained in Sumerian and Akkadian; Roman elites were bilingual in Latin and Greek; Ptolemaic Egypt operated in Greek, Demotic Egyptian, and learned Hieroglyphic Egyptian simultaneously

## COUNTER-ARGUMENTS

- **Borrowability hierarchy debates**: **Thomason and Kaufman** (*Language Contact, Creolization, and Genetic Linguistics*, 1988) proposed a hierarchy of borrowability (lexical → phonological → morphological → syntactic), but subsequent studies — **Matras and Sakel** (2007), **Haspelmath** (2009) — have challenged the universality of this hierarchy, showing that morphological borrowing can occur without extensive lexical borrowing in some contact situations
- **Substrate methodology criticism**: Substrate explanations for linguistic features — attributing innovations to influence from a prior language lost through shift — have been criticized as unfalsifiable when the proposed substrate language is unattested. **Roger Blench** and others have noted the difficulty of distinguishing substrate influence from language-internal change or coincidence when the supposed source language left no independent record
