## QUICK SUMMARY

A **dead language** is one that no longer has any native speakers — no community transmits it to children as a first language through normal intergenerational communication. Of the approximately **7,000 languages** spoken today, linguists estimate that **one language dies approximately every two weeks**, projecting that **50–90% of current languages may be extinct by 2100** (Krauss, 1992; UNESCO Atlas). Yet the concept of "dead language" encompasses a wide spectrum: from languages like **Sumerian** (last native speakers ~2000 BCE, though used as a scholarly language for two more millennia) and **Etruscan** (poorly understood, no modern descendants) that are truly dead, to **Latin** — arguably the most famous "dead" language — which, while having no native speakers since the early medieval period, never truly died: it evolved into the Romance languages (French, Spanish, Italian, Portuguese, Romanian), persisted as the scholarly, ecclesiastical, and scientific lingua franca of Europe for over a millennium, and remains the official language of Vatican City. The distinction between "dead" and "dormant" is important: a dormant language has no living speakers but retains a community that identifies with it and possesses sufficient documentation to potentially **revive** it — the most spectacular example being **Modern Hebrew** (*Ivrit*), which was revived from a liturgical/literary language with no native speakers into a thriving national language with **9+ million speakers** by Eliezer Ben-Yehuda and the Zionist movement beginning in the late 19th century — the only fully successful revival of a "dead" language to full community use in human history. Other revival efforts (Cornish, Manx, Wampanoag/Wôpanâak, some Australian Aboriginal languages) are ongoing with varying degrees of success. Languages die through **language shift** — when a community gradually abandons its ancestral language for a socially dominant one, usually under pressure from colonialism, economic marginalization, compulsory education in the dominant language, media dominance, or active suppression. The loss of each language represents the loss of a unique **cognitive framework**, ecological knowledge system, oral literature tradition, and cultural identity — prompting urgent documentation efforts (ELDP, ELAR, DoBeS) and revitalization programs worldwide.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Experimentally Confirmed)

### 1.1 Categories of Dead and Dying Languages
- Linguists distinguish several types:
  - **Extinct languages**: no speakers, no connected community, and usually insufficient documentation for revival — e.g., **Etruscan** (pre-Roman Italy; ~13,000 inscriptions survive but the language remains poorly understood), **Hurrian** (ancient Near East), **Pictish** (Scotland)
  - **Dead but documented languages**: no native speakers, but extensive records allow continued study and use — e.g., **Latin** (massive literary corpus; still used in Catholic Church, scientific nomenclature, and academic study), **Classical Sanskrit** (vast literary/philosophical corpus; liturgical use continues), **Ancient Greek** (Koine, Attic, etc. — foundational texts of Western philosophy, science, literature), **Sumerian** (earliest attested language, c. 3000 BCE; extensive cuneiform corpus)
  - **Dormant languages**: no current speakers, but community identification and documentation provide a basis for potential revival — e.g., **Cornish** (last traditional speaker died ~1777; revival begun late 19th century; now ~600 speakers), **Manx** (last native speaker Ned Maddrell died 1974; revival has produced ~1,800 speakers and a Manx-medium primary school)
  - **Moribund languages**: still spoken but only by elderly speakers; no children are learning them — intergenerational transmission has ceased. These are functionally already on a path to death unless intervention occurs. UNESCO estimates ~2,500 languages are in this category

### 1.2 Notable Dead Languages
- **Sumerian** (~3100–2000 BCE as a spoken language):
  - The oldest attested language in the world; spoken in southern Mesopotamia (modern Iraq)
  - A **language isolate** — no known relatives
  - Replaced as a spoken language by Akkadian (Semitic) by ~2000 BCE but survived as a prestige scholarly and liturgical language for ~2,000 years after its death as a native language (comparable to Latin's post-death persistence)
  - ~100,000 cuneiform tablets preserve Sumerian texts

- **Latin** (last native speakers ~6th–8th century CE):
  - Evolved into the Romance languages through normal linguistic change — Latin "died" only in the sense that no one spoke Classical Latin natively; the spoken Latin of late antiquity simply *became* French, Spanish, etc.
  - Persisted as the language of the Catholic Church, European scholarship, science, law, and diplomacy until the 17th–18th centuries
  - Still used: Vatican City official language; scientific nomenclature (biology, medicine, law); academic and liturgical use

- **Hittite** (c. 1650–1178 BCE):
  - A dead Indo-European language of Anatolia; deciphered by Bedřich Hrozný in 1915
  - Important for comparative linguistics: Hittite preserved archaic features (e.g., laryngeal consonants) that confirmed predictions of the laryngeal theory in Proto-Indo-European reconstruction

- **Coptic** (descended from Ancient Egyptian):
  - The last phase of the Egyptian language, written in a modified Greek alphabet
  - Ceased as a community spoken language by the 17th century; survives as the liturgical language of the Coptic Orthodox Church

### 1.3 Causes of Language Death
- Languages die primarily through **language shift** — a community's gradual or rapid transition to a different language:
  - **Colonial/imperial domination**: indigenous languages suppressed through boarding schools (e.g., Native American residential schools, Australian Aboriginal children's removal), legal prohibitions, and social stigmatization
  - **Economic pressure**: the dominant language is required for employment, education, and social advancement — parents rationally (but consequentially) choose to raise children in the dominant language
  - **Media and technology**: television, radio, internet, and social media overwhelmingly in dominant languages; absence of minority-language media accelerates shift
  - **Urbanization**: rural-to-urban migration breaks traditional language transmission chains
  - **Genocide and catastrophic population loss**: extreme cases where entire speaker communities are destroyed (e.g., Tasmanian Aboriginal languages)
  - **Social prestige**: internalized ideology that the indigenous language is "backward" or "useless" — language attitudes as a driver of shift

### 1.4 The Hebrew Revival
- **Modern Hebrew** is the only fully successful revival of a language with no native speakers:
  - **Eliezer Ben-Yehuda** (1858–1922): the primary architect of the revival; moved to Palestine in 1881 and raised the first native Hebrew-speaking child in modern times (his son Ben-Zion); compiled the first modern Hebrew dictionary; coined thousands of new words for modern concepts
  - Hebrew had persisted for ~1,700 years as a **literary, liturgical, and scholarly language** — Jews across the diaspora could read and write Hebrew but used other languages (Yiddish, Ladino, Arabic, etc.) as native spoken languages
  - The revival succeeded through: ideological commitment (Zionist language ideology); the establishment of Hebrew-language schools; community adoption in the Yishuv (pre-state Jewish community in Palestine); and ultimately, state language policy after Israel's founding (1948)
  - Today: **~9 million speakers** (5 million L1), a fully modern language with native-speaker communities, literature, media, and slang
  - **Unique conditions**: a massive existing literary corpus; a pre-existing reading community; strong ideological motivation; institutional state support; immigrant communities needing a shared language. These conditions are difficult to replicate

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Supported by Multiple Scholars / Strong Circumstantial Evidence)

### 2.1 The Scale of Language Loss
- **Krauss** (1992): estimated that **90% of the world's languages could be extinct or moribund by 2100** — the most widely cited (and most alarming) projection
- **UNESCO Atlas of the World's Languages in Danger** (2010 edition): classifies ~2,500 languages as endangered (vulnerable, definitely endangered, severely endangered, critically endangered, or extinct)
- More conservative estimates (e.g., Ethnologue): suggest 40–50% may be at risk — still representing thousands of languages
- The rate of language loss is **accelerating** — more languages have died in the past century than in any comparable period in human history

### 2.2 What Is Lost When a Language Dies?
- Each language encodes unique:
  - **Grammatical structures**: some languages have grammatical categories and distinctions found in no other language (e.g., evidentiality systems marking information source; spatial reference systems based on absolute rather than relative directions)
  - **Ecological knowledge**: indigenous languages often contain encyclopedic knowledge of local flora, fauna, ecology, and land management encoded in vocabulary and place names — this "biocultural diversity" is lost with the language
  - **Oral literature**: stories, myths, songs, proverbs, and oral histories transmitted solely in the language — often unrecorded
  - **Cultural identity**: language is the primary vehicle of cultural transmission; its loss frequently triggers broader cultural dissolution

### 2.3 Other Revival Efforts
- Beyond Hebrew, several notable revival/reclamation projects:
  - **Cornish** (*Kernewek*): declared extinct (last traditional speaker Dolly Pentreath died 1777); revival begun in early 20th century by Henry Jenner; gained UNESCO "critically endangered" (rather than extinct) status; ~600 speakers today, including children raised as speakers
  - **Manx** (*Gaelg*): last native speaker Ned Maddrell died 1974; revival through schools and community programs; ~1,800 speakers today; one Manx-medium primary school
  - **Wampanoag/Wôpanâak** (Massachusetts, USA): no speakers for over 150 years; **Jessie Little Doe Baird** (MacArthur Fellow, 2010) led reclamation using colonial-era Wampanoag-language documents; children now being raised speaking the language
  - **Australian Aboriginal languages**: several (e.g., Kaurna, Ngarrindjeri) being reclaimed from historical documentation

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Limited Evidence / Emerging Hypotheses)

### 3.1 Technology as a Tool for Revitalization
- Digital tools (language-learning apps, social media in minority languages, AI-assisted translation and documentation) may create new possibilities for language revitalization — but whether technology can substitute for the community-based intergenerational transmission that is the "gold standard" for language vitality is uncertain
- Some experiments (Duolingo's Hawaiian and Navajo courses; First Voices digital platform for Indigenous Canadian languages) show promise but have not yet produced large-scale results

### 3.2 Can All Dead Languages Be Revived?
- The Hebrew model required extraordinary conditions (large corpus, reading community, state support, ideological motivation). Whether languages with minimal documentation, no literate tradition, and small/displaced communities can be revived to full community use remains open — most linguists suggest "reclamation" (partial community use, ceremonial/educational) is more realistic than full revival for most dead/dormant languages

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — Fringe / Not Supported by Evidence)

### 4.1 Language Death Is Natural and Harmless
- While language change and replacement have occurred throughout history, the **current rate** of language loss is unprecedented and overwhelmingly driven by colonial and economic forces — not by speakers' free choice. The framing of language death as "natural" obscures the structural violence that causes it

### 4.2 A World With Fewer Languages Would Be Better
- Claims that linguistic diversity impedes communication and development ignore the evidence that multilingualism is cognitively, culturally, and socially beneficial — and that the loss of linguistic diversity parallels and compounds the loss of biological and cultural diversity
