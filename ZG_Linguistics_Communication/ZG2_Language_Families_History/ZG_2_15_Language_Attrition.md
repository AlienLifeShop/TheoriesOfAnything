## QUICK SUMMARY

**Language attrition** — the process by which a previously acquired language is gradually lost by an individual speaker due to reduced use and exposure — is one of the most fascinating and practically important phenomena in the language sciences. While much linguistic research focuses on how languages are learned, attrition examines the mirror process: how linguistic knowledge deteriorates, restructures, or becomes inaccessible when a speaker is immersed in a new dominant language and loses regular contact with their first language (L1). The classic scenario is the immigrant who arrives in a new country, acquires the host language (L2), and over years or decades finds their L1 skills diminishing — vocabulary becomes harder to retrieve, grammatical structures simplify or show interference from L2, accent shifts, and fluency and confidence decline. But attrition is not simply "forgetting" in the sense of complete erasure — research by **Monika Schmid** (2002, 2011) and **Barbara Köpke** (various, with Schmid 2004) has shown that attrition is better understood as a change in **activation thresholds** and **cross-linguistic influence** (CLI): knowledge of the L1 is not necessarily lost from memory but becomes harder to access due to competition from the now-dominant L2, reduced input and use, and gradual restructuring. Evidence includes the **"savings effect"** — relearning an attrited language is dramatically faster than learning it from scratch, even decades after last exposure — suggesting that substantial implicit knowledge remains intact. Age of emigration is the single strongest predictor: speakers who emigrate as adults (past adolescence) show primarily lexical and fluency effects but preserve core grammar; speakers who emigrate as children (particularly before age 10–12) can experience much more profound attrition, including loss of morphosyntactic competence — and in extreme cases (international adoptees adopted before age 5–6 from monolingual L1 environments into monolingual L2 environments), the L1 can become completely inaccessible to conscious use, though fMRI studies (Pallier et al., 2003; Pierce et al., 2014) demonstrate that neural traces of early linguistic experience persist. Language attrition differs from **language death** (a sociolinguistic process where an entire community abandons a language — see ZG_5_11) and from **incomplete acquisition** (where heritage language speakers never fully acquire certain features of their L1 because input was limited from childhood). The field has important implications for immigration policy, heritage language education, identity politics, and understanding the nature of human memory.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Experimentally Confirmed)

### 1.1 What Is Language Attrition?
- **Language attrition**: the decline or restructuring of a previously fully acquired language in an individual — distinguished from:
  - **Language death/shift**: a community-level phenomenon — an entire group abandons a language across generations (sociolinguistic process)
  - **Incomplete acquisition**: the failure to fully acquire certain features of a heritage language due to reduced input from childhood — the speaker never had full competence in those features (as opposed to attrition, where competence was once present and then declined)
  - **Aphasia**: language loss due to brain damage (neurological, not environmental)

- **Core mechanism**: reduced use of and exposure to the L1, combined with increased use of and exposure to the L2 — leads to:
  - **Lexical attrition**: word retrieval difficulties (tip-of-the-tongue states), reduced vocabulary range, increased reliance on borrowings or code-switches from L2
  - **Phonological/phonetic attrition**: shifts in vowel quality, consonant production, and prosody — L1 pronunciation may be influenced by L2 norms
  - **Morphosyntactic attrition**: simplification or restructuring of grammar (loss of gender agreement, case marking errors, word order changes influenced by L2)
  - **Pragmatic/discourse attrition**: changes in discourse organization, politeness strategies, and interactional patterns

### 1.2 Key Findings from Research
- **Monika Schmid's** research (2002, 2011, 2013):
  - *Language Attrition* (2011): comprehensive overview establishing the field's empirical foundations
  - Studied L1 attrition in German emigrants in the Netherlands, Canada, and English-speaking countries — found that:
    - **Age of emigration** is the most powerful predictor: emigrating before ~age 12 is associated with significantly more attrition than emigrating as an adult
    - **Length of residence** in the L2 environment correlates weakly with attrition — most attrition occurs in the first ~10 years of reduced L1 use, then stabilizes
    - **L1 use and contact**: regular L1 use (speaking with family, consuming L1 media, visiting the L1 country, maintaining L1 social networks) is protective — but even substantial L1 use does not fully prevent all attrition
    - **Attitude and identity**: speakers who maintain strong identification with their L1 culture tend to show somewhat less attrition — but the effect is modest and mediated by actual use patterns
    - **Education level**: higher education in the L1 before emigration provides a more robust L1 representation

- **Activation Threshold Hypothesis** (Paradis, 2004, 2007):
  - L1 knowledge is not erased but its **activation threshold** rises — more activation energy is needed to access L1 items due to reduced stimulation and competition from the frequently activated L2
  - Explains why attrited speakers often understand the L1 better than they can produce it (comprehension requires less activation than production)
  - Also explains temporary attrition effects: speakers returning to the L1 environment after years of L2 dominance often experience rapid recovery — the knowledge was not lost but inhibited

- **Savings effect** (evidence from international adoptees):
  - **Pallier et al. (2003)**: Korean-born adults adopted into French-speaking families as young children — showed no conscious knowledge of Korean and could not distinguish Korean from other languages — BUT fMRI showed no distinctive Korean brain activation patterns, suggesting complete cognitive reorganization
  - **Pierce et al. (2014)**: Chinese-born children adopted into French-speaking families — showed distinctive neural processing patterns for Chinese tones despite no conscious knowledge of Chinese — suggesting persistent implicit L1 traces
  - **Oh et al. (2010)**: Korean adoptees relearning Korean showed faster acquisition of Korean phonological contrasts than naive learners — a "savings effect" — unconscious residual L1 knowledge facilitates relearning even after decades of non-use

### 1.3 Vulnerability of Different Linguistic Domains
- **Most vulnerable (earliest to attrite)**:
  - **Lexicon**: vocabulary retrieval is the most sensitive to attrition — rare or low-frequency words are lost first; high-frequency, basic vocabulary is most resistant
  - **Fluency**: speaking rate, pausing patterns, and spontaneous speech become slower and less fluid

- **Intermediate vulnerability**:
  - **Phonology**: foreign accent/pronunciation shifts are common — vowel systems may restructure, VOT (voice onset time) may shift toward L2 norms
  - **Morphology**: inflectional morphology (case, gender, verb conjugation) is vulnerable, especially where the L2 lacks corresponding categories

- **Most resistant (last to attrite)**:
  - **Core syntax**: basic word order and clause structure are highly resistant to attrition in adult emigrants
  - **Phonemic contrasts**: the ability to perceive L1 phonemic distinctions (even if production shifts) is relatively robust

### 1.4 Age Effects and the Critical Period
- **Pre-puberty emigrants** (before ~12): at greatest risk for profound attrition, including structural/grammatical attrition — the L1 may not be fully stabilized (or fully acquired) before the shift to L2 dominance
- **Post-puberty emigrants** (after ~12–15): L1 grammar is largely stable and resistant to attrition; lexical and fluency effects are the main manifestations
- **Adult emigrants**: even after decades of L2 immersion, adult attriters typically retain core L1 grammar — their L1 may sound "accented" by L2 and show lexical gaps, but structural competence remains largely intact
- This pattern is consistent with (though does not prove) a **sensitive/critical period** for L1 acquisition — once past, the L1 representation is robust; if the period is interrupted, the L1 may not be fully consolidated

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Supported by Multiple Scholars / Strong Circumstantial Evidence)

### 2.1 Heritage Language Speakers
- **Heritage speakers** — individuals who grew up hearing/speaking a minority language (L1) at home but were educated and socialized in the majority language (L2) — often show a combination of incomplete acquisition and attrition:
  - They may never have fully acquired certain L1 features (especially literacy-related, formal register, and low-frequency structures) AND lose competence in features they did acquire as they become increasingly L2-dominant
  - Their language competence is different from both monolingual L1 speakers and L2 learners — a distinct bilingual competence, not a "deficient" version of either language (Polinsky, 2018)

### 2.2 Cross-Linguistic Influence (CLI) in Attrition
- L2 influence on the attrited L1 is bidirectional and pervasive:
  - **Lexical borrowing/code-switching**: inserting L2 words into L1 speech (the most visible sign of attrition)
  - **Convergence**: L1 structures become more similar to L2 over time — e.g., German speakers in English-speaking countries may gradually shift toward SVO word order in subordinate clauses (where German requires SOV)
  - **Simplification**: structures unique to the L1 (and absent from L2) are particularly vulnerable — gender agreement, case marking, and verb morphology may simplify

### 2.3 Reversibility
- Attrition is **partially reversible** through re-immersion:
  - Returning to the L1 environment after years of L2 dominance typically produces significant recovery — lexical retrieval improves rapidly (days to weeks), pronunciation shifts back, and fluency recovers
  - Complete recovery may not occur, especially for child attriters — but adult attriters often show impressive recovery with re-exposure
  - The degree of recovery depends on age at emigration, length of separation, and quality/quantity of re-exposure

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Limited Evidence / Emerging Hypotheses)

### 3.1 Neural Mechanisms of Attrition
- fMRI and ERP available evidence suggests that attrition involves changes in neural activation patterns — reduced L1 activation in classical language areas and increased inhibitory control when suppressing L2 during L1 production — but the detailed neural mechanisms are not yet well understood
- Whether attrition involves actual neural *loss* (pruning of connections) or purely *inhibitory* changes (raising activation thresholds without structural loss) is debated — the savings effect supports the inhibition view, but adoptee studies showing complete L1 inaccessibility suggest that very early attrition may involve more profound reorganization

### 3.2 Attrition in Old Age
- Anecdotal and clinical evidence suggests that some elderly bilingual immigrants "return" to their L1 as cognitive decline progresses — particularly in cases of dementia, the more recently acquired L2 may be selectively impaired, with the L1 becoming relatively more accessible. This is poorly studied and the evidence is mixed, but it would be consistent with the Activation Threshold Hypothesis

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — Fringe / Not Supported by Evidence)

### 4.1 "You Can't Forget Your Mother Tongue"
- While popular belief holds that one's first language is "permanent," research clearly shows that L1 competence can decline significantly under conditions of reduced use — especially if the shift occurs in childhood. The distinction between conscious accessibility and implicit retention is crucial: explicit L1 skills can be lost while implicit traces persist

### 4.2 "Attrition Means You Were Never a 'Real' Bilingual"
- Attrition is a normal consequence of changing linguistic environments, not a sign of deficiency. All bilinguals experience some degree of attrition in their less-used language — this is a feature of the adaptive human language system, not a pathology

## COUNTER-ARGUMENTS

- **Competing attrition models**: Whether first-language attrition reflects genuine structural erosion (permanent loss of grammatical representations), activation threshold shifts (competence remains but accessibility decreases), or feature reassembly under L2 influence remains disputed. **Monika Schmid** (*Language Attrition*, 2011) argued for an activation-based model, while **Silvina Montrul** has argued that heritage speaker attrition involves true representational differences — the theoretical distinction has practical implications for the reversibility of language loss
- **Critical period for attrition**: Whether there is a critical period after which L1 attrition becomes impossible (often proposed around age 12) is contested — evidence from international adoptees (**Pallier et al.**, 2003) suggests near-total L1 attrition even after childhood, while other studies show persistent L1 traces in cortical processing
