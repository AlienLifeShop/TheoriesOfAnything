## QUICK SUMMARY

**Conversation Analysis (CA)** is a rigorous empirical approach to studying the organization of naturally occurring talk-in-interaction, founded by the sociologist **Harvey Sacks** in collaboration with **Emanuel Schegloff** and **Gail Jefferson** in the 1960s–1970s at UCLA, rooted in **Harold Garfinkel's ethnomethodology** — the study of the methods that ordinary people use to produce and make sense of their everyday social activities. CA's foundational insight is that conversation — the most basic form of human social interaction — is not chaotic or random but **exquisitely organized** at multiple levels: participants take turns speaking with remarkable precision (typical gaps and overlaps average ~200 milliseconds across languages); they organize their talk into recognizable sequences (question → answer, invitation → acceptance/decline, greeting → greeting); and they systematically manage problems of hearing, speaking, and understanding through **repair** mechanisms. The **turn-taking system** described by Sacks, Schegloff & Jefferson (1974, "A Simplest Systematics for the Organization of Turn-Taking in Conversation") is one of the most cited papers in the social sciences: it models conversation as operating through **Turn-Constructional Units (TCUs)** — units of talk (a sentence, clause, phrase, or word) that constitute a recognizable turn at talk — and **Transition Relevance Places (TRPs)** — the points at which a speaker's turn is potentially complete and another speaker may (or should) take a turn. Three ordered rules govern speaker selection at TRPs: (1) current speaker may select next speaker; (2) if not, any participant may self-select; (3) if neither, current speaker may continue. This system is **locally managed** (participants negotiate turn-by-turn, not by a pre-set schedule) and **interactionally managed** (allocation is a joint achievement). CA's method is rigorously empirical: it works exclusively with recordings of naturally occurring interaction (not interviews, experiments, or invented examples), transcribed in fine-grained detail using the **Jefferson Transcription System** — which notates overlapping speech, pauses (timed to tenths of a second), intonation, volume, speed, laughter, breathing, and non-verbal actions. Beyond mundane conversation, CA has been applied to **institutional talk** (doctor-patient consultations, courtroom interaction, classroom discourse, emergency calls, news interviews) — revealing how institutional roles and tasks shape the organization of talk.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Experimentally Confirmed)

### 1.1 Origins and Foundations
- **Harvey Sacks** (1935–1975): sociologist at UCLA — began studying naturally occurring talk (initially, telephone calls to a suicide prevention center) and discovered that conversation has highly organized, orderly properties that can be studied empirically
  - Sacks' **Lectures on Conversation** (published posthumously, 1992, ed. Gail Jefferson) — the foundational texts of CA
  - Influenced by Harold Garfinkel's **ethnomethodology**: the study of the practical methods that social actors use to produce and recognize orderly social actions — conversation is a key site where social order is produced

- **Emanuel Schegloff**: co-developer of CA with Sacks — particularly important for work on sequence organization, repair, and "pre-sequences" (pre-invitations, pre-requests, pre-announcements)

- **Gail Jefferson** (1938–2008): developed the **Jefferson Transcription System** — the standard notation system for CA, providing fine-grained representation of talk features:
  - `[` for overlap onset, `=` for latching (no gap), `(0.5)` for timed pauses, `(.)` for micro-pauses
  - `.` for falling intonation, `?` for rising intonation, `↑↓` for pitch shifts
  - `>text<` for faster speech, `<text>` for slower speech
  - `CAPS` for louder, `°text°` for softer
  - `heh`, `hah`, `.hh` for laughter and inbreaths
  - `:` for sound stretching

### 1.2 The Turn-Taking System (Sacks, Schegloff & Jefferson, 1974)
- **Turn-Constructional Unit (TCU)**: the basic unit of a turn at talk — a potentially complete linguistic unit that is recognizably complete in context (a sentence, clause, phrase, or even a single word like "yeah"):
  - Participants project the possible completion point of a TCU based on syntactic, prosodic, and pragmatic cues — they can predict when a turn is about to end

- **Transition Relevance Place (TRP)**: the point at the end of a TCU where speaker transition becomes relevant:
  - Rule 1: If the current speaker has selected a next speaker (by addressing them, asking a question to them), the selected speaker has the right and obligation to take the next turn
  - Rule 2: If no next speaker is selected, any other participant may self-select (first starter gets the floor)
  - Rule 3: If no one self-selects, the current speaker may continue (producing another TCU)
  - These rules apply recursively at each TRP

- **Key properties of the system**:
  - **Overwhelmingly one-at-a-time**: despite no formal rules, speakers overwhelmingly avoid simultaneous talk — overlaps are typically brief and occur at or near TRPs
  - **Minimal gaps, minimal overlaps**: Stivers et al. (2009) — cross-linguistic study of 10 typologically diverse languages showed that average gaps between turns cluster around **~200 milliseconds** — remarkably consistent across cultures — suggesting a universal cognitive constraint on turn-taking
  - **Locally managed**: the system operates turn-by-turn, not by pre-arrangement — highly flexible and context-sensitive
  - **Interactionally managed**: turn allocation is a joint achievement — speakers and hearers collaborate to produce orderly transitions

### 1.3 Sequence Organization
- **Adjacency pair**: the basic unit of sequence — two turns by different speakers, produced adjacently, where the first pair part (FPP) makes a specific second pair part (SPP) conditionally relevant:
  - Greeting — Greeting: "Hi!" → "Hi!"
  - Question — Answer: "What time is it?" → "Three o'clock"
  - Invitation — Acceptance/Decline: "Want to come to dinner?" → "Sure!" / "Sorry, I can't"
  - Request — Grant/Refusal: "Can you pass the salt?" → "Here you go" / "Sorry, I don't see it"
  - **Conditional relevance**: if the FPP is produced, the SPP is *expected* — its absence is noticeable and accountable (if you ask a question and get no answer, you notice and may pursue one)

- **Preference organization**: when an FPP makes two or more alternative SPPs relevant (e.g., invitation → acceptance or decline), one is structurally **preferred** (not psychologically preferred, but *unmarked*):
  - **Preferred responses**: typically produced without delay, directly, and briefly (e.g., "Sure!")
  - **Dispreferred responses**: typically delayed, hedged, account-providing, and structurally complex (e.g., "Well, um, the thing is... I've already got plans... maybe next time?")
  - This asymmetry is systematic and cross-cultural — it minimizes threat to social solidarity

- **Pre-sequences**: utterances that precede the base adjacency pair and check preconditions:
  - Pre-invitation: "Are you doing anything tonight?" (checks availability before issuing invitation)
  - Pre-request: "Do you have a minute?" (checks availability before making request)
  - Pre-announcement: "You'll never guess what happened!" (projects an upcoming telling)

### 1.4 Repair
- **Repair**: the set of practices for dealing with problems of speaking, hearing, and understanding in conversation:
  - **Trouble source**: any element of talk that causes a problem (mishearing, misunderstanding, misstep, word search)
  - **Four types**:
    1. **Self-initiated self-repair**: speaker detects and fixes their own error ("Turn left — I mean, right")
    2. **Self-initiated other-repair**: speaker signals a problem that the other repairs
    3. **Other-initiated self-repair**: recipient signals a problem (e.g., "Huh?", "What?", "Who?") — the original speaker then repairs
    4. **Other-initiated other-repair**: recipient corrects the speaker ("You mean Thursday, not Wednesday")
  - **Preference for self-repair** (Schegloff, Jefferson & Sacks, 1977): the system is organized to maximize the opportunity for the speaker who produced the trouble source to repair it themselves — this minimizes the interactional delicacy of correcting another person

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Supported by Multiple Scholars / Strong Circumstantial Evidence)

### 2.1 Institutional Talk
- CA has been extensively applied to **institutional** settings, where the turn-taking system is typically modified:
  - **Doctor-patient interaction** (Heritage & Maynard, 2006): asymmetric turn allocation — doctors ask most questions, patients provide answers; diagnosis delivery follows specific sequential patterns
  - **Courtroom interaction**: strict turn allocation through a chairperson; question-answer format imposed; witnesses constrained in turn types
  - **Classroom discourse**: Mehan's **IRE/IRF** sequence (Initiation-Response-Evaluation/Feedback) — a hallmark of teacher-fronted classroom talk
  - **Emergency calls** (Zimmerman, 1992): highly structured openings, specific interrogative sequences, pre-allocated institutional roles
  - **News interviews** (Clayman & Heritage, 2002): interviewers ask questions, interviewees answer — departures from this are noticeable and accountable

### 2.2 Multimodal CA
- CA has expanded to include the analysis of **embodied action** — gaze, gesture, body posture, facial expression, object manipulation, and spatial positioning:
  - Goodwin (1981): showed that gaze direction is systematically related to turn-taking — speakers look away during turn beginnings and return gaze to recipients near turn completion
  - Mondada (2016): developed multimodal transcription conventions that integrate verbal, gestural, and material actions into a single analytic framework

### 2.3 CA and Technology-Mediated Interaction
- CA methods applied to mediated interaction: telephone calls (Schegloff, 1968, 1986 — foundational CA work used telephone data), video calls, text chat, voice-activated assistants, and human-robot interaction
- Reveals how turn-taking practices adapt to technological affordances (e.g., delay in video calls disrupts the precision timing of turn transitions; text messaging creates new sequential patterns without real-time temporal constraints)

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Limited Evidence / Emerging Hypotheses)

### 3.1 Universal Turn-Taking?
- Stivers et al. (2009) and Levinson & Torreira (2015) provide evidence that the ~200ms gap timing is cross-linguistically universal — potentially reflecting a cognitive/neural constraint (the time needed for language planning) — but whether the *social rules* (preference organization, repair preference) are also universal remains under investigation with increasing typological diversity

### 3.2 CA and Neuroscience
- Emerging research at the intersection of CA and neuroscience explores how the brain achieves the precise timing needed for turn-taking — predictive processing and motor planning are likely involved, but the neural mechanisms are not yet fully understood

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — Fringe / Not Supported by Evidence)

### 4.1 "Conversation Is Chaotic and Unorganized"
- Casual observation might suggest that everyday talk is disorderly. CA has conclusively demonstrated that conversation is an intricately organized social activity, governed by systematic practices that participants orient to with remarkable precision

### 4.2 "CA Only Describes Trivial Details"
- The fine-grained details that CA reveals (pauses, overlaps, repair initiations, intonation contours) are not trivial — they are the mechanisms through which social actions are accomplished. Understanding these details is essential for fields like medicine (interaction affects diagnosis and patient outcomes), law, and education
