## QUICK SUMMARY

**Lexicography** — the art and science of dictionary making — is among the oldest scholarly enterprises concerned with language, stretching from ancient Mesopotamian word lists (Sumerian-Akkadian bilingual glossaries, c. 2300 BCE) through monumental single-author dictionaries to today's corpus-driven, digitally delivered reference works. The first major English dictionary was **Robert Cawdrey's** *Table Alphabeticall* (1604), a "hard word" dictionary listing ~2,500 difficult terms. **Samuel Johnson's** *A Dictionary of the English Language* (1755) was the landmark achievement: compiled singlehandedly over nine years, it defined ~42,773 words with literary quotations illustrating usage — establishing the model of the authoritative, illustrative dictionary. Johnson's definitions combined precision and wit (famous examples: *lexicographer* — "a writer of dictionaries; a harmless drudge"; *oats* — "a grain which in England is generally given to horses, but in Scotland supports the people"). In America, **Noah Webster** produced *An American Dictionary of the English Language* (1828), which codified American spelling reforms (*color* for *colour*, *center* for *centre*, *defense* for *defence*) and established a national linguistic identity. The **Oxford English Dictionary (OED)**, conceived in 1857 by the Philological Society of London and published in fascicles from 1884 (first editor: **James Murray**), adopted the **historical principle**: tracing every word's history from its earliest recorded occurrence through successive changes in form and meaning, with dated quotations — the most comprehensive historical dictionary ever compiled (currently ~600,000 entries, 3.5 million quotations). **Peter Mark Roget's** *Thesaurus of English Words and Phrases* (1852) organized English vocabulary by concept rather than alphabet — a complementary approach to the dictionary. In the 20th century, **corpus-based lexicography** (pioneered by John Sinclair with **Collins COBUILD**, 1987) revolutionized dictionary making by using large electronic corpora of actual usage as the primary evidence base — replacing lexicographer intuition with empirical frequency, collocation, and register data. Modern lexicography is digital: online dictionaries (OED Online, Merriam-Webster.com, Wiktionary) provide instant search, hyperlinked cross-references, multimedia (pronunciation audio, images), regular updates, and user-generated content — transforming the dictionary from a fixed printed artifact into a continuously evolving digital resource.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Experimentally Confirmed)

### 1.1 Ancient and Medieval Lexicography
- **Earliest known word lists**: Sumerian-Akkadian bilingual glossaries from Mesopotamia (c. 2300 BCE) — lists of Sumerian words with Akkadian equivalents, organized by topic (animals, plants, professions, body parts)
- **Chinese lexicography**: *Erya* (c. 3rd century BCE) — the oldest surviving Chinese dictionary, organizing vocabulary by semantic categories. *Shuowen Jiezi* (100 CE, Xu Shen) — the first systematic analysis of Chinese character structure and etymology
- **Arabic lexicography**: *Kitab al-Ayn* (al-Khalil ibn Ahmad, 8th century) — the first comprehensive Arabic dictionary, ordered by phonetic principles; later Arabic dictionaries adopted alphabetical root-based ordering
- **Medieval European glossaries**: Latin-vernacular word lists; Robert Estienne's *Thesaurus Linguae Latinae* (1531) — the first great Latin dictionary

### 1.2 Major English Dictionaries
- **Robert Cawdrey**, *A Table Alphabeticall* (1604): the first English dictionary — ~2,500 "hard words" (Latinate and borrowed terms) with brief English definitions, aimed at "Ladies, Gentlewomen, or any other unskilfull persons"
- **Samuel Johnson**, *A Dictionary of the English Language* (1755):
  - ~42,773 headwords defined with ~114,000 illustrative quotations from English literature (Shakespeare, Milton, Dryden, Pope)
  - Combined prescriptive intent (Johnson aimed to "fix" the language) with descriptive practice (the quotations showed actual usage)
  - First English dictionary to use systematic illustrative quotation
  - Compiled by Johnson with six assistants over nine years — an extraordinary individual achievement
  - Remained the preeminent English dictionary for over a century

- **Noah Webster**, *An American Dictionary of the English Language* (1828):
  - ~70,000 entries — reflecting Webster's patriotic mission to establish an independent American English
  - Introduced American spelling reforms: dropped *-u-* from *colour* → *color*, *honour* → *honor*; changed *-re* to *-er* (*centre* → *center*); simplified *-ce* to *-se* (*defence* → *defense*)
  - Added American words and senses absent from British dictionaries
  - Company eventually became **Merriam-Webster** (1843+)

- **Oxford English Dictionary (OED)**:
  - Conceived 1857 by the **Philological Society of London** (Richard Chenevix Trench proposed a comprehensive dictionary on "historical principles")
  - First editor: **James Murray** (1879–1915) — worked from the Scriptorium at Oxford, coordinating thousands of volunteer readers who submitted quotation slips
  - Published in fascicles 1884–1928 (10 volumes + supplements); 2nd edition 1989 (20 volumes, ~615,000 entries); 3rd edition in progress (OED Online, continually revised, ~600,000+ entries)
  - **Historical principle**: every word traced from its earliest recorded occurrence, with dated quotations showing changes in form, meaning, and usage
  - ~3.5 million quotations spanning 1,000+ years of English
  - The most comprehensive historical dictionary of any language

### 1.3 Corpus-Based Lexicography
- **John Sinclair** and **Collins COBUILD** (*Collins Birmingham University International Language Database*), 1987:
  - First dictionary compiled primarily from corpus evidence — the Bank of English corpus (initially ~20 million words, eventually 500+ million)
  - Definitions written in full-sentence format reflecting how words actually occur in context
  - Frequency information, collocation patterns, and register guidance derived directly from corpus data
  - Influenced all subsequent learner dictionaries (LDOCE, OALD, MED)

- Modern dictionary publishers maintain large proprietary corpora and use corpus tools (concordancers, collocational statistics, frequency lists) as the primary basis for entry writing, sense division, and example selection

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Supported by Multiple Scholars / Strong Circumstantial Evidence)

### 2.1 Theoretical Lexicography
- Key debates in lexicographic theory:
  - **Prescriptive vs. descriptive**: Should dictionaries tell people how they *should* use language, or record how they *do* use it? Most modern linguistic dictionaries are descriptive, recording actual usage with labels (informal, slang, offensive, dated) rather than proscribing
  - **Lumping vs. splitting**: How many senses should a word entry distinguish? Sinclair's approach favored fewer, corpus-driven sense distinctions; traditional lexicography (OED) tends to split finely
  - **The entry as unit**: What constitutes a headword? (Lemma selection, treatment of multi-word units, phrasal verbs, idioms, compounds)

### 2.2 Learner Dictionaries
- Dictionaries designed for non-native speakers of English:
  - **Oxford Advanced Learner's Dictionary (OALD)** (A. S. Hornby, 1948): the first learner dictionary — used a controlled defining vocabulary and coded grammatical patterns
  - **Longman Dictionary of Contemporary English (LDOCE)** (1978): restricted defining vocabulary to 2,000 words
  - **Collins COBUILD** (1987): full-sentence definitions from corpus data
  - **Macmillan English Dictionary (MED)** (2002): corpus-based, with frequency stars indicating word commonness
  - These dictionaries provide pronunciation (IPA), example sentences, collocation information, usage notes, and cultural notes — optimized for language learning

### 2.3 Specialized Dictionaries and Thesauri
- **Peter Mark Roget**, *Thesaurus of English Words and Phrases* (1852): organized vocabulary by **concept** rather than alphabet — six classes (Abstract Relations, Space, Matter, Intellect, Volition, Affections) — enabling users to find the right word for a meaning rather than the meaning for a word
- Etymology dictionaries: tracing word histories (e.g., the *Oxford Dictionary of English Etymology*, 1966)
- Pronunciation dictionaries: e.g., Daniel Jones's *English Pronouncing Dictionary* (1917)
- Dialect dictionaries: e.g., Joseph Wright's *English Dialect Dictionary* (1898–1905)
- Slang dictionaries: e.g., Eric Partridge's *Dictionary of Slang and Unconventional English* (1937)

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Limited Evidence / Emerging Hypotheses)

### 3.1 The Future of Lexicography in the AI Era
- Will traditional dictionaries become obsolete as NLP tools (large language models, search engines, AI assistants) provide instant contextual definitions and usage examples?
- **Wiktionary** (2002+): collaboratively edited, multilingual, free dictionary — covers 7+ million entries across hundreds of languages. Questions about quality control and editorial standards vs. coverage and accessibility
- Whether LLMs will replace or complement human-curated dictionaries for language learners, translators, and linguists is an active question — LLMs hallucinate and lack the editorial rigor of professional lexicography

### 3.2 Non-Alphabetical Ordering and Digital Affordances
- Digital dictionaries need not follow alphabetical order — they can organize entries by semantic field, frequency, register, etymology, or user behavior
- Hyperlink networks, search suggestions, and algorithmic recommendations may reshape how dictionary content is accessed and structured

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — Fringe / Not Supported by Evidence)

### 4.1 "The Dictionary Is the Final Authority on Language"
- Dictionaries record language use; they do not determine it. Language is created by speakers/writers, and dictionaries follow. The authority of a dictionary rests on the quality of its evidence and editorial judgment, not on any inherent power to legislate usage

### 4.2 "If a Word Isn't in the Dictionary, It Isn't a Real Word"
- New words are created constantly through productive morphological and semantic processes. Dictionary inclusion follows coinage and adoption — it does not define legitimacy. Many established, widely used words enter major dictionaries years or decades after becoming common
