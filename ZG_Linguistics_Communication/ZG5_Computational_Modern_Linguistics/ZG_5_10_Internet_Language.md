## QUICK SUMMARY

**Internet language** — the varieties of written, spoken, and multimodal language shaped by digital communication technologies — represents one of the most rapid and widespread shifts in human communicative practice in history. From early Usenet newsgroups and IRC chat rooms through SMS texting, instant messaging, social media platforms, and AI chatbots, digital environments have fostered distinctive linguistic features: abbreviations (LOL, BRB, IMHO), creative respelling (u, 2nite, b4), hashtags, @-mentions, reaction GIFs, memes, and most notably **emoji** — a system of standardized pictographic characters originating from Japanese mobile phone culture in the 1990s (Shigetaka Kurita, NTT DoCoMo, 1999) that has expanded to over 3,600 characters (Unicode 15.1) and functions as a supplementary quasi-writing system conveying tone, affect, irony, and interpersonal stance in text-based communication. David Crystal's landmark *Language and the Internet* (2001, 2nd ed. 2006) identified internet language as a genuinely new communicative medium — neither speech nor writing but a hybrid with unique properties he termed **"Netspeak"**: it has the informality, spontaneity, and interactivity of conversation, combined with the permanence, visual channel, and revisability of writing. Research in **computer-mediated communication (CMC)** linguistics has shown that internet language is not "lazy" or "degraded" — rather, it is a highly creative, rule-governed domain where users display sophisticated sociolinguistic competence: code-switching between registers (formal email vs. casual DM), performing identity through linguistic choices, and innovating new pragmatic conventions (e.g., the "period of displeasure" — using a sentence-final period in text messages to signal coldness or passive aggression, documented by Klin et al. 2015).

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Experimentally Confirmed)

### 1.1 Computer-Mediated Communication (CMC) as a Linguistic Domain
- **CMC** (computer-mediated communication): the overarching term for human communication conducted via digital devices — includes:
  - **Synchronous CMC**: real-time interaction (chat, instant messaging, video calls)
  - **Asynchronous CMC**: non-real-time interaction (email, forums, social media posts, comments)
  - **One-to-one** (DMs, texts), **one-to-many** (posts, tweets), **many-to-many** (forums, group chats)

- **Susan Herring** (1996, 2004): pioneered the study of CMC as a distinct linguistic domain — developed the **Computer-Mediated Discourse Analysis (CMDA)** framework, analyzing language at structural (typography, morphology, syntax), meaning (semantics, pragmatics), interaction (turn-taking, topic management), and social (identity, community) levels

- **David Crystal** (2001, 2006): *Language and the Internet* — argued that internet language is a *fifth medium* alongside speaking, writing, signing, and print — characterized by special properties:
  - Lack of simultaneous feedback (no facial expressions, tone of voice)
  - Compensated by: emoticons, emoji, capitalization for emphasis, creative respelling, excessive punctuation/repetition ("noooo," "yesssss," "!!!!!")

### 1.2 Emoji: Origin and Function
- **Emoji** (Japanese: 絵文字 = "picture character"):
  - **Shigetaka Kurita** (1999): designed the first set of 176 emoji for NTT DoCoMo's i-mode mobile internet platform in Japan — simple 12×12 pixel pictograms for weather, emotions, traffic, and common objects, inspired by manga emoticons, weather symbols, and kanji
  - Preceded by **emoticons** — typed character sequences representing facial expressions: :-)  :-(  ;-)  :D — used in CMC since at least 1982 (Scott Fahlman's proposal on a Carnegie Mellon bulletin board)
  - **Unicode standardization** (2010): Emoji were added to the Unicode Standard (version 6.0), enabling cross-platform compatibility — the Unicode Consortium now manages emoji proposals, additions, and standardization
  - **Functions** (linguistic research):
    - **Affect/emotion marking**: conveying tone, mood, or emotional attitude (😊 = friendly; 😢 = sad)
    - **Pragmatic modification**: softening face-threatening acts, marking irony/sarcasm, signaling illocutionary force ("That's great 🙄" — sarcasm marker)
    - **Discourse management**: turn-closing, reaction/acknowledgment, phatic communion
    - **Identity expression**: skin tone modifiers (added Unicode 8.0, 2015), gender-inclusive emoji, cultural/national flag emoji

- **Frequency**: In 2023, approximately 10 billion emoji were used daily across messaging platforms worldwide (Emojipedia data). The most-used emoji consistently include 😂 (Face with Tears of Joy — Oxford Dictionaries' 2015 "Word of the Year"), ❤️, and 🤣

### 1.3 Linguistic Features of Internet Language
- **Abbreviations and acronyms**: LOL (laughing out loud), BRB (be right back), IMHO (in my humble opinion), TBH (to be honest), SMH (shaking my head) — these function as pragmatic particles and discourse markers, not merely abbreviations. **LOL** has been analyzed as having grammaticalized from a literal description of laughter to a discourse particle signaling amusement, lightness, or mitigation (Tagliamonte & Denis, 2008)

- **Creative respelling and letter-sound substitution**: "u" (you), "2" (to/too/two), "b4" (before), "gr8" (great) — most prevalent in SMS texting culture (mid-2000s peak), now declining in many platforms as full keyboards replaced T9 input

- **Hashtags** (#): emerged on Twitter (Chris Messina's 2007 proposal) — function as topical metadata, community markers, ironic commentary, and emphatic stance markers (e.g., #blessed, #NotAllMen)

- **The pragmatic period** (Klin et al., 2015): experimental study showed that text messages ending with a period (e.g., "Sure.") were rated as less sincere than the same messages without a period ("Sure") — the period has acquired a new pragmatic function in texting: signaling finality, curtness, or passive aggression. This demonstrates that punctuation norms in digital contexts diverge from print conventions

### 1.4 Internet Language and Literacy
- Research consistently shows that texting and internet language use does **not** damage conventional literacy skills:
  - **Plester, Wood & Joshi (2009)**: children who used more "textisms" (abbreviations, respellings) in text messages scored *higher* on standardized spelling and reading tests — because textism use requires phonological awareness and metalinguistic skill
  - **Crystal (2008)**, *Txtng: The Gr8 Db8*: systematically debunked claims that texting damages literacy — showed that texters are more literate than non-texters, and that creative respelling has a long history in English (from medieval manuscripts to Victorian postcards)

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Supported by Multiple Scholars / Strong Circumstantial Evidence)

### 2.1 Memes as Linguistic-Cultural Units
- **Internet memes** (Dawkins' original "meme" concept applied to digital culture by Limor Shifman, 2014): culturally shared, iteratively created content that spreads through copying with variation:
  - Function as intertextual discourse — participants display cultural competence by creating and interpreting meme formats
  - Memes carry ideological, political, and social commentary through layered semiotic codes (image + text + context + intertextual references)
  - Meme "literacy" is a form of digital cultural capital

### 2.2 Code-Switching in Digital Spaces
- Users routinely code-switch between registers in digital communication:
  - Formal register in work emails vs. informal register in friend group chats
  - Different platforms elicit different registers: LinkedIn (professional), Twitter/X (public-informal), Reddit (pseudonymous-vernacular), Snapchat (intimate-ephemeral)
  - Multilingual code-switching in digital spaces mirrors offline multilingual practice — users switch languages for audience design, identity marking, and topic management

### 2.3 Multimodality
- Digital communication is increasingly **multimodal** — combining text, images, video, audio, emoji, GIFs, stickers, voice messages, and reactions:
  - GIFs function as "gestural" supplements — conveying embodied reactions in a text-only medium
  - Voice messages add prosodic/tonal information unavailable in text
  - The shift from text-dominated to multimodal communication challenges traditional text-based linguistic analysis → requires multimodal discourse analysis frameworks (Kress & van Leeuwen)

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Limited Evidence / Emerging Hypotheses)

### 3.1 Emoji as an Emerging Writing System?
- Scholars have speculated that emoji could evolve into a full supplementary writing system — but current evidence suggests emoji function as **paralinguistic** or **pragmatic** supplements to text rather than as an independent meaning-bearing system:
  - Emoji sequences are ambiguous without accompanying text
  - No standardized grammar governs emoji ordering
  - Emoji meaning varies significantly by platform, culture, age, and context (🍑 and 🍆 have widely known secondary meanings)

### 3.2 AI-Generated Language and the Future of Digital Communication
- Large language models (ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude) are increasingly generating text in digital communication contexts — blurring the line between human-authored and machine-authored digital language. The long-term effects on internet language norms, writing styles, and communicative practice are unknown but actively researched

---

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — Fringe / Not Supported by Evidence)

### 4.1 "The Internet Is Destroying Language"
- Extensive empirical published evidence demonstrates no evidence that internet language degrades language skills, literacy, or cognitive function. Internet communication is an *additional* register — users who are most active online tend to be more, not less, linguistically skilled. Language change drove by digital contexts is natural sociolinguistic evolution, not decay

### 4.2 "Emoji Are Universal"
- While emoji are standardized in Unicode, their interpretation varies by culture, age group, gender, and platform — what signifies humor in one culture may signify confusion or offense in another. Emoji are not a universal language
