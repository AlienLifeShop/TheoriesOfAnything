## QUICK SUMMARY

**Pragmatics** is the study of how context contributes to meaning — how speakers use language to accomplish actions, how listeners infer intended meanings beyond what is literally said, and how the social, physical, and discursive context shapes the interpretation of utterances. While **semantics** studies the conventional (literal, context-independent) meanings of words and sentences, pragmatics studies meaning-in-use — the gap between what is said and what is communicated. The field was founded through three seminal contributions: **J.L. Austin's** (1962) **speech act theory** — the insight that utterances are not merely descriptions of the world (statements that are true or false) but **actions** performed with language: promising, requesting, apologizing, ordering, warning, christening, marrying, sentencing. Austin distinguished **locutionary acts** (the act of saying something meaningful), **illocutionary acts** (the force of the utterance — what action it performs: a promise, a request, a declaration), and **perlocutionary acts** (the effect on the hearer — persuading, frightening, amusing). His student **John Searle** (1969) systematized speech act theory, classifying illocutionary acts into five categories: **representatives** (assertions, claims), **directives** (requests, commands), **commissives** (promises, threats), **expressives** (thanks, apologies), and **declarations** (sentencing, christening — acts that change the world by being uttered). **H.P. Grice** (1975) proposed the **Cooperative Principle** — the idea that conversation operates on the assumption that participants are cooperating to communicate effectively — implemented through four **maxims**: **Quality** (be truthful), **Quantity** (be appropriately informative), **Relation** (be relevant), and **Manner** (be clear). Crucially, Grice showed that speakers often **flout** these maxims — deliberately violating them to generate **conversational implicatures** (meanings communicated through the gap between what is said and what is cooperatively expected). When someone asks "Can you pass the salt?" they are not inquiring about the hearer's physical ability — the literal meaning is flouted to communicate an **indirect speech act** (a request). **Relevance Theory** (Sperber & Wilson, 1986/1995) proposed a more cognitive framework, arguing that all pragmatic inference is driven by a single principle: humans automatically seek **maximal cognitive relevance** — the greatest contextual (cognitive) effect for the least processing effort. **Brown and Levinson's** (1987) **politeness theory** analyzed how speakers manage **face** (the public self-image that every person claims) through linguistic strategies — explaining why people use indirect speech, hedges, and elaborate politeness formulas to mitigate **face-threatening acts** (requests, criticisms, disagreements).

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Experimentally Confirmed)

### 1.1 Speech Act Theory
- **J.L. Austin** (1962, *How to Do Things with Words*):
  - Challenged the assumption that language's primary function is describing the world (making true/false statements)
  - **Performatives** vs. **constatives**: some utterances do not describe but **perform** actions — "I promise to pay you" (promising), "I name this ship the *Queen Elizabeth*" (naming), "I sentence you to 10 years" (sentencing). These are not true or false but **felicitous** or **infelicitous** (successful or unsuccessful)
  - **Felicity conditions**: for a speech act to succeed, specific conditions must be met — the speaker must have authority (only a judge can sentence), the circumstances must be appropriate (you can't christen a ship already christened), the act must be performed sincerely and correctly
  - Austin ultimately concluded that ALL utterances are speech acts — every utterance simultaneously has locutionary (meaning), illocutionary (force), and perlocutionary (effect) dimensions

- **John Searle** (1969, *Speech Acts*; 1979, *Expression and Meaning*):
  - Systematized Austin's framework: **five categories of illocutionary acts**:
    1. **Representatives/assertives**: commit the speaker to truth — asserting, claiming, reporting
    2. **Directives**: attempt to get the hearer to do something — requesting, commanding, advising
    3. **Commissives**: commit the speaker to future action — promising, threatening, offering
    4. **Expressives**: express the speaker's psychological state — thanking, apologizing, congratulating
    5. **Declarations**: bring about a change in the world — firing, sentencing, marrying, war-declaring
  - **Indirect speech acts**: utterances where the literal meaning differs from the intended force — "It's cold in here" (literally an assertion → indirectly a request to close the window). Conventional indirectness ("Could you pass the salt?" = request, not question about ability) is so routinized that it is processed as rapidly as direct speech

### 1.2 Grice's Cooperative Principle and Implicature
- **H.P. Grice** (1975, "Logic and Conversation"):
  - **Cooperative Principle**: "Make your conversational contribution such as is required, at the stage at which it occurs, by the accepted purpose or direction of the talk exchange in which you are engaged"
  - Four **maxims**:
    1. **Quality**: "Try to make your contribution one that is true" — do not say what you believe to be false; do not say that for which you lack evidence
    2. **Quantity**: "Make your contribution as informative as is required; do not make it more informative than required"
    3. **Relation** (Relevance): "Be relevant"
    4. **Manner**: "Be perspicuous" — avoid obscurity, ambiguity; be brief and orderly

  - **Conversational implicature**: arising when a speaker appears to violate (flout) a maxim — the hearer reasons that the speaker must be communicating something beyond the literal meaning:
    - Example: A: "How was the concert?" B: "Well, the venue was nice." → B implicates (by violating Quantity — not answering the actual question) that the concert itself was not good
    - **Scalar implicature**: "Some students passed" implicates "not all students passed" — *some* triggers the inference that the stronger term *all* does not apply
    - Implicatures are **cancelable** (can be explicitly denied: "Some students passed — in fact, all of them did") and **non-detachable** (the same implicature arises regardless of how the utterance is phrased, as long as the same content is expressed)

### 1.3 Relevance Theory
- **Dan Sperber and Deirdre Wilson** (1986/1995, *Relevance: Communication and Cognition*):
  - Replaced Grice's four maxims with a single cognitive principle:
    - **Communicative Principle of Relevance**: every act of ostensive communication (intentional signaling) communicates a presumption of its own optimal relevance — the addressee is entitled to expect sufficient cognitive effects for minimal processing effort
  - **Cognitive effects**: strengthening existing beliefs, contradicting them, or creating new contextual implications — the more effects, the more relevant
  - **Processing effort**: the less effort required to extract those effects, the more relevant
  - The addressee follows a path of least effort, testing interpretations in order of accessibility, and stops when an interpretation meets their expectations of relevance
  - Relevance theory provides a unified account of metaphor, irony, and loose talk — as cases where the most relevant interpretation is not the literal one

### 1.4 Deixis
- **Deixis** (from Greek *deiktikos* "pointing"): linguistic expressions whose interpretation depends on the context of utterance:
  - **Person deixis**: I, you, we, they — who is speaking, addressed, discussed
  - **Spatial deixis**: here, there, this, that — location relative to the speaker
  - **Temporal deixis**: now, then, yesterday, tomorrow — time relative to the utterance
  - **Social deixis**: formal/informal pronouns (French *tu/vous*, German *du/Sie*), honorifics, titles — encoding social relationships
  - **Discourse deixis**: this, that, the following — referring to parts of the ongoing discourse

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Supported by Multiple Scholars / Strong Circumstantial Evidence)

### 2.1 Politeness Theory
- **Brown and Levinson** (1987, *Politeness: Some Universals in Language Usage*):
  - **Face**: the public self-image every competent adult claims — comprising:
    - **Positive face**: the desire to be approved of, liked, respected
    - **Negative face**: the desire not to be imposed upon, to have freedom of action
  - **Face-threatening acts (FTAs)**: certain speech acts inherently threaten face — requests threaten negative face (imposing on the hearer), criticisms threaten positive face (disapproving of the hearer)
  - Speakers use **politeness strategies** to mitigate FTAs:
    1. **Bald on-record**: "Give me that" — direct, no mitigation (appropriate when urgency or social distance is minimal)
    2. **Positive politeness**: attend to the hearer's wants, use in-group markers, compliment — "Hey buddy, could you hand me that?"
    3. **Negative politeness**: be indirect, hedge, apologize for the imposition — "I'm sorry to bother you, but would it be possible for you to hand me that?"
    4. **Off-record**: hint, be vague — "It sure is hard to reach from here"
    5. **Don't do the FTA**: avoid the threatening act entirely
  - **Criticisms**: the framework has been critiqued for Western/individualistic bias; Japanese, Korean, and other East Asian politeness systems may operate on different principles (Ide, 1989: **discernment** — socially required politeness divorced from strategic choice); Watts (2003) argues for a discursive, practice-based approach

### 2.2 Presupposition
- **Presuppositions**: background assumptions triggered by specific linguistic expressions that are taken for granted (not asserted) in an utterance:
  - "John stopped smoking" presupposes that John used to smoke
  - "The king of France is bald" presupposes that there is a king of France (Russell's famous example)
  - "Have you stopped cheating?" (loaded question) presupposes that the addressee was cheating
  - Presuppositions typically survive under negation: "John didn't stop smoking" still presupposes he used to smoke
  - The **projection problem**: how presuppositions interact with complex sentence structures (negation, conditionals, disjunctions) is a major research area

### 2.3 Information Structure
- How speakers organize information in utterances to reflect what is **given** (already known) vs. **new** (the main informative contribution):
  - **Topic**: what the utterance is about (typically given information)
  - **Focus**: the most informative part (typically new information) — often marked by prosodic stress ("JOHN ate the cake" vs. "John ate the CAKE")
  - **Topic-comment** and **focus-background** structures vary cross-linguistically and interact with word order, morphology, and intonation

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Limited Evidence / Emerging Hypotheses)

### 3.1 Experimental Pragmatics
- A growing field applying psycholinguistic methods (reaction times, eye-tracking, ERPs, fMRI) to study pragmatic processing:
  - Early evidence suggests that **literal meaning is not always computed first** — in some contexts, the pragmatic (intended) meaning is accessed directly
  - Children are often "more logical" than adults in pragmatic tests — they interpret "some" literally (including "all") rather than pragmatically (excluding "all"), suggesting that scalar implicature requires pragmatic maturity
  - Whether pragmatic inference is fast and automatic or slow and effortful is debated — relevance theory predicts fast processing, while neo-Gricean approaches allow for slower computation

### 3.2 Pragmatics of Digital Communication
- How pragmatic principles apply to texting, social media, and AI communication — where tone of voice, facial expression, and shared physical context are absent — is an emerging area
  - Emoji, punctuation choices (period = finality/coldness), response timing, and read receipts function as pragmatic cues in digital contexts
  - Whether this represents new pragmatic conventions or adaptation of existing principles is under investigation

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — Fringe / Not Supported by Evidence)

### 4.1 "Literal Meaning" Is Always Computed First
- The traditional "standard pragmatic model" (Grice himself sometimes implied this) assumed that listeners first compute literal meaning, notice a maxim violation, and then inferentially derive the implicature. Research increasingly shows that pragmatic meaning can be computed **in parallel** with or even **before** literal meaning in many contexts

### 4.2 Grice's Maxims Are Universal Norms
- Grice's maxims describe tendencies, not universal rules. Different cultures, contexts, and individual styles systematically weight the maxims differently — some cultures value elaborate indirectness (flouting Manner for politeness); some value understatement (flouting Quantity); the maxims are not culture-free universal norms but useful analytical tools

## COUNTER-ARGUMENTS

- **Gricean vs. Relevance Theory**: **H.P. Grice's** framework of conversational maxims and implicature — the standard pragmatic theory — has been challenged by **Sperber and Wilson's** Relevance Theory (1986), which reduces Grice's multiple maxims to a single cognitive principle of relevance and argues that pragmatic inference is driven by the search for optimal relevance rather than by adherence to conversational norms. The debate has spawned neo-Gricean (**Levinson**, 2000) and post-Gricean approaches, with no consensus
- **Semantics-pragmatics boundary**: Where the boundary between semantically encoded meaning and pragmatically inferred meaning lies — and whether a sharp boundary exists at all — is contested. **Contextualists** (**Recanati**, **Carston**) argue that pragmatic processes penetrate what was traditionally considered semantic content, while **minimalists** (**Borg**, **Cappelen and Lepore**) defend a sharp semantics-pragmatics distinction
