## QUICK SUMMARY

**Metaphor theory** — the study of how figurative language works and what it reveals about human thought — underwent a revolutionary transformation in the late 20th century with the publication of **George Lakoff and Mark Johnson's** *Metaphors We Live By* (1980), which argued that metaphor is not merely a decorative literary device but a fundamental cognitive mechanism through which humans understand abstract concepts in terms of concrete, embodied experience. Their **Conceptual Metaphor Theory (CMT)** proposes that abstract thought is inherently metaphorical: we understand TIME in terms of SPACE ("the weeks ahead," "we're approaching the deadline"), ARGUMENT in terms of WAR ("defend your position," "attack the weak point"), LOVE in terms of JOURNEY ("we've come a long way," "we're at a crossroads"), and LIFE in terms of JOURNEY ("she's gone far," "he's at a crossroads"). Each conceptual metaphor is a systematic **mapping** from a **source domain** (concrete, bodily, familiar — space, motion, force, containers) to a **target domain** (abstract — time, emotions, causation, morality). These mappings are not arbitrary literary creations but deeply conventional, cross-linguistically widespread, and rooted in **primary metaphors** arising from universal bodily experiences (Grady, 1997): e.g., AFFECTION IS WARMTH (from the co-occurrence of being held close and feeling warm as an infant). A complementary theory — **Conceptual Integration (Blending) Theory** (Gilles Fauconnier and Mark Turner, *The Way We Think*, 2002) — offers a more dynamic model: meaning construction involves the real-time blending of elements from two or more mental spaces into a new "blended space" that has emergent structure not present in either input space. Blending theory accounts for creative, novel, and counterfactual metaphors that CMT's fixed mappings struggle with (e.g., "If Clinton were the Titanic, the iceberg would sink" — a blend that selects and compresses elements from both inputs to produce a novel meaning). The implications extend far beyond linguistics: metaphor documented evidence has demonstrated that metaphorical framing influences reasoning and decision-making (Thibodeau & Boroditsky, 2011 — "crime is a beast" vs. "crime is a virus" elicits different policy preferences), that metaphor is pervasive in science and law, and that understanding metaphor requires embodied, situated cognition — directly challenging the traditional view of thought as abstract, propositional, and language-independent.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Experimentally Confirmed)

### 1.1 Classical and Traditional Views
- **Aristotle** (*Poetics*, *Rhetoric*): defined metaphor as "giving a thing a name that belongs to something else" — a matter of linguistic substitution and comparison. Treated metaphor as a rhetorical/poetic device — part of style, not substance
- **Traditional "comparison" theory**: metaphor = implicit simile or abbreviated comparison ("Achilles is a lion" = "Achilles is *like* a lion in being fierce/brave")
- **"Substitution" theory**: metaphor replaces a literal expression that could be used instead — it is decorative, not necessary for meaning
- **Max Black (1962)**: "interaction theory" — metaphor creates meaning through the *interaction* of two subjects (the principal subject and the subsidiary subject), each filtering the other — moved beyond simple comparison/substitution

### 1.2 Conceptual Metaphor Theory (Lakoff & Johnson)
- **Core claim**: metaphor is primarily a matter of **thought**, not language — linguistic metaphors are surface expressions of underlying **conceptual metaphors** (systematic cross-domain mappings in the conceptual system):
  - **Source domain**: concrete, familiar, bodily (SPACE, MOTION, FORCE, CONTAINERS, OBJECTS, UP/DOWN, NEAR/FAR)
  - **Target domain**: abstract (TIME, EMOTION, CAUSATION, MORALITY, QUANTITY, STATUS, MIND)
  - **Mapping**: systematic correspondence from source to target (not random or arbitrary)

- **Major examples**:
  - **ARGUMENT IS WAR**: "Your claims are *indefensible*," "He *attacked* every weak point," "Her criticisms were right *on target*," "He *shot down* all my arguments"
  - **TIME IS MONEY**: "*Spend* time wisely," "Don't *waste* my time," "That's *worth* an hour," "I can't *afford* that time"
  - **LIFE IS A JOURNEY**: "He's *gone far* in life," "She's at a *crossroads*," "We've *arrived* at our goal," "There's no *turning back*"
  - **MORE IS UP / LESS IS DOWN**: "Prices *rose*," "Numbers *fell*," "Turn *up* the volume," "spirits are *low*"
  - **UNDERSTANDING IS SEEING**: "I *see* what you mean," "That's a *clear* explanation," "She was *blind* to the truth"

- **Invariance Hypothesis** (Lakoff, 1993): the image-schematic structure of the source domain is preserved in the mapping — you cannot map source relations that contradict the inherent structure of the target

- **Unidirectionality**: metaphorical mappings are typically asymmetric — we understand the abstract in terms of the concrete, not vice versa. We say "the future is *ahead*" (SPACE → TIME) but not "the front of the car is in the *future*"

### 1.3 Primary Metaphors and Embodiment
- **Joseph Grady (1997)**: proposed that complex conceptual metaphors are built from **primary metaphors** — minimal, universal mappings grounded in recurring correlations in bodily experience:
  - **AFFECTION IS WARMTH**: from the experience of being held (physical warmth + feeling of being loved)
  - **IMPORTANCE IS SIZE**: from the experience of large objects being harder to ignore/move
  - **DIFFICULTIES ARE BURDENS**: from the experience of carrying heavy objects
  - **KNOWING IS SEEING**: from the correlation of seeing something and having information about it
  - **INTIMACY IS CLOSENESS**: from the correlation of physical proximity and emotional closeness
- These primary metaphors combine to form complex metaphors: THEORIES ARE BUILDINGS = ORGANIZATION IS PHYSICAL STRUCTURE + PERSISTING IS REMAINING ERECT + etc.

### 1.4 Experimental Evidence
- **Thibodeau & Boroditsky (2011)**: participants who read that "crime is a *beast* ravaging the city" were more likely to recommend enforcement/punishment solutions; those who read "crime is a *virus* infecting the city" preferred social reform/prevention — metaphorical framing influenced reasoning even when participants were unaware of the metaphor's influence
- **Williams & Bargh (2008)**: participants holding a warm cup of coffee rated a person as having a "warmer" personality (more generous, caring) than those holding iced coffee — supporting AFFECTION IS WARMTH as a real cognitive mapping, not just a linguistic convention
- **Casasanto (2009)**: experimental evidence that spatial metaphors for time vary cross-linguistically but consistently reflect language-specific patterns — Mandarin speakers are more likely to use vertical (上/下 = up/down) metaphors for time than English speakers, and this affects their non-linguistic reasoning

### 1.5 Blending Theory (Fauconnier & Turner)
- **Conceptual Integration Theory** (Fauconnier & Turner, 2002, *The Way We Think*):
  - Meaning construction involves the dynamic, real-time blending of elements from two (or more) **input mental spaces** into a **blended space** (the blend):
    - **Input Space 1** + **Input Space 2** → **Generic Space** (shared abstract structure) + **Blended Space** (selective projection of elements from both inputs, plus emergent structure)
  - **Emergent structure**: the blend has properties not present in either input — creativity arises from this emergence
  - Example: "The surgeon is a butcher" — input 1: surgeon performing surgery; input 2: butcher cutting meat; blend: the surgeon operates with the lack of skill/care associated with butchery (the incompetence inference is emergent — it exists in neither input alone)
  - Example: "If Clinton were the Titanic, the iceberg would sink" — blends the Clinton-scandal frame and the Titanic-disaster frame, compressing and inverting relations to produce emergent meaning (Clinton is unsinkable)
  - Blending theory is more general than CMT — it accounts for analogy, counterfactuals, novel metaphors, composed metaphors, and non-metaphorical meaning construction

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Supported by Multiple Scholars / Strong Circumstantial Evidence)

### 2.1 Metonymy
- **Metonymy**: a figure of speech where one entity stands for a related entity (not based on similarity but on **contiguity** or association):
  - PRODUCER FOR PRODUCT: "I'm reading *Shakespeare*"
  - INSTITUTION FOR PEOPLE: "The *White House* declined to comment"
  - CONTAINER FOR CONTENTS: "Have a *glass*" (of wine)
- Lakoff & Johnson (1980): metonymy is also conceptual, not just linguistic, and interacts with metaphor — some expressions involve both (e.g., "lend me a *hand*" = HAND for HELP, both metonymic and metaphorical)

### 2.2 Metaphor in Science, Law, and Politics
- **Metaphor in science**: scientific models are often metaphorical — the "tree of life," "genetic code," "black holes," "wave-particle duality," "selfish gene" — these metaphors shape scientific reasoning, not just communication (Boyd, 1993; Brown, 2003)
- **Metaphor in law**: legal reasoning relies on metaphorical framing — "weighing" evidence, "balancing" rights, "bright-line" rules, "slippery slopes" — Winter (2001) argued that legal categories are often structured by metaphor and prototype
- **Metaphor in politics**: Lakoff's *Moral Politics* (1996) and *Don't Think of an Elephant!* (2004): argued that American political discourse is structured by two competing metaphorical models of the nation-as-family — the STRICT FATHER model (conservative) and the NURTURANT PARENT model (liberal)

### 2.3 Critiques and Alternatives
- **McGlone (2007)**: argued that many CMT claims are unfalsifiable — almost any linguistic expression can be post hoc analyzed as metaphorical; questions whether experimental evidence really supports "deep" cognitive metaphor or merely shows effects of contextual priming
- **Murphy (1996)**: questioned whether conceptual metaphors are stored as fixed mappings or are constructed online — much metaphor comprehension may be better explained by analogical reasoning without permanent mappings
- **Relevance Theory** (Sperber & Wilson; Carston): metaphor comprehension involves pragmatic inference (finding the optimally relevant interpretation), not activation of stored mappings — no special cognitive mechanism needed; metaphor lies on a continuum with "loose" language use

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Limited Evidence / Emerging Hypotheses)

### 3.1 Neural Substrate of Metaphor
- fMRI published findings demonstrate that metaphor processing activates both left-hemisphere language areas and right-hemisphere regions (particularly right inferior frontal gyrus and right temporal lobe) — suggesting metaphor involves additional processing beyond literal language (Rapp et al., 2004). However, the precise neural mechanisms are debated and may depend on whether the metaphor is conventional or novel

### 3.2 Metaphor and Culture
- Whether conceptual metaphors are universal (grounded in universal embodiment) or culturally variable is actively debated — evidence suggests that primary metaphors (WARMTH = AFFECTION, UP = MORE) are near-universal, but complex metaphors show significant cross-cultural variation (Kövecses, 2005)

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — Fringe / Not Supported by Evidence)

### 4.1 "Metaphor Is Just Decoration"
- The classical view that metaphor is merely ornamental — a poetic or rhetorical flourish that adds beauty but not meaning — is incompatible with extensive evidence that metaphorical framing affects reasoning, decision-making, and conceptualization even in purely non-literary contexts

### 4.2 "All Thought Is Metaphorical"
- While Lakoff and Johnson's claims are sometimes interpreted as "all abstract thought is metaphorical," this strong version is contested. Many abstract concepts (e.g., mathematical relations, logical operations) may be understood non-metaphorically, and the role of metaphor in expert scientific reasoning is debated

## COUNTER-ARGUMENTS

- **Conceptual Metaphor Theory critiques**: **Lakoff and Johnson's** Conceptual Metaphor Theory (*Metaphors We Live By*, 1980) has been challenged on empirical and theoretical grounds — **McGlone** (2007) argued that the experimental evidence for automatic metaphorical mappings is weaker than claimed; **Kövecses** (2005) noted that CMT struggles to explain the cultural specificity of many metaphors; and **Glucksberg** (2001) proposed that metaphors are processed as categorizations rather than mappings
- **Blending Theory as alternative**: **Fauconnier and Turner's** Conceptual Blending Theory (2002) offers a more general cognitive framework that subsumes metaphor as a special case of cross-domain integration — proponents argue it handles novel and complex metaphors better than CMT, while CMT defenders argue blending theory lacks the predictive constraints of source-target domain mappings
