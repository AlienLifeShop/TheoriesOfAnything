## QUICK SUMMARY

**Linguistic typology** is the systematic study of structural similarities and differences across the world's languages — asking what properties are **universal** (shared by all or nearly all languages), what properties are **variable** (differing between languages in patterned ways), and what **constraints** limit the range of possible human languages. The field was transformed by **Joseph Greenberg** (1963, "Some Universals of Grammar with Particular Reference to the Order of Meaningful Elements"), who examined a sample of 30 genetically and geographically diverse languages and proposed **45 universals** — many in the form of **implicational universals** (if a language has property X, it will also tend to have property Y). For example: Greenberg's **Universal 3** — "Languages with dominant VSO order are always prepositional" — and **Universal 4** — "Languages with normal SOV order are overwhelmingly postpositional." These correlations between word order features (verb-object order predicting preposition/postposition order, noun-adjective order, noun-genitive order, etc.) revealed that languages form internally consistent typological clusters. The most investigated typological dimension is **basic word order** — the relative order of Subject (S), Verb (V), and Object (O) in a simple declarative transitive clause: **SOV** (Subject-Object-Verb: Japanese, Turkish, Hindi, Korean — the most common type, ~45% of languages) and **SVO** (Subject-Verb-Object: English, Mandarin, French, Swahili — ~35%) together account for ~80% of all languages; **VSO** (Verb-Subject-Object: Irish, Arabic, Tagalog — ~10%) is third; while **VOS**, **OVS**, and **OSV** are rare but attested. **Morphological typology** classifies languages along a continuum from **isolating** (one morpheme per word, little affixation: Vietnamese, Mandarin) through **agglutinative** (words built from clearly separable morphemes: Turkish, Swahili, Hungarian) to **fusional** (morphemes merged/fused with multiple meanings per affix: Latin, Russian, Arabic) and **polysynthetic** (extremely complex words incorporating what would be entire sentences in other languages: Mohawk, Yupik, Chukchi). Modern typology, vastly expanded through resources like the **World Atlas of Language Structures (WALS)** (Haspelmath et al., 2005 — a database of 2,678 languages coded for 192 structural features), now covers phonological universals (nearly all languages have /a, i, u/ vowels; all have voiceless stops; nasal vowels imply oral vowels), syntactic universals, morphological systems, and semantic/pragmatic patterns — providing the empirical foundation for debates about the nature of human language, the role of cognition versus culture in shaping grammar, and the extent to which linguistic diversity is constrained by universal properties of the human mind.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Experimentally Confirmed)

### 1.1 Greenberg's Word Order Universals
- **Joseph Greenberg** (1963) pioneered the modern study of language universals:
  - Used a genetically and areally balanced sample of 30 languages
  - Proposed 45 universals, many **implicational** (if P, then Q):
    - **Universal 1**: "In declarative sentences with nominal subject and object, the dominant order is almost always one in which the subject precedes the object" — S before O is virtually universal (~97% of languages)
    - **Universal 2**: "In languages with prepositions, the genitive almost always follows the governing noun, while in languages with postpositions it almost always precedes"
    - **Universal 3**: "Languages with dominant VSO order are always prepositional"
    - **Universal 4**: "With overwhelmingly greater than chance frequency, languages with normal SOV order are postpositional"
    - **Universal 20**: "When any or all of the items (demonstrative, numeral, descriptive adjective) precede the noun, they are always in that order; when any or all follow, they are in the reverse order, or a modification of it"

- **Word order distribution** (Dryer, 2013, WALS data):
  - **SOV**: ~45% (565 languages in WALS sample) — Japanese, Korean, Turkish, Hindi, Basque, Quechua
  - **SVO**: ~35% (488 languages) — English, Mandarin, French, Swahili, Russian
  - **VSO**: ~9% (95 languages) — Irish, Welsh, Arabic, Hawaiian, Tagalog
  - **VOS**: ~3% (25 languages) — Fijian, Malagasy, some Mayan
  - **OVS**: ~1% (11 languages) — Hixkaryana, some Cariban languages
  - **OSV**: rare (<1%, ~4 languages) — Warao, some Amazonian languages
  - The strong preference for S before O reflects cognitive principles (agent before patient)

### 1.2 Morphological Typology
- The classic classification (originally 19th century: Schlegel, Humboldt, Sapir):
  - **Isolating (analytic)**: words tend to be single morphemes; grammatical relations expressed through word order and separate particles — Mandarin Chinese, Vietnamese, Thai
  - **Agglutinative**: words built by stringing clearly identifiable morphemes together — each morpheme carries one meaning, boundaries are transparent — Turkish (*ev-ler-im-de* "in my houses": house-PL-1SG.POSS-LOC), Swahili, Hungarian, Finnish, Japanese
  - **Fusional (inflectional)**: morphemes fused together — each affix may carry multiple meanings simultaneously, boundaries are opaque — Latin (*amō* = am- root + -ō 1SG.PRES.IND.ACT — a single suffix encodes person, number, tense, mood, voice), Russian, Arabic
  - **Polysynthetic**: extremely complex words incorporating multiple roots and affixes — a single word may express what English requires an entire sentence for — Mohawk (*Washakotya'tawitsherahetkvhta'se* ≈ "he made the thing that one puts on one's body ugly for her"), Yupik, Chukchi

- Modern view: these are **continua**, not discrete types — most languages show a mix of strategies, and a single language may be isolating in one domain and agglutinative in another

### 1.3 Phonological Universals
- Extensively documented patterns across the world's languages:
  - **Vowels**: virtually all languages have /a/, /i/, /u/ (the maximally dispersed vowel triangle). The most common vowel system is 5-vowel /a, e, i, o, u/ (appearing in ~30% of languages). No language has only one vowel
  - **Consonants**: all languages have at least one stop consonant; all have at least one nasal; all have /t/ or a close equivalent. The most common consonant inventory size is 20–25 segments. Range: Rotokas (11 consonants) to ǃXóõ (~115+ consonants, including extensive click inventory)
  - **Syllable structure**: all languages allow CV (consonant-vowel) syllables; not all allow complex onsets (CCV) or codas (CVC). If a language allows complex codas, it allows simple codas
  - **Implicational universals**: if a language has voiced fricatives, it has voiceless fricatives; if it has nasal vowels, it has oral vowels; if it has affricates, it has fricatives; if it has ejectives, it has plain stops

### 1.4 WALS and Large-Scale Typology
- **World Atlas of Language Structures (WALS)** (Haspelmath et al., 2005; continuously updated online at wals.info):
  - **2,678 languages** coded for **192 structural features** (phonological, morphological, syntactic, lexical)
  - Each feature is mapped geographically, revealing both universal tendencies and **areal patterns** (features shared by geographically proximate but genetically unrelated languages — suggesting contact or shared environmental/cultural pressures)
  - Has become the standard reference for cross-linguistic comparison and the empirical backbone of typological research

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Supported by Multiple Scholars / Strong Circumstantial Evidence)

### 2.1 Functional Explanations for Universals
- Why do languages show the patterns they do?
  - **Processing efficiency**: word orders may reflect cognitive preferences for ease of production and comprehension — e.g., the universal preference for S before O may reflect a cognitive tendency to mention agents before patients
  - **Harmony principles** (Dryer, 1992): the correlation between verb-object order and adposition order (prepositions with VO, postpositions with OV) may reflect a preference for consistent head-direction in phrase structure (head-initial or head-final)
  - **Frequency and economy**: frequently used distinctions tend to be expressed by shorter/simpler forms (Zipf's Law); rarely needed distinctions are expressed periphrastically or not at all
  - **Learnability**: language structures that are easier for children to acquire may be favored in language change, creating evolutionary pressures toward typologically common patterns

### 2.2 Areal Typology (Sprachbund)
- Languages in geographic contact often share structural features regardless of genetic relationship — **Sprachbund** (linguistic area):
  - **Balkan Sprachbund**: Romanian, Bulgarian, Albanian, Greek, Macedonian, and others share features (postposed definite article, loss of infinitive, future with "will"-derived aux, case merger) despite belonging to different IE branches
  - **Mainland Southeast Asian area**: Vietnamese, Thai, Mandarin, Khmer — tonal, isolating, SVO, serializing tendencies
  - **Ethiopia/Horn of Africa**: Amharic (Semitic), Oromo (Cushitic), and Nilo-Saharan languages share features including SOV order, converb constructions, and ideophones
  - Areal typology reveals that both genetic inheritance and contact influence typological patterns

### 2.3 Semantic and Pragmatic Universals
- Beyond phonology, morphology, and syntax, typologists study universal patterns in meaning:
  - **Color term universals** (Berlin & Kay, 1969): languages acquire basic color terms in a roughly predictable order
  - **Kinship systems**: limited number of kinship classification types (Murdock's typology, Greenberg's universals)
  - **Metaphoric mappings**: consistent cross-linguistic patterns in metaphor (UP = MORE/GOOD; CONTAINER = CATEGORIES; JOURNEY = LIFE) — possibly reflecting embodied cognition

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Limited Evidence / Emerging Hypotheses)

### 3.1 Why Are Some Types Rare?
- OVS and OSV word orders are extremely rare — why?
  - The most common explanation: cognitive preference for agent (S) before patient (O) is near-universal, making O-before-S orders marked/unusual
  - Researchers suggest that rare types may be diachronically unstable — they arise through language change but quickly shift toward more common types
  - Whether there are **absolute** universals (properties shared by literally all languages with no exceptions) or only very strong statistical tendencies remains debated — proposed "absolute universals" sometimes encounter counterexamples as more languages are studied

### 3.2 Evolutionary Typology
- An emerging approach applies evolutionary and computational methods (phylogenetic trees, Bayesian inference) to typological data:
  - Analyzing how typological features change over time along language family trees
  - Testing whether certain feature combinations are stable or transient
  - Whether typological distributions reflect functional adaptation, historical accident, or both — this is a frontier question

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — Fringe / Not Supported by Evidence)

### 4.1 Morphological Types Reflect Cultural Advancement
- 19th-century linguists (Schlegel, Schleicher) ranked morphological types in an evolutionary sequence — with fusional (European) languages at the top. This is scientifically baseless: all morphological types are fully functional, and languages regularly change from one type to another (Latin's fusional system became French's more analytic/isolating system)

### 4.2 SOV Is the "Original" Human Word Order
- While SOV is the most common type and may have been more common in earlier reconstructable stages of some families, the claim that all human languages were originally SOV is not demonstrable. Word order changes frequently in language evolution, and there is no basis for claiming a single original word order for all human language

## COUNTER-ARGUMENTS

- **Nature of language universals**: **Joseph Greenberg's** absolute and implicational universals have been challenged from multiple directions — **Matthew Dryer** (1998) argued that many proposed universals are better characterized as statistical tendencies across language families; **Martin Haspelmath** has questioned whether cross-linguistic "categories" (e.g., "noun," "passive") are comparable enough across unrelated languages to support universal claims; and **Nicholas Evans and Stephen Levinson** (*The Myth of Language Universals*, 2009) argued that linguistic diversity is far greater than universalist frameworks acknowledge
- **Functional vs. formal explanations**: Whether typological patterns reflect innate grammatical constraints (Chomskyan UG) or functional pressures of communication, processing, and acquisition remains a fundamental divide — **Newmeyer** (1998) defended formal explanations while **Bybee**, **Croft**, and **Haspelmath** have argued for usage-based and functional accounts
