## QUICK SUMMARY

Cuneiform — from Latin *cuneus* ("wedge") — is the earliest known writing system, invented in southern Mesopotamia (modern Iraq) by the Sumerians circa 3400–3100 BCE in the city of Uruk. It began as a system of pictographic notations on clay tablets used for administrative accounting (recording commodities, labor, and livestock) and evolved over approximately 800 years into a full phonetic writing system capable of expressing any utterance in Sumerian, Akkadian, Hittite, Elamite, Hurrian, Urartian, and Old Persian — ultimately serving as the primary medium of literate civilization across the ancient Near East for over three millennia until its extinction around the 1st century CE. The system's origins have been traced to even earlier clay token systems used for accounting from ~8000 BCE (Schmandt-Besserat 1992), and its decipherment in the 19th century by Rawlinson, Hincks, and Oppert opened the entire ancient Mesopotamian literary, legal, and scientific corpus to modern scholarship.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Experimentally Confirmed)

### 1.1 Origin and Chronology
- The earliest known cuneiform tablets come from **Uruk Level IV** (~3400–3100 BCE) — approximately 5,000 proto-cuneiform tablets recording administrative transactions (grain, livestock, beer rations, land measurements, labor quotients) were recovered from the Eanna precinct (Nissen, Damerow & Englund 1993, *Archaic Bookkeeping*)
- Proto-cuneiform used ~1,200 distinct signs, most pictographic (a head of grain for "barley," a bowl for "food/eat") combined with numerical notation systems — it was **not yet phonetic** and cannot be "read aloud" as a specific language (Englund 1998)
- By ~2600 BCE (Early Dynastic IIIa), cuneiform had evolved into a mixed logographic-syllabic system fully capable of recording the Sumerian language, including the first literary texts — the Abu Salabikh corpus contains the earliest literary compositions, including the *Instructions of Shuruppak*

### 1.2 The Token Theory of Writing Origins
- **Denise Schmandt-Besserat (1992, *Before Writing*)** proposed that cuneiform evolved from a system of small clay tokens (spheres, cones, discs, tetrahedra) used for accounting from ~8000 BCE across the ancient Near East — tokens represented specific commodities; they were sealed inside clay **bullae** (hollow envelopes) for record-keeping; envelope markings replicating the enclosed tokens became external notations; these notations evolved into proto-cuneiform signs
- The token-to-tablet transition is archaeologically documented: bullae containing tokens with surface impressions matching the contents have been found at Susa, Uruk, Habuba Kabira, and other sites (Schmandt-Besserat 1992; Damerow 1999)
- **Critique**: Michalowski (1993) and Englund (1998) have questioned the one-to-one correspondence between tokens and proto-cuneiform signs; the relationship may be more complex than a direct evolutionary sequence

### 1.3 Physical Medium and Technique
- Cuneiform was impressed onto **wet clay tablets** using a cut reed stylus (a *calamus*) held at an angle — the triangular cross-section of the reed produced the characteristic wedge-shaped impressions
- Tablets ranged from ~2 cm (small receipts) to ~30+ cm (literary, legal, and scholarly texts); some multicolumn tablets reached enormous size (the Weld-Blundell prism, Ashmolean Museum, is 20 cm tall with 4 columns × 8 faces)
- Clay tablets are remarkably **durable** — paradoxically, destruction of archives by fire (as at Ebla, Nineveh, and Hattusa) baked the tablets, preserving them for millennia. Hundreds of thousands of cuneiform tablets survive in museum collections worldwide

### 1.4 Decipherment
- **Henry Rawlinson** (1835–1846) copied the trilingual **Behistun Inscription** (Old Persian, Elamite, Babylonian) on a cliff face in western Iran — Rawlinson, Edward Hincks (Ireland), Jules Oppert (France), and William Henry Fox Talbot independently deciphered Babylonian cuneiform by 1857, confirmed by the Royal Asiatic Society's famous test (all four scholars produced acceptably similar translations of an unpublished inscription)
- Georg Friedrich Grotefend (1802) had earlier partially deciphered Old Persian cuneiform using king-name patterns, though his work was initially ignored

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Independent Invention vs. Stimulus Diffusion
- The relationship between Sumerian cuneiform (~3400 BCE) and Egyptian hieroglyphics (~3200 BCE) is debated: scholars argue Egypt independently invented writing, while others propose **stimulus diffusion** — the idea of writing traveled from Mesopotamia to Egypt (via trade contacts), but Egyptians invented their own entirely different system (Baines 2004; → [ZG_1_03](ZG_1_03_Egyptian_Hieroglyphics.md))
- Most current scholarship favors limited stimulus diffusion: the *concept* of using visual marks for record-keeping may have spread, but the specific sign systems are independent inventions

### 2.2 Scribal Education and Literacy Rates
- Scribal schools (*é-dub-ba-a*, "tablet house") trained specialists through a curriculum of sign lists, vocabulary exercises, mathematical problems, and literary copying — the curriculum is well-attested from thousands of school exercise tablets (Veldhuis 1997; Robson 2001)
- Literacy was restricted to a small professional class — estimates suggest **1–5%** of the Mesopotamian population was literate at any given time (Civil 1975; Charpin 2010), though the definition of "literate" is contested (could more people read than write?)
- The **Ebla archive** (~2400 BCE, Syria) contained ~1,800 cuneiform tablets revealing a sophisticated bureaucratic state using cuneiform to write Eblaite (a Semitic language), demonstrating cuneiform's spread beyond Sumer

### 2.3 Cuneiform as Multi-Language Script
- Over its 3,000-year lifespan, cuneiform was adapted to write at least **15 languages** across multiple language families: Sumerian (language isolate), Akkadian/Babylonian/Assyrian (Semitic), Elamite (isolate or Elamo-Dravidian?), Hittite and Luwian (Indo-European), Hurrian and Urartian (Hurro-Urartian), Old Persian (Indo-European, simplified cuneiform), and others
- This adaptability required significant modification: Sumerian cuneiform used ~600 signs combining logograms and syllabograms; each adaptation reduced or modified the sign inventory for the target language

### 2.4 The Library of Ashurbanipal
- **Ashurbanipal** (r. 668–627 BCE) assembled at Nineveh the largest known cuneiform library: ~30,000 tablets and fragments covering literature, science, medicine, divination, lexicography, and correspondence — the primary source for the standard Babylonian version of the *Epic of Gilgamesh* (→ [A_1_04](../../A_Foundations/A1_Mesopotamian_Near_Eastern/A_1_04_Enki_Enlil_and_Sumerian_Leaders.md))
- Ashurbanipal's collecting represented a deliberate program of knowledge preservation — his colophons state he personally could read Sumerian and Akkadian, an unusual claim for a king

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Pre-Uruk Antecedents
- Scholars propose that **tally marks** and geometric signs on artifacts from sites older than Uruk (e.g., the ~7000 BCE tokens at Jarmo, the Tartaria tablets from Romania ~5300 BCE) represent proto-writing systems that may have contributed to cuneiform's development — but continuity is undemonstrated (Robinson 2007)
- The Tartaria tablets' resemblance to Sumerian signs was initially striking but is now considered coincidental by most specialists

### 3.2 Cognitive Revolution and Externalized Memory
- **Malafouris (2013, *How Things Shape the Mind*)** and other material engagement theorists argue that the invention of writing was not merely a tool for recording thought but fundamentally **altered human cognition** — externalized memory enabled new forms of reasoning, abstraction, and social complexity that were cognitively impossible without material inscription
- While plausible and increasingly supported by cognitive archaeology, this claim remains difficult to test empirically

### 3.3 Musical Notation in Cuneiform
- The **Hurrian Hymn No. 6** from Ugarit (~1400 BCE) — inscribed in cuneiform — is the oldest known substantially complete notated musical composition, using interval names to specify tuning and melodic structure (Kilmer 1974; West 1994)
- Interpretation of the notation remains debated: multiple competing transcriptions exist, and whether the notation prescribes specific pitches or only intervals is unresolved

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Cuneiform as Alien Communication
- **[DEBUNKED]** Claims that cuneiform was gifted to humanity by extraterrestrial beings (e.g., Sitchin 1976, *The 12th Planet*) are contradicted by the clear archaeological evidence of gradual development from tokens through pictographs to abstract signs over millennia — there is no discontinuity requiring an external explanation

### 4.2 "Overnight" Invention of Writing
- **[DEBUNKED]** The claim that writing appeared suddenly with no antecedents is refuted by the token/bulla evidence and by the gradual evolution of proto-cuneiform sign complexity over several centuries at Uruk (Nissen et al. 1993)

### 4.3 Universal Decipherment Claims
- **[DEBUNKED]** Claims that cuneiform tablets contain knowledge of advanced technology (nuclear weapons, space travel, genetic engineering) are based on mistranslations and selective readings — professional Assyriologists reject these interpretations as linguistically unsupported (Heimpel 2002; Michalowski 2006)

## COUNTER-ARGUMENTS & CRITICISMS

- Schmandt-Besserat's token theory, while influential, has been criticized for overstating the one-to-one link between tokens and specific proto-cuneiform signs (Englund 1998; Zimansky 1993)
- Literacy rate estimates are highly uncertain — the distinction between reading and writing ability, and between functional literacy and full scholarly competence, complicates all estimates
- The "priority debate" (whether Sumerian or Egyptian writing came first) may be moot if both systems were independently invented — recent discoveries at Abydos (Dreyer 1998) push Egyptian writing closer to Sumerian dates
