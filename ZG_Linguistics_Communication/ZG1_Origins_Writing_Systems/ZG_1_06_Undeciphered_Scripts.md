## QUICK SUMMARY

Despite two centuries of decipherment successes — from Egyptian hieroglyphics to Linear B to Mayan glyphs — several ancient scripts remain undeciphered, each presenting unique challenges that have resisted sustained scholarly effort. The most prominent include: the **Indus (Harappan) script** (~2600–1900 BCE, ~4,200 inscribed objects, average text length ~5 signs, underlying language unknown, possibly Dravidian); **Rongorongo** (~Easter Island, pre-1864, ~26 surviving artifacts inscribed in reverse boustrophedon, language likely Rapanui, corpus too small for standard decipherment methods); **Proto-Elamite** (~3100–2900 BCE, Iran, ~1,600 tablets, partially understood numerals but underlying language unknown); **Linear A** (~1800–1450 BCE, Crete, shares sign forms with deciphered Linear B but records an unknown Minoan language); and the **Phaistos Disc** (a unique stamped clay disc from Crete with ~242 sign tokens in 45 distinct types, possibly a forgery or unique document). These scripts collectively illustrate the limits of decipherment methodology: successful reading requires a decipherable corpus size, a known or relatable language, and ideally bilingual texts — conditions that none of these scripts fully satisfy.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Experimentally Confirmed)

### 1.1 The Indus (Harappan) Script
- The Indus script is attested on ~4,200 inscribed objects (primarily steatite seals, tablets, pottery, copper tools, and ivory objects) from sites across the Indus Valley civilization (~2600–1900 BCE), including Mohenjo-daro, Harappa, Dholavira, Lothal, and Kalibangan
- The sign inventory comprises **~400–450 distinct signs** — too many for an alphabet (~20–30 signs), possibly a logosyllabic system (Parpola 1994; Mahadevan 1977)
- **Texts are extremely short**: average length ~5 signs; the longest known text (a three-sided seal from Dholavira) contains only ~26 signs — this small corpus size is the primary obstacle to decipherment
- No **bilingual inscription** has been found — the script disappeared when the Indus civilization declined (~1900 BCE), and no successor script or tradition of reading survived

### 1.2 Rongorongo
- **Rongorongo** is a system of glyphs found on ~26 surviving wooden artifacts from **Rapa Nui (Easter Island)**, first reported by Europeans in 1864 — all surviving tablets and objects are now in museum collections worldwide
- The script employs **~120 basic signs** (with ~600 allographic variants) written in **reverse boustrophedon**: lines alternate direction, and the reader rotates the tablet 180° at each line end (Fischer 1997)
- No Rapanui islander could read the tablets when European missionaries inquired in the late 19th century — the tradition of reading had been lost, likely due to population collapse from European-introduced diseases and Peruvian slave raids (1862)
- The corpus is too small for standard statistical decipherment methods — no bilingual texts exist, and the script may represent a limited recording system (genealogies, chants, calendars) rather than full-text writing

### 1.3 Proto-Elamite
- **Proto-Elamite** is attested on ~1,600 clay tablets from Susa, Tepe Yahya, Malyan, and other Iranian sites (~3100–2900 BCE) — making it nearly contemporary with Sumerian proto-cuneiform
- The **numerical system** has been partly deciphered (Damerow & Englund 1989): multiple counting systems for different commodity types are identifiable, showing that Proto-Elamite tablets are primarily administrative/accounting documents
- The **non-numerical signs** (~1,000 distinct types) remain undeciphered — the underlying language is unknown (possibly an ancestor of historical Elamite, possibly a different language entirely)
- The corpus was published by the CDLI (Cuneiform Digital Library Initiative), enabling computational analysis

### 1.4 Linear A
- **Linear A** — the Minoan palace script used on Crete and surrounding islands (~1800–1450 BCE) — shares many sign forms with Linear B, which Ventris deciphered as Mycenaean Greek in 1952
- Applying Linear B phonetic values to Linear A signs produces **non-Greek** words — the underlying **Minoan language** is unknown, with proposed identifications including Semitic (Gordon 1966), Luwian (Palmer 1965), and a pre-IE isolate (most scholars)
- The corpus contains ~1,400 inscriptions, mostly on clay tablets (accounting contexts similar to Linear B) plus libation tables, pottery, and a few stone inscriptions — larger than Rongorongo but insufficient for standalone decipherment without knowledge of the language

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 The "Farmer Debate" on the Indus Script
- **Farmer, Sproat & Witzel (2004)** controversially proposed that the Indus "script" is **not writing** at all — arguing that the short text lengths, limited sign combinations, and lack of longer texts indicate a non-linguistic symbol system (comparable to heraldic emblems, potters' marks, or religious insignia)
- **Parpola (2009)**, Mahadevan, and others vigorously dispute this, pointing to: consistent directional reading order, sign-ordering regularities consistent with linguistic structure, numerical signs integrated with non-numerical signs, and statistical properties resembling other writing systems (Rao et al. 2009)
- The debate remains unresolved — the "entropy" argument (Rao et al.) has been criticized by Sproat (2014) as insufficiently discriminating between linguistic and non-linguistic systems

### 2.2 Proposed Indus–Dravidian Connection
- **Asko Parpola** (1994, *Deciphering the Indus Script*) has spent decades arguing that the Indus script records an early **Dravidian** language — based on: Dravidian language family's presence in the region (Brahui in Baluchistan), proposed readings of common sign sequences as Dravidian words (e.g., the "fish" sign as *mīn*, homophonous with "star" in Dravidian languages, appearing with counted marks that might represent celestial bodies)
- While Parpola's hypothesis is the most detailed and internally consistent proposal, it has not achieved consensus — the short text lengths make confirmation impossible without new evidence (longer texts or bilinguals)

### 2.3 Rongorongo and Proto-Rapanui
- **Steven Roger Fischer (1997)** proposed a partial decipherment based on a single chanted text (the "Santiago Staff" recitation recorded in 1886), arguing that Rongorongo texts are structured around copulation triads representing genealogical/cosmogonic narratives — this interpretation has not been accepted by most specialists (Pozdniakov & Pozdniakov 2007)
- Most scholars agree the script likely records the **Rapanui language** (an Eastern Polynesian language), but the size and nature of the corpus may make full decipherment permanently impossible

### 2.4 The Phaistos Disc
- A unique fired clay disc (~15 cm diameter) from the Minoan palace at Phaistos, Crete (~1700 BCE), bearing 242 sign tokens of 45 distinct types stamped in a spiral pattern on both sides
- Its uniqueness (no other objects with these signs have been found) makes decipherment virtually impossible — the sign inventory is too small for statistical analysis, and the lack of any comparable corpus eliminates the possibility of pattern recognition
- Scholars (Duhoux 2008; Owens 2014) have proposed partial readings; others have questioned its authenticity entirely (Eisenberg 2008), though most Minoan specialists accept it as genuine

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Computational Breakthroughs
- Machine learning and AI approaches have been proposed for undeciphered scripts — Luo et al. (2019) demonstrated neural decipherment methods on known scripts (Linear B, Ugaritic), but application to truly unknown scripts is constrained by the need for a related known language as anchor
- Computational approaches may narrow the space of possible readings or identify structural features, but are unlikely to achieve standalone decipherment without additional data

### 3.2 Indus Sign Board at Dholavira
- A large sign board (~3 m wide) at Dholavira bearing 10 large Indus signs may be a public inscription (site name? ruler's title?) — if interpretable, this would represent the longest single Indus text and potentially provide a key, but no consensus reading exists

### 3.3 Vinča Symbols
- Symbols incised on pottery and figurines from the **Vinča culture** (Balkans, ~5500–4500 BCE) have been proposed as Europe's earliest writing — but most scholars consider them cult or ownership marks rather than a systematic writing system, as they lack the complexity and combinatorial patterns characteristic of true writing (Winn 1981)

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Claimed Full Decipherments of the Indus Script
- **[DEBUNKED]** Numerous claimed "decipherments" of the Indus script have been published — Heras (1953, Sanskrit), Rao (1982, Proto-Dravidian), Jha & Rajaram (2000, Sanskrit-Vedic) — none has achieved academic acceptance, as each relies on arbitrary sign-value assignments that cannot be independently verified against the short corpus

### 4.2 Rongorongo as Evidence of Trans-Oceanic Contact
- **[UNSUPPORTED]** Claims that Rongorongo resembles Chinese, Indus, or Sumerian writing — implying ancient trans-oceanic contact — are based on superficial visual similarities between unrelated sign systems; systematic comparison shows no structural relationship (Robinson 2002)

### 4.3 The Phaistos Disc as a Game Board or Forgery
- **[CONTESTED]** Claims that the Phaistos Disc is a modern forgery (Eisenberg 2008) remain a minority view — the clay composition, firing technique, and excavation context are consistent with genuine Minoan manufacture (Duhoux 2008), but the disc's uniqueness keeps the question open

## COUNTER-ARGUMENTS & CRITICISMS

- The Farmer-Sproat-Witzel "not writing" hypothesis for the Indus script remains the most provocative challenge — if correct, it invalidates the premise of decipherment entirely
- Rongorongo's extreme corpus limitation means even if the correct reading were proposed, it might be impossible to verify scientifically
- The field faces a "selection bias" in decipherment history — successful cases get celebrated, but the majority of attempts on remaining scripts have failed, suggesting the low-hanging fruit was picked first
