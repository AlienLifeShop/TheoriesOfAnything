## QUICK SUMMARY

Psycholinguistics investigates the cognitive processes underlying language comprehension, production, and acquisition — and the relationship between language and thought has been one of the most debated questions in cognitive science.
The **Sapir-Whorf hypothesis** exists along a continuum: **strong linguistic determinism** (language determines thought — largely rejected) and **weak linguistic relativity** (language influences thought — substantially supported by experimental evidence).
Lera Boroditsky and others have demonstrated that speakers of different languages differ in how they conceptualize time, space, color, number, and causality — with language shaping habitual attention and cognitive patterns without imprisoning thought.
Daniel Everett's controversial claims about the **Pirahã** language of Amazonia — allegedly lacking recursion, number words, and color terms — challenged Chomsky's universal grammar framework and ignited fierce debate about linguistic universals.
Research on **bilingualism** reveals cognitive consequences of managing two language systems, including enhanced executive function in some domains (though this "bilingual advantage" remains contested), and code-switching effects on thought and identity.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Language influences color perception

Color naming studies provide the strongest evidence for linguistic relativity:
- Russian speakers, who have obligatory terms for light blue (*goluboy*) and dark blue (*siniy*), discriminate between these shades faster than English speakers, who use a single term "blue" (Winawer et al., 2007).
- The Berinmo (Papua New Guinea) distinguish *nol* and *wor* (a boundary cutting through what English speakers call "green") more readily at that boundary — matching the Whorfian prediction (Roberson et al., 2000).
- Crucially, these effects are strongest in the right visual field (left hemisphere, where language is processed), suggesting language acts as an online influence on perception (Gilbert et al., 2006).
- Berlin & Kay (1969) established that basic color term systems across languages follow a universal evolutionary sequence — demonstrating that universal constraints *and* linguistic variation both shape color cognition.

### 1.2 Spatial cognition varies with language

Languages differ in how they encode spatial relationships:
- **Absolute frame:** Guugu Yimithirr (Australia) and Tzeltal (Mexico) speakers use cardinal directions (north/south/east/west) rather than egocentric terms (left/right). These speakers maintain constant awareness of cardinal orientation, even indoors (Levinson, 2003).
- **Relative frame:** English and most European languages use speaker-centered terms (left/right/front/behind).
- Speakers of absolute-frame languages perform differently on spatial reasoning tasks — they solve mirror-image problems using cardinal reference, not egocentric reference (Majid et al., 2004).

### 1.3 Language acquisition — universal milestones

Despite typological variation, children across languages follow remarkably similar developmental milestones:
- Babbling: ~6 months (including deaf children exposed to sign language).
- First words: ~12 months.
- Two-word combinations: ~18–24 months.
- Grammar explosion: ~24–36 months.
- Critical/sensitive period: language acquisition facility declines after puberty (Lenneberg, 1967; Johnson & Newport, 1989 — demonstrated with age-of-arrival effects in immigrant language attainment).

### 1.4 Time conceptualization varies with language

Boroditsky (2001) and Boroditsky et al. (2011) demonstrated cross-linguistic differences in time conceptualization:
- English speakers think of time as horizontal (past=left, future=right).
- Mandarin speakers also use vertical metaphors (earlier=up, later=down).
- Aymara speakers (Andes) orient time with the past in front (visible/known) and the future behind (unseen) — reversing the English metaphor (Núñez & Sweetser, 2006).
- Kuuk Thaayorre speakers (Australia) arrange time from east to west, based on the sun's path (Boroditsky & Gaby, 2010).

### 1.5 Sentence processing — incremental and predictive

Eye-tracking and ERP published findings demonstrate that language comprehension is:
- **Incremental:** listeners/readers integrate each word immediately into the developing interpretation (Tanenhaus et al., 1995 — visual-world paradigm).
- **Predictive:** comprehenders actively predict upcoming words (as shown by the N400 ERP component — Kutas & Hillyard, 1980 — a negative deflection ~400 ms after semantically unexpected words).
- **Constraint-based:** multiple sources of information (syntax, semantics, pragmatics, world knowledge) are used in parallel to resolve ambiguity in real time.

## 2. CREDIBLE BUT DEBATED CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated)

### 2.1 Pirahã and universal grammar

Daniel Everett (2005) claimed that Pirahã, spoken by ~400 people in Amazonian Brazil, lacks:
- **Recursion** (embedding clauses within clauses) — the feature Chomsky claimed was the core of universal grammar.
- Number words (beyond rough approximations of "few" and "many").
- Fixed color terms, creation myths, and kinship terms beyond two generations.

This sparked fierce controversy:
- Supporters argue it invalidates Chomsky's universal grammar and the centrality of recursion (Hauser, Chomsky & Fitch, 2002).
- Critics (Nevins, Pesetsky & Rodrigues, 2009) argue that Everett's fieldwork data are insufficient to support such strong conclusions and that Pirahã may use recursion in ways not yet recognized.
- The debate remains unresolved, partly due to the difficulty of accessing and studying this endangered language.

### 2.2 Bilingual advantage in executive function

Bialystok (2001) proposed that bilingual children and adults show enhanced executive function — better at ignoring irrelevant information, switching between tasks, and inhibiting prepotent responses:
- Multiple studies found faster performance on Stroop tasks, Simon tasks, and task-switching paradigms for bilinguals.
- However, large-scale studies and meta-analyses (Paap & Greenberg, 2013; Lehtonen et al., 2018) have found small or null effects, suggesting the "bilingual advantage" may be smaller than initially claimed or limited to specific populations and conditions.
- The debate continues, with methodological issues (how bilingualism is defined, sample characteristics, task sensitivity) complicating resolution.

### 2.3 Universal grammar and language nativism

Chomsky (1965, 1986) argued that children possess an innate **Universal Grammar (UG)** — a set of abstract grammatical principles common to all languages:
- **Poverty of the stimulus argument:** children acquire grammar rapidly despite receiving degraded, fragmentary input — suggesting innate knowledge guides acquisition.
- **Criticism:** usage-based approaches (Tomasello, 2003) argue that children learn grammar through general cognitive mechanisms (pattern recognition, analogy, statistical learning) applied to rich social input — no innate grammar module is needed.
- Construction grammar and connectionist models offer alternative accounts of language acquisition without UG.

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Language and moral judgment

Costa et al. (2014) found that people making moral judgments in a foreign language make more utilitarian choices (e.g., in trolley-problem scenarios) than in their native language — the "foreign language effect." This suggests that emotional resonance is weaker in a second language, but the generalizability and mechanisms are still under investigation.

## 4. DUBIOUS OR FRINGE CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Strong linguistic determinism — language imprisons thought

The strong version of the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis — that language *determines* thought and makes certain concepts literally unthinkable without the corresponding words — is rejected by mainstream cognitive science. People can think, imagine, and solve problems that go beyond their linguistic resources. Language *influences* habitual thought patterns but does not constrain the boundaries of cognition.

### 4.2 Some languages are more "primitive" than others

All known human languages are fully complex, expressive systems capable of conveying any concept needed by their speech communities. There is no hierarchy of linguistic sophistication — every language has complex grammar, rich vocabulary, and the capacity for creating new expressions to handle novel situations.
