## QUICK SUMMARY

**Social stratification** refers to the ranking of individuals and groups in hierarchies of wealth, power, and prestige. The two foundational approaches are **Karl Marx** (1818–1883) — class is defined by relationship to the means of production: the **bourgeoisie** (owners of capital) exploit the **proletariat** (workers who sell their labor); class conflict is the engine of history, and capitalism inevitably produces crises, concentration of wealth, and proletarian revolution — and **Max Weber** (1864–1920) — who distinguished three dimensions of stratification: **class** (economic position — market situation), **status** (social prestige and honor — lifestyle groups), and **party** (political power — organized groups seeking influence); Weber argued Marx's economic reductionism missed the independent significance of status and political power. **Pierre Bourdieu** (*Distinction*, 1979/1984) synthesized these traditions, arguing that social classes are distinguished not just by economic capital but by **cultural capital** (education, knowledge, tastes) and **social capital** (networks, connections) — class reproduction operates through the transmission of all three forms of capital across generations, with taste and cultural consumption serving as markers of class distinction. **Contemporary inequality**: Thomas Piketty (*Capital in the Twenty-First Century*, 2014) documented that when the rate of return on capital (r) exceeds economic growth (g), wealth inequality increases — the period of relative equality (1914–1980) was historically exceptional, driven by wars and progressive taxation; since 1980, inequality has returned toward early 20th-century levels. The world's richest 1% own ~46% of global wealth (Credit Suisse, 2022); the Gini coefficient (0 = perfect equality, 1 = perfect inequality) ranges from ~0.25 (Scandinavian countries) to ~0.65 (South Africa). **Social mobility** — the ability to move between class positions — varies dramatically: Scandinavian countries and Canada show relatively high intergenerational mobility, while the US, UK, and India show lower mobility; Raj Chetty's research (2014) using US tax data revealed stark geographic variation in upward mobility within the US.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Scholarly Consensus)

### 1.1 Rising Wealth Inequality
- Piketty's documentation of rising wealth inequality since 1980, based on extensive historical tax data from 20+ countries (World Inequality Database), is empirically robust: the top 1%'s share of income and wealth increased substantially in the US (top 1% income share: ~10% in 1980 → ~20% by 2020) and most OECD countries; the central thesis (r > g → wealth concentration) is an empirical pattern, though its inevitability is debated

### 1.2 Intergenerational Mobility Variation
- Corak (2013) documented the "Great Gatsby Curve" — countries with higher inequality have lower intergenerational income mobility; Chetty et al. (2014) showed that a child born into the bottom income quintile in the US has a ~7.5% chance of reaching the top quintile, with dramatic variation by geography, race, and gender — these findings are based on comprehensive administrative data for millions of individuals

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Bourdieu's Cultural Capital
- Bourdieu's theory of cultural reproduction through taste and education is supported by extensive empirical research but criticized for determinism — social mobility does occur; cultural omnivores (Peterson & Kern, 1996) challenge the neat alignment of class and taste; and the specific cultural markers of class vary across societies and change over generations

### 2.2 The "Deaths of Despair" Thesis
- Case & Deaton (2015, 2020) documented rising mortality among white, non-college-educated Americans from suicide, drug overdose, and alcoholic liver disease — "deaths of despair" linked to deindustrialization, economic precarity, and social disintegration; while the mortality data is robust, whether these deaths reflect a specifically class-based phenomenon (vs. racial or regional patterns) and the causal mechanisms are debated

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Universal Basic Income Reducing Inequality
- Proposals for UBI (universal basic income) as a response to growing inequality and automation are gaining policy attention (Finland pilot, 2017–2018; Stockton SEED program, 2019–2021) — pilot results show improved well-being and modest labor supply effects, but whether UBI is fiscally feasible at scale and whether it would significantly reduce structural inequality remains uncertain

---

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Classless Society Achieved
- **[DEBUNKED]** Claims that class has become irrelevant in post-industrial societies (frequently heard in the 1990s) are contradicted by growing wealth and income inequality in most developed countries, persistent class-based health disparities, educational stratification, and class-correlated political polarization — class may operate differently than in Marx's time, but it remains a fundamental axis of social stratification

### Counter-Arguments
- Marx's prediction of proletarian revolution has not materialized in advanced capitalist countries — the working class has been fragmented by national, racial, and cultural divisions rather than unified by common class interest
- High inequality may be acceptable if absolute poverty is declining — focusing on relative inequality rather than absolute standards of living may misplace priorities in a world where billions have been lifted from extreme poverty
- Social mobility statistics depend heavily on how classes are defined and measured — different definitions produce different mobility rates, and mobility between adjacent quintiles may mask limited movement at the extremes
