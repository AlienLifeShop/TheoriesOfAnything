## QUICK SUMMARY

**Criminology** studies the nature, causes, consequences, and control of criminal behavior, while **deviance** encompasses behavior that violates social norms, whether or not it is legally criminal. **Classical theories**: Émile Durkheim (*The Rules of Sociological Method*, 1895) argued deviance is not pathological but a normal, functional feature of all societies — it clarifies moral boundaries, promotes social change, and strengthens group solidarity through punishment rituals. **Key sociological theories**: (1) **Strain theory** (Robert K. Merton, 1938) — crime results from the gap between culturally prescribed goals (wealth, success) and the legitimate means available to achieve them; those who lack legitimate opportunities may innovate (crime), retreat (drug use), or rebel; (2) **Social disorganization theory** (Shaw & McKay, 1942 — Chicago School) — crime concentrates in neighborhoods with poverty, residential instability, and weak institutional structures, regardless of the ethnic composition of residents; (3) **Differential association** (Edwin Sutherland, 1947) — criminal behavior is learned through interaction with others, particularly intimate groups, where individuals acquire techniques, motives, and rationalizations; Sutherland also coined **"white-collar crime"** (1939), demonstrating that crime by respectable, high-status persons (fraud, embezzlement, price-fixing) causes greater economic harm than street crime; (4) **Labeling theory** (Howard Becker, *Outsiders*, 1963) — deviance is not inherent in acts but created by social reactions; the "deviant" label, once applied, becomes a master status that shapes identity and constrains future behavior, potentially deepening criminal involvement (the "secondary deviance" amplification spiral); (5) **Rational choice theory** — individuals weigh costs and benefits of crime; deterrence depends on certainty, severity, and speed of punishment. **Mass incarceration**: the US incarceration rate exploded from ~200 per 100,000 in the 1970s to ~700 per 100,000 by 2008 (the highest in the world), driven by the War on Drugs, mandatory minimums, three-strikes laws, and racial disparities — Black Americans are incarcerated at ~5× the rate of whites (Bureau of Justice Statistics); Michelle Alexander (*The New Jim Crow*, 2010) argues mass incarceration functions as a system of racial control analogous to Jim Crow. **Broken windows theory** (Wilson & Kelling, 1982) — minor disorder (graffiti, broken windows) signals community decline and invites serious crime; this influenced aggressive policing policies (stop-and-frisk, zero-tolerance) but empirical evidence is mixed and critics argue these policies disproportionately target minority communities.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Scholarly Consensus)

### 1.1 US Mass Incarceration
- The US incarceration rate is the highest in the world (~1.9 million incarcerated in 2024, ~5% of the world's population but ~20% of its prisoners); racial disparities are stark (Black men born in 2001 have a ~1 in 3 lifetime probability of imprisonment vs. ~1 in 17 for white men — Bonczar, 2003); these figures are well-documented by Bureau of Justice Statistics and correctional data

### 1.2 Social Disorganization Theory
- Shaw & McKay's (1942) finding that crime concentrates in specific neighborhoods regardless of ethnic composition has been replicated extensively; Sampson, Raudenbush & Earls (1997, "Neighborhoods and Violent Crime" — *Science*) demonstrated that "collective efficacy" (social cohesion combined with willingness to intervene) predicts lower violence independent of demographic composition

### 1.3 White-Collar Crime
- Sutherland's concept of white-collar crime directed attention to corporate and occupational crime; subsequent research confirms that economic crimes by corporations cause greater total harm (in dollars, lives, environmental damage) than street crime — the 2008 financial crisis, involving systematic fraud in mortgage-backed securities, caused ~$22 trillion in US lost output (Dallas Fed estimate)

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Broken Windows Theory
- The original Wilson & Kelling (1982) hypothesis lacks robust experimental support: the most cited test (Braga et al., 2015 — systematic review) finds modest effects of disorder-focused policing on crime, but the "broken windows" mechanism (disorder → fear → serious crime) is not well-established; aggressive policing based on the theory has been associated with civil rights violations and erosion of police-community trust

### 2.2 Labeling Theory and Recidivism
- Evidence supports labeling theory's prediction that criminal justice contact increases future offending for some populations: youth who are formally processed show higher recidivism than comparable youth diverted from the system (Petrosino et al., 2010 — Campbell Collaboration review); however, labeling theory underspecifies which labels "stick" and under what conditions

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Prison Abolition
- The prison abolition movement (Angela Davis, Ruth Wilson Gilmore, *Golden Gulag*, 2007; Mariame Kaba) argues that prisons are inherently harmful, racially structured, and should be replaced by community-based accountability and investment — while generating growing academic and activist interest, no large-scale society has abolished incarceration, and the feasibility of non-carceral responses to violent crime remains debated

---

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Crime Is Determined by Biology/Race
- **[DEBUNKED]** Claims linking crime to racial biology (Lombroso's 19th-century "criminal type," modern genetic determinism) are contradicted by the consistent finding that crime rates correlate with socioeconomic conditions, not race — when controlling for poverty, unemployment, and neighborhood disadvantage, racial differences in offending diminish substantially; crime is a social phenomenon, not a biological one

### Counter-Arguments
- Some deterrence works: evidence supports the certainty (though not necessarily severity) of punishment as a deterrent; completely eliminating punishment could increase crime in the absence of alternative accountability mechanisms
- Labeling theory may downplay victims' experiences — focusing on how criminals are socially constructed risks minimizing the real harm suffered by crime victims
- White-collar crime scholarship may conflate regulatory violations with morally equivalent offenses — not all corporate misconduct involves the same moral gravity as violent crime
