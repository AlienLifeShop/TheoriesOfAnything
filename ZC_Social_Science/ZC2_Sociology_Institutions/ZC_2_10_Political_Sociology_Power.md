## QUICK SUMMARY

**Political sociology** examines the social bases of political power — how authority is produced, maintained, legitimated, and contested. **Max Weber** (1864–1920) defined the **state** as the institution that successfully claims a monopoly on the legitimate use of physical force within a territory (*Politics as a Vocation*, 1919) and identified three types of legitimate authority: **traditional** (custom and heredity — monarchies), **charismatic** (extraordinary personal qualities — prophets, revolutionary leaders), and **rational-legal** (impersonal rules and bureaucratic procedures — modern states). Weber's analysis of **bureaucracy** — hierarchical, rule-bound, meritocratic, impersonal — identified both its efficiency and its "iron cage" potential (routine, dehumanization, resistance to change). **Antonio Gramsci** (1891–1937, developed his thought in *Prison Notebooks* while imprisoned by Mussolini) introduced **cultural hegemony** — dominant classes maintain power not primarily through coercion but through intellectual and cultural leadership that makes the existing order seem natural, inevitable, and commonsensical; subordinate groups may consent to their own domination until counter-hegemonic movements create alternative worldviews. **C. Wright Mills** (*The Power Elite*, 1956) argued that American democracy is dominated by interlocking elites in the military, corporate, and political spheres — decisions are made by a small, socially homogeneous group while the public is depoliticized through mass media and consumerism. **Michel Foucault** reconceptualized power: rather than something possessed by individuals or classes, power operates through **discourses, institutions, and practices** that constitute subjects and regulate behavior (the concept of **governmentality** — government through techniques of population management: statistics, public health, education, urban planning). **Key topics**: democratic backsliding and **authoritarian resilience** (Freedom House reports that democracy has declined globally for 18 consecutive years, 2006–2023, with 72% of the world's population living under autocracy in 2023); **social movements** (Charles Tilly, Sidney Tarrow — political contention as collective action by ordinary people making claims on authorities, deploying "repertoires of contention"); **civil society** (Alexis de Tocqueville's observation that American democracy depends on voluntary associations; Robert Putnam's *Bowling Alone*, 2000, arguing that declining civic participation threatens democratic health); and **class and politics** (the relationship between economic class position and political behavior — class voting has declined in most Western democracies but class-based inequality continues to shape political outcomes).

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Scholarly Consensus)

### 1.1 Weber's Theory of Legitimate Authority
- Weber's three types of legitimate domination (traditional, charismatic, rational-legal) remain a foundational typology in political sociology — while empirical cases involve mixtures and hybrid forms, the conceptual framework is universally cited and has proven productive for analyzing authority across historical and cultural contexts

### 1.2 Democratic Recession
- Freedom House's *Freedom in the World* reports document a continuous global decline in democratic governance indicators since 2006; specific cases include erosion of judicial independence, press freedom, and electoral integrity in Hungary, Turkey, India, the Philippines, and Brazil — though whether this represents a "democratic recession" (Larry Diamond) or normal fluctuation is debated

### 1.3 Mills's Power Elite Concept
- Mills's observation that political, military, and corporate elites overlap in personnel, social background, and worldview has been empirically supported for the US: revolving doors between government, military, and corporate leadership are documented; economic inequality correlates with political influence (Gilens & Page, 2014, found that US policy outcomes correlate with elite preferences and organized interest group positions, not average citizen preferences)

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Gramsci's Cultural Hegemony
- Gramsci's framework has been widely applied to analyze how dominant ideologies (neoliberalism, nationalism, consumer capitalism) maintain structural inequalities without overt coercion — however, the concept's breadth makes it difficult to falsify, and critics argue that attributing consent to ideological manipulation underestimates the agency and critical capacity of ordinary people

### 2.2 Putnam's Social Capital Decline
- Putnam's thesis that civic participation in the US declined significantly since the 1960s (fewer associations, less volunteering, less trust) is supported by survey data but challenged by evidence that new forms of civic engagement (online activism, informal networks, issue-based organizing) may substitute for traditional associational membership — whether digital engagement produces equivalent social capital is debated

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Digital Authoritarianism as a New Model
- Some analysts argue that digital surveillance technology has enabled a new form of authoritarian governance that can monitor populations without the crude repression of 20th-century dictatorships (China's model of economic growth + digital control + limited political participation) — whether this model is stable and exportable to other contexts remains uncertain

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Democracy Is Naturally Expanding
- **[DEBUNKED]** The post-Cold War thesis that democracy would inevitably spread worldwide (Francis Fukuyama, "The End of History?", 1989) has been contradicted by the democratic recession documented since 2006, the resilience of authoritarian regimes (China, Russia, Saudi Arabia), and the internal fragility of established democracies — democratization is contingent, reversible, and contested, not teleological

### Counter-Arguments
- Elite theory may be overstated: democratic mechanisms (elections, courts, free press, civil society organizations) do constrain elite power, even if imperfectly — the alternative to imperfect democracy may be worse, not better
- Foucault's diffuse conception of power may make resistance seem futile — if power is everywhere and constitutes subjects, where does agency for change come from? This has been a persistent critique of Foucauldian political analysis
- Gramsci's framework risks conspiratorial thinking — attributing working-class acquiescence to ideological manipulation rather than to rational calculation, genuine cultural values, or material benefits of the existing order
