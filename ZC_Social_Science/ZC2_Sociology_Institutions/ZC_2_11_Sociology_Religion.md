## QUICK SUMMARY

The **sociology of religion** examines religion as a social phenomenon — how religious beliefs, practices, and institutions shape and are shaped by social structures. **Foundational approaches**: Émile Durkheim (*The Elementary Forms of Religious Life*, 1912) argued that religion is fundamentally social — the distinction between the **sacred** (set apart, revered) and the **profane** (ordinary, mundane) reflects the distinction between the collective and the individual; when people worship gods, they are actually worshipping society itself, and religious rituals produce "collective effervescence" — intense shared emotional experiences that reinforce group solidarity. Max Weber (*The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism*, 1905) proposed that Calvinist theology (predestination, worldly asceticism, the calling [*Beruf*]) inadvertently fostered the rationalized, disciplined economic behavior underlying modern capitalism; this thesis exemplified Weber's broader argument about **rationalization** and **disenchantment** (*Entzauberung*) — the progressive replacement of magical, religious, and traditional worldviews by calculative, scientific, and bureaucratic ones. **The secularization thesis** (Bryan Wilson, Steve Bruce) — the prediction that modernization inevitably produces religious decline — dominated the sociology of religion for decades but has been challenged: (1) the US remains both highly modern and highly religious (~65% identify as Christian, 2023, though declining from ~90% in the 1970s — Pew); (2) religious revivals in the Global South (Pentecostal Christianity growing rapidly in Latin America, sub-Saharan Africa, and East Asia — estimated 600+ million adherents worldwide); (3) Islam's continued vitality worldwide; (4) the rise of **fundamentalism** (Karen Armstrong, Martin Riesebrodt — aggressive assertion of religious authority in reaction to perceived secularizing threats — across Christianity, Islam, Judaism, Hinduism, and Buddhism). Peter Berger, once a proponent of secularization, reversed his position (*The Desecularization of the World*, 1999), arguing that the modern world is "furiously religious" and that Europe's secularity is the exception, not the norm. **Civil religion** (Robert Bellah, 1967) — the quasi-religious dimensions of national life (sacred documents, founding myths, rituals, holidays, the sanctification of "the American way of life") — operates alongside and sometimes in tension with institutional religion. **New Religious Movements (NRMs)** — from Scientology to the Unification Church to neo-paganism — raise questions about the boundaries of religion, the dynamics of conversion, and state regulation of religious freedom.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Scholarly Consensus)

### 1.1 Weber's Protestant Ethic Thesis
- Weber's argument linking Calvinist theology to the "spirit" of rational capitalism remains one of the most debated theses in social science; while the specific causal mechanism is contested (capitalism developed in non-Protestant contexts; correlation ≠ causation), the broader insight — that religious worldviews shape economic behavior and institutional development — is widely accepted and has generated extensive comparative research

### 1.2 European Secularization
- Western Europe has experienced measurable religious decline: church attendance, belief in God, and religious self-identification have all decreased substantially since the 1960s (Voas & Chaves, 2016 — "fuzzy fidelity" declining generationally); Scandinavia and the Netherlands are among the most secular societies globally — though residual "cultural Christianity" (participation in rites of passage, identification with heritage) persists

### 1.3 Global Pentecostal Growth
- Pentecostalism/Charismatic Christianity has grown from ~5% of global Christians in 1970 to ~25%+ by 2020 (~600+ million adherents), making it one of the fastest-growing religious movements in history — particularly strong in sub-Saharan Africa, Latin America, and East Asia; growth is driven by emotional worship, healing claims, prosperity theology, and adaptation to local cultures

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Secularization as Exception, Not Rule
- Berger's (1999) reversal — arguing that secularization is the exception (primarily European) rather than the rule — is supported by global evidence of religious vitality but contested by scholars (Norris & Inglehart, 2004) who argue that existential security explains the pattern: wealthy, secure societies secularize; insecure societies remain religious; the US is an outlier partly because of its higher inequality

### 2.2 Religious Market Theory
- Stark & Finke (*Acts of Faith*, 2000) proposed a "rational choice" model of religion: religious participation is highest where multiple denominations compete (pluralism) rather than where a monopolistic state church dominates — supported by some US evidence but poorly fitting the European pattern (pluralistic Netherlands is highly secular)

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Spiritual But Not Religious (SBNR) as New Category
- The "spiritual but not religious" category (SBNR) has grown rapidly in Western surveys (~27% of US adults, 2023 — Pew) — whether this represents a genuine new form of spiritual life or transitional stage toward full secularity is debated; the category's coherence and long-term stability are uncertain

---

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Religion Is Disappearing
- **[DEBUNKED]** The claim that religion is disappearing globally is contradicted by demographic and survey data: world religious affiliation is projected to remain at ~80%+ through 2050 (Pew, 2015); the "nones" (religiously unaffiliated) are growing primarily in North America, Europe, and East Asia, while global Christianity and Islam continue to grow in absolute numbers — religion is transforming, not vanishing

### Counter-Arguments
- Durkheim's equation of religion with society may be reductive — religious experience includes genuinely transcendent, mystical, and personal dimensions not reducible to social function
- Weber's disenchantment narrative may be too linear — contemporary interest in astrology, alternative spirituality, conspiracy theories, and charismatic politics suggests re-enchantment rather than progressive rationalization
- Sociological analysis of religion as a social phenomenon may offend believers by treating transcendent truth claims as sociologically explicable rather than divinely revealed — the tension between methodological agnosticism and believers' self-understanding is inherent to the field
