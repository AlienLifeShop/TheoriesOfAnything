## QUICK SUMMARY

The **sociology of disaster** studies the social dimensions of catastrophic events — earthquakes, floods, hurricanes, pandemics, industrial accidents, nuclear meltdowns, wildfires, and increasingly, climate-driven extreme events — examining how societies prepare for, experience, respond to, and recover from disasters, and how social structures (class, race, gender, age, disability, nationality) shape vulnerability, exposure, impact, and recovery. The field's foundational insight is that **disasters are not "natural"** — a hazard (earthquake, hurricane, flood) becomes a disaster only when it intersects with social vulnerability; the same earthquake in a wealthy nation with enforced building codes and emergency infrastructure causes far fewer deaths than in a poor nation without them; "there is no such thing as a natural disaster" (Neil Smith, 2006). The field was established by **Enrico Quarantelli** and Russell Dynes at the University of Delaware's Disaster Research Center (founded 1963) and Samuel Henry Prince's pioneering study of the 1917 Halifax Explosion. Key frameworks include: (1) the **vulnerability paradigm** (Wisner et al., *At Risk*, 1994/2004) — vulnerability is produced by social, economic, and political processes; the "Pressure and Release" (PAR) model traces how root causes (poverty, marginalization, lack of access to resources, governance failures) create "unsafe conditions" that amplify hazard impacts; (2) the **social amplification of risk** framework (Kasperson et al., 1988) — risk events interact with social, institutional, and communicative processes to amplify or attenuate public perception and response; media coverage, expert disagreement, institutional trust, and cultural values shape whether a hazard is perceived as manageable or catastrophic; (3) **resilience** — the capacity of communities and systems to absorb, adapt to, and recover from disturbance; critiqued when used to place responsibility on communities to "bounce back" without addressing the structural causes of vulnerability. Major findings include: **disasters reveal and intensify existing inequalities** — Hurricane Katrina (2005) devastated predominantly Black and poor communities in New Orleans, exposing racialized patterns of neglect in infrastructure, evacuation planning, and recovery resources; the COVID-19 pandemic disproportionately affected communities of color, essential workers, and low-income populations. Research consistently challenges popular misconceptions: post-disaster human behavior is largely prosocial (mutual aid, cooperation, self-organization) rather than characterized by "panic" and "looting" — the "panic myth" persists in media and official discourse despite decades of disconfirming evidence.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 Disasters Are Social
- **Vulnerability paradigm**: Wisner, Blaikie, Cannon, and Davis (*At Risk*, 1994/2004) — disasters result from the intersection of hazards (natural or technological) with vulnerability (produced by poverty, marginalization, environmental degradation, and governance failure); the PAR model: Root causes (economic/political structures) → Dynamic pressures (urbanization, environmental degradation) → Unsafe conditions (fragile buildings, dangerous locations, low income, no preparedness) → Disaster when a hazard triggers; disaster risk = hazard × vulnerability / capacity
- **Quarantelli and the Disaster Research Center**: decades of research (1963–present) established that disasters are social phenomena — their causes, impacts, and outcomes are shaped by social structures rather than by the physical characteristics of the hazard alone; Quarantelli argued that conventional categories ("natural disaster," "man-made disaster") are misleading — all disasters are mediated by human decisions, policies, and institutional arrangements

### 1.2 Disasters Reveal Inequality
- **Hurricane Katrina (2005)**: the paradigmatic case — pre-existing patterns of racial segregation, poverty, infrastructure neglect, and political marginalization meant that predominantly Black communities (Lower Ninth Ward) were most exposed to flooding, least prepared, last evacuated, and slowest to receive recovery resources; the failures were systemic — FEMA, Army Corps of Engineers levee design, and local government all reflected structural racism and class inequality
- **COVID-19 pandemic**: disproportionate infection and death rates among communities of color, essential workers, incarcerated populations, and residents of congregate settings — reflecting pre-existing health disparities, occupational exposure (essential work in service sectors, meatpacking, agriculture), crowded housing, and unequal access to healthcare

### 1.3 Prosocial Post-Disaster Behavior
- **The "panic myth"**: media and official narratives consistently portray post-disaster behavior as panicked, irrational, and often criminal (looting, violence); decades of disaster research (Quarantelli, 1960s–2000s; Tierney, Bevc, and Kuligowski, 2006; Solnit, *A Paradise Built in Hell*, 2009) consistently find the opposite — post-disaster communities exhibit high levels of mutual aid, self-organized rescue, sharing of resources, and cooperative behavior; "elite panic" (authorities' fear of social breakdown) drives militarized responses that often hinder rather than support community-led recovery

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Social Amplification of Risk
- **Kasperson et al. (1988)**: the social amplification of risk framework (SARF) — risk events are "amplified" or "attenuated" as they are communicated through social "stations" (media, institutions, social groups, individuals); media framing, expert credibility, institutional trust, stigmatization, and cultural values filter risk perception; some hazards receive disproportionate attention relative to their statistical risk (terrorism, shark attacks), while others receive insufficient attention (air pollution, climate change); SARF links risk perception to broader social, political, and economic consequences

### 2.2 Resilience and Critique
- **Community resilience**: the concept has become central to disaster policy and climate adaptation — emphasizing communities' adaptive capacity, social capital (trust, networks, reciprocity), and institutional learning; critiqued by Cretney (2014) and others who argue that "resilience" discourse can normalize inequality — "bouncing back" to unjust pre-disaster conditions is not a desirable goal; genuine disaster recovery requires addressing root causes of vulnerability — poverty, discrimination, governance failure — not merely encouraging communities to "be resilient"

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Cascading and Compound Disasters
- **Systemic risk**: as societies become more interconnected and climate change intensifies extreme events, "cascading" or "compound" disasters (multiple hazards interacting — pandemic + economic crisis + extreme weather + political instability) may become more frequent and may exceed the capacity of existing disaster management frameworks; whether current institutions can adapt to systemic, multi-hazard risk or whether fundamentally new governance architectures are needed is an active area of debate

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Disasters Are "Great Equalizers"
- **[CONTRADICTED]** The claim that disasters "don't discriminate" — that they affect everyone equally — is consistently contradicted by disaster research; disasters disproportionately harm the poor, marginalized, disabled, elderly, and communities of color, who have fewer resources to prepare, evacuate, and recover; wealth, social capital, political access, and spatial location all shape disaster outcomes; disasters exacerbate pre-existing inequalities rather than equalizing them

## COUNTER-ARGUMENTS & CRITICISMS

1. **Furedi — The vulnerability paradigm pathologizes affected populations.** Frank Furedi has argued that the dominant vulnerability framework in disaster sociology risks portraying affected communities as passive victims rather than capable agents, and that the therapeutic culture surrounding disaster response can undermine community self-reliance by institutionalizing helplessness. (Furedi, "The Changing Meaning of Disaster," *Area* 39.4, 2007: 482–489. DOI: 10.1111/j.1475-4762.2007.00764.x)

2. **Drabek — The "panic myth" rejection is itself overstated.** Thomas Drabek has noted that while mass panic is indeed rare, selective denial, non-compliance with evacuation orders, and delayed response are well-documented phenomena that the prosocial-behavior narrative sometimes minimizes; effective emergency management must account for these behavioral realities rather than overcorrecting against the panic narrative. (Drabek, *Human System Responses to Disaster*, Springer, 1986, pp. 112–145)

3. **Tierney — Resilience discourse can depoliticize disaster.** Kathleen Tierney has cautioned that resilience, when used by governments and international organizations, can shift responsibility from states to communities by emphasizing local adaptive capacity while ignoring the structural inequalities and policy failures that created vulnerability — making "resilience" a conservative concept that normalizes injustice. (Tierney, "Resilience and the Neoliberal Project," *American Behavioral Scientist* 59.10, 2015: 1327–1342. DOI: 10.1177/0002764215591187)

4. **Kelman — "Natural disaster" terminology persists because it serves political interests.** Ilan Kelman has argued that despite decades of scholarly consensus that disasters are social, the phrase "natural disaster" persists in policy and media because it serves political interests — deflecting responsibility from governments to "acts of God" and obscuring accountability for planning failures, land-use decisions, and inadequate infrastructure. (Kelman, *Disaster by Choice*, Oxford UP, 2020, pp. 1–25)

5. **Bankoff — The vulnerability paradigm reflects Western assumptions about risk.** Greg Bankoff has argued that the vulnerability paradigm treats disaster-prone regions (Global South) as inherently deficient, replicating colonial "tropicality" discourses by framing developing countries as dangerous and pathological; a more balanced approach would recognize indigenous and local risk-management traditions rather than imposing external analytical frameworks. (Bankoff, "Rendering the World Unsafe: 'Vulnerability' as Western Discourse," *Disasters* 25.1, 2001: 19–35. DOI: 10.1111/1467-7717.00159)
