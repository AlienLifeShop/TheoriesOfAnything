## QUICK SUMMARY

The **sociology of technology** (a core subfield of **Science and Technology Studies** — STS) investigates how social, economic, political, and cultural factors shape the development, design, adoption, and consequences of technologies — and conversely, how technologies reshape social structures, power relations, identities, and everyday life. The field emerged in the 1980s as a corrective to **technological determinism** — the widely held assumption that technology develops according to an internal logic (following "the best" technical solution) and then unilaterally causes social change (e.g., "the printing press caused the Reformation"; "the internet democratizes information"). Against this view, STS scholars demonstrated that technologies are **socially constructed** — their design, meaning, and function are shaped by the interests, negotiations, and power relations of relevant social groups rather than by purely technical criteria. The **Social Construction of Technology** (SCOT) framework (Pinch and Bijker, 1984) showed how technologies exhibit "interpretive flexibility" — different social groups attribute different meanings, problems, and criteria of success to the same artifact (the early bicycle was a sport machine for young men, a dangerous contraption for women wielding long skirts, and a practical vehicle for urbanites, and its design emerged from and resolved these competing interpretations). **Actor-Network Theory** (ANT — Latour, Callon, Law, 1980s) went further, arguing that the boundary between "social" and "technical" is itself constructed — technologies and humans form hybrid networks of associations ("actants"), and agency is distributed across human and non-human entities; a speed bump "acts" to slow cars just as a police officer would; a laboratory "enrolls" microbes, instruments, and funding agencies into networks that produce scientific facts; ANT's radical principle of "symmetry" — treating human and non-human actors with the same analytical vocabulary — was intellectually provocative and remains hotly debated. Major topics include: the politics of design (Langdon Winner's "Do Artifacts Have Politics?" — the design of Long Island parkway overpasses too low for buses effectively excluded low-income and Black New Yorkers from public beaches), the domestication of technology (how users adapt and transform technologies in unintended ways), digital divides, algorithmic governance, surveillance technologies, and the co-production of technology and social order.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 Foundational Frameworks
- **SCOT (Social Construction of Technology)**: Pinch and Bijker (1984) — technologies are shaped by negotiation among "relevant social groups" who define problems differently and evaluate solutions according to different criteria; technologies achieve "closure" when one design stabilizes — either through "rhetorical closure" (one interpretation becomes dominant) or "redefinition of the problem" (the technology is adapted to satisfy multiple groups); the bicycle case study: the high-wheeled "ordinary" bicycle was interpreted as thrilling by young sporting men but dangerous by others → the "safety bicycle" (low, equal-sized wheels, chain drive) achieved closure by redefining the bicycle as a practical transport device
- **Actor-Network Theory (ANT)**: Latour (*Science in Action*, 1987), Callon ("Some Elements of a Sociology of Translation," 1984), Law (*After Method*, 2004) — reality is composed of heterogeneous networks of human and non-human "actants" — microbes, texts, laboratories, politicians, technologies — that are enrolled, aligned, and translated into stable assemblages; "black boxing" describes how successful networks become taken-for-granted (a working computer is a "black box" — its internal complexity is invisible until it breaks down); controversial for granting analytical agency to non-humans
- **Langdon Winner, "Do Artifacts Have Politics?" (1980)**: argued that technologies can embody political values — either because specific designs are chosen for political purposes (low overpasses to exclude buses) or because certain technologies are inherently compatible with particular power structures (nuclear power requires centralized, authoritarian management; solar power is compatible with decentralized, democratic governance); foundational essay in the politics of technology

### 1.2 Technological Determinism Critique
- **Against determinism**: the sociology of technology demonstrates that there is rarely a single "best" technical solution — technologies reflect choices among competing designs, each favored by different social groups; "failed" technologies (Betamax vs. VHS, Dvorak vs. QWERTY keyboard) often had superior technical characteristics but lost due to marketing, standardization, network effects, and path dependency rather than technical inferiority
- **Path dependency and lock-in**: Arthur (1989), David (1985) — early choices in technological development create self-reinforcing feedback loops that "lock in" particular technologies — even suboptimal ones — because switching costs increase over time; the QWERTY keyboard (designed to prevent typewriter jams in the 1870s) persists despite claims that alternative layouts are faster — not because it is technically optimal but because it is entrenched

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Domestication Theory
- **User adaptation**: Silverstone and Haddon (1996) — technologies are not simply adopted as designed but are "domesticated" by users — appropriated, objectified (given symbolic meaning), incorporated (integrated into routines), and converted (used to display competence); mobile phones were designed for business calls but rapidly domesticated as personal communication, social media, and entertainment devices — users transform technologies beyond designer intentions

### 2.2 Algorithmic Governance
- **Algorithms as social actors**: contemporary STS increasingly studies algorithms, AI, and platforms as sociotechnical systems — search algorithms, recommendation engines, and predictive policing systems embed social values (biases, assumptions, power relations) in code; Safiya Noble (*Algorithms of Oppression*, 2018) demonstrated how Google search results reproduced racial and gender stereotypes; algorithms are not neutral but "politics by other means"

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Posthuman Assemblages
- **Beyond human-centered analysis**: some STS scholars argue that as AI, biotechnology, and digital platforms increasingly mediate human action, traditional sociological categories (agency, intention, consciousness) must be radically rethought to account for human-machine assemblages that blur the boundary between tool and user; whether existing social theory can accommodate these assemblages or requires fundamental reconceptualization remains an open theoretical debate

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Technology Is Value-Neutral
- **[INCORRECT]** The claim that "technology is just a tool — it depends on how it's used" — while superficially appealing — is rejected by STS scholarship; technologies embody the values, assumptions, and interests of their designers and the institutions that deploy them; design choices constrain and enable particular uses; technologies create affordances (possibilities for action) that are not socially neutral — a gun affords killing in a way that other tools do not; algorithms reproduce the biases of their training data

## COUNTER-ARGUMENTS & CRITICISMS

**1. SCOT and ANT Offer No Normative Framework for Evaluating Technology**
Winner (1993, "Upon Opening the Black Box and Finding It Empty," *Science, Technology, & Human Values* 18(3): 362–378, DOI: 10.1177/016224399301800306) criticized the social construction of technology (SCOT) approach for its inability to distinguish between technologies that serve human wellbeing and those that cause harm — by treating all "relevant social groups" symmetrically, SCOT provides no basis for evaluating whether a technology’s development should have been different.

**2. Actor-Network Theory’s Symmetry Between Humans and Non-Humans Is Philosophically Problematic**
Collins and Yearley (1992, "Epistemological Chicken," in *Science as Practice and Culture*, University of Chicago Press) argued that treating bacteria, scallops, and door-closers as "actants" with the same analytical status as humans is an ontological confusion that obscures rather than illuminates the distinctive role of human intentionality, meaning-making, and moral responsibility in sociotechnical systems.

**3. Technological Determinism Is Not Entirely Wrong**
Smith and Marx (1994, *Does Technology Drive History? The Dilemma of Technological Determinism*, MIT Press, ISBN 978-0262691673) argued that while "hard" technological determinism (technology determines social outcomes in a linear fashion) is untenable, "soft" determinism — that material properties of technologies significantly constrain and enable social possibilities — has substantial empirical support and should not be dismissed in favor of purely social explanations.

**4. STS Relativism Undermines Public Trust in Evidence-Based Policy**
Jasanoff (2005, *Designs on Nature: Science and Democracy in Europe and the United States*, Princeton University Press, ISBN 978-0691130422) acknowledged the tension between STS’s analytical stance (all knowledge is co-produced with social order) and the practical need for authoritative scientific claims in democratic policy-making (climate policy, vaccine regulation).

**5. The QWERTY Example Has Been Challenged**
Liebowitz and Margolis (1990, "The Fable of the Keys," *Journal of Law & Economics* 33(1): 1–25, DOI: 10.1086/467198) disputed David’s (1985) QWERTY path-dependency argument, demonstrating that evidence for Dvorak keyboard superiority is weak and that QWERTY persistence may reflect genuine efficiency rather than lock-in — undermining the most famous example of technological path dependency in STS.
