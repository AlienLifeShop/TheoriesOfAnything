## QUICK SUMMARY

**Comparative politics** is the systematic study of political systems, institutions, processes, and behavior across countries, regions, and historical periods — using comparison as a methodological strategy to explain why political outcomes (democratization, authoritarianism, political violence, welfare policy, economic development, corruption) vary across contexts. It is one of the three traditional subfields of political science (alongside political theory and international relations) and addresses fundamental questions: Why are some countries democratic and others authoritarian? What explains transitions to and from democracy? How do different institutional designs (presidential vs. parliamentary systems, federal vs. unitary states, proportional vs. majoritarian electoral systems) shape political outcomes? What causes political instability, civil war, or state failure? The field classifies **regime types** along a spectrum from full democracy to closed autocracy, with various "hybrid regimes" in between (competitive authoritarianism, electoral authoritarianism, illiberal democracy). **Democratization theory** has identified multiple waves: Huntington's "three waves of democratization" (1820s–1920s, 1943–1962, 1974–present — beginning with Portugal's Carnation Revolution and the fall of Southern European dictatorships, extending through Latin American transitions and post-Soviet democratization); since 2006, scholars have documented democratic backsliding and "autocratization" in established and new democracies alike (Turkey, Hungary, Poland, India, the United States, Brazil), prompting debate about a potential "reverse wave." Key institutional debates include: **presidentialism vs. parliamentarism** (Linz argued presidential systems are more prone to democratic breakdown due to fixed terms, winner-take-all elections, and dual legitimacy conflicts; others counter that institutional performance depends on context), the effects of **electoral systems** (proportional representation produces multiparty legislatures and coalition governments; majoritarian/plurality systems produce two-party systems — Duverger's Law), and the design of **federal** vs. **unitary** states. The **comparative method** itself — small-N case comparison (Mill's methods), large-N statistical analysis, most-similar/most-different systems designs, process tracing — is a core contribution to social science methodology beyond political science.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 Regime Classification
- **Regime types**: political systems are classified along a democracy-autocracy continuum; standard quantitative measures include Polity (–10 to +10), Freedom House (Free/Partly Free/Not Free), V-Dem (Varieties of Democracy — multidimensional indicators); as of 2023−24, approximately 70-80 countries are classified as democratic, 50-60 as hybrid regimes, and 50-60 as authoritarian — with significant variation depending on the measure used
- **Hybrid regimes**: Levitsky and Way (*Competitive Authoritarianism*, 2010) — many regimes hold elections but violate democratic norms through uneven playing fields, media manipulation, harassment of opposition, and selective rule of law; "competitive authoritarian" regimes are distinct from both full democracies and closed autocracies; examples: Russia, Venezuela, Turkey, Cambodia

### 1.2 Democratization and Backsliding
- **Huntington's three waves** (*The Third Wave*, 1991): described successive historical pulses of democratization, each followed by partial reversal ("reverse waves"); the third wave (beginning 1974) produced the largest expansion of democracy in history — Southern Europe (Spain, Portugal, Greece), Latin America, Eastern Europe, parts of Sub-Saharan Africa, and East Asia
- **Democratic backsliding**: since approximately 2006, scholars (V-Dem, Freedom House) have documented declining democratic quality in established and new democracies — erosion of judicial independence, media freedom, opposition rights, and institutional checks; Bermeo (2016) identified "executive aggrandizement" — gradual power concentration by elected leaders — as the primary mechanism, replacing military coups as the dominant threat to democracy

### 1.3 Institutional Design
- **Presidential vs. parliamentary systems**: Linz ("The Perils of Presidentialism," 1990) argued that presidentialism increases the risk of democratic breakdown because: (a) winner-take-all presidential elections raise the stakes, (b) fixed terms prevent flexible resolution of political crises, (c) dual democratic legitimacy (president and legislature both claim mandates) creates gridlock and constitutional crisis; parliamentary systems allow votes of no confidence for orderly government change; Linz's thesis was challenged by Mainwaring and Shugart (1997), who showed that institutional effects depend on party system fragmentation and other contextual variables
- **Duverger's Law**: Duverger (1954) — plurality/first-past-the-post electoral systems tend to produce two-party systems (strategic voting concentrates support on viable candidates), while proportional representation produces multiparty systems; one of the strongest empirical regularities in comparative politics, though with notable exceptions (India, Canada)

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Modernization Theory vs. Structural Approaches
- **Lipset (1959)**: hypothesized that economic development promotes democracy — wealthier, more educated, more urbanized societies are more likely to sustain democratic institutions; statistically robust correlation (virtually no rich country has reverted to stable autocracy — with oil-rich states as exceptions), but the causal mechanism is debated; Acemoglu and Robinson (*Economic Origins of Dictatorship and Democracy*, 2006) argue that democratization is driven by distributional conflict between elites and masses, not simply by economic growth

### 2.2 State Capacity and Governance
- **State building**: Fukuyama (*The Origins of Political Order*, 2011; *Political Order and Political Decay*, 2014) argues that political development requires three pillars — a strong state (administrative capacity, rule enforcement), rule of law (constraints on state power), and accountability (democratic participation) — and that the sequence and balance among these elements shape political outcomes; states that democratized before building administrative capacity often developed patronage politics and weak governance

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Digital Authoritarianism
- **Technology and regime resilience**: authoritarian regimes increasingly use digital surveillance, AI-powered censorship, and social media manipulation to control populations — China's social credit system, internet shutdowns in Myanmar, AI-generated propaganda; whether these technologies fundamentally change the balance between state control and popular resistance, enabling durable "digital authoritarianism" resistant to the democratizing pressures identified by earlier theory, is a rapidly evolving empirical question

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Democracy Is the "Natural" Endpoint of Political Development
- **[UNSUPPORTED]** Teleological claims that all societies will inevitably democratize (Fukuyama's "end of history" thesis, 1989 — often oversimplified in popular reception) are contradicted by the persistence and adaptation of authoritarian regimes, democratic backsliding, and the diversity of hybrid political forms; democracy is historically contingent, requires active maintenance, and can be reversed; comparative politics demonstrates that there is no single pathway to political development

## COUNTER-ARGUMENTS

- **Modernization theory vs. structural approaches**: Classical modernization theory (**Lipset**, 1959) predicted that economic development leads to democratization. **Acemoglu and Robinson** (*Why Nations Fail*, 2012) and structuralists argue that institutions and power distributions matter more than wealth — extractive institutions can persist despite economic growth, and some wealthy authoritarian states (petro-states, China) defy the Lipset hypothesis
- **Democratic backsliding causation**: The causes of democratic erosion in established democracies (Hungary, Poland, the United States) are debated — **Steven Levitsky and Daniel Ziblatt** (*How Democracies Die*, 2018) emphasized the breakdown of informal norms and elite gatekeeping, while **Pippa Norris and Ronald Inglehart** (*Cultural Backlash*, 2019) pointed to cultural reactions against progressive value change. **Adam Przeworski** remains skeptical that any general theory can predict democratic survival
- **Presidentialism debate**: **Juan Linz** (1990) argued that presidential systems are more prone to democratic breakdown than parliamentary systems because of dual legitimacy and executive-legislative deadlocks. **Scott Mainwaring** qualified this to presidential systems combined with multiparty systems, while **José Antonio Cheibub** (2007) argued that the correlation disappears when controlling for the circumstances under which presidential democracies are adopted
