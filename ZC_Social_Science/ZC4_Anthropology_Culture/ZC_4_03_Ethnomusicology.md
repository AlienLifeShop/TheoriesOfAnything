## QUICK SUMMARY

**Ethnomusicology** — the study of music in its cultural context, or more precisely, the study of **music as culture** and **culture as expressed through music** — emerged in the mid-20th century from the older discipline of "comparative musicology" (*vergleichende Musikwissenschaft*) and has grown into a field that examines how sound, meaning, identity, power, and social organization intersect in every human society. The field's two founding formulations capture its dual nature: **Alan Merriam** (*The Anthropology of Music*, 1964) defined it as "the study of music in culture" — treating music as a form of human behavior embedded in social context, analyzable through the methods of anthropology; **Mantle Hood** (1969) defined it as "the study of all music" — contrasting it with historical musicology's focus on Western art music. **John Blacking** (*How Musical Is Man?*, 1973) pushed the definition further: music is "humanly organized sound" — this definition includes all intentionally structured acoustic communication, regardless of whether it fits Western categories of "music," and implies that musicality is a universal human capacity, not a specialized talent. The field's core questions include: Is music universal? (Yes — every known human society makes music; no society has been found without it — but musical systems vary enormously in their scales, timbres, rhythms, structures, and social functions); What does music do in society? (it marks ritual, expresses and constructs identity, regulates emotion, coordinates group action, transmits cultural knowledge, mediates political power, defines social boundaries); How does music relate to language? (complex and debated — music and language share neural circuitry, both are productive combinatorial systems, but they differ in semantic specificity and referential function). Key intellectual lineages include: **Erich von Hornbostel and Curt Sachs** (comparative musicology, instrument classification — the Hornbostel-Sachs system remains standard); **Béla Bartók** and **Zoltán Kodály** (systematic folk-music collecting in Hungary and neighboring regions, using the phonograph — establishing methodological standards for field recording); **Alan Lomax** (*cantometrics*, 1968 — a controversial but influential cross-cultural coding system that correlated singing style with social organization; Lomax argued that societies with egalitarian social structures tended toward **group polyphony** and relaxed vocal production, while hierarchical societies favored **solo singing** and tense vocal production); **Bruno Nettl** (*The Study of Ethnomusicology*, 1983, revised 2005 — the field's definitive methodological textbook); **Steven Feld** (*Sound and Sentiment*, 1982 — a landmark study of the Kaluli of Papua New Guinea, analyzing how their music, mythology, and ecology are deeply intertwined through the sounds of the rainforest and the metaphor of birdsong and weeping). Key case studies in the ethnomusicological canon include: **Javanese and Balinese gamelan** (large tuned-percussion orchestras using bronze metallophone, gong, and drum — studied by Jaap Kunst, later by Judith Becker and others; gamelan music uses non-Western tuning systems — *slendro* (5-tone) and *pelog* (7-tone) — and operates through communal interlocking patterns rather than individual virtuosity); **Indian classical music** (the **raga** system — melodic frameworks with ascending/descending patterns, characteristic phrases, ornaments, and emotional-temporal associations (*rasa* and *samay*) — studied by Daniélou, Powers, and Clayton); **West African griot traditions** (hereditary musician-historians — the *djeli* or *griot* of the Mande peoples are living libraries of genealogy, history, and social knowledge, performing on the kora, ngoni, and balafon); and **Aboriginal Australian songlines** (Dreaming tracks — sung narratives that encode geographic, ecological, and spiritual knowledge across vast landscapes). Contemporary ethnomusicology addresses globalization, digital media, cultural appropriation, the "world music" market, music and conflict, music and healing, and the politics of archiving and representing other people's music.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Ethnographic / Analytical / Peer-Reviewed)

### 1.1 Foundational Concepts
- **Merriam (1964)**: proposed a tripartite model for studying music: (1) concepts about music (beliefs, values), (2) behavior related to music (performance practices, learning processes), and (3) sound itself (acoustic analysis) — all three must be studied together for a complete understanding; this model remains influential
- **Blacking (1973)**: drew on his fieldwork among the **Venda** of South Africa to argue that music-making is a fundamental human capacity, not a specialized skill — "There is so much music in the world that it is reasonable to suppose that music, like language and possibly religion, is a species-specific trait of man"; Venda children's songs demonstrated sophisticated structural principles (call-and-response, polyrhythm, harmonic movement) despite being composed by non-specialists

### 1.2 Cross-Cultural Documentation
- **Lomax, *Folk Song Style and Culture* (1968)**: cantometrics coded singing styles from over 4,000 recordings across 233 cultural groups on 37 parameters — correlations between singing style and social variables (e.g., vocal width correlated with sexual permissiveness, group polyphony with egalitarian social structure) were statistically significant but have been criticized for oversimplification and cultural essentialism
- **Nettl (2005)**: documented that while every society makes music, the concept of "music" as a distinct category (separate from speech, dance, ritual, work) is not universal — many languages have no word for "music" as an abstract category; what Western observers classify as "music" may be locally understood as prayer, play, work, or healing

### 1.3 Gamelan and Non-Western Tuning
- Javanese gamelan instruments use two tuning systems: **slendro** (5 nearly equidistant tones per octave — approximately 240 cents apart, though each gamelan has its own unique tuning) and **pelog** (7 unequally spaced tones) — neither corresponds to Western equal temperament; every gamelan is tuned as a unique set, and instruments from different gamelans cannot be mixed; this demonstrates that "natural" tuning is culturally constructed

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated)

### 2.1 Music and Social Structure Correlations
- Lomax's cantometrics correlations are debated: critics (Nettl, Feld) argue that reducing complex musical systems to numerical codes distorts their meaning, and that the correlations may reflect recording bias or sampling problems; defenders argue that the broad patterns hold even if specific correlations are imperfect

### 2.2 Music as Evolutionary Adaptation
- Whether music is an evolutionary adaptation (sexual selection — Miller 2000; group cohesion — Brown 2000), a byproduct of adaptations for language and auditory processing ("auditory cheesecake" — **Pinker 1997**), or a gene-culture coevolutionary product (Patel 2008) is actively debated — the universality and antiquity of music favor an adaptive account, but the specific selective pressures remain unclear

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Music Before Language
- Scholars (Mithen, *The Singing Neanderthals*, 2005) propose that a proto-musical communication system ("Hmmmmm" — Holistic, Manipulative, Multi-modal, Musical, Mimetic) preceded and facilitated the evolution of language — this is speculative and difficult to test, but supported by the observation that music and language share neural substrates

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Hierarchical Ranking of Musical Traditions
- **[REJECTED]** The 19th-century view that Western tonal music represents the "highest" development of music and that non-Western traditions are "primitive" — ethnomusicology was founded in explicit opposition to this view; musical complexity, sophistication, and expressiveness are present in all documented traditions

---

## COUNTER-ARGUMENTS

- **Music as adaptation vs. by-product**: **Steven Pinker** famously dismissed music as "auditory cheesecake" — a pleasurable by-product of language, auditory scene analysis, and emotional processing with no adaptive function. **Ian Cross**, **Aniruddh Patel**, and others have countered that music's universality, developmental priority, and role in social bonding suggest genuine adaptive significance. The debate remains unresolved
- **Lomax cantometrics controversy**: **Alan Lomax's** cross-cultural coding system correlating singing styles with social complexity was criticized for methodological weaknesses — subjective coding categories, small and unrepresentative samples, and ecological fallacy (attributing group-level correlations to individual behavior). **Victor Grauer** (2006) computationally revived phylogenetic approaches to musical evolution, though methodological concerns persist
- **Comparative musicology tensions**: The field has long debated whether cross-cultural comparison is valid or whether each musical tradition must be understood on its own terms — **Bruno Nettl** (2005) advocated for cautious comparison while **John Blacking** emphasized that music is fundamentally social behavior, not an autonomous aesthetic object
