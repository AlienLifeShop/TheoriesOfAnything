## QUICK SUMMARY

**Structuralism** — the intellectual movement that sought to uncover the **deep, universal structures** underlying the surface diversity of human cultures, languages, myths, kinship systems, and social institutions — was the dominant paradigm in the French and broader European human sciences from the 1950s through the 1970s. Its foundational premise is that meaning is produced not by individual elements in isolation but by **systems of relationships and oppositions** — a principle derived from the structural linguistics of **Ferdinand de Saussure** (*Cours de linguistique générale*, 1916): language is a system of **signs**, each composed of a **signifier** (the sound-image, e.g., the word "tree") and a **signified** (the concept); the relationship between signifier and signified is **arbitrary** (there is no natural connection between the word "tree" and the concept of a tree); and meaning is produced by **difference** within the system (we know what "tree" means because it is not "free," not "three," not "bush") — not by reference to the external world. **Claude Lévi-Strauss** (1908–2009) — the most influential structuralist in the social sciences — applied Saussure's linguistic model to anthropology, arguing that: **(1) Myth**: myths are not random fantasies but structured systems that express and mediate fundamental **binary oppositions** (nature/culture, raw/cooked, life/death, male/female); in the four-volume *Mythologiques* (1964–1971), Lévi-Strauss analyzed hundreds of South and North American myths to demonstrate that they form a single system of transformations — each myth is a variation on a limited set of structural themes, like different performances of the same underlying score. The key example is the opposition **raw vs. cooked** (*Le Cru et le cuit*, 1964): cooking transforms nature (raw food) into culture (cooked food), making the culinary act the primordial cultural transformation — an act of mediation between nature and culture. **(2) Kinship**: Lévi-Strauss reinterpreted kinship not as a system of descent (the British functionalist view) but as a system of **exchange** — the incest taboo forces groups to exchange women, creating the alliances (**affinal ties**) that constitute society; the "elementary structures" of kinship (cross-cousin marriage prescriptions) are formal systems analyzable through mathematical models. **(3) Totemism**: Lévi-Strauss (*Totemism*, 1962; *The Savage Mind*, 1962) argued that totemism is not a primitive religion but a system of **classification** — using natural species (animals, plants) as a code for thinking about social differences: "animals are good to think with" (*bonnes à penser*); the system is logical and intellectual, not mystical. **(4) Bricolage**: Lévi-Strauss contrasted the thinking of "traditional" societies (the **bricoleur**, who works with a finite set of pre-existing materials and tools, recombining them for new purposes) with the thinking of modern science (the **engineer**, who designs new tools for specific purposes) — but argued that both are valid forms of intellectual activity; the "savage mind" is not inferior but differently structured. **Roland Barthes** (*Mythologies*, 1957; *Elements of Semiology*, 1964) applied structural analysis to popular culture — advertising, wrestling, steak-frites, the Citroën DS — demonstrating that everyday cultural phenomena are sign systems that naturalize ideology (bourgeois French culture presents itself as universal, natural, and common-sense through the semiotic process of **mythology** — a second-order sign system that drains history from the sign and presents the culturally constructed as natural). **Pierre Bourdieu** (1930–2002) — who transcended the structuralism/post-structuralism divide — developed a **theory of practice** that resolved the dichotomy between structure and agency: **(a) Habitus** — the system of durable, transposable dispositions (ways of perceiving, feeling, and acting) acquired through socialization and embodied in the body; habitus generates practices that are "objectively adapted" to social conditions without being consciously calculated; **(b) Field** — a structured arena of social competition (the literary field, the academic field, the political field) with its own rules, stakes, and hierarchies; **(c) Capital** — the resources actors deploy in fields: economic capital (wealth), cultural capital (education, taste, knowledge), social capital (networks, connections), and symbolic capital (prestige, honor, recognition); **(d) Practice** — the improvised, strategic actions generated by the interaction of habitus and field — neither purely determined by structure nor purely free. Bourdieu's *Distinction: A Social Critique of the Judgement of Taste* (1979) demonstrated that aesthetic taste (preferring Bach over Brahms, abstract art over representational art, nouvelle cuisine over traditional food) is **not natural or individual** but is systematically correlated with social class (educational capital and economic capital), functioning as a mechanism of **social distinction** and class reproduction.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Published Works / Academic Analysis)

### 1.1 Lévi-Strauss and Structural Anthropology
- **Lévi-Strauss, *Structural Anthropology* (1958)**: programmatic statement — the anthropologist should look for the unconscious structures underlying cultural phenomena, just as the linguist looks for the phonemic structures underlying speech; the method involves: identifying binary oppositions, mapping transformations between variants, and reducing apparent diversity to underlying formal patterns
- **Lévi-Strauss, *Mythologiques* (1964–1971)**: the most ambitious application of structural analysis ever attempted — 4 volumes, ~2,000 pages, analyzing over 800 myths from the Americas; demonstrated that myths from societies with no historical contact share structural features (opposition mediation, transformation sequences) — evidence, Lévi-Strauss argued, that the human mind operates according to universal unconscious logical structures

### 1.2 Bourdieu's Theory of Practice
- **Bourdieu, *Distinction* (1979)**: based on extensive survey data from France in the 1960s–70s — demonstrated that cultural consumption (preferences in music, art, food, sport, clothing) correlates with class position more reliably than economic indicators alone; taste functions as a **marker of social class** and a **mechanism of social reproduction** — the educational system rewards the cultural capital of dominant classes, naturalizing class privilege as "talent" or "intelligence"
- **Bourdieu, *The Logic of Practice* (1980)**: the concept of habitus resolves the structure/agency problem — agents act strategically and creatively, but within the parameters set by their internalized dispositions; the options that "feel natural" or "seem obvious" are shaped by class, gender, and cultural location

### 1.3 Barthes and Cultural Semiotics
- **Barthes, *Mythologies* (1957)**: short essays analyzing the semiotics of French popular culture — wrestling as spectacle of justice, the new Citroën as mythology of technology, the face of Garbo as sign of beauty; the theoretical conclusion (*Myth Today*): mythology is a "second-order semiological system" that converts history into nature — ideology operates by making the contingent appear necessary

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Universality of Binary Opposition
- Critics of Lévi-Strauss (Leach 1970; Sperber 1975) argued that binary opposition is too reductive — real myths are polysemic (carrying multiple meanings) and cannot be reduced to simple either/or contrasts; Lévi-Strauss responded that binary opposition is a methodological tool, not a claim that thought is literally binary
- The question of whether structural patterns reflect universal cognitive architecture or the analyst's projection remains debated

### 2.2 Post-Structuralist Critique
- **Derrida (1967, *Of Grammatology*; *Writing and Difference*)**: challenged structuralism's claim to identify stable, centered structures — Derrida argued that meaning is always **deferred** (*différance*) and structures lack a fixed center; every opposition (nature/culture, speech/writing) deconstructs upon examination, revealing that the "subordinate" term is constitutive of the "dominant" term
- **Foucault**: rejected the notion of universal deep structures, arguing instead for historical discontinuities and power-saturated discourse formations (see ZC_4_06)

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Structuralism and Cognitive Science
- Some cognitive scientists (Sperber, Boyer) have proposed that Lévi-Strauss's structural patterns reflect actual cognitive constraints (categorical perception, binary classification tendencies, modular information processing) — this would rehabilitate structuralism on empiricist grounds, but the connection between abstract mythic structures and specific neural mechanisms remains undemonstrated

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Structuralism as Complete Explanation
- **[OVERSIMPLIFIED]** The claim that structural analysis fully explains any cultural phenomenon — even Lévi-Strauss acknowledged that his analyses were "one way of reading" myths, not the only way; structuralism is a powerful analytical method but does not account for historical change, individual agency, power, or affect

---

## COUNTER-ARGUMENTS

- **Post-structuralist critique**: **Jacques Derrida** ("Structure, Sign, and Play," 1966) challenged structuralism's reliance on stable binary oppositions and fixed centers of meaning, arguing that meaning is always deferred and unstable (*différance*). This critique undermined Lévi-Strauss's confidence that deep structures of the human mind could be definitively mapped through mythological analysis
- **Bourdieu's habitus as deterministic**: **Pierre Bourdieu's** concept of habitus — durable dispositions shaped by social position — has been criticized as overly deterministic, leaving insufficient room for individual agency, creativity, and social change. **Jeffrey Alexander** and cultural sociologists have argued that Bourdieu reduces culture to a tool of social reproduction rather than a realm of autonomous meaning-making
- **Binary opposition universality**: Whether binary opposition is a universal feature of human cognition (as Lévi-Strauss claimed) or a projection of Western logical traditions onto non-Western thought has been debated — **Rodney Needham** (1973) and **Dan Sperber** argued that the concept of opposition itself is more varied and culture-specific than structuralism assumes
