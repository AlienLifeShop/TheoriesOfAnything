## QUICK SUMMARY

**Science and Technology Studies (STS)** is an interdisciplinary field examining how science and technology are shaped by social, political, and cultural processes — and how they in turn reshape society. STS emerged from the convergence of the sociology of science (see ZC_3_02), philosophy of science, and the history of technology in the 1970s–80s. **Social Construction of Technology (SCOT)** (Pinch & Bijker, 1984) argued that technologies do not develop along a single inevitable path — different **relevant social groups** attribute different meanings to the same artifact (the bicycle had different meanings for sportsmen, women in restrictive clothing, and elderly riders), and through social negotiation, one design achieves "closure" and stabilizes; this challenges **technological determinism** — the assumption that technology develops autonomously and drives social change in one direction. **Bruno Latour's Actor-Network Theory (ANT)** (*Reassembling the Social*, 2005) rejected the distinction between human and nonhuman actors, arguing that agency is distributed across networks of people, institutions, instruments, and natural phenomena — Pasteur did not simply "discover" microbes; microbes, laboratories, public health institutions, and farmers all acted in the network that produced germ theory; ANT's principle of **generalized symmetry** treats human and nonhuman actants on equal analytical footing. **Donna Haraway** ("A Cyborg Manifesto," 1985; *Staying with the Trouble*, 2016) developed feminist STS, arguing that the boundaries between human and machine, nature and culture, male and female are socially constructed and politically consequential — the "cyborg" metaphor captures the entanglement of technology with bodies, identities, and power relations. **Sheila Jasanoff** (*States of Knowledge*, 2004) theorized **co-production** — science and social order are produced together; scientific knowledge shapes and is shaped by legal frameworks, political institutions, and cultural values; different "civic epistemologies" (national styles of evaluating science for policy) explain why the same scientific evidence produces different policy outcomes in different countries. **Responsible Innovation (RI)** — the attempt to integrate societal considerations into research and development processes — has been adopted by EU research funding (Horizon 2020/Europe) and involves anticipation, inclusion, reflexivity, and responsiveness to societal concerns.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Scholarly Consensus)

### 1.1 Technologies Are Socially Shaped
- Extensive historical and empirical research confirms that technologies are not neutral, inevitable products of scientific progress but are shaped by social interests, economic incentives, political decisions, design choices, and cultural values — the QWERTY keyboard, the nuclear reactor, the automobile-centered city, and the internet all could have developed differently; "there is nothing inevitable about the particular form taken by any technology" is a well-established STS finding

### 1.2 Co-Production of Science and Policy
- Jasanoff's documentation that scientific knowledge and political order are mutually constitutive is supported by comparative studies: the same evidence about GMO safety, climate change, or pharmaceutical risk produces different policy frameworks in different countries — the US, EU, and Japan handle biotechnology regulation differently not because they have different science but because they have different political cultures, legal traditions, and institutional arrangements for evaluating expertise

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Actor-Network Theory
- ANT has generated productive empirical studies and challenged conventional social science categories, but it faces persistent criticisms: the symmetry between human and nonhuman actors is philosophically contentious (do microbes really "act" in the same sense as scientists?); ANT descriptions can be endlessly detailed without clear criteria for what counts as a relevant actor; and the framework has been accused of political quietism — by treating all actants symmetrically, ANT may obscure power relations and moral responsibility that are uniquely human

### 2.2 Technological Momentum
- Thomas Hughes (*Networks of Power*, 1983) proposed that large technological systems (electricity grids, telecommunications) develop their own **momentum** — once established, they resist change due to institutional, economic, and material lock-in, without being fully "deterministic"; this intermediate position between technological determinism and social constructivism is widely accepted but the precise balance between social choice and technological constraint remains debated case by case

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Anticipatory Governance of Emerging Technologies
- The idea that STS methods can enable proactive governance of emerging technologies (AI, synthetic biology, nanotechnology) — anticipating social impacts before technologies are established — is intellectually appealing and institutionally supported (EU Responsible Innovation framework); but whether anticipatory governance can actually shape technological development (vs. merely providing post-hoc justification for decisions already made on commercial grounds) is an open question with limited evidence of transformative impact

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Technological Progress as Inevitable and Beneficial
- **[DEBUNKED]** The popular belief that technological progress follows a single inevitable trajectory and is inherently beneficial ("technology always makes things better") is contradicted by STS research showing: (1) multiple possible development paths exist at each juncture; (2) technologies reflect the interests and biases of their designers; (3) technologies produce unintended consequences (social media and mental health, fossil fuel technology and climate change, nuclear power and weapons proliferation); and (4) benefits and risks are unevenly distributed across social groups

### Counter-Arguments
- SCOT's emphasis on social negotiation can understate the role of material constraints — not all technologies are equally viable; physics, chemistry, and biology impose limits on what technologies can do, regardless of social interpretation; bridges built with inadequate materials collapse regardless of social construction
- ANT's refusal to distinguish between human and nonhuman agency may be analytically useful but is ethically problematic — moral responsibility, political accountability, and democratic governance require distinguishing between people who can be held responsible and objects that cannot
- STS scholarship is sometimes accused of implicit anti-scientism — by emphasizing the social construction of scientific knowledge, it risks providing ammunition for climate change deniers, anti-vaccine movements, and other groups that reject scientific consensus for political rather than epistemological reasons
