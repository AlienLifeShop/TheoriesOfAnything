## QUICK SUMMARY

Industrial-organizational (I/O) psychology applies psychological principles to workplace behavior — encompassing personnel selection, performance evaluation, motivation, leadership, organizational culture, team dynamics, and occupational health.
The field divides into "industrial" (personnel selection, training, performance appraisal) and "organizational" (motivation, job satisfaction, leadership, group processes) sides. **Personnel selection** is arguably the most scientifically rigorous area — Meta-analyses by Schmidt & Hunter (1998) established the validity hierarchy of selection methods: general mental ability (GMA) tests are the strongest single predictor of job performance across all job types (r = .51), with structured interviews (r = .51), work sample tests (r = .54), and integrity tests (r = .41) also showing high validity.
**Job satisfaction** — among the most studied variables in all of psychology — has a modest positive relationship with job performance (Judge et al., 2001 meta-analysis: ρ = .30), stronger relationships with organizational citizenship behavior and turnover intentions, and is predicted by both dispositional factors (personality, positive/negative affectivity) and situational factors (autonomy, coworker relationships, pay fairness).
**Burnout** (Maslach & Jackson, 1981) — characterized by emotional exhaustion, depersonalization, and reduced personal accomplishment — affects ~28% of workers across occupations and predicts absenteeism, turnover, medical errors, and cardiovascular disease.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Personnel selection validity

- **Schmidt & Hunter (1998) meta-analysis — validity of 19 selection methods:** GMA tests r = .51, work sample tests r = .54, structured interviews r = .51, unstructured interviews r = .38, job knowledge tests r = .48, integrity tests r = .41, conscientiousness r = .31, reference checks r = .26, years of education r = .10, years of job experience r = .18, graphology r = .02, age r = −.01.
- **GMA (general mental ability):** The strongest single predictor of job performance across all occupations; validity increases with job complexity (r = .58 for high-complexity jobs); predicts training success (r = .56) as well as on-the-job performance.
- **Structured interviews:** Questions standardized, behaviorally anchored rating scales used — dramatically more valid and reliable than unstructured interviews; reduce adverse impact compared to GMA tests while maintaining similar predictive validity.
- **Adverse impact and fairness:** The tension between maximizing prediction validity (cognitive tests are most valid) and minimizing group differences (cognitive tests show the largest racial/ethnic group differences, d ≈ 1.0 Black-White); band scoring, alternative predictors, and Pareto-optimal selection strategies attempt to balance validity and diversity goals.

### 1.2 Job satisfaction

- **Judge et al. (2001) meta-analysis:** Job satisfaction–job performance correlation ρ = .30 — meaningful but the relationship is more modest than practitioners assume.
- **Hackman & Oldham (1976) Job Characteristics Model:** Five core job dimensions — skill variety, task identity, task significance, autonomy, and feedback — determine motivating potential score → experienced meaningfulness, responsibility, and knowledge of results → satisfaction, internal motivation, and performance.
- **Organizational justice (Colquitt, 2001):** Distributive justice (fairness of outcomes), procedural justice (fairness of processes), interactional justice (fairness of interpersonal treatment, information sharing) — all independently predict job satisfaction, organizational commitment, and organizational citizenship behavior; procedural justice is particularly important for organizational trust.
- **Dispositional sources:** ~30% of job satisfaction variance attributable to stable individual differences (genetic component from twin studies: Arvey et al., 1989); positive/negative affectivity, core self-evaluations (self-esteem, self-efficacy, locus of control, emotional stability) all predict satisfaction across jobs and time.

### 1.3 Performance appraisal

- **Rating biases:** Halo effect (overall impression bleeds into specific dimensions), leniency/severity, central tendency, recency effect, and similar-to-me bias — all well-documented in meta-analyses.
- **Multi-source feedback (360-degree):** CEO, supervisor, peer, subordinate, and self-ratings show low convergence (r ≈ .20–.35 between sources; Conway & Huffcutt, 1997), suggesting different raters observe different behaviors — multi-source feedback provides broader but not necessarily more accurate assessment.
- **Forced ranking ("rank and yank"):** Popularized by GE (Jack Welch) — terminating bottom 10% annually; criticized for damaging cooperation, introducing political behavior, and conflating normal performance variation with incompetence; most Fortune 500 companies that adopted it have since abandoned the system.

### 1.4 Work motivation theories (applied)

- **Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory (1959):** Motivators (achievement, recognition, responsibility, growth) → satisfaction; hygiene factors (salary, supervision, working conditions, policies) → dissatisfaction when absent but not satisfaction when present. Simple and influential but oversimplified — meta-analyses show hygiene factors also contribute to satisfaction.
- **Equity theory (Adams, 1963):** Workers compare their input-output ratio with referent others — perceived inequity creates tension leading to behavioral (changing effort, quitting) or cognitive (rationalizing, changing referent) adjustments.
- **Goal-setting theory (Locke & Latham):** See [T18](../T_Psychology_Social/T18_Psychology_Motivation_Drive.md) — the single most replicated finding in I/O psychology.

## 2. CREDIBLE BUT DEBATED CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated)

### 2.1 Hawthorne Effect

- **Original studies (1924–1932):** Western Electric Hawthorne Works — researchers manipulated illumination, rest breaks, and work hours; productivity increased regardless of changes → interpreted as workers responding to being observed/studied.
- **Re-analysis (Levitt & List, 2011):** Original data show much more modest and inconsistent effects than traditionally claimed; demand characteristics, selection effects, and researcher-participant interactions complicate interpretation.
- **Current status:** The "Hawthorne effect" persists as a useful concept (research reactivity) but the original studies do not cleanly support the sweeping interpretations in textbooks.

### 2.2 Employee engagement

- **Gallup Q09:** Widely used engagement survey — Harter et al. (2002) meta-analysis found engaged work units show higher customer satisfaction, profitability, and lower turnover.
- **Criticism (Macey & Schneider, 2008):** "Engagement" lacks clear conceptual definition — variously confused with satisfaction, motivation, commitment, and job involvement; the unique contribution beyond existing constructs is unclear.
- **Burnout-engagement continuum (Maslach, 2001):** Engagement as the opposite pole of burnout — characterized by energy, involvement, and efficacy vs. exhaustion, cynicism, and inefficacy.

### 2.3 Organizational culture

- **Schein (2010):** Three levels — artifacts (visible structures, dress codes), espoused values (stated principles), and basic underlying assumptions (unconscious beliefs that drive behavior) — culture is deep and resistant to change.
- **Competing Values Framework (Cameron & Quinn, 2011):** Four culture types: Clan (collaborative), Adhocracy (creative), Market (competitive), Hierarchy (controlling) — validated psychometrically but criticized for oversimplification.
- **Culture fit vs. culture add:** Hiring for "culture fit" may reproduce homogeneity and exclude diverse perspectives; growing emphasis on "culture add" — people who enrich existing culture rather than simply matching it.

### 2.4 Workplace burnout

- **Maslach & Jackson (1981):** Burnout defined by three dimensions: emotional exhaustion (depleted energy), depersonalization/cynicism (psychological withdrawal), and reduced personal accomplishment/efficacy.
- **WHO ICD-11 (2019):** Burnout classified as an "occupational phenomenon" (not a medical condition) — resulting from chronic workplace stress that has not been successfully managed.
- **Job Demands-Resources model (Bakker & Demerouti, 2007):** Burnout results from high demands (workload, emotional demands, time pressure) combined with low resources (autonomy, social support, feedback, development opportunities).
- **Prevalence:** ~28% of workers across occupations report burnout symptoms; highest in healthcare, education, and social services; predicts cardiovascular disease, depression, and mortality.

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 AI and algorithmic hiring

AI-powered resume screening, video interview analysis, and gamified assessments — promise increased efficiency and reduced human bias, but risk encoding historical biases into algorithms, lack transparency (black-box models), and raise ethical/legal concerns about algorithmic fairness; validation evidence remains limited.

### 3.2 Remote work productivity effects

COVID-19 forced mass remote work adoption — early evidence suggests productivity effects vary by job type, individual differences, and home environment; social isolation and blurred boundaries may increase burnout; long-term organizational outcomes remain unclear.

---

## 4. DUBIOUS OR FRINGE CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Graphology predicts job performance

Handwriting analysis used in personnel selection (still prevalent in some European countries) — meta-analysis: r = .02 with job performance (Schmidt & Hunter, 1998); no better than chance; no valid theoretical mechanism.

### 4.2 Learning styles matching improves training

The belief that matching training delivery to individual learning styles (visual, auditory, kinesthetic) improves learning — Pashler et al. (2008) systematic review: no credible evidence supports the mesh hypothesis; the VARK model lacks empirical support despite widespread use in corporate training.
