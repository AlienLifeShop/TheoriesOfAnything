## QUICK SUMMARY

The **genetic history of the Americas** — from the initial peopling of the New World to the devastating population collapse after European contact — is one of the most intensively studied and rapidly evolving areas of paleogenomics. Modern and ancient DNA data now converge on a model in which a **single founding population** ancestral to all Native Americans diverged from East Asian source populations ~**23,000-26,000 years ago**, experienced a prolonged **Beringian standstill** (genetic isolation in the Beringia land bridge region for ~8,000-10,000 years), and then expanded rapidly into the Americas by at least ~**16,000-15,000 years ago** — probably via a **Pacific coastal route** (given that the interior ice-free corridor between the Laurentide and Cordilleran ice sheets did not become fully passable until ~14,000-13,000 years ago). The first securely dated ancient genome from the Americas is the **Anzick-1 child** (Montana, ~12,600 years ago, Clovis culture) — whose genome (Rasmussen et al., 2014) confirmed direct ancestry to modern Native Americans. The **Kennewick Man/The Ancient One** (Washington State, ~8,500 years ago) was genetically closer to modern Native Americans than to any other population, resolving a decades-long repatriation dispute. After initial colonization, the founding population diversified into **two basal branches**: **Southern Native Americans** (ancestral to Central and South American populations) and **Northern Native Americans** (ancestral to some North American groups). Additional complexity comes from later gene flow events: **Population Y** (a deeply divergent ancestry component found in some Amazonian groups, with possible affinity to Australasians/Melanesians, detected by Skoglund et al., 2015 — suggesting an earlier or separate migration wave); **Na-Dene/Paleo-Eskimo** and **Inuit/Paleo-Eskimo** gene flows from Asia (representing later, independent migrations ~5,000-1,000 years ago); and post-contact European and African admixture during the colonial era. The **genetic bottleneck** associated with European contact — driven by **epidemic disease** (smallpox, measles, influenza), warfare, and displacement — reduced Native American populations by an estimated **90-95%** in many regions, with devastating consequences for genetic diversity visible in modern genomes.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 Single Founding Population Model
- Modern and ancient DNA converge on a **single major founding event**:
  - **Mitochondrial DNA**: all Native Americans carry haplogroups **A, B, C, D, and X** — all derived from East Asian lineages
  - **Y chromosome**: the dominant founding haplogroup is **Q** (specifically Q-M3 in the Americas)
  - **Genome-wide data**: principal component and admixture analyses consistently show all Native American populations forming a single cluster relative to other world populations, with internal structure reflecting geographic spread
  - **Raghavan et al. (2015)**: confirmed a single founding event from a Siberian/East Asian source, with no support for separate European or Polynesian origins for main founding ancestry

### 1.2 Beringian Standstill
- **Tamm et al. (2007)**; **Moreno-Mayar et al. (2018)**: genetic data support the **Beringian standstill model**:
  - The ancestral founding population separated from East Asian relatives ~23,000-26,000 years ago
  - This population remained genetically isolated in **Beringia** (the now-submerged land bridge between Siberia and Alaska) for ~8,000-10,000 years — accumulating unique American-specific mitochondrial, Y-chromosomal, and autosomal variants
  - Expansion into the Americas began after ~16,000 years ago, coinciding with deglaciation and the opening of coastal routes

### 1.3 Anzick-1 and Ancient Genomes
- **Rasmussen et al. (2014)**: sequenced the genome of the **Anzick-1 child** (~12,600 BP, Montana, Clovis burial):
  - The oldest directly dated human genome from the Americas at the time of publication
  - Genetically ancestral to modern Central and South American Native populations
  - Confirmed that Clovis people were ancestors of at least some modern Native Americans — not a separate, unrelated population
- **Rasmussen et al. (2015)**: **Kennewick Man/The Ancient One** (~8,500 BP, Washington State):
  - Genome showed closest affinity to modern Native Americans (particularly Colville and other Pacific Northwest tribes)
  - Resolved a 20-year legal and scientific dispute — the remains were repatriated to tribal claimants in 2017

### 1.4 Dual Basal Branches
- **Moreno-Mayar et al. (2018, Nature)**: genome-wide analysis of 49 ancient individuals from across the Americas revealed that the founding population diversified into at least two basal branches:
  - **Southern Native Americans (SNA)**: gave rise to most Central and South American populations
  - **Northern Native Americans (NNA)**: contributed ancestry to some North American populations
  - Both branches diverged rapidly after ~14,000 years ago and then diversified into the mosaic of populations found historically

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Coastal Migration Route
- The **Pacific coastal route** hypothesis is supported by the chronological and geographic pattern:
  - The **ice-free corridor** between the Laurentide and Cordilleran ice sheets was not ecologically viable for human transit until ~14,000-13,000 years ago — too late to explain the earliest sites (Monte Verde, Chile: ~14,500 BP; Paisley Caves, Oregon: ~14,300 BP)
  - A coastal route along the Pacific from Beringia to South America would have been available earlier (~16,000 BP), supported by kelp forest productivity (Erlandson et al., 2007)
  - Archaeological evidence for the coastal route is limited because Pleistocene coastlines are now submerged

### 2.2 Population Y / Australasian Signal
- **Skoglund et al. (2015)**: discovered that some Amazonian groups (Suruí, Karitiana) share a small but significant genetic affinity with Australasian populations (Aboriginal Australians, Andamanese) that is not present in other Native Americans:
  - Called "**Population Y**" (after Ypykuéra, Tupi for "ancestor")
  - Possible explanations: an earlier trans-Pacific or circum-Pacific migration predating the main founding event; or gene flow from an early diverging lineage within the founding population
  - Remains one of the most debated findings in American paleogenomics — aDNA from early South American remains has not yet confirmed the signal

### 2.3 Post-Contact Population Collapse
- Genetic evidence confirms the devastating demographic collapse following European contact:
  - **O'Fallon and Fehren-Schmitz (2011)**: mitochondrial diversity data show a sharp decline in effective population size beginning ~500 years ago
  - Estimated **90-95% population reduction** in many regions — driven by epidemic disease (smallpox, measles, influenza, typhus), warfare, enslavement, and displacement
  - The collapse is visible in modern Native American genomes as reduced genetic diversity and extended homozygosity relative to other world populations

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Very Early Occupation (>20,000 BP)
- Some sites (White Sands footprints, New Mexico — Bennett et al., 2021: ~21,000-23,000 BP; Chiquihuite Cave, Mexico — Ardelean et al., 2020: ~26,000-33,000 BP) suggest human presence in the Americas before the Last Glacial Maximum — but these dates are debated and not yet universally accepted

### 3.2 Multiple Migration Waves
- Beyond the main founding event and later Na-Dene/Inuit migrations, some models propose additional early entry events — but genomic evidence for more than one major founding event remains inconclusive

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Solutrean Hypothesis (European Origin)
- **[CONTRADICTED]** The hypothesis that Clovis people descended from Ice Age Europeans (Solutrean culture) who crossed the Atlantic has no support from ancient DNA — all early American genomes show East Asian/Beringian ancestry, not European

### 4.2 Kennewick Man Was "Caucasoid"
- **[CONTRADICTED]** Initial morphological analyses suggested non-Native affinity, but aDNA analysis (Rasmussen et al., 2015) definitively showed closest genetic relationship to modern Native Americans — undermining claims of non-Native ancestry

---

## COUNTER-ARGUMENTS

- **Pre-Clovis chronology debated**: while sites like **Monte Verde** (Chile, ~14,500 BP, validated by **Tom Dillehay**), **White Sands** (New Mexico, ~23,000–21,000 BP, Bennett et al. 2021, *Science*), and **Bluefish Caves** (Yukon, ~24,000 BP) suggest pre-Clovis human presence, each has faced methodological scrutiny — the White Sands footprint dates depend on seeds of *Ruppia cirrhosa* (aquatic plant), and **Vance Holliday** and **Loren Davis** have debated whether freshwater reservoir effects could make the radiocarbon dates artificially old
- **Solutrean hypothesis rejected**: the proposal by **Dennis Stanford** and **Bruce Bradley** (Smithsonian, 2012, *Across Atlantic Ice*) that Clovis technology derives from European Solutrean cultures via Atlantic ice-edge migration has been decisively rejected by the archaeological and genetic communities — **Jennifer Raff** (University of Kansas, 2022, *Origin*) demonstrated that all ancient American genomes show exclusively East Asian/Siberian ancestry, with no detectable European admixture prior to post-Columbian contact
