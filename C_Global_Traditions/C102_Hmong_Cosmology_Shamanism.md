## QUICK SUMMARY

The Hmong — a Hmong-Mien-speaking people originating in highland southern China with diaspora communities across Laos, Thailand, Vietnam, and the United States — maintain one of the most elaborate shamanic traditions surviving in the modern world. The txiv neeb (shaman, literally "father/person of the healing spirit") enters trance states to negotiate with spirits, retrieve lost souls, and guide the deceased to the ancestral homeland through the Qhuab Ke ("Showing the Way") funeral chant. Hmong cosmology divides existence between the visible world (ntiaj teb) and the spirit world (yeeb ceeb), with human beings possessing multiple souls (typically three or more) that can become separated through fright, illness, or spiritual attack. Creation myths describe sky people descending to earth, a universal flood survived by a brother-sister pair, and the origins of death and shamanism. The paj ntaub (flower cloth/story cloth) tradition encodes cosmological, historical, and migratory narratives in textile form, representing one of the most significant non-literate knowledge preservation systems in Southeast Asia.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Historical and Linguistic Context
- The Hmong belong to the Hmong-Mien (Miao-Yao) language family, one of the major language families of East and Southeast Asia. Historical records place ancestral Hmong-Mien populations in central and southern China, with references in Chinese texts dating back over 2,000 years.
- Chinese dynastic histories reference "Miao" peoples from the Han dynasty onward, though the relationship between historical "Miao" and modern Hmong is complex — "Miao" was used as a catch-all term for various non-Chinese highland peoples.
- Successive waves of southward migration from China into mainland Southeast Asia occurred from the 18th century onward, driven by Qing dynasty expansion and internal conflicts. Hmong settled in the highlands of Laos, Vietnam, Thailand, and Myanmar.
- Following the Vietnam War/Secret War in Laos (1964–1975), approximately 130,000 Hmong refugees resettled in the United States, with additional communities in France, Australia, and Canada — creating one of the largest post-war refugee diasporas from Southeast Asia.
- The Secret War (CIA-backed Hmong resistance to the Pathet Lao and North Vietnamese) resulted in the deaths of an estimated 30,000 Hmong soldiers and the displacement of hundreds of thousands of civilians.
- Hmong communities in the United States are concentrated in Minnesota, Wisconsin, and California, where they constitute the largest Asian-American group in several cities.
- The refugee experience profoundly impacted Hmong religious practice — access to the plants, animals, and landscapes integral to traditional ceremony was disrupted, requiring creative adaptation.
- Nicholas Tapp (1989), Gary Yia Lee (1982, 1994), and Keith Quincy (1988) provide foundational ethnographic and historical accounts.

### 1.2 Shamanic Practice: The Txiv Neeb
- The txiv neeb (often anglicized as "shaman") is the central spiritual practitioner in Hmong tradition. The role is typically acquired through a calling illness (neeb tsis tau) — a severe, unexplainable sickness that resolves only when the afflicted person accepts the neeb spirit's call.
- The calling process involves a senior txiv neeb recognizing the neeb spirit's presence, performing diagnostic rituals, and initiating the new shaman through an apprenticeship period that can last years.
- During healing ceremonies (ua neeb), the txiv neeb enters trance by sitting on a wooden bench (rooj txag), covering the face with a cloth veil, and being guided into the spirit world by rhythmic percussion, chanting, and the ringing of finger bells (tswb).
- In trance, the txiv neeb's neeb spirit (a powerful auxiliary spirit) travels to the spirit world (yeeb ceeb) to negotiate with spirits who have captured the patient's soul, perform combat with hostile spirits, or retrieve lost souls from dangerous spiritual territories.
- The txiv neeb maintains a altar (thaj neeb) in the home, which houses the neeb spirit's implements — a split buffalo horn (kuam), a gong, a rattle sword, and other sacred objects.
- Dwight Conquergood (1989), Patricia Symonds (2004), and Jean Mottin (1982) provide detailed ethnographic descriptions of txiv neeb practice.
- In diaspora communities, txiv neeb continue to practice in the United States, often negotiating between traditional healing methods and Western biomedical contexts — some hospitals in Minnesota and California now accommodate txiv neeb ceremonies.

### 1.3 The Qhuab Ke — "Showing the Way" Funeral Chant
- The Qhuab Ke (also Txiv Xaiv Qhuab Ke) is a multi-hour funeral chant performed by a specialist (txiv qhuab ke) that guides the deceased person's soul on the journey back to the ancestral homeland.
- The chant narrates the soul's passage in reverse — from the place of death, through the villages and mountains of the Hmong migration route, past dangerous spirit checkpoints, and ultimately to the realm of the ancestors where the soul awaits reincarnation.
- The Qhuab Ke encodes geographical, historical, and cosmological knowledge — the route described in the chant is understood by some scholars (Lemoine, 1983; Tapp, 2001) to preserve cultural memory of the south-to-north migration route back to the Chinese ancestral homeland.
- The funeral sequence includes the sacrifice of animals (cattle, pigs, chickens) to feed the soul on its journey, the burning of spirit money, and the playing of the qeej (reed mouth organ), whose melodic patterns are understood to communicate directly with the dead.
- The reed mouth organ (qeej) is the instrumental voice of the dead — its tones follow the tonal patterns of the Hmong language, effectively "speaking" ritual phrases. A qeej player (txiv qeej) accompanies the deceased at every stage of the funeral.

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Multiple Soul Concepts
- Hmong cosmology attributes multiple souls to each human being — the most common formulation describes three souls (plig), though some sub-groups describe seven or more.
- The most vulnerable soul (the ntsuj plig) can become separated from the body due to fright, shock, or spiritual attack — a condition called poob plig (soul loss).
- Diagnosis typically involves divination using split buffalo horns (kuam), egg reading (saib qe), or direct spiritual communication during a txiv neeb's trance.
- The hu plig (soul-calling) ceremony is one of the most commonly performed Hmong rituals, in which a shaman or elder calls the lost soul back to the body.
- A white cotton string (khi tes) is tied around the patient's wrist after the ceremony to secure the soul to the body.
- Upon death, the souls divide: one soul remains at the grave, one goes to the spirit world to await reincarnation, and one returns to the ancestral homeland.
- Soul loss (poob plig) is the most common diagnosis for illness in Hmong traditional healing — the soul (or one of the souls) may wander away due to fright, wander during dreams and fail to return, or be captured by malevolent spirits (dab).
- The hu plig ceremony (soul calling) is one of the most frequently performed Hmong rituals — a shaman or family elder calls the lost soul back, typically by tying a string around the wrist (khi tes) to secure the soul to the body.
- Anne Fadiman's *The Spirit Catches You and You Fall Down* (1997), while a journalistic account, brought Hmong soul concepts and healing practices to broad English-language attention and highlighted conflicts between Hmong and Western medical frameworks.

### 2.2 Creation and Flood Myths
- Hmong creation narratives describe an original state of undifferentiated sky and earth, from which the world is shaped, often through the actions of primordial beings or nature spirits.
- The flood myth, widespread among Hmong sub-groups, describes a catastrophic deluge from which only a brother-sister pair survives — typically by floating in a wooden drum or a sealed gourd. After the flood, the siblings marry (often reluctantly, with divine signs confirming the necessity) and become the ancestors of all humanity. Different ethnic groups emerge from the varied children of this union.
- Charles Johnson (1985) collected the most extensive published compilation of Hmong myths and legends, documenting dozens of creation, flood, and origin narratives from refugee communities in the United States.
- The flood myth's structural parallels with Chinese, Southeast Asian, and global flood narratives are noted by scholars (Dundes, 1988; Tapp, 2001), though direct historical connections are difficult to establish.

### 2.3 Paj Ntaub — Story Cloth and Knowledge Encoding
- Paj ntaub (花布, "flower cloth") is the traditional Hmong textile art of intricate appliqué and reverse-appliqué needlework, creating abstract geometric designs of extraordinary complexity.
- Following the Hmong diaspora, a new genres of "story cloth" emerged — narrative textiles depicting historical events (the Secret War, refugee camps, migration to America), daily life, and mythological scenes in pictorial (rather than abstract geometric) form.
- The geometric patterns of traditional paj ntaub are interpreted by some scholars as encoding cosmological information — spirals, crosses, and labyrinthine designs may represent the txiv neeb's spirit journey paths, cosmic geography, or clan identity markers.
- Joua Bee Vang and others (Peterson, 1988; Mallinson et al., 1988) document the paj ntaub tradition as a non-literate knowledge preservation system that maintains cultural identity across displacement and diaspora.

### 2.4 Animistic Cosmology
- The Hmong cosmos is divided between the visible world (ntiaj teb) and the spirit world (yeeb ceeb, literally "shadow world") — these worlds exist in continuous interaction, with spirits (dab) constantly present in the visible world.
- Dab is a general term for spirits — it can refer to malevolent spirits, ancestral spirits, nature spirits, or the spirits of specific places. The term does not carry the exclusively negative connotation of the English "demon."
- The household spirits (dab qhuas, dab xwm kab) are honored at specific locations within the home — the xwm kab altar, located on the wall opposite the front door, is the most important domestic religious space, housing the ancestral spirits who protect the family.
- Ntxwj Nyug (sometimes rendered "Nyuj Vaj Tuam Teem") is the ruler of the spirit world — the judge who determines the fate of souls after death, assigns them to punishment or reincarnation, and maintains cosmic justice.

### 2.5 Clan System and Social Organization
- Hmong society is organized into patrilineal clans (xeem), estimated at 18–20 major clans (e.g., Vang, Yang, Lee, Xiong, Her, Moua, Thao, Vue, Chang, Lor). Clan membership determines marriage rules (exogamous — one must marry outside one's own clan), funeral obligations, and mutual aid networks.
- Each clan traces descent from a common ancestor, and inter-clan relationships are maintained through a complex web of marriage alliances and reciprocal obligations established across generations.
- The tus neeb (wedding ceremony) involves extensive negotiations between the bride's and groom's clans, including bride price (nqi mis) and elaborate ritual protocols that bind the two clans in a new relationship.
- The kev cai (customary law) system governs clan affairs, conflict resolution, and ceremonial responsibilities — it operates as an oral legal tradition maintained by clan elders (txiv laug) and is remarkably consistent across geographically dispersed Hmong communities.

### 2.6 New Year and Seasonal Ceremonies
- Hmong New Year (Noj Peb Caug / Noj Tsiab) is the most important calendrical celebration, occurring after the harvest season (typically November–December). It marks the end of the agricultural year and the transition to a new cycle.
- New Year festivities include ball-tossing courtship games (pov pob) between young men and women of different clans, performances of traditional music (including the qeej and the ncas jaw harp), bull fighting, and communal feasting.
- The ceremony includes a ritual "calling the souls home" (hu plig) for all family members, ensuring that all souls are properly attached as the new year begins.
- In diaspora communities (United States, France, Australia), Hmong New Year celebrations have become major cultural events that affirm ethnic identity and intergenerational knowledge transmission — the most prominent celebrations in the US draw tens of thousands of attendees.

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Opium and Shamanic Trance
- In highland Southeast Asia, opium poppy cultivation was a major economic activity for Hmong communities. Some researchers (Lemoine, 1972; Tapp, 1989) report that opium was occasionally used in shamanic contexts — either consumed by the txiv neeb to facilitate trance entry or used as an offering to spirits.
- The extent and significance of opium use in Hmong shamanism is debated — most txiv neeb describe trance entry as achieved through percussion, chanting, and the neeb spirit's power rather than pharmacological means.
- The association of Hmong with opium cultivation has been politically charged, and scholars caution against reducing Hmong shamanism to a drug-induced phenomenon.

### 3.2 Sky People Origins
- Some Hmong creation narratives describe an origin from the sky — the first Hmong descended from the celestial realm to earth, and the Qhuab Ke funeral chant guides the soul back to this celestial origin.
- Whether these "sky origin" narratives are ancient cosmological conceptions, poetic metaphors for mountain-dwelling peoples who live "above the clouds," or relatively recent elaborations is uncertain.

### 3.3 Proto-Hmong Writing Systems
- Some Hmong traditions describe a lost writing system — legends tell of Hmong books that were eaten or destroyed during migration, explaining why the Hmong lacked a literate tradition until the development of the Romanized Popular Alphabet (RPA) in 1953 by missionaries and the Pahawh Hmong script by Shong Lue Yang in the 1960s.
- Archaeological and linguistic evidence for a pre-modern Hmong writing system has not been found, though the legends may preserve cultural memory of contact with literate Chinese civilization.

### 3.4 Hmong Diaspora and Cultural Preservation
- The Hmong diaspora following the Vietnam War/Secret War in Laos (1964–1975) created one of the most significant challenges to cultural continuity — traditional practices adapted to urban American, French, and Australian settings in ways that both preserved and transformed them.
- Txiv neeb (shamans) continue to practice in Minneapolis, Sacramento, Fresno, and other diaspora centers — negotiating between traditional healing and Western biomedical systems. Hospitals in areas with significant Hmong populations have increasingly accommodated shamanic practices alongside medical treatment.
- The Hmong American experience has been marked by generational tension: elders who carry traditional knowledge often struggle to transmit it to younger generations educated in Western schools and immersed in English-language media.
- Documentary films such as *The Split Horn: Life of a Hmong Shaman in America* (2001, Taggart Siegel) and academic studies (Conquergood, 1989; Fadiman, 1997) have brought Hmong spiritual practices to broader public awareness.
- Despite the challenges of displacement, Hmong communities have demonstrated remarkable cultural resilience — the continued practice of txiv neeb healing, the vitality of Hmong New Year celebrations, and the production of paj ntaub story cloths in diaspora represent active cultural maintenance rather than mere preservation.

### 3.5 The Qeej and Musical Cosmology
- The qeej (Hmong free-reed mouth organ, made of bamboo pipes set in a wooden wind chest) is the most sacred Hmong musical instrument — its tones exactly follow the tonal patterns of the Hmong language, allowing it to "speak" ritual phrases.
- The qeej is essential at funerals, where the txiv qeej (qeej player) plays for the deceased continuously, guiding the soul through each stage of the funeral ritual — specific melodies correspond to specific stages of the soul's journey.
- Learning to play the qeej requires years of apprenticeship, and master qeej players hold significant cultural prestige — the instrument is considered to have been given to the Hmong by the Lord of the Spirit World.
- In diaspora, qeej performance has become a marker of cultural identity and continuity — qeej competitions are held at major Hmong festivals, and efforts to teach the instrument to younger generations have intensified.

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source)

- Claims linking Hmong shamanism to extraterrestrial contact based on the "sky people" origin narratives remove cultural context and impose foreign categories on indigenous tradition.
- Assertions that Hmong paj ntaub patterns contain encoded star maps or mathematical formulas lack systematic demonstration, though the geometric sophistication of the textiles is genuine.
- The identification of Hmong as descendants of lost civilizations (Atlantis, Mu) has no linguistic, genetic, or historical support.
- Claims that sudden unexpected nocturnal death syndrome (SUNDS) — which affected some Hmong refugees in the 1980s — was caused by literal spirit attacks impose a literal interpretation on a complex phenomenon with both cultural and biomedical dimensions.
- The reduction of Hmong shamanism to "mere superstition" or "mental illness" reflects ethnocentric bias rather than engagement with the rich phenomenological and social dimensions of txiv neeb practice.

### Key Terms and Concepts

- **Txiv neeb**: Shaman; literally "father/master of the healing spirits."
- **Plig**: One of multiple souls; the vital soul most vulnerable to loss.
- **Neeb**: Healing or shamanic spirits who assist the txiv neeb.
- **Qhuab Ke**: "Showing the Way"; funeral chant guiding the soul home.
- **Paj ntaub**: "Flower cloth"; embroidered textile encoding cosmological narratives.
- **Qeej**: Bamboo free-reed mouth organ; speaks the "language of the dead."
- **Dab**: Spirit; generic term for spiritual entities, benign or malign.
- **Tus Plig**: Soul-calling ceremony to restore a lost plig.
- **Ntuj**: Sky or heaven; the upper realm in Hmong cosmology.
