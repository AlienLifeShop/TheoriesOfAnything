## QUICK SUMMARY

Cherokee (Tsalagi) cosmology structures the universe as a three-tiered system: Galunlati (the Upper World of order, purity, and spiritual beings), Elohi (the Middle World of everyday human existence), and the Under World (a realm of chaos, change, and dangerous power inhabited by creatures like the Uktena horned serpent). The creation narrative describes the earth as a great island suspended from the sky vault by four cords at its cardinal points, shaped by the Great Buzzard whose wings carved the mountains and valleys of Cherokee country. Central myths include Selu (Corn Mother) and Kanati (Lucky Hunter), whose story encodes agricultural and hunting knowledge, and the Water Spider who brought fire to humanity. The Cherokee emphasis on the sacred number seven — seven clans, seven directions, seven ceremonies — permeates the entire cosmological and social system, while Sequoyah's invention of the Cherokee syllabary (1821) represents one of the most remarkable achievements in knowledge preservation in human history.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Ethnographic Documentation
- James Mooney's *Myths of the Cherokee* (1900), based on fieldwork among the Eastern Band Cherokee in North Carolina from 1887–1890, remains the most comprehensive collection of Cherokee mythology, recording 126 myths and legends from native speakers.
- Mooney worked closely with the Cherokee medicine man and storyteller Swimmer (Ayúnini), whose knowledge represented one of the last fully intact traditional bodies of Cherokee sacred narrative.
- The Payne-Butrick Papers (1830s) provide earlier documentation of Cherokee beliefs by missionary Daniel S. Butrick, recording cosmological concepts and ceremonial practices before the Trail of Tears (1838–39), thus preserving pre-removal traditions.
- Raymond Fogelson (1971, 1980) and Charles Hudson (1976) integrated archaeological, ethnohistorical, and ethnographic data to reconstruct the broader Southeastern Indian cosmological system within which Cherokee beliefs operate.

### 1.2 Three-Tiered Cosmological Structure
- The Cherokee universe is organized vertically into three realms:
  - **Upper World (Galunlati):** A realm of perfect order, associated with the color white, the direction above, and spiritual purity. Thunderbeings (Ani-Hyuntikwalaski) dwell here as agents of cosmic order.
  - **Middle World (Elohi):** The earth, suspended from the sky vault by four cords attached at the cardinal directions. Humans inhabit this world and must maintain balance between Upper and Under World forces.
  - **Under World:** A realm beneath the earth and waters, associated with chaos, change, fertility, and dangerous power. The Uktena and other serpentine beings dwell here.
- Charles Hudson (1976) demonstrates that this tripartite structure mirrors the broader Southeastern Ceremonial Complex shared across Mississippian cultures, including Cahokia, Moundville, and Etowah.
- The earth is described as a great floating island on water, suspended by four cords from the sky vault — when the cords break, the earth will sink back into the water and all will be darkness again (the "Darkening Land" prophecy).

### 1.3 The Great Buzzard and Earth Formation
- In the creation narrative, before the earth was dry, the Great Buzzard (Tsulʼkälûʼ) flew low over the soft, wet ground. Where his wings struck the earth, valleys formed; where they rose, mountains appeared — thus shaping the mountain landscape of Cherokee country (western North Carolina, eastern Tennessee).
- This etiological myth directly connects Cherokee homeland geography to cosmogonic events, sacralizing the physical landscape as the product of a primordial being's creative action.
- The narrative is documented in Mooney (1900) and corroborated by multiple independent Cherokee informants across different communities.

### 1.4 The Cherokee Syllabary
- Sequoyah (George Gist, c. 1770–1843) created the Cherokee syllabary — a writing system of 85 characters representing syllables — completing it around 1821.
- Sequoyah was the only known individual in world history to create an entirely new writing system without being literate in any existing script.
- The syllabary was adopted with extraordinary rapidity: within five years, a majority of the Cherokee Nation was literate.
- Within a few years of its introduction, Cherokee literacy rates exceeded those of surrounding European-American settlements, and the Cherokee Nation established a bilingual newspaper, the *Cherokee Phoenix* (1828).
- The syllabary remains in active use today and is taught in Cherokee immersion schools in Oklahoma and North Carolina.
- The syllabary enabled the transcription of sacred formulas, medicinal knowledge, and ceremonial texts into written form — the *Swimmer Manuscript* (collected by Mooney) contains hundreds of these formulas in Cherokee script.
- Jack Frederick Kilpatrick and Anna Gritts Kilpatrick (1965, 1967) published extensive collections of Cherokee ritual texts written in the syllabary, revealing a literate sacred tradition that operated alongside oral transmission.

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Selu and Kanati: Corn Mother and Lucky Hunter
- Selu (Corn) and Kanati (Lucky Hunter) are the primordial couple whose story encodes the fundamental Cherokee understanding of subsistence, gender complementarity, and the origins of agriculture and hunting.
- Selu produces corn and beans by rubbing her stomach and armpits — when her sons spy on her and discover this "secret," they perceive it as witchcraft. Selu instructs them to kill her and drag her body across the ground; where her blood falls, corn grows.
- Kanati keeps all game animals sealed in a cave; when his sons release them prematurely, animals scatter across the earth, making hunting difficult and uncertain.
- The myth establishes the complementary economic roles of women (agriculture) and men (hunting) while warning against the violation of sacred boundaries — the sons' transgressive curiosity brings hardship.
- Alan Kilpatrick (1997) and Michelene Pesantubbee (2005) analyze Selu as an expression of feminine sacred power that parallels corn origin narratives across Eastern Woodlands and Mesoamerican cultures.

### 2.2 Uktena — The Horned Serpent
- The Uktena is described as a great serpent with horns (or antlers), a blazing crystal (ulûñsûʼtĭ) set in its forehead, and iridescent scales — it inhabits deep pools, mountain passes, and liminal waterways.
- The ulûñsûʼtĭ (transparent crystal) in the Uktena's forehead is the most powerful talisman in Cherokee tradition — whoever possesses it gains extraordinary powers of prophecy, healing, and hunting success.
- The hero Āgänunitsi ("Groundhog's Mother") is the only figure who successfully obtains the ulûñsûʼtĭ by killing the Uktena — a narrative that parallels dragon-slaying motifs worldwide.
- The Uktena bears structural similarities to the Great Serpent of Mississippian iconography, depicted at sites like Spiro and Etowah, suggesting the Cherokee tradition preserves elements of the broader Southeastern Ceremonial Complex (Lankford, 2007).
- James Lankford (2007) connects the Uktena to a pan-Southeastern "Tie-Snake" tradition in which horned serpents guard the boundary between the Middle World and the Under World.

### 2.3 The Sacred Number Seven
- Seven is the fundamental sacred number in Cherokee cosmology, pervading social and ceremonial structures:
  - **Seven Clans:** Ani-Waya (Wolf), Ani-Kawi (Deer), Ani-Tsisqua (Bird), Ani-Wodi (Paint/Red), Ani-Sahoni (Blue/Panther), Ani-Gatgewi (Wild Potato), Ani-Gilohi (Long Hair/Twister).
  - **Seven Directions:** The four cardinal directions plus above, below, and center (the sacred fire).
  - **Seven Ceremonies:** Historically, the Cherokee held seven major ceremonies annually, including the Green Corn Ceremony (the most important), the New Moon Festival, and the Bounding Bush Feast.
- The Green Corn Ceremony (Selu Festival) marked the first harvest of corn and involved purification, forgiveness of debts and grudges, rekindling of the sacred fire, and ritual bathing — structurally parallel to New Year ceremonies across Mesoamerica and the Southeast.
- During the Green Corn Ceremony, old fires in every household were extinguished and relit from the new sacred fire — symbolizing cosmic renewal and the forgiveness of all offenses except murder.
- Raymond Fogelson (1971) documents the seven-clan system as a fundamental organizational principle that regulated marriage (exogamy), seating in council houses, ceremonial roles, and social identity.

### 2.4 Water Spider and the Origin of Fire
- In the Cherokee fire-origin myth, after Thunderbeings place fire in a hollow sycamore tree on an island, various animals attempt to retrieve it and fail — the raven's feathers are scorched black, the screech owl's eyes are reddened by smoke.
- The Water Spider (Kananeʼskĭ Amaʼyĕhĭ) succeeds by spinning a small bowl (tusiʼ) on her back, crossing the water, placing a single coal in the bowl, and carrying it safely back.
- This myth establishes the Water Spider as a culture hero — notably a small, female figure succeeding where larger, more powerful beings failed — encoding values of ingenuity over brute force.

### 2.5 The Ball Game (Anetso) and Ceremonial Life
- The Cherokee ball game (anetso, also known as "little brother of war") was a ritually charged athletic contest played between towns, involving teams of 12–22 players using paired sticks (kabucha) to carry and throw a small ball.
- Games were preceded by elaborate ceremonial preparation: fasting, all-night dancing, ritual scratching of players' bodies (to draw blood and promote ritual purity), and conjuring by medicine men to influence the outcome.
- The ball game served as a surrogate for warfare — inter-town disputes, territorial claims, and grievances were resolved through competition rather than armed conflict.
- James Mooney (1890) documented extensive ball game rituals, including the use of sacred formulas (idi:gawe:sdi) recited by the conjuror (didanvwisgi) to weaken opponents and strengthen his own team.
- The game was played on a field with a single post (in some variants, two posts or goalposts), and competitions could last from sunrise to sunset, with betting stakes that could include entire personal fortunes.

### 2.6 Cherokee Medicine and the Sacred Formulas
- The Cherokee developed a sophisticated medical tradition encompassing herbalism, ritual bathing (going to water), and the recitation of sacred formulas (igawesdi) — specific songs and prayers directed at specific illnesses.
- Mooney's *Sacred Formulas of the Cherokees* (1891) published translations of manuscript formulas written in the Cherokee syllabary, revealing a medical system that classified diseases by their spiritual causation (animal revenge, human sorcery, violation of taboo, accidental transgression).
- The concept of animal revenge (ani:sgaya) held that diseases were sent by animals as punishment for overhunting or disrespect — each animal species was associated with a particular disease, and the cure often involved invoking a "plant ally" that opposed the offending animal.
- "Going to water" (ama-diyi) was the most common Cherokee healing practice: the patient immersed in running water (river or stream) while the medicine person recited the appropriate formula — typically at dawn, facing east, for four or seven consecutive days.

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Mississippian Continuity
- The extent to which Cherokee mythology preserves Mississippian-era beliefs (pre-1400 CE) versus representing post-Mississippian developments remains debated. Cherokee oral tradition arguably preserves elements — including the Uktena, the sky/earth/water triad, and the falcon warrior motif — that connect to the Southeastern Ceremonial Complex.
- Vernon James Knight (1986) and George Lankford (2007) argue for deep continuity between Mississippian iconography and Cherokee/Creek/Chickasaw mythology, while others caution that oral traditions inevitably shift over centuries.

### 3.2 The Darkening Land Prophecy
- Cherokee tradition speaks of a time when the earth will "grow old and die" — the four cords holding the earth-island to the sky vault will break, and the world will sink into the primordial waters.
- Some contemporary Cherokee interpret this as a prophecy of ecological collapse or the end of the current world age, drawing parallels with Hopi prophecy and Maya calendar cycles.
- Whether this represents an ancient eschatological tradition or a post-contact elaboration influenced by Christian apocalypticism is uncertain.

### 3.3 Cherokee-Iroquoian Deep History
- Cherokee is an Iroquoian language, distantly related to Mohawk, Seneca, and other northern Iroquoian languages — the split is estimated at 3,500–4,000 years ago.
- Some scholars propose that shared mythological motifs between Cherokee and Iroquois traditions (earth-diver creation, Sky Woman, twin heroes) preserve elements of a Proto-Iroquoian mythology predating the language split.

### 3.4 The Trail of Tears and Knowledge Disruption
- The forced removal of the Cherokee from their southeastern homeland to Indian Territory (present-day Oklahoma) in 1838–39 — the Trail of Tears — resulted in the death of approximately 4,000 Cherokee (out of roughly 16,000 removed) and caused devastating disruption to traditional cultural knowledge.
- Sacred sites, ceremonial grounds, and the landscape connections that anchored Cherokee cosmology were severed by removal — medicine people lost access to specific plants, water sources, and mountain sites essential to their practice.
- Despite this catastrophe, Cherokee cultural knowledge proved remarkably resilient: the syllabary enabled written preservation, and medicine people in both Oklahoma and the Eastern Band (those who avoided removal) maintained oral and ceremonial traditions.
- The persistence of the Keetoowah Society (Ani-Kituhwagi, the "original people") as a ceremonial and cultural organization has been instrumental in preserving traditional Cherokee religion through the removal era and subsequent pressures.

### 3.5 Astronomical Knowledge and Calendar
- Cherokee tradition includes a complex understanding of stellar phenomena: the Pleiades (Ani-tsutsa, "The Boys") are associated with a myth of seven boys who danced into the sky; Orion's belt is part of a narrative involving a hunting dog.
- The Cherokee calendar was lunisolar, with ceremonies timed to both lunar phases and agricultural seasons — the Green Corn Ceremony coincided with the first ripening of corn, while other ceremonies aligned with the new moon.
- Some researchers (Hudson, 1976) propose that Cherokee town house orientations and ceremonial ground layouts incorporated astronomical alignments, though systematic archaeoastronomical studies of Cherokee sites are limited compared to those of Southwestern and Mesoamerican sites.
- The relationship between Cherokee astronomy and the broader Southeastern Ceremonial Complex's astronomical dimensions (documented at Cahokia's Woodhenge) remains an active area of inquiry.

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source)

- Claims that the Cherokee are descendants of a "lost tribe of Israel" or that the Cherokee syllabary derives from Hebrew script have been thoroughly debunked. Sequoyah's syllabary was an independent invention, and Cherokee language belongs definitively to the Iroquoian family.
- Assertions that the Uktena crystal (ulûñsûʼtĭ) represents advanced technology or extraterrestrial artifacts lack any basis in Cherokee tradition or material culture.
- New Age appropriations of Cherokee "medicine wheel" traditions often distort or fabricate practices that have no documented basis in historical Cherokee ceremonialism.
- The claim that Cherokee myths encode knowledge of advanced pre-Columbian technology (flight, electricity, etc.) misreads the cosmological and moral dimensions of the narratives.
- The persistent "Welsh Indians" theory — proposing that Cherokee were descended from a 12th-century Welsh expedition led by Prince Madoc — has been thoroughly debunked by linguistic and genetic evidence.

### Key Terms and Concepts

- **Galunlati**: The sky vault or upper world in Cherokee cosmology.
- **Usunhi'yi**: The "Darkening Land" in the west, associated with death.
- **Didanawisgi**: Cherokee conjuror or medicine person.
- **Anetso**: "The Little Brother of War"; Cherokee stickball game.
- **Igawesdi**: Sacred formulas used for healing, love, and protection.
- **Ani-Kutani**: Ancient Cherokee priestly clan, overthrown in a remembered revolt.
- **Selu**: Corn Mother; first woman in Cherokee origin narrative.
- **Keetoowah**: Sacred fire and the oldest Cherokee ceremonial society.
- **Nvwoti**: Cherokee medicine or spiritual power.
