## QUICK SUMMARY

The Taíno, an Arawakan-speaking people who inhabited the Greater Antilles (Hispaniola, Cuba, Jamaica, Puerto Rico) and the Bahamas at the time of European contact in 1492, maintained a complex cosmological system centered on a supreme deity (Yúcahu), a primordial mother goddess (Atabey), and an elaborate spirit world accessed through cohoba rituals involving the inhalation of psychoactive snuff derived from Anadenanthera peregrina seeds. Their material culture is distinguished by cemís (three-pointed stone and cotton objects housing spiritual power), sophisticated agricultural systems (conucos), monumental ball courts (batey), and a stratified society governed by caciques and spiritually guided by bohiques (shamans/priests). Though often dismissed as a "simple" culture by early colonial sources, archaeological and ethnohistorical research has revealed a society of considerable complexity that was nearly obliterated within decades of European contact yet whose legacy persists in Caribbean culture, language, and the ongoing Taíno revitalization movement.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Historical and Archaeological Context
- The Taíno were the largest Indigenous group in the Caribbean at European contact, with population estimates ranging from 250,000 to several million across the Greater Antilles (estimates vary widely; Keegan, 1992; Wilson, 1990).
- Taíno culture represents the culmination of a long migration and cultural development sequence: Archaic peoples → Saladoid culture (from the Orinoco River Basin, arriving c. 500 BCE) → Ostionoid → Taíno (emerging c. 1200 CE).
- Archaeological sites across Puerto Rico, Hispaniola, and Cuba reveal sophisticated settlement patterns including plazas, ball courts, causeways, and ceremonial centers — notably Caguana (Puerto Rico), with ten batey courts and monumental petroglyphs.
- Ramón Pané's *Relación acerca de las antigüedades de los indios* (c. 1498), commissioned by Columbus, is the earliest European ethnographic document of New World peoples — the sole surviving account of Taíno mythology recorded from direct informants.

### 1.2 Cemí Worship and Material Culture
- Cemís (also zemís) are the physical embodiments or dwelling places of spiritual power — manufactured from stone (especially the distinctive three-pointed form), wood, cotton, bone, shell, and coral.
- Three-pointed cemís (trigonolitos) are found across the Greater Antilles and represent the most iconic Taíno artifact. Their precise symbolic meaning remains debated, but they are associated with agricultural fertility — some were buried in conucos (mound gardens) to promote yuca/cassava growth.
- Larger wooden and cotton cemís housed the relics of powerful ancestors and were kept in special structures. The bohique (shaman-priest) served as intermediary between the community and the cemí spirits.
- José R. Oliver (2009) provides the most comprehensive archaeological analysis of cemí practice, arguing that cemís functioned as "relational beings" — not mere objects but active agents in social and cosmological networks.

### 1.3 The Cohoba Ritual
- The cohoba ceremony involved the inhalation of a psychoactive powder derived primarily from the seeds of Anadenanthera peregrina (yopo), which contains DMT (N,N-dimethyltryptamine), 5-MeO-DMT, and bufotenin.
- The ritual was performed by caciques (chiefs) and bohiques using elaborately carved inhaling tubes and platforms, often in the form of seated figures (duho/dujo) with trays on their heads.
- Archaeological evidence for cohoba paraphernalia includes carved inhaling tubes, snuff trays, vomiting spatulas (used for ritual purging before inhalation), and ceramic figures depicting the inhalation posture — found across Hispaniola, Puerto Rico, and the Lesser Antilles.
- During cohoba, the cacique or bohique communicated with cemí spirits to receive guidance on warfare, agriculture, weather, and healing — Pané (c. 1498) records that the cemí would "speak" through the intoxicated practitioner.
- Peter T. Furst (1976) and Johannes Wilbert (1987) contextualized cohoba within the broader South American tradition of shamanic use of psychoactive plants, linking Taíno practice to Amazonian ayahuasca and yopo traditions.

### 1.4 Batey (Ball Game) and Ceremonial Plazas
- The batey was a rubber-ball game played on rectangular courts bounded by stone slabs, often decorated with petroglyphs. Courts at Caguana (Puerto Rico) measure up to 50 meters in length.
- The game served ceremonial, political, and social functions — inter-village disputes could be resolved through batey competitions. Spanish chroniclers described both men's and women's games.
- The ball was made of local rubber (from Castilla elastica), and players struck it with hips, shoulders, and elbows — similar to but distinct from the Mesoamerican ballgame.
- Alegría (1983) and Rouse (1992) document the batey as a key institution of Taíno social organization, linking the ball court complexes to cacique authority and ceremonial life.

### 1.5 Social Organization and Political Structure
- Taíno society was organized into a hierarchy of caciques (chiefs), nitaínos (sub-chiefs/nobles), naborías (commoners), and bohiques (ritual specialists).
- Caciques governed hereditary chiefdoms (cacicazgos) that varied in scale from single villages to multi-community polities controlling thousands of people.
- Succession was typically matrilineal: a cacique was succeeded by his sister's son rather than his own son.
- Women could serve as caciques; Anacaona of Xaragua (Hispaniola) is the most famous female cacica, documented by both Las Casas and Oviedo.
- Inter-cacicazgo relations involved marriage alliances, tributary relationships, and batey competitions as alternatives to warfare.
- The largest cacicazgos of Hispaniola at the time of contact encompassed tens of thousands of people and extensive agricultural territories.

### 1.6 Maritime Knowledge and Inter-Island Trade
- The Taíno navigated extensively between Caribbean islands using large dugout canoes (piraguas) capable of carrying 40–50 people.
- Columbus and later Spanish chroniclers describe encountering Taíno trading parties far from their home islands.
- Inter-island trade networks connected communities from the Bahamas to the Lesser Antilles, exchanging raw materials (jade-like stone, shell, gold), finished goods (ceramics, cotton cloth), and food.
- Maritime knowledge included reading ocean currents, prevailing winds, star positions, and the behavior of migratory sea birds.

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Taíno Cosmology and Deities
- Yúcahu Bagua Maórocoti: the supreme deity, lord of cassava (yuca) and the sea, associated with fertility, sustenance, and masculine creative power. The name translates approximately to "Spirit of Cassava, Wealth of the Sea."
- Atabey (also Atabeira, Attabeira): the mother of Yúcahu, goddess of fresh water, fertility, and childbirth — a primordial feminine principle associated with rivers, springs, and lunar cycles.
- Guabancex: a fierce storm goddess who controls hurricanes (the word "hurricane" derives from the Taíno juracán). She is accompanied by Guataúba (thunder herald) and Coatrisquie (flood waters).
- The cosmological structure includes a primordial cave origin: in Pané's account, humans emerge from the cave Cacibajagua on the island of Hispaniola, while the sun and moon emerge from another cave, Amayaúna.
- The cacique of the dead, Maquetaurie Guayaba, rules Coaybay, the land of the dead — described as a place on the western end of Hispaniola where spirits (opía) dwell, emerging at night to feast on guava fruit.

### 2.2 Deminán Caracaracol and the Four Brothers
- The origin narrative recorded by Pané tells of Deminán Caracaracol and his three brothers — born from the body of Itiba Cahubaba, who died in childbirth.
- The brothers steal a calabash (gourd) from an old man (Bayamanaco), and when they drop it, the water that pours out becomes the ocean, with fish spilling out to populate the seas — a Caribbean variant of the world-flood origin.
- From a turtle (or from the back of Deminán, who is struck with a calabash and develops a swelling), the earth/an island emerges — connecting to the broader "Turtle Island" motif found across Indigenous American traditions.
- Stevens-Arroyo (1988) analyzes the Deminán narrative as a charter myth legitimizing cacique authority through descent from these primordial culture heroes.

### 2.3 Social Structure and the Bohique
- Taíno society was hierarchically organized: naboría (commoners) → nitaínos (sub-chiefs/nobles) → caciques (paramount chiefs) → the bohique (shaman-priest class) operated alongside the political hierarchy.
- Bohiques underwent rigorous initiation including prolonged fasting, isolation, cohoba ingestion, and communication with the spirit world — they served as healers, diviners, weather prognosticators, and keepers of oral tradition.
- Gender roles were complex: female caciques (cacicas) are documented in Spanish sources, and women participated in certain cohoba ceremonies — Anacaona, cacica of Xaragua (western Hispaniola), is the most famous female leader, celebrated for her areíto (ceremonial song-dance) compositions.
- The areíto was a ceremonial performance combining song, dance, and narrative — it served as the primary vehicle for transmitting historical knowledge, genealogies, and mythological narratives across generations.

### 2.4 Areíto — Ceremonial Song-Dance as History
- The areíto (also areyto) was the primary Taíno performance art — a communal song-dance-narrative event that served as the main vehicle for transmitting historical memory, genealogical knowledge, and mythological narratives.
- Las Casas (c. 1559) describes areítos involving hundreds of participants, lasting from sunset to dawn, with a lead singer narrating while dancers followed in choreographed patterns — the audience responded in chorus, creating a call-and-response structure.
- Areítos commemorated military victories, chiefly genealogies, the deeds of ancestors, and mythological events — they constituted the Taíno "library," carrying the entirety of communal knowledge in performative form.
- Cacica Anacaona of Xaragua was celebrated as a composer of areítos, and her court was renowned across Hispaniola for the quality of its performances — she was executed by the Spanish governor Ovando in 1503, and with her death, much of the western Hispaniola areíto tradition was lost.

### 2.5 Ecological Knowledge and Agricultural Systems
- The Taíno conuco (mound garden) system was a sophisticated raised-bed agricultural technique: mounds of soil one to three feet high were arranged in rows, planted with yuca (cassava), sweet potato, maize, beans, squash, and peppers.
- Cassava (yuca) was the staple crop, processed into casabe (flatbread) through an elaborate procedure involving grating, pressing (to extract toxic cyanide compounds), and baking on a griddle (burén) — the processing technology was essential, as bitter cassava is lethal if consumed unprocessed.
- The Taíno managed marine resources through sophisticated fishing techniques including fish weirs (corrals), hook-and-line fishing, poison fishing using plants, and the use of remora fish (guaicán) attached to lines to catch sea turtles — a practice that astonished European observers.
- Maritime canoe technology allowed inter-island communication and trade: large dugout canoes (canoa — a Taíno word adopted into European languages) could carry 40–50 people, enabling regular travel between the Greater Antilles.

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Cohoba and Visionary Cosmology
- The role of DMT-containing cohoba in shaping Taíno cosmological beliefs — including visions of spirit beings, underworld journeys, and communication with cemís — suggests a pharmacologically mediated religious experience (Furst, 1976).
- Whether the specific imagery of Taíno cosmology (cave origins, spirit transformations, the Coaybay afterlife) was directly shaped by the phenomenology of DMT experience remains speculative but plausible, given the central role of cohoba in Taíno spiritual practice.
- Comparative analysis with Amazonian ayahuasca traditions, which produce similar visionary content (serpents, jaguars, spirit communication), supports the hypothesis of pharmacological influence on mythology.

### 3.2 Connections to South American Traditions
- The Taíno's Arawakan linguistic heritage connects them to the Orinoco River Basin and broader Amazonian world, and scholars (Siegel, 1979; Oliver, 2009) trace continuities in cosmological concepts — including the emphasis on shamanic transformation, animal-spirit intermediaries, and the centrality of cassava cultivation — between mainland and Caribbean traditions.
- The three-pointed cemí form has been compared to volcanic mountain shapes, phalluses, and cassava tubers, but its ultimate symbolic origin remains uncertain.

### 3.3 Taíno Astronomical Knowledge
- Spanish accounts mention Taíno observation of stellar patterns for agricultural timing, but detailed astronomical knowledge was lost before systematic documentation.
- Some researchers (Curet, 2005) propose that batey court orientations at Caguana and other sites may reflect astronomical alignments (solstice, equinox), though rigorous archaeoastronomical studies remain limited.

### 3.4 Taíno Legacy in Modern Caribbean Culture
- Despite the near-total destruction of Taíno society within 50 years of European contact, Taíno cultural heritage persists in Caribbean culture: linguistic borrowings (canoa/canoe, hamaca/hammock, huracán/hurricane, tabaco/tobacco, barbacoa/barbecue, maíz/maize), agricultural practices (conuco cultivation, cassava processing), and genetic legacy.
- Mitochondrial DNA studies (Martínez-Cruzado, 2010) reveal that approximately 61% of Puerto Ricans carry Indigenous maternal lineage DNA, contradicting the colonial narrative of total Taíno extinction.
- The contemporary Taíno revitalization movement (active since the 1990s) has generated both Indigenous empowerment and scholarly debate — the question of cultural continuity versus reconstruction is politically sensitive and academically unresolved.
- Material culture elements — the use of the güiro (gourd scraper) in Caribbean music, the bohío (thatched house) architectural form, and the persistence of certain medicinal plant knowledge — represent possible survivals of Taíno tradition, though unbroken chains of transmission are difficult to establish.

### 3.5 Relationship to South American Cosmologies
- The Taíno cosmological emphasis on transformative shamanic practice, psychoactive plant use, serpentine imagery, and ancestral cave origins connects them to a broader Amazonian/Orinoco religious complex.
- Parallels with mainland Arawakan-speaking groups (Lokono, Warao) in creation mythology, spirit hierarchies, and the centrality of the shaman as mediator suggest a shared cultural substrate predating Caribbean migration.
- The three-pointed cemí form has analogues in South American stone and ceramic traditions, though precise formal connections remain debated among archaeologists (Oliver, 2009).
- Whether the hierarchical cacique-bohique-naboría social structure represents a Caribbean innovation or a development of existing South American organizational models is an ongoing question in Caribbean archaeology.

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source)

- Claims that the Taíno had contact with ancient Egyptians, Phoenicians, or Atlanteans based on perceived similarities in pyramid/mound construction have no archaeological or linguistic support.
- Modern "Taíno revival" movements sometimes assert unbroken cultural continuity from pre-contact times, which conflicts with the well-documented historical reality of near-total population collapse (90%+ mortality within 50 years of contact) and extensive cultural disruption, though genetic studies (Martínez-Cruzado, 2010) confirm substantial Indigenous ancestry in modern Caribbean populations.
- Attributing specific advanced technologies (metallurgy, writing systems) to the Taíno goes beyond the archaeological evidence.
- Claims that cohoba rituals produced genuine "interdimensional" travel rather than pharmacologically induced visionary states impose modern paranormal categories on an indigenous spiritual practice.
- The assertion that specific cemí artifacts are "power generators" or "alien technology" reflects a failure to engage with the rich symbolic and relational meanings documented in the ethnographic record.

### Key Terms and Concepts

- **Cemí**: Carved spirit representation; also the spiritual power residing within it.
- **Cohoba**: Psychoactive snuff made from *Anadenanthera peregrina* seeds.
- **Behique**: Taíno healer-shaman who communicated with spirits via cohoba.
- **Cacique**: Chief or leader of a Taíno polity.
- **Batey**: Ball court and the rubber-ball game played on it.
- **Areíto**: Communal song-dance-narrative performance for transmitting history.
- **Guanín**: Gold-copper alloy disc with cosmological significance.
- **Duho**: Low ceremonial stool used during cohoba rituals.
- **Yúcahu**: Supreme spirit associated with cassava and the sea.
- **Atabey**: Mother goddess of fresh water and fertility.
