## QUICK SUMMARY

Dozens of ancient and medieval scripts remain **partially or wholly undeciphered**, representing lost linguistic traditions whose content may hold key information about ancient cultures, trade networks, religion, and technology. The failure to read these scripts constitutes one of the great information losses in human history. The most prominent undeciphered writing systems include: **Linear A** (Minoan Crete, c. 1800–1450 BCE): approximately 1,400 known inscriptions in a syllabic script inherited by Linear B (deciphered by Michael Ventris in 1952 as Mycenaean Greek), but the underlying Minoan language remains unknown; **Proto-Elamite** (Iran, c. 3100–2900 BCE): one of the earliest writing systems, with ~1,600 inscribed tablets — largely numerical/administrative but the language is unidentified, representing a civilization of enormous importance in Near Eastern history; the **Indus Valley script** (c. 2600–1900 BCE): approximately 4,200 inscribed objects with ~400–600 distinct signs — debated even as to whether it represents a true writing system or proto-writing, with the language unknown (Dravidian, early Indo-Aryan, and language-isolate hypotheses all proposed); **Rongorongo** (Easter Island/Rapa Nui): ~26 surviving inscribed wooden objects with ~120 glyphs, possibly the only independent invention of writing in Oceania — or potentially influenced by Spanish colonial contact; and the **Voynich Manuscript** (dated 1404–1438 by radiocarbon): a 240-page illustrated manuscript in an unknown script and language, held at Yale's Beinecke Library, which has resisted all decipherment attempts despite extensive cryptographic analysis. Other significant undeciphered or partially deciphered scripts include **Cypro-Minoan** (Cyprus, c. 1550–1050 BCE), the **Phaistos Disc** (unique artifact, c. 1700 BCE), **Isthmian/Epi-Olmec** (Mesoamerica, c. 300 BCE–500 CE), and various Paleohispanic scripts.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Scholarly Consensus)

### 1.1 Linear A
- **Corpus**: ~1,400 inscriptions, primarily on clay tablets from Hagia Triada, Knossos, Phaistos, and Zakros; also found on stone offering tables, pottery, and metal objects
- **Script**: syllabic, related to Linear B (deciphered 1952, Ventris & Chadwick); approximately 75–80 syllabary signs plus logograms and numerical signs
- **Status**: the sign values can be partially inferred from Linear B equivalences (the script was borrowed), but the resulting phonetic readings do not produce recognizable words in any known language family — the **Minoan language** remains unclassified
- **Scholarly consensus** (Godart & Olivier, *Recueil des inscriptions en linéaire A*, 1976–1985): the corpus is too small and too formulaic (mostly administrative lists and religious dedications) to permit structural decipherment without a bilingual text or knowledge of the underlying language

### 1.2 Indus Valley Script
- **Corpus**: ~4,200 inscribed objects — primarily stamp seals, plus tablets, pottery, copper tools, and other artifacts — from Harappa, Mohenjo-daro, Dholavira, and other Indus Valley Civilization sites
- **Sign inventory**: scholarly estimates range from ~400 to ~600 distinct signs; the average inscription length is only **~5 signs** (the longest known is ~26 signs)
- **Debate**: Farmer, Sproat & Witzel (2004) argued the signs do not represent a true writing system (they are too short and lack clear syntactic structure), proposing instead non-linguistic symbolic communication; Parpola (1994) and others counter that the corpus was written primarily on perishable materials (now lost) and that the surviving seal inscriptions are abbreviated formulae
- **Language candidates**: Dravidian (Parpola), early Indo-Aryan (contested), a language isolate, or an unknown Austroasiatic/other language — no consensus

### 1.3 Proto-Elamite
- **Corpus**: ~1,600 clay tablets from Susa and other Iranian sites, dated c. 3100–2900 BCE — making it one of the oldest known scripts (roughly contemporary with early Sumerian proto-cuneiform)
- **Status**: the numerical system has been largely decoded (Damerow & Englund, 1989), revealing a complex administrative accounting system; the non-numerical signs (~1,000 sign types) remain undeciphered
- **Open-access project**: Oxford's Cuneiform Digital Library Initiative has digitized the corpus

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Rongorongo
- **Corpus**: 26 surviving wooden objects (mostly tablets and staffs) with incised glyphs, using ~120 basic elements; the texts, if read, would likely be the **only indigenous Polynesian writing system**
- **Barthel** (1958): the first systematic catalog of Rongorongo signs; subsequent analysis by Fischer (1997) proposed partial readings connecting glyphs to Rapanui-language cosmogonic chants, but this remains debated
- **Key question**: whether Rongorongo represents an independent invention of writing (potentially pre-European contact) or was inspired by exposure to Spanish documents during the 1770 visit of Felipe González de Ahedo; radiocarbon dating of the wood substrates is complicated by the use of repurposed driftwood

### 2.2 Voynich Manuscript
- **Physical**: parchment codex, 240 pages, with illustrations of unidentified plants, astronomical diagrams, bathing/biological scenes, and pharmaceutical-style layouts; radiocarbon dated to **1404–1438 CE** (University of Arizona, 2009)
- **Script**: the "Voynichese" alphabet comprises ~20–25 distinct characters; statistical analysis (Amancio et al., 2013) shows word-frequency patterns consistent with natural language rather than random generation, but the text has resisted all cryptanalysis including Friedman, Tiltman, and modern computational approaches
- **Hypotheses**: natural language in an unknown cipher (most common view); an artificial/constructed language; an elaborate hoax (proposed by Gordon Rugg, 2004, demonstrating that Cardan grille methods could produce text with similar statistical properties); glossolalia or spiritual writing

### 2.3 Cypro-Minoan and Phaistos Disc
- **Cypro-Minoan**: ~250 inscriptions from Bronze Age Cyprus (c. 1550–1050 BCE), potentially related to Linear A; three variants identified (CM1, CM2, CM3); the small corpus and uncertain language affiliation prevent decipherment
- **Phaistos Disc** (c. 1700 BCE): a unique fired-clay disc from Crete with 45 distinct signs stamped (not incised) in a spiral; its uniqueness means there is no comparative corpus, making decipherment essentially impossible with current evidence — many "decipherments" have been proposed, none widely accepted

### 2.4 Etruscan — Partially Understood
- **Etruscan**: unlike in truly undeciphered scripts, the Etruscan alphabet is read (derived from Greek); the language itself is understood to a moderate degree — grammar, phonology, and a vocabulary of ~200 words are established — but Etruscan remains a **linguistic isolate** with no proven relationships to other language families
- ~13,000 inscriptions survive; the longest texts (Liber Linteus, Tabula Capuana, Cippus Perusinus) are largely funerary and religious, limiting vocabulary expansion

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Indus Script as Dravidian
- Asko Parpola's decades-long work (1994, 2015) proposes systematic Dravidian readings of Indus signs — particularly reading the common "fish" sign as *mīn* (Dravidian for both "fish" and "star"), interpreting it as a rebus writing system
- While influential, this remains speculative; without a bilingual text or confirmed language family, any proposed decipherment is inherently unverifiable

### 3.2 Independent Invention Hypothesis for Rongorongo
- If Rongorongo predates European contact (1722), it would represent one of only 3–4 independent inventions of writing in human history (alongside Mesopotamia, China, and possibly Mesoamerica); scholars argue the complexity and unique features of the script support independence
- However, definitive dating of the tradition (as opposed to the wood artifacts) remains impossible

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Claimed Decipherments
- **[DEBUNKED]** Numerous claimed decipherments of the Voynich Manuscript (as Latin abbreviations, Ukrainian, Hebrew, etc.) have failed peer review or independent verification
- **[DEBUNKED]** Several proposed Phaistos Disc "translations" project modern language onto an unknown script with a single exemplar — methodologically unsound

### Counter-Arguments
- The small corpus size for many undeciphered scripts (especially Rongorongo and the Phaistos Disc) means that decipherment may be genuinely impossible without new discoveries of comparable material or bilingual texts
