## QUICK SUMMARY

The invention of **standardized currency and coinage** transformed human economic interaction and represents one of the great innovations in the history of exchange. Remarkably, coinage appears to have been **independently invented** at least twice — in **Lydia/western Anatolia** (c. 650–600 BCE, electrum coins) and in **China** (c. 600–500 BCE, bronze spade and knife money, later round coins with square holes) — with a possible third independent origin in **India** (c. 600–400 BCE, punch-marked silver coins). Before coined money, various cultures used **commodity currencies**: cowrie shells (widespread across Africa, South and East Asia, and Oceania from at least 1600 BCE), grain, salt, cattle, and standardized metal ingots (Mesopotamian silver shekels, Egyptian *deben* copper rings). The diffusion of Lydian-style coinage through the Greek world and into Persia, the Mediterranean, and ultimately the Roman Empire represents one of the most consequential technology transfers in economic history. The Greek **drachma** and Roman **denarius** became the world's first international currencies, facilitating trade from Britain to India. Meanwhile, Chinese **cash coins** (*ban liang* and later *wu zhu*) spread to Korea, Japan, Vietnam, and Southeast Asia, creating a separate monetary sphere that endured for over two millennia. In the Americas, **cacao beans** served as currency in Mesoamerica, while **shell beads (wampum)** functioned as both ceremonial objects and exchange medium among Eastern North American peoples. The history of currency reveals deep connections between trade networks, political authority, cultural exchange, and the codification of value — and the loss of many pre-monetary exchange systems represents an under-studied area of economic anthropology.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Scholarly Consensus)

### 1.1 Lydian Invention of Coinage
- The earliest coins are **electrum** (gold-silver alloy) pieces from western Anatolia, dated c. 650–600 BCE; the most famous early specimens are associated with **Sardis** (capital of Lydia) and the Temple of Artemis at Ephesus
- **Croesus of Lydia** (r. c. 560–546 BCE): credited with introducing **bimetallic coinage** — separate gold and silver coins of guaranteed purity, replacing the variable-composition electrum coins; this innovation was epochal for standardized exchange
- Archaeological evidence (excavations at Sardis by C.H. Greenewalt Jr. and others) confirms metallurgical workshops for refining gold from electrum, supporting Lydian technological priority

### 1.2 Greek and Roman Coinage Diffusion
- Greek city-states adopted coinage rapidly after c. 600 BCE; the **Athenian owl tetradrachm** (c. 510 BCE onward) became the first widely accepted international trade coin, found in hoards from Spain to Afghanistan
- The **Persian daric** (gold) and **siglos** (silver) — issued under Darius I (c. 520 BCE) — standardized exchange across the Achaemenid Empire, explicitly modeled on Lydian precedent
- The **Roman denarius** (introduced c. 211 BCE) became the basis of Mediterranean commerce for 450+ years; Roman coin hoards have been found as far as Scandinavia, India, Sri Lanka, and Vietnam, demonstrating the coin's reach beyond the empire's borders
- Roman silver denarii found in **South Indian hoards** (Sangam-era trade, 1st–3rd century CE) constitute some of the most dramatic evidence of ancient long-distance economic integration

### 1.3 Chinese Independent Invention
- Chinese monetary evolution: Shang dynasty **cowrie shells** → Zhou dynasty **bronze spade money** and **knife money** (c. 700–300 BCE) → Qin dynasty standardization under **Qin Shi Huang** (221 BCE) of the round **ban liang** coin with square hole
- The round-coin-with-square-hole design endured from 221 BCE to 1911 CE (~2,100 years), making it the longest-lived coin form in history
- Chinese cash coins were adopted as currency in **Japan** (708 CE, Wadō kaichin), **Korea**, and **Vietnam** — creating a distinct East Asian monetary sphere independent of Mediterranean coinage traditions

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Indian Punch-Marked Coins
- Indian **punch-marked coins** (PMCs): irregular silver pieces bearing multiple stamped symbols, dated c. 600–400 BCE; found across the subcontinent from the Ganges valley to the Deccan
- Whether PMCs represent an independent invention or were influenced by knowledge of Lydian/Persian coinage is debated; the morphological differences (irregular shapes, multiple punches vs. die-struck imaging) suggest at least a substantially independent tradition

### 2.2 Cowrie Shells as Global Currency
- **Cowrie shells** (primarily *Monetaria moneta*): used as currency across an enormous geographic range — documented in Shang dynasty China (c. 1600–1046 BCE), sub-Saharan Africa (widely used until the 20th century), South Asia, Southeast Asia, and the Pacific Islands
- The cowrie trade connected the Maldive Islands (a primary source) to economies across the Indian Ocean; European slave traders introduced massive quantities of cowrie shells into West Africa (17th–19th centuries), contributing to inflation and economic disruption

### 2.3 Cacao and Mesoamerican Exchange
- **Cacao beans** served as currency in Aztec and Maya economies; tribute lists from the Aztec Empire (Codex Mendoza) record payments in cacao beans, and Spanish colonial documents provide exchange rates (e.g., 1 turkey ≈ 200 cacao beans)
- The cacao tree's limited growing range (tropical lowlands) made the beans a natural commodity currency that linked highland and lowland economies

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Pre-Coinage Metal Standards
- Mesopotamian **silver shekel** standards (attested from Ur III, c. 2100 BCE): while not coined money, silver was weighed and exchanged in standard quantities — scholars (M. Powell, 1996) argue this represents proto-monetary practice; the extent to which ordinary transactions used silver vs. barley/commodity exchange remains debated

### 3.2 Proto-Money and Gift Economies
- Anthropological debate over the "barter myth": David Graeber (*Debt: The First 5,000 Years*, 2011) argued that barter economies as described by Adam Smith never existed; instead, credit, gift exchange, and social obligation preceded commodity exchange — this remains a heterodox but influential position

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Ancient Global Monetary System
- **[DEBUNKED]** Claims of a unified ancient global monetary standard (connecting Mesopotamia, China, and the Americas) are not supported by evidence; the independent emergence of currency systems argues against any single master system

### Counter-Arguments
- The spread of coinage from Lydia to Rome and from Qin China to Japan demonstrates that successful monetary innovations diffuse rapidly when trade conditions permit, but this process was incremental and well-documented, not evidence of pre-existing global connections
