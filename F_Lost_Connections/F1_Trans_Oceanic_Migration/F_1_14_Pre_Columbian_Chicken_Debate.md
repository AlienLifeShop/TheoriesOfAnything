## QUICK SUMMARY

The **pre-Columbian chicken debate** centers on whether domestic chickens (*Gallus gallus domesticus*) — an Old World species originally domesticated in Southeast Asia — reached South America **before** European contact (1492+), via **Polynesian voyagers** making trans-Pacific crossings. If confirmed, pre-Columbian chickens in the Americas would constitute one of the strongest pieces of evidence for **Polynesian-South American contact** and join the **sweet potato** (*Ipomoea batatas*, a confirmed pre-Columbian transfer from South America to Polynesia) as proof of trans-Pacific exchange. The debate was energized by a 2007 study (Storey et al.) reporting ancient chicken bones from the archaeological site of **El Arenal** (Arauco Peninsula, Chile), radiocarbon-dated to **c. 1321–1407 CE** (pre-Columbus) and yielding a mitochondrial DNA haplogroup matching Polynesian chicken lineages. If correct, this would demonstrate that Polynesians brought chickens to South America ~100–200 years before Europeans arrived. However, subsequent studies (Thomson et al. 2014) challenged both the radiocarbon dates (potential marine carbon contamination from the chickens' diet) and the DNA identification (the haplogroup is not uniquely Polynesian — it is common worldwide). The debate remains **unresolved**: the El Arenal dates may be genuinely pre-Columbian (the marine reservoir effect may be minimal), and other lines of evidence (the **Araucana chicken** breed, linguistic parallels, the sweet potato transfer) support Polynesian-South American contact — but definitive proof awaits more rigorous ancient DNA and dating studies from sealed, pre-contact archaeological contexts.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Background: Chickens in the Old World
- **Domestic chicken** (*Gallus gallus domesticus*): descended from the red junglefowl (*Gallus gallus*) of Southeast Asia — domesticated by at least **~8,000 BP** (possibly multiple domestication centers: Thailand, China, Indus Valley)
- Chickens spread throughout Asia, reaching China by ~6000 BCE, the Near East by ~2000 BCE, Egypt by ~1400 BCE, Mediterranean Europe by ~800 BCE, West Africa by ~500 CE
- **Polynesian chickens**: chickens were part of the Polynesian "transported landscape" (along with dogs, pigs, and rats) — carried throughout Polynesia during the phases of colonization from ~3,000 BP onward. Chickens are present in archaeological sites across Polynesia (Samoa, Marquesas, Hawaii, Easter Island, New Zealand)
- **No chickens in the Americas before European contact** was the traditional assumption — challenged by the El Arenal findings

### 1.2 The El Arenal Findings (Storey et al. 2007)
- **Site**: El Arenal-1 (EL-1), Arauco Peninsula, south-central Chile — a **Mapuche** archaeological site
- **Findings**: 50+ chicken bones excavated from stratified deposits
- **Radiocarbon dates**: bones directly dated to **c. 1321–1407 CE** (2σ calibrated range) — potentially ~80–170 years before Columbus
- **Ancient DNA**: mtDNA analysis identified haplogroup **E** — the predominant haplogroup in ancient Polynesian chicken populations (including Easter Island and Tonga samples)
- **Storey et al.'s interpretation**: Polynesian voyagers reached South America and introduced chickens before European contact — supported by the pre-Columbian dates and the Polynesian DNA match
- The study was published in *PNAS* (2007) and generated widespread media attention and scholarly debate

### 1.3 The Sweet Potato as Parallel Evidence
- The **sweet potato** (*Ipomoea batatas*) is a well-established case of pre-Columbian trans-Pacific transfer:
  - Origin: domesticated in tropical South America
  - Present in Polynesia **before European contact** — confirmed by archaeological finds in the Cook Islands (c. 1000–1100 CE, Hather and Kirch 1991), Hawaiian and Easter Island contexts
  - Linguistic evidence: Quechua *kumal*/*kumar* and Polynesian *kumara*/*kumala* — a borrowing that implies direct contact
  - The direction of transfer is debated: Polynesians may have voyaged to South America and returned with the sweet potato, or South Americans may have reached Polynesia (or natural dispersal, though this is considered unlikely for a cultivated plant requiring human management)
- If the sweet potato was transferred, **chickens could have been exchanged in the same or a similar voyage**

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Challenges to the El Arenal Evidence
- **Thomson et al. (2014)** contested the El Arenal findings on two grounds:
  - **Radiocarbon contamination**: chickens that consume marine-derived food (fish scraps, shellfish) incorporate marine carbon, which has an older ¹⁴C age (the marine reservoir effect). This could make the El Arenal bones appear ~100–200 years older than their true age, potentially placing them in the post-contact period (~1500s CE)
  - **DNA haplogroup**: haplogroup E is **not uniquely Polynesian** — it is the most common chicken mitochondrial lineage worldwide, found in European, Asian, and African chickens. Therefore, the El Arenal chicken could be of European origin
- **Storey et al.'s response (2013)**: argued that marine reservoir effects were likely minimal (the chickens' diet was primarily terrestrial), and that the combination of pre-Columbian dates + Polynesian-type DNA remains the most parsimonious explanation. They also noted that the specific E subclade matched Pacific specimens more closely than European ones

### 2.2 The Araucana Chicken
- The **Araucana breed** — a South American chicken breed raised by the Mapuche people of Chile — has several unusual features:
  - **Rumplessness** (absence of tail feathers) and **tufted ears** — traits rare or absent in European breeds but present in some Asian breeds
  - **Blue-green egg color** — caused by an endogenous retroviral insertion; this trait has been linked to Asian chicken lineages
  - Researchers have proposed that the Araucana derives from a pre-Columbian (Polynesian or Asian) introduction rather than from European chickens
  - Genetic studies of the Araucana are ongoing but not yet conclusive on the pre-Columbian origin question

### 2.3 Broader Trans-Pacific Contact Evidence
- Beyond chickens and sweet potatoes, other proposed evidence for pre-Columbian Polynesian-South American contact includes:
  - **Bottle gourd** (*Lagenaria siceraria*): pre-Columbian in both the Old and New Worlds, possibly transported by humans across the Pacific (though natural oceanic drift is also possible)
  - **Craniometric similarities**: between some Polynesian and South American populations (debated)
  - **Easter Island connections**: Easter Island (Rapa Nui) is the closest Polynesian island to South America (~3,700 km) — a logical staging point for trans-Pacific contact
  - **Genomic evidence**: Ioannidis et al. (2020, *Nature*) reported pre-Columbian Polynesian genetic admixture in some South American Indigenous populations — an admixture event dated to ~c. 1200 CE, consistent with the chicken dates

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Multiple Pre-Columbian Contact Events
- If chickens, sweet potatoes, and DNA admixture all indicate contact, there may have been **multiple voyaging events** rather than a single contact episode — perhaps a period of intermittent Polynesian-South American exchange between ~1000–1400 CE. This is plausible given Polynesian navigational capabilities but lacks sufficient archaeological documentation to confirm

### 3.2 South American Voyagers to Polynesia
- The reverse scenario — South American peoples voyaging westward to Polynesia — has also been proposed (most famously by Thor Heyerdahl's Kon-Tiki expedition, 1947). While the sweet potato transfer could go either direction, genetic and archaeological evidence overwhelmingly supports **Polynesian expansion into the Pacific**, not South American. However, the possibility of occasional South American maritime activity in the Pacific cannot be entirely excluded

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Polynesians Colonized South America
- **[UNSUPPORTED]** Claims that Polynesians established permanent colonies in South America or significantly influenced pre-Columbian South American civilizations go far beyond the evidence. Contact appears to have been **brief and limited** — possibly one or a few voyaging events resulting in crop/animal exchange, not colonization

### 4.2 Chickens Prove Lost Continent
- **[NO EVIDENCE]** Claims that pre-Columbian chickens prove the existence of Mu, Lemuria, or other lost Pacific continents as waypoints are not supported by any geological or historical evidence

## Counter-Arguments & Criticisms

The hypothesis of Polynesian introduction of chickens to South America before European contact remains debated. Storey et al. (2007) reported pre-Columbian chicken bones at El Arenal in Chile, but Thomson et al. (2014) questioned the radiocarbon dating and ancient DNA results, suggesting contamination and post-Columbian introduction. The ancient DNA sequence initially attributed to Polynesian chickens was later found in global chicken populations, undermining the specificity of the genetic link. The debate highlights the difficulty of establishing pre-Columbian transoceanic contact from limited archaeological evidence.
