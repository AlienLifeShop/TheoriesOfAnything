## QUICK SUMMARY

**Beringia** — the vast landmass that periodically connected northeastern Asia to northwestern North America across what is now the **Bering Strait** and the shallow Chukchi and Bering Seas — was one of the most consequential geographic features in human history, serving as the primary pathway for the **peopling of the Americas**. During glacial periods, when sea levels dropped by **~120–130 m** below modern levels, the shallow continental shelf between Siberia and Alaska was exposed as a broad, unglaciated plain up to **~1,600 km wide** (north–south) — not a narrow "bridge" but a **subcontinental landscape** the size of the modern contiguous United States west of the Mississippi. This landscape, known as the **mammoth steppe**, supported a rich biome of grasses, sedges, and herbaceous plants that sustained woolly mammoths, horses, bison, saiga antelope, and their predators. Humans entered Beringia from Siberia sometime between **~30,000 and ~16,000 years ago**, and current genetic and archaeological evidence suggests a period of **"Beringian Standstill"** — lasting perhaps 5,000–10,000 years — during which a founding population lived in Beringia itself, genetically isolated from both Asian and later American populations, before dispersing southward into the Americas. The route of southward dispersal remains debated: the traditional **ice-free corridor** between the Cordilleran and Laurentide ice sheets (opened ~14,000–13,500 BP) was long considered the primary pathway, but growing evidence supports an earlier **Pacific coastal route** (the "Kelp Highway Hypothesis") — maritime migration along the productive kelp-forest coastline from Beringia to the Pacific Northwest and beyond. Beringia is now entirely submerged (except for the Diomede Islands and St. Lawrence Island), making direct archaeological investigation of this lost landscape extremely difficult.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Paleogeography of Beringia
- **Bering Strait** today: ~85 km wide, maximum depth ~50 m — one of the world's shallowest oceanic straits
- **During the LGM** (~26–19 ka): sea level drop of ~120–130 m exposed the entire Bering and Chukchi Sea shelves, creating a land connection spanning from the Verkhoyansk Range (Siberia) to the Brooks Range (Alaska) — an area of approximately **1.6 million km²**
- **Beringia was not glaciated**: despite the extreme cold, aridity prevented ice-sheet formation — Central Beringia was an ice-free refugium surrounded by the Cordilleran and Laurentide ice sheets to the east and permafrost regions to the north
- **Environment**: reconstructed as **mammoth steppe** — a grass-dominated biome (analogous to no modern environment) with mosaic tundra-steppe, supporting high megafaunal biomass. Pollen, insect, and plant macrofossil evidence confirms this reconstruction (Guthrie 2001; Elias and Crocker 2008)
- **Last submergence**: rising post-glacial seas flooded the land bridge between ~13,000–11,000 BP, with final severance of the land connection ~10,000–11,000 BP

### 1.2 Human Presence in Beringia
- **Siberian side**: archaeological sites document human presence in northeastern Siberia by at least ~32,000 BP:
  - **Yana RHS** (Yana Rhinoceros Horn Site, 71°N latitude): ~32,000 BP — the oldest known human site in the Arctic, with ivory foreshafts and stone tools
  - **Diuktai Cave** and related sites (30,000–12,000 BP): microblade technology characteristic of the Siberian Upper Paleolithic
- **Alaskan side**: earliest confirmed sites date to ~14,000–15,000 BP:
  - **Swan Point** (central Alaska): ~14,000 BP — microblade-bearing cultural horizon, technologically related to Siberian complexes
  - **Broken Mammoth, Mead, and Healy Lake**: ~13,000–12,000 BP
  - Earlier sites may exist but are either submerged or undiscovered
- **Submerged evidence**: the majority of any Beringian habitation occurred on now-submerged land — direct archaeological evidence from the land bridge itself is essentially inaccessible (some dredging has recovered mammoth bones and isolated artifacts, but no systematic excavation of submerged Beringian sites has been possible)

### 1.3 The Beringian Standstill Model
- **Tamm et al. (2007)** and **Fagundes et al. (2008)** proposed the "Beringian Standstill" or "Beringian Incubation Model" based on mtDNA analysis:
  - A founding population entered Beringia from Siberia ~25,000–20,000 BP
  - This population was **genetically isolated** in Beringia for approximately **5,000–10,000 years** (during the LGM, when ice sheets blocked passage south)
  - During this standstill, uniquely American mitochondrial lineages (haplogroups A, B, C, D, X) diversified
  - After ~16,000–15,000 BP, the population dispersed into the Americas as routes opened (either coastal or interior)
- **Support**: the genetic divergence dates, the founder effect, and the limited number of founding maternal lineages are consistent with a bottleneck in a geographically constrained refugium
- **Refinements**: Moreno-Mayar et al. (2018) using ancient DNA from 11,500-year-old Alaskan infants (Upward Sun River site) confirmed a deep split between "Ancient Beringians" and ancestral Native Americans, supporting the standstill concept

### 1.4 Routes South
- **Ice-free corridor**: between the Cordilleran (Pacific coast) and Laurentide (continental) ice sheets — opened ~14,000–13,500 BP (Alberta/British Columbia):
  - Initially considered the primary route; however, paleoenvironmental evidence (Pedersen et al. 2016) suggests the corridor was a **barren, unvegetated** landscape until ~12,600 BP — too late and too harsh for initial colonization
- **Pacific coastal route** ("Kelp Highway"): maritime migration along the kelp-forest-rich Pacific coast from Beringia to the Pacific Northwest and beyond:
  - Now considered more likely for the initial entry — supported by pre-14,000 BP sites in South America (Monte Verde, ~14,500 BP) and evidence of coastal adaptations
  - Direct evidence is limited because LGM-era coastlines are now submerged under ~100 m of water

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Timing of Entry — Pre-LGM vs. Post-LGM
- The exactness of when humans entered the Americas remains debated:
  - **Conservative view**: ~16,000–15,000 BP, via the coastal route after LGM ice-sheet retreat
  - **Earlier entry**: sites like **Bluefish Caves** (Yukon, ~24,000 BP, Bourgeon et al. 2017 — butchered horse bones) suggest pre-LGM human presence in eastern Beringia. **White Sands footprints** (New Mexico, ~23,000–21,000 BP, Bennett et al. 2021) suggest surprisingly early southward dispersal — controversial but increasingly accepted
  - If pre-LGM entry is confirmed, the standstill model requires modification — humans may have arrived in small numbers much earlier, with the standstill population representing a later, larger wave that contributed the majority of Native American ancestry

### 2.2 Beringia as Refugium
- Beringia was not only a migration corridor but potentially a **long-term habitation zone**:
  - The "Beringian Refuge" may have supported small human populations for millennia, subsisting on megafauna and plant resources
  - Genetic evidence suggests at least **two distinct populations** inhabited Beringia: "Ancient Beringians" (who remained in Alaska) and the ancestors of all other Native Americans (who dispersed south)
  - Some Dené-Yeniseian linguistic connections (Vajda 2010) between Na-Dene languages (North America) and Ket (Siberia) suggest ongoing or repeated contact across the Beringian region even after the land bridge was submerged

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Solutrean Hypothesis (Atlantic Crossing)
- The hypothesis that pre-Clovis Americans included people who crossed the Atlantic from Ice Age Europe (Solutrean culture → Clovis, Stanford and Bradley 2012) has been **largely rejected** by geneticists — all pre-Columbian Native American DNA is most closely related to East Asian/Siberian populations, not European. Technological similarities between Solutrean and Clovis points are now considered convergent

### 3.2 Multiple Migration Waves
- Some models propose **3+ distinct migration waves** into the Americas — one ancestral to most Native Americans, one ancestral to Na-Dene speakers, and one to Arctic/Paleo-Eskimo populations. While genetic evidence supports at least two to three waves, the number of founding events and their relationship to Beringian standstill populations continues to be refined

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Recent Crossing by Land
- **[CONTRADICTED]** Claims that the Bering land bridge was crossable within the past few thousand years are contradicted by geological evidence — the Bering Strait has been continuously flooded since ~10,000–11,000 BP (though winter sea ice may have allowed occasional crossings)

### 4.2 No Land Bridge Needed
- **[CONTRADICTED]** Some alternative models claim that the Americas were populated by boat from Africa, Southeast Asia, or Polynesia without any Beringian connection. While limited maritime contact may have occurred (see F_1_14 — Pre-Columbian Chicken Debate), the overwhelming genetic evidence confirms that the founding population of the Americas entered via Beringia
