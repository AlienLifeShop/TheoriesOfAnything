## QUICK SUMMARY

The development of **metallurgy** — the extraction and working of metals from ores — is one of the most consequential technological achievements in human history, and one of the best arenas for examining the fundamental question: **did key technologies spread from a single origin (diffusion) or arise independently in multiple locations (convergent invention)?** The archaeological and metallurgical evidence now demonstrates conclusively that the answer is **both** — with different metals and techniques showing different patterns. **Copper smelting** appears to have been invented at least twice independently: in the **Fertile Crescent/Anatolia** (~7000–5000 BCE) and in the **Andes** (~3000 BCE) — and possibly independently in **China** (~3000–2500 BCE) and **sub-Saharan Africa** (~2500–1000 BCE). **Bronze alloying** (copper + tin or arsenic) developed in the Near East (~3300 BCE) and spread via trade networks, but China developed its own distinctive bronze tradition with different alloy recipes, casting techniques (piece-mold rather than lost-wax), and artistic forms — suggesting **parallel development** with some degree of contact. **Iron smelting** was long thought to have originated solely in Anatolia (~1200 BCE) and diffused outward, but evidence now supports **independent invention** of iron smelting in sub-Saharan Africa (~1000–800 BCE, based on evidence from Nigeria, Tanzania, and the Great Lakes region) — challenging the diffusionist assumption that metallurgical knowledge flowed exclusively from the Near East. The **lost-wax casting** technique (investment casting) appears independently in at least three regions: the Near East, sub-Saharan Africa, and Mesoamerica/South America. **Mesoamerican metallurgy** (copper, gold, silver, bronze — arriving relatively late, ~600–900 CE, from South America via maritime exchange) developed independently of Old World metallurgy entirely. These cross-cultural comparisons reveal that while **trade networks** were powerful vectors for technological diffusion across connected regions (Eurasia, Africa), human inventiveness also produced **convergent solutions** to the same physical and chemical challenges — particularly in regions isolated from each other by oceans or deserts.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Copper Metallurgy: Multiple Origins
- **Near East/Anatolia** (~7000–5000 BCE):
  - **Native copper** working (cold hammering): attested from ~9000 BCE (Çayönü, Turkey) — predating smelting by millennia
  - **Smelting from ore**: earliest convincing evidence ~5000 BCE (Tal-i Iblis, Iran; Belovode, Serbia, ~5000 BCE — one of the earliest copper smelting sites in the world)
  - Arsenical copper (natural arsenic-copper alloys) used before deliberate tin-bronze alloying — found across the Fertile Crescent and the Balkans by 4000 BCE
- **South America/Andes** (~3000 BCE):
  - **Independent origin**: copper metallurgy in the central Andes (especially modern Peru and Bolivia) developed **without contact** with Old World traditions:
    - Early smelted copper objects from the Old Copper Complex of the Andes (~2000–1000 BCE) — progressing to sophisticated alloying (copper-arsenic, copper-tin, copper-silver/tumbaga) by the 1st millennium BCE
    - Distinctive Andean techniques: sheet-metal hammering, gilding, depletion gilding, and electrochemical plating — unknown in the Old World until much later
- **China** (~3000–2500 BCE):
  - Copper and bronze metallurgy appeared in China by ~3000–2500 BCE (Qijia culture) — whether through diffusion from the steppe/Central Asia or through independent development is debated:
    - Chinese bronze technology developed its own distinctive **piece-mold casting** technique (unique to China — contrast with lost-wax casting dominant in the Near East and Mediterranean)
    - Chinese bronze alloy recipes and decorative traditions are sufficiently different from Near Eastern traditions to suggest significant independent development, even if the basic concept of smelting may have arrived via Central Asian contact

### 1.2 Iron Smelting: The African Independence Question
- **Anatolia/Near East** (~1200 BCE):
  - The traditional narrative: iron smelting emerged in the Hittite realm (~1500–1200 BCE) and spread after the Late Bronze Age Collapse — replacing bronze across the Near East, Mediterranean, and Europe
  - **Bloomery process**: the dominant early iron-making technology — producing a spongy mass of iron (bloom) that required repeated hammering and folding to drive out slag and produce wrought iron
- **Sub-Saharan Africa** (~1000–800 BCE and possibly earlier):
  - Archaeological evidence from **multiple African sites** demonstrates iron smelting independently or very early:
    - **Taruga** (Nigeria, Nok culture): iron smelting furnaces dated to ~800–500 BCE — among the earliest in sub-Saharan Africa
    - **Great Lakes region** (Rwanda, Burundi, Tanzania): iron smelting evidence from ~800–500 BCE — with some controversial earlier dates (2nd millennium BCE from sites like KM2 and KM3 in northwestern Tanzania, though dating is debated)
    - **Termit** (Niger): iron artifacts with dates potentially pushing back to ~1500 BCE — contested but not definitively refuted
  - **Carbon steel production**: some African bloomery traditions (e.g., the Haya people of Tanzania) produced **high-carbon steel** through sophisticated preheating techniques — documented by Schmidt and Avery (1978, *Science*) — demonstrating advanced metallurgical knowledge that was independently developed
  - **Independent invention vs. diffusion debate**: the current scholarly consensus leans slightly toward the possibility of independent African iron smelting — but the question remains one of the most debated in African archaeology

### 1.3 Bronze Alloying
- **Near East** (~3300 BCE): deliberate tin-bronze alloying (Cu + ~10% Sn) began in Mesopotamia/Anatolia — creating a harder, more castable alloy that initiated the Bronze Age:
  - Tin sources were geographically restricted (Afghanistan/Badakhshan, Cornwall, Iberia, Central Asia) — making tin trade a critical driver of long-distance exchange networks (see F_3_04)
  - The Near Eastern bronze tradition spread across Europe, the Mediterranean, Central Asia, and South Asia via trade and migration
- **China** (~2000 BCE, Erlitou culture): Chinese bronze metallurgy produced some of the most remarkable cast objects in human history:
  - **Piece-mold casting**: a technique unique to China — sectional ceramic molds assembled around a core, producing complex forms (ritual vessels: *ding*, *gui*, *jue*) with sharp, detailed surface decoration impossible through lost-wax alone
  - Chinese bronze alloy recipes included higher levels of lead (Cu-Sn-Pb) than Near Eastern bronzes — optimized for piece-mold casting fluidity
  - Whether the fundamental concept of bronze alloying reached China via Central Asian steppe contact or was independently developed is debated — but the execution was unquestionably distinctively Chinese

### 1.4 Mesoamerican Metallurgy: Late and Independent
- **Metallurgy in Mesoamerica** arrived **late** (~600–900 CE) and from **South America** — not from the Old World:
  - Gold, silver, and copper working spread from the Andes and Intermediate Area (Colombia, Ecuador, Panama) to West Mexico via maritime trade along the Pacific coast
  - West Mexican copper bells, gold ornaments, and lost-wax castings show direct technological links to South American traditions
  - **Bronze alloying** (Cu-Sn, Cu-As) appeared in Mesoamerica only in the Postclassic period (~1200–1521 CE) — much later than in the Old World
  - This demonstrates complete **independent development** of metallurgy in the Americas — unconnected to Old World traditions

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Lost-Wax Casting: Convergent Invention
- The **lost-wax (cire perdue)** technique — creating a wax model, encasing it in ceramic, melting out the wax, and pouring metal into the resulting cavity — appears independently in:
  - **Near East/Levant** (~4000 BCE): the earliest known lost-wax cast object, the Nahal Mishmar copper hoard (Cave of the Treasure, Israel)
  - **South America** (~500 BCE): independently developed for gold and copper casting
  - **Sub-Saharan Africa**: lost-wax casting traditions in Benin (Nigeria), Ife, and other West African centers — though the degree of contact with North African/Mediterranean traditions is debated
- The technique's independent appearance in multiple continents supports the **convergent invention** model — the same physical principle (wax melts, metal fills) being discovered wherever communities worked with metals and ceramics

### 2.2 Diffusion Across Eurasia
- Across the connected Eurasian landmass, metallurgical knowledge clearly **diffused** along trade networks:
  - Copper and bronze technology spread from the Near East → Balkans → Europe via the Danube corridor and Mediterranean maritime routes
  - Iron technology spread from Anatolia → Greece → Italy → Celtic Europe → Germany/Scandinavia over ~700 years (~1200–500 BCE)
  - The Silk Road and steppe networks carried metallurgical knowledge between China, Central Asia, and the Near East
  - But **each receiving culture adapted and transformed** the technology to local needs — producing distinctive regional traditions (e.g., Celtic ironwork, Japanese sword-making, Indian wootz steel)

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Pre-2500 BCE African Copper/Iron
- Some dates for early African metallurgy (particularly iron) in the 2nd–3rd millennium BCE remain contested — if confirmed, they would further support independent African invention but currently lack the stratigraphic security and radiocarbon consistency required for full scholarly acceptance

### 3.2 Accidental Discovery vs. Deliberate Experimentation
- The question of how smelting was first discovered — whether through accidental heating of copper ores in ceramic kilns, campfire contexts, or deliberate experimentation — remains unresolved. The transition from native copper working to smelting involved a conceptual leap (understanding that fire + specific stones = metal) whose cognitive and social context is speculative

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 All Metallurgy from One Source
- **[CONTRADICTED]** The extreme diffusionist claim that all metallurgical knowledge worldwide originated in a single center (usually the Near East) and spread outward is contradicted by the independent development of metallurgy in the Americas and the strong evidence for independent African iron smelting

### 4.2 Ancient Advanced Alloys Lost to History
- **[UNSUPPORTED]** Claims that ancient civilizations possessed metallurgical knowledge vastly superior to modern understanding (e.g., "orichalcum" as a lost super-alloy, iron pillars that "never rust" as evidence of unknown alloys) are not supported — the Delhi Iron Pillar's corrosion resistance, for example, is well-explained by its high phosphorus content and dry atmospheric conditions, not by lost technology
