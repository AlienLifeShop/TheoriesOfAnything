## QUICK SUMMARY

Pottery is the most abundant artifact category in archaeological sites worldwide — more pottery sherds have been excavated than any other class of human-made object — making ceramics the foundation of archaeological chronology, trade-network reconstruction, and cultural-identity mapping.
The **Jōmon pottery** of Japan (c. 16,000 BP) is among the oldest known pottery in the world, produced by hunter-gatherers thousands of years before agriculture — overturning the assumption that pottery requires sedentary farming societies. Chinese pottery traditions from Xianrendong Cave may be even older (~20,000 BP).
**Lapita pottery** (c. 1600–500 BCE) serves as the primary archaeological tracer for the Austronesian expansion — the greatest maritime migration in human history — tracking the settlement of Oceania from Taiwan through Melanesia to Western Polynesia.
The Greek **red-figure and black-figure** vase traditions provide an unparalleled visual record of ancient Greek mythology, daily life, and ideology. Chinese **celadon** and later **porcelain** production represents one of the most technically sophisticated ceramic achievements, driving global trade via the Maritime Silk Road over centuries.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 World's oldest pottery — Jōmon and Chinese cave sites

The earliest known pottery vessels:
- **Xianrendong Cave, China (c. 20,000–18,000 BP):** pot sherds with charred residue (Wu et al., 2012) — the oldest securely dated pottery in the world.
- **Jōmon pottery, Japan (c. 16,500 BP):** the vessels from Odai Yamamoto I site are among the earliest; the Jōmon tradition continued for ~14,000 years (c. 16,500–2,300 BP), making it the longest continuously practiced pottery tradition.
- **Yuchanyan Cave, China (c. 16,000 BP):** additional early pottery context.
- All earliest pottery worldwide was produced by **hunter-gatherers** — not farmers — primarily for processing and storing aquatic and plant foods.
- This overturns the older "Neolithic package" assumption that pottery, agriculture, and sedentism evolved together.

### 1.2 Lapita pottery and Austronesian migration

Lapita pottery (c. 1600–500 BCE) is the defining archaeological marker of the Austronesian expansion:
- **Distribution:** from the Bismarck Archipelago through Melanesia to Fiji, Tonga, and Samoa — the "Lapita cultural complex" spans ~4,500 km.
- **Distinctive features:** dentate-stamped (comb-impressed) decoration with geometric, anthropomorphic, and face motifs; often red-slipped.
- Petrographic analysis of Lapita sherds reveals **long-distance transport** of pots (or clay) — some sherds at remote Polynesian sites contain minerals only found hundreds of kilometers away.
- The eastward boundary of Lapita pottery (Western Polynesia) marks the pause-point before later Polynesian voyages reached Hawaii, New Zealand, and Rapa Nui — societies that had lost pottery technology.

### 1.3 Greek vase painting as historical record

Attic vase painting (c. 700–323 BCE) provides the most extensive surviving body of ancient Greek pictorial art:
- **Black-figure technique** (c. 630–480 BCE): figures painted in black slip on orange clay, with details incised through to the clay body.
- **Red-figure technique** (c. 530–323 BCE, invented c. 530 BCE): background painted black, figures reserved in orange clay with details painted in — allowing greater naturalism.
- Subject matter: mythological narratives (~70% of surviving images), daily life (symposia, athletics, workshops), warfare, funerary scenes.
- Named artists are identifiable: Exekias, Euphronios, the Berlin Painter — constituting the first art-historical personalities in Western art.
- Greek vases were major trade commodities — found across the Mediterranean, Black Sea, and Celtic Europe, enabling reconstruction of trade networks.

### 1.4 Chinese porcelain — technological mastery

Chinese ceramic technology represents the most sustained innovation in world pottery:
- **Celadon glaze** (Yue ware, c. 200 CE onward): jade-green iron-reduction glaze achieved through controlled kiln atmospheres.
- **True porcelain** (tang dynasty, c. 7th century CE onward): fired at temperatures >1,300°C, creating vitrified, translucent bodies — not achieved in Europe until Meissen (1709 CE).
- **Song dynasty (960–1279):** five classic kiln traditions (Ru, Guan, Ge, Jun, Ding) — representing perhaps the finest ceramics ever produced.
- **Blue-and-white porcelain** (Yuan dynasty, 14th century): cobalt-decorated, mass-produced for export to Southeast Asia, the Middle East, and eventually Europe.
- Chinese porcelain was a primary driver of Maritime Silk Road trade — sherds at East African, Middle Eastern, and Southeast Asian sites trace trade volumes and routes.

### 1.5 Pottery dating methods

Pottery is critical for archaeological chronology through multiple methods:
- **Seriation** (Petrie, 1899): ordering pottery types by style to establish relative chronology — the foundational method of archaeological typology.
- **Thermoluminescence (TL) dating:** measures radiation damage accumulated since the clay was last fired — provides absolute dates up to ~100,000 years.
- **Optically stimulated luminescence (OSL):** related technique for dating the last light exposure of mineral grains.
- **Residue analysis:** lipid, protein, and isotope analysis of residues absorbed into pot walls reveals what was cooked, stored, or fermented — providing direct evidence of ancient diets and food practices (Evershed et al., 1999).

## 2. CREDIBLE BUT DEBATED CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated)

### 2.1 Pottery invention as independent vs. diffused

Whether pottery was invented once and diffused or independently invented multiple times:
- Current evidence supports **multiple independent inventions:** East Asia (~20,000 BP), North Africa/Sahara (~12,000 BP), Amazon (~8,000 BP), and possibly other regions.
- The enormous time gap between the earliest East Asian pottery and the earliest Near Eastern pottery (~10,000 BP) argues against a single diffusion.
- However, within East Asia, the relationship between Chinese and Japanese early pottery traditions (and whether the technology diffused or was independently developed) remains debated.

### 2.2 Jōmon complexity without agriculture

The long-lived Jōmon culture challenges the equation of complexity with agriculture:
- Jōmon pottery became increasingly elaborate over time — the Middle Jōmon "flame-style" vessels (c. 5,000 BP) are among the most ornate prehistoric ceramics worldwide.
- Jōmon people practiced sophisticated marine and forest exploitation, built permanent settlements, and created complex material culture — all without cereal agriculture.
- This complicates the standard narrative that cultural complexity requires agricultural surplus.

### 2.3 The "potter's wheel revolution"

The potter's wheel (invented in Mesopotamia, c. 4500–4000 BCE — earlier than the transport wheel):
- Enabled standardized mass production and faster forming.
- Whether the wheel was adopted primarily for efficiency or for aesthetic reasons (achieving perfect symmetry) is debated.
- Many sophisticated pottery traditions worldwide (Jōmon, Lapita, pre-Columbian Americas) never adopted the wheel, demonstrating that high-quality ceramics do not require it.

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Pottery as acoustic technology

Researchers have noted that certain ancient vessels produce specific resonant frequencies when struck or when air is blown across their openings — Peruvian whistling vessels, for example, produce complex acoustic interference patterns. Whether these acoustic properties were intentional features is uncertain for most vessels.

## 4. DUBIOUS OR FRINGE CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Ancient civilizations possessed industrial-grade kiln technology

While ancient kiln technology was impressive (Chinese kilns reaching >1,300°C, Roman kiln complexes), it operated within the capabilities of wood, charcoal, and coal firing — no evidence suggests temperatures or technologies beyond what combustion physics allows.

### 4.2 Pottery styles prove direct contact between Old and New World civilizations

Superficial similarities between Jōmon and Valdivia (Ecuador) pottery, once cited as evidence of trans-Pacific contact (Meggers et al., 1965), are now considered coincidental convergence. Radiocarbon dates and stratigraphic evidence show independent development.
