## QUICK SUMMARY

**Landscape painting** — the artistic representation of natural scenery — is among the most culturally revealing genres in the history of art, because the way a culture depicts nature reveals its deepest assumptions about the human relationship to the environment, the divine, beauty, and power. Two great traditions dominate the history: **Chinese shanshui painting** ("mountain-water painting," 山水画) and **European landscape painting**, each developing over centuries with profoundly different philosophical foundations. **Chinese shanshui** — a continuous tradition from the 5th century CE onward, reaching its heights in the Song dynasty (960–1279) — treats landscape not as a faithful depiction of a specific place but as a meditation on the cosmic relationship between humanity, nature, and the Dao. Guo Xi (*Early Spring*, 1072), Fan Kuan (*Travelers Among Mountains and Streams*, ~1000), and Ma Yuan exemplify the tradition — vast mountains, misty voids, tiny human figures suggesting humility before nature, executed in ink wash on silk or paper. **European landscape painting** was slow to achieve independent status — long subordinated to history, portrait, and religious painting. **Claude Lorrain** (17th century) established the ideal classical landscape; the **Romantic movement** (late 18th–19th century) elevated landscape to the expression of the **sublime** — Caspar David Friedrich (*Wanderer above the Sea of Fog*, 1818), J.M.W. Turner (light, atmosphere, proto-abstraction), and the American **Hudson River School** (Thomas Cole, Frederic Church, Albert Bierstadt — the American wilderness as spiritual revelation and national identity). **Impressionism** (Monet, Pissarro, Sisley) dissolved landscape into light and color through **plein air** painting. Contemporary landscape art engages with environmental crisis — ecocritical art, land art (Smithson, Goldsworthy), and ecological documentation.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 Chinese Shanshui Tradition
- **Origins**: landscape as independent subject emerges in Chinese painting by the 5th century CE — Zong Bing's essay "Introduction to Painting Landscape" (~440 CE) established the philosophical basis: landscape painting as a means of experiencing the Dao
- **Song dynasty masters**: Fan Kuan (*Travelers Among Mountains and Streams*, ~1000 CE — monumental vertical composition; imposing cliff, distant mist, tiny human figures), Guo Xi (*Early Spring*, 1072 — atmospheric depth, "dragon veins" of mountain formations), Ma Yuan and Xia Gui (Southern Song — asymmetric "one-corner" compositions, lyrical simplicity)
- **Philosophical foundations**: Daoist and Buddhist concepts — the void (empty space as positive compositional element, not absence), the complementarity of mountain and water (stability and movement, solid and fluid), the smallness of humans within cosmic nature
- **Technique**: ink and wash on silk or paper; "boneless" (mogu) and "outline" (gongbi) methods; the "Six Principles" of Xie He (~500 CE) — especially **qi yun sheng dong** (spirit resonance/vitality)

### 1.2 European Landscape Traditions
- **Claude Lorrain** (1600–1682): established the **classical ideal landscape** — golden light, ancient ruins, pastoral foreground, atmospheric distance, balanced composition — enormously influential on European taste (the "Claude glass" — a tinted mirror used by tourists to view scenery as if through a Claude painting)
- **Romantic landscape**: Caspar David Friedrich (*Wanderer above the Sea of Fog*, 1818; *Monk by the Sea*, 1810) — landscape as expression of the sublime, solitude, and spiritual yearning; J.M.W. Turner — increasingly abstract explorations of light, atmosphere, and the dissolution of form; John Constable — detailed, naturalistic English countryside (*The Hay Wain*, 1821)
- **Hudson River School** (1820s–1870s): Thomas Cole (*The Course of Empire*, 1836), Frederic Church (*Niagara*, 1857; *Heart of the Andes*, 1859), Albert Bierstadt (*Among the Sierra Nevada*, 1868) — the American wilderness as expression of divine presence, national destiny, and the sublime

### 1.3 The Sublime and the Picturesque
- **Edmund Burke** (*A Philosophical Enquiry into the Origin of Our Ideas of the Sublime and Beautiful*, 1757): the **sublime** — the aesthetic experience of vastness, power, darkness, and infinity that overwhelms the viewer with delightful terror — distinct from the **beautiful** (smallness, smoothness, delicacy)
- **The picturesque** (William Gilpin, Uvedale Price, 1790s): an aesthetic category between the sublime and the beautiful — roughness, irregularity, variety, and intricacy, as found in ruins, wild gardens, and rugged landscapes

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Impressionism and the Plein Air Revolution
- **Impressionism** (1860s–1880s): Claude Monet (*Impression, Sunrise*, 1872; *Haystacks* series, 1890–1891; *Water Lilies*, 1896–1926), Camille Pissarro, Alfred Sisley — broke landscape painting open by working **en plein air** (outdoors), using visible brushstrokes, bright unmixed colors, and capturing transient effects of light and atmosphere
- **Innovation**: abandoning the studio-based finished composition for direct, immediate response to visual experience — landscape as perception rather than ideal; serial painting (Monet's series — same subject at different times/seasons) foregrounded the subjective, temporal nature of seeing

### 2.2 Land Art and Contemporary Ecological Art
- **Land art / Earthworks** (1960s–1970s): Robert Smithson (*Spiral Jetty*, 1970), Michael Heizer (*Double Negative*, 1969–1970), Walter De Maria (*The Lightning Field*, 1977), Andy Goldsworthy (ephemeral natural sculptures photographed before their decay) — moved landscape art from representation to direct engagement with the earth itself
- **Ecocritical art**: contemporary artists addressing environmental crisis through landscape — photography documenting environmental degradation (Edward Burtynsky, industrial landscapes), climate-themed installations, activist art

### 3.1 Landscape Art as Environmental Ethics
- Some art historians and environmental philosophers (T.J. Demos, *Against the Anthropocene*, 2017) argue that landscape painting has always encoded a political relationship to the land — from the possessive "prospect" view of colonial landscape art (the viewer surveying land available for exploitation) to contemporary ecological art that attempts to foster environmental responsibility through aesthetic engagement. Whether art can effectively change environmental attitudes and behavior at scale remains unproven

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Landscape Painting Is a Purely Western Art Form
- **[FACTUALLY WRONG]** Chinese shanshui painting predates the emergence of European landscape painting as an independent genre by roughly a millennium. Landscape was the most prestigious genre in Chinese painting from the Song dynasty onward — whereas in Europe, landscape was considered a lower genre than history painting until the 18th–19th centuries
