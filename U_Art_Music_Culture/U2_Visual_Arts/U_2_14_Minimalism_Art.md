## QUICK SUMMARY

**Minimalism** — emerging in the early 1960s in New York as a radical reaction against the emotional excess of Abstract Expressionism — reduced art to its most fundamental elements: simple geometric forms, industrial materials, serial repetition, and the literal presence of the object in space. The term (initially used pejoratively) encompasses both **minimal art** in the visual arts and **minimal music** — two parallel but distinct movements that share a commitment to reduction, repetition, and perceptual intensity. In visual art, the major figures — **Donald Judd** (rectilinear boxes in aluminum and Plexiglas, "specific objects" that were neither painting nor sculpture), **Dan Flavin** (sculptures composed entirely of commercial fluorescent light tubes), **Carl Andre** (floor-level arrangements of standardized industrial units — bricks, metal plates), **Sol LeWitt** (whose wall drawings and modular structures gave birth to Conceptual Art's premise that the idea is the work), **Robert Morris** (large-scale geometric forms, anti-form), and **Frank Stella** ("What you see is what you see" — stripe paintings eliminating illusionism) — rejected representation, metaphor, narrative, and the artist's personal emotional expression. The object was simply itself: material, form, scale, and the viewer's spatial awareness of it. In music, **minimalism** emerged slightly later through **La Monte Young** (sustained tones, drones, pure tuning), **Terry Riley** (*In C*, 1964 — a landmark of patterned repetition), **Steve Reich** (phasing processes — *It's Gonna Rain*, *Piano Phase*; gradual process as "a compositional process and a sounding music that are one and the same thing"), and **Philip Glass** (additive rhythmic structures — *Music in Twelve Parts*, *Einstein on the Beach*). Both artistic and musical minimalism were shaped by responses to industrial materials, seriality, systems theory, and non-Western aesthetics (Japanese Zen, Balinese gamelan, West African drumming), and their legacies suffuse late-20th and 21st-century art, design, architecture, and electronic music.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 Minimal Art: Origins and Key Works
- **Context**: by the late 1950s, Abstract Expressionism (Pollock, de Kooning, Rothko) dominated American art; minimalism reacted against its emphasis on subjective expression, gestural brushwork, and metaphysical claims — replacing the "heroic" artist's touch with impersonal fabrication and geometric simplicity
- **Donald Judd** (1928–1994): rejected the categories "painting" and "sculpture"; his "specific objects" (1965 essay) — typically rectangular boxes in anodized aluminum, Plexiglas, or painted steel, mounted on walls or arranged in vertical stacks — emphasized material presence, color, and spatial interval; fabricated industrially to eliminate hand-making
- **Dan Flavin** (1933–1996): sculptures using exclusively standard commercial fluorescent light fixtures in various colors; light as sculptural material — *Monument for V. Tatlin* series (1964–1990); the work dissolves the art object's boundary, filling surrounding space with colored light
- **Carl Andre** (b. 1935): arranged standardized industrial materials (firebricks, metal plates, timber) in grid or linear configurations directly on the floor — *Equivalent VIII* (1966, 120 firebricks); eliminated the pedestal; art as "matter in its least altered state"
- **Frank Stella** (b. 1936): early "Black Paintings" (1958–1960, black enamel stripes separated by thin lines of raw canvas) eliminated illusionism and composition; "What you see is what you see" — the painting is a flat object, not a window onto another reality

### 1.2 Sol LeWitt and Conceptual Minimalism
- **Sol LeWitt** (1928–2007): modular white structures (open cubes in all permutations) and **wall drawings** (instructions written in text, executed by assistants/institutions); his pivotal essay "Paragraphs on Conceptual Art" (1967) argued that "the idea becomes a machine that makes the art" — bridging minimalism and conceptualism
- LeWitt demonstrated that the artist's contribution is the concept/system, not the hand-made object — a foundational principle for Conceptual Art

### 1.3 Precursors and Related Figures
- **Ad Reinhardt** (1913–1967): "ultimate paintings" — five-foot-square canvases painted entirely in near-black (actually dark reds, blues, greens visible only under sustained looking); "Art is art. Everything else is everything else"
- **Agnes Martin** (1912–2004): hand-drawn grids and pale horizontal bands on canvas — often classified with minimalism but self-identified as an Abstract Expressionist; her work is contemplative, luminous, and subtly varied rather than industrially precise
- **Yves Klein** (1928–1962): monochrome paintings in **International Klein Blue** (IKB, 1957); *The Void* (1958, empty gallery); precursor to minimalism through radical reduction

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Minimal Music
- **La Monte Young** (b. 1935): sustained tones and drones — *Composition 1960 #7* (a perfect fifth "to be held for a long time"); pure tuning (just intonation); his ongoing work *The Well-Tuned Piano* (1964–present, performances lasting 5+ hours); the Dream House (ongoing sound/light environment with Marian Zazeela)
- **Terry Riley** (b. 1935): ***In C*** (1964) — 53 short melodic patterns played by any number of instruments, each musician moving through the patterns at their own pace against a steady pulse — a landmark of modular repeated-pattern composition
- **Steve Reich** (b. 1936): **phasing process** — two identical recordings or instrumental parts gradually going out of sync (*It's Gonna Rain*, 1965; *Piano Phase*, 1967); essay "Music as a Gradual Process" (1968): "I want to be able to hear the process happening throughout the sounding music"; later works integrate West African drumming patterns (*Drumming*, 1971) and Balinese gamelan influences
- **Philip Glass** (b. 1937): additive/subtractive rhythmic processes — *Music in Twelve Parts* (1971–1974, 4+ hours); opera *Einstein on the Beach* (1976, with Robert Wilson — 5 hours, no intermission, no narrative); later film scores (*Koyaanisqatsi*, 1982)

### 2.2 Non-Western Influences
- **John Cage's Zen Buddhism**: Cage's *4'33"* (1952, three movements of silence / environmental sound) — not minimalism per se but a radical precursor; his study of Zen with D.T. Suzuki at Columbia influenced the minimalists' interest in stillness, repetition, and ego dissolution
- **Steve Reich in Ghana** (1970): studied Ewe drumming at the University of Ghana — influenced *Drumming* and subsequent works; *Gamelan* influences on *Music for 18 Musicians* (1974–1976)
- **Japanese aesthetics**: *wabi-sabi* (beauty in imperfection, impermanence, incompleteness), *ma* (negative space), and Zen garden design informed both visual and musical minimalism — though the degree of direct influence versus parallel development is debated

### 2.3 Legacy and Influence
- Minimalism's visual legacy: architecture (**Tadao Ando**, **John Pawson** — spare, material-focused design), product design (Apple's Jony Ive era), fashion (Helmut Lang, Jil Sander)
- Musical minimalism evolved into: **post-minimalism** (John Adams — *Nixon in China*; more harmonically and texturally complex), **ambient music** (Brian Eno — *Music for Airports*, 1978), **electronic dance music** (techno, house — repetitive patterns, minimal variation), and contemporary film scoring

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Minimalism Is Easy / Anyone Could Make It
- **[NOT SUPPORTED]** The common objection that minimal art requires no skill or thought (the "my kid could do that" dismissal) misunderstands the movement's aims. Minimalist works are the product of rigorous conceptual decisions about material, proportion, scale, spatial relationship, and context. Judd's meticulous specifications for industrial fabrication, Flavin's precise arrangements of light and color, and Reich's mathematical processes in phasing — all demand sophisticated thinking about perception, materiality, and system. The simplicity of appearance masks the complexity of decision-making
