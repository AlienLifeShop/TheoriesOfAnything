## QUICK SUMMARY

Cultural evolution theory applies Darwinian principles — variation, selection, inheritance — to the transmission and transformation of cultural information (beliefs, technologies, norms, institutions). The dual inheritance theory developed by Robert Boyd and Peter Richerson proposes that humans are shaped by two parallel inheritance systems: genetic and cultural, each with its own dynamics but interacting through gene-culture coevolution. Unlike Dawkins's memetics (which models individual memes as replicators), dual inheritance theory emphasizes population-level processes: prestige bias, conformity bias, cultural group selection, and the "ratchet effect" of cumulative culture that has made humans the planet's dominant species despite modest individual cognitive advantages over other great apes.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Empirical)

### 1.1 Dual Inheritance Theory (DIT)
- Boyd & Richerson (*Culture and the Evolutionary Process*, 1985): formalized mathematical models of cultural transmission
- Cultural inheritance is Lamarckian (acquired traits can be transmitted), unlike genetic inheritance
- Three core transmission biases: content bias (some ideas are intrinsically easier to learn), prestige bias (copy successful individuals), conformity bias (copy the majority)
- DIT is not "social Darwinism" — it studies cultural change mechanisms, not ranking cultures
- The framework has generated empirically testable predictions about technology adoption, norm spread, and institutional change

### 1.2 Cumulative Cultural Evolution (The Ratchet Effect)
- Michael Tomasello (*The Cultural Origins of Human Cognition*, 1999) described the "ratchet effect": innovations are preserved and built upon across generations
- No individual human invented modern technology — each generation inherits and incrementally improves the previous generation's cultural toolkit
- Experimental evidence: children outperform chimpanzees not through individual intelligence but through social learning fidelity
- The ratchet requires both innovation (new variants) and high-fidelity transmission (faithful copying) — humans excel at both
- Dean et al. (2012) showed that cumulative culture requires teaching, imitation, and prosociality

### 1.3 Gene-Culture Coevolution: Empirical Cases
- Lactose tolerance: dairying cultures evolved lactase persistence independently (European LCT, East African C/G-13907) — culture drove genetic selection
- Amylase gene copy number: populations with starch-heavy diets have more AMY1 copies (Perry et al., 2007)
- Sickle cell trait: malaria-resistant heterozygotes selected in agricultural populations that created mosquito-breeding rice paddies
- These cases demonstrate that cultural practices can create selective pressures that shape the human genome

### 1.4 The WEIRD Problem
- Henrich, Heine & Norenzayan (2010): most behavioral science research samples Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic populations
- WEIRD subjects are statistical outliers on many psychological measures (individualism, analytic thinking, fairness norms)
- This sampling bias means "universal" claims about human psychology often reflect WEIRD cultural evolution, not species-typical cognition
- The paper has been cited over 10,000 times and reshaped research methodology standards

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Cultural Group Selection
- Hypothesis: competition between culturally distinct groups can drive the spread of cooperative norms
- Groups with better cooperative institutions outcompete and replace groups with less effective ones
- Henrich (*The Secret of Our Success*, 2016) argues this explains human ultrasociality: religion, law, and kinship norms are culturally evolved group-level adaptations
- Controversial: critics (West, El Mouden & Gardner, 2011) argue individual-level selection suffices
- Historical evidence: the spread of institutional religions (Christianity, Islam) correlates with military and economic expansion

### 2.2 Cultural Phylogenetics
- Applying phylogenetic tree methods (from biology) to trace cultural lineage
- Language family trees (Bayesian phylogenetics: Gray & Atkinson, 2003) used to date Proto-Indo-European
- Folktale phylogenetics: da Silva & Tehrani (2016) traced "Little Red Riding Hood" and "The Wolf and the Kids" to distinct ancestral tales
- Musical instrument evolution and textile technology also analyzed phylogenetically
- Limitation: cultural traits transfer horizontally (borrowing) more readily than genes, complicating tree models

### 2.3 Cultural Niche Construction
- Organisms modify their environments, creating selective pressures on themselves and other species
- Humans are extreme niche constructors: agriculture, cities, institutions all reshape selective landscapes
- Odling-Smee, Laland & Feldman (2003) formalized niche construction theory
- Examples: cooking pre-digests food, relaxing selection on jaw strength and gut size, allowing brain expansion; literacy reshapes visual cortex

### 2.4 Prestige vs Dominance
- Henrich & Gil-White (2001): two routes to social influence — prestige (freely conferred deference) and dominance (coerced submission)
- Prestige bias drives cultural learning: people preferentially copy high-status individuals
- This explains celebrity influence, expert authority, and the spread of cultural practices from elite centers
- Dominance-based influence operates differently: compliance without internalization

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Cultural Intelligence Hypothesis
- The idea that human cognitive evolution was driven primarily by selection for social learning capacity rather than individual problem-solving
- "Collective brain" (Muthukrishna & Henrich, 2016): larger connected populations produce more cultural complexity
- Implication: the Tasmanian effect — isolated populations lose technologies (Tasmania lost bone tools, fishing, fire-making after sea-level rise isolated the island ~12,000 BP)
- If correct, individual human intelligence matters less than population size and connectivity
- The hypothesis explains why Neanderthals (with comparable brain size) produced less cumulative culture

### 3.2 Cultural Evolution of Religion
- Ara Norenzayan (*Big Gods*, 2013): moralizing high gods culturally evolved to solve cooperation problems in large-scale societies
- "Watched people are nice people" — belief in omniscient, punishing deities promotes prosociality
- Whitehouse's modes of religiosity theory: imagistic (intense, rare) vs doctrinal (frequent, routine) rituals serve different group-bonding functions
- These theories treat religion as a cultural adaptation rather than a byproduct — the adaptationist framing is debated

---

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source)

- "Social Darwinism" — using evolutionary language to justify racial hierarchies or colonial exploitation is a misapplication rejected by modern cultural evolutionists
- Claims that cultural evolution is "just progress" — an outdated unilinear model (Morgan, Tylor) replaced by branching, non-directional models
- The idea that cultural evolution can be fully reduced to individual meme replication without population-level dynamics
