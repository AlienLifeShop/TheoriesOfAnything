## QUICK SUMMARY

Graffiti — unsanctioned inscriptions on public surfaces — is among humanity's oldest and most persistent forms of expression, from the **11,000+ inscriptions** preserved at Pompeii (79 CE volcanic burial) to modern street art's global cultural movement.
Pompeii's graffiti (catalogued in the *Corpus Inscriptionum Latinarum*, CIL IV) includes political endorsements, obscene jokes, love declarations, commercial advertisements, and literary quotations — offering an unfiltered view of ordinary Roman life.
Modern graffiti emerged from the **New York City subway writing movement** (late 1960s–1970s), evolved through **hip-hop visual culture** (tagging, throw-ups, wildstyle), and was transformed by artists like **Jean-Michel Basquiat** (SAMO, 1977–1981) and **Banksy** (c. 2000–present) into a globally recognized art form that deliberately occupies the tension between vandalism and art, illegality and cultural prestige.
The **Berlin Wall** (1961–1989) became the world's most politically significant graffiti surface — unauthorized Western-side art contrasting with the bare Eastern side as a visual metaphor for freedom of expression.
Graffiti challenges institutional art definitions: who has the right to inscribe public space, and does requiring permission negate graffiti's essential character?

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Pompeii graffiti — window into Roman daily life

The eruption of Vesuvius (August 24, 79 CE) preserved thousands of wall inscriptions:
- **CIL IV** (*Corpus Inscriptionum Latinarum*, volume IV): the scholarly catalogue of Pompeii's inscriptions — over **11,000 graffiti** documented.
- Categories: electoral notices (*programmata* — "Vote for Lucius!"), commercial signs, sexual boasts and insults, love declarations ("Successus the weaver loves the innkeeper's slave girl"), gladiator fan graffiti, quotations from Virgil and Ovid, price lists, and scatological humor.
- **Political graffiti**: Pompeii's elections generated wall campaigns — candidates' supporters (including fullones/laundrymen, prostitutes, and thieves, possibly sarcastically) publicly endorsed their favorites.
- **Literacy evidence**: the range and frequency of graffiti suggests relatively widespread functional literacy in Roman urban populations — even slaves and lower-class individuals left inscriptions.
- Famous examples: "I wonder, O wall, that you have not yet collapsed, so laden with scrawlings" (CIL IV 2487) — meta-commentary on graffiti from antiquity.

### 1.2 Earlier ancient graffiti traditions

Graffiti predates Pompeii:
- **Egyptian graffiti**: tourists visiting the Valley of the Kings left graffiti as early as the New Kingdom (c. 1100 BCE) commenting on the tombs — "I came and I did not understand anything" (Tomb of Ramesses VI visitor).
- **Greek graffiti**: names, declarations, and ephebic inscriptions at the Temple of Ramesses II at Abu Simbel (Greek mercenaries, c. 591 BCE) — among the oldest "tourist graffiti" in the world.
- **Viking runes**: Norse visitors carved runic graffiti in Hagia Sophia (Istanbul) — "Halvdan was here" (discovered 1964) — and in the Neolithic passage tomb at Maeshowe (Orkney, c. 1153 CE), where Norse Crusaders left 30 runic inscriptions including boasts and sexual comments.

### 1.3 New York City subway graffiti movement (1960s–1980s)

Modern graffiti's foundational period:
- **TAKI 183** (Demetrius, a Greek-American teenager, 1969–1971): widely credited as the first prolific tagger — "TAKI 183" written on subway stations across Manhattan, profiled in *The New York Times* (1971).
- **Tagging**: the signature/name as fundamental unit; evolved into **throw-ups** (quick, bubble-letter pieces) and **wildstyle** (complex, interlocking letterforms readable only to initiates).
- **Whole-car masterpieces** (1970s–early 1980s): elaborate, multi-color works covering entire subway cars — artists including SEEN, DONDI, LEE, LADY PINK, FUTURA 2000, and PHASE 2.
- **"War on Graffiti"** (1980s–1990s): New York City's "Broken Windows" policing theory (Wilson & Kelling, 1982) targeted graffiti as a gateway to crime — MTA invested $300 million in cleaning trains, and by 1989 all graffitied cars were removed from service.
- The subway era's end pushed graffiti culture globally — European, Latin American, and Asian cities became new canvases.

### 1.4 Hip-hop visual culture and beyond

Graffiti as the visual pillar of hip-hop culture (alongside DJing, MCing, and breakdancing):
- **Jean-Michel Basquiat** (1960–1988): began as SAMO (with Al Diaz), spraying cryptic, poetic phrases across Lower Manhattan (1977–1979, e.g., "SAMO© AS AN END TO BOGUS PSEUDO INTELLECTUALISM"). Transitioned from street to gallery — his canvases now sell for $100+ million.
- **Keith Haring** (1958–1990): chalk drawings in blank New York subway advertising panels (1980–1985) — universally accessible visual vocabulary, political activism (anti-apartheid, AIDS awareness).
- **"Post-graffiti" movement** (1980s): galleries (Fun Gallery, Tony Shafrazi) brought street artists indoors — creating the commercial art-world integration that many graffitists rejected.

### 1.5 Banksy and contemporary street art

The anonymous Bristol-based artist Banksy (active c. 1990s–present):
- **Stencil technique**: allows rapid execution and precise, reproducible imagery — political satire, social commentary, and visual wit.
- Key works: *Girl with Balloon* (2002, Waterloo Bridge), *Kissing Coppers* (2004, Brighton), *Flower Thrower* (2003, Palestine/Israel separation wall), Dismaland (2015, dystopian theme park).
- **Self-shredding artwork** (2018): *Girl with Balloon* partially shredded itself at Sotheby's immediately after selling for £1.04 million — renamed *Love Is in the Bin*, it later sold for £18.5 million (2021).
- Banksy embodies the paradox of subversive art's cooptation — works intended as anti-capitalist gestures become some of the most expensive artworks on the market.

### 1.6 Berlin Wall as political canvas (1961–1989)

The Berlin Wall's graffiti represents the most politically charged surface art in modern history:
- **Western side**: freely accessible, covered in graffiti, murals, and political statements from the early 1980s onward — artists included Thierry Noir (first to paint the Wall systematically, 1984) and Keith Haring.
- **Eastern side**: bare concrete, surrounded by the *Todesstreifen* (death strip) — the absence of graffiti was itself a statement about repression.
- **East Side Gallery** (post-1989): 1.3 km of the remaining Wall painted by 118 artists from 21 countries — now the world's longest open-air gallery.
- Dmitri Vrubel's *My God, Help Me to Survive This Deadly Love* (Brezhnev-Honecker kiss, 1990): the most iconic Berlin Wall mural.

## 2. CREDIBLE BUT DEBATED CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated)

### 2.1 Whether graffiti is art or vandalism

The definitional debate:
- **Legal frameworks**: virtually all jurisdictions classify unsanctioned graffiti as vandalism/criminal damage — property owners bear cleaning costs.
- **Art-world integration**: Basquiat, Haring, Banksy, Shepard Fairey (OBEY), JR, Invader, and Os Gêmeos command museum exhibitions and million-dollar valuations.
- **Institutional paradox**: "authorized graffiti" (commissioned murals, legal walls) may lose the transgressive character that defines graffiti — one cannot create subversive art *with permission* (Cresswell, 1996).
- **Community impact**: research is mixed — some studies show graffiti correlates with neighborhood decline (Broken Windows thesis); others show commissioned murals improve community pride and reduce actual vandalism.

### 2.2 Graffiti as resistance to spatial control

Theoretical perspectives (Lefebvre, de Certeau):
- Graffiti asserts the right to inscribe public space against property law and institutional control — a "tactics vs. strategy" conflict (de Certeau, 1984).
- Political graffiti in authoritarian contexts (Syria, Myanmar, Hong Kong, Iran) functions as resistance when other forms of expression are suppressed.
- However, much graffiti is *not* politically motivated — territorial tags, ego assertions, and aesthetic practice constitute the majority. Political interpretation risks romanticizing the mundane.

### 2.3 Aboriginal rock art as ancestral "graffiti"

Australian Aboriginal rock art (some dated to 28,000+ BP) and South African San rock art:
- These are sometimes discussed alongside graffiti as "writing on walls" — but the comparison is problematic.
- Indigenous rock art is sacred, ceremonial, and cosmologically significant — categorizing it as "graffiti" (unauthorized, secular, subversive) imposes inappropriate Western frameworks.

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Prehistoric cave "hand stencils" as the first graffiti

Hand stencils in Upper Paleolithic caves (Cueva de las Manos, 7300 BCE; Sulawesi, 39,900 BP) — are these the earliest "I was here" inscriptions? The analogy is suggestive but the ritual or symbolic context of cave hand stencils is likely far more complex than modern "tagging."

## 4. DUBIOUS OR FRINGE CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Pompeii graffiti proves Romans had modern-equivalent popular culture

While Pompeii's graffiti is relatable, projecting modern concepts of "popular culture" onto Roman society obscures fundamental differences in social structure, literacy, class, and the role of public inscription. The graffiti is evidence of *Roman* communicative practices, which partially resemble but are not equivalent to modern ones.

### 4.2 Graffiti writers communicate with entities through wall inscriptions

Occult claims about graffiti as spirit communication or sigil magic have no evidential basis.
