## QUICK SUMMARY

**Documentary film and photography** — creative works purporting to represent reality directly, serving as witness, evidence, and social commentary — occupy a uniquely charged position between art and journalism, truth and construction. The term "documentary" was coined by **John Grierson** (1926, reviewing Robert Flaherty's *Moana*) — he defined it as "the creative treatment of actuality," a formulation that immediately encodes the genre's central paradox: documentary claims to show reality, but the filmmaker or photographer inevitably selects, frames, edits, and structures that reality. **Robert Flaherty's *Nanook of the North*** (1922) — often cited as the first feature-length documentary — depicted Inuit life with compelling intimacy while also staging scenes, using outdated hunting methods, and casting Flaherty's own partner as "Nanook's wife." This tension between authentic witness and creative construction runs through the entire history of the genre. **Photography as evidence**: since its invention (1839), photography has been treated as inherently truthful ("the camera never lies") — a presumption exploited by photojournalism (Robert Capa's D-Day images, Nick Ut's "Napalm Girl," Kevin Carter's Sudanese famine photograph) and challenged by the long history of photographic manipulation (from 19th-century combination printing to Stalin-era airbrushing of purged officials to contemporary deepfakes). **Susan Sontag** (*On Photography*, 1977; *Regarding the Pain of Others*, 2003) offered the most influential critical analysis: photographs do not simply record reality — they frame it, aestheticize it, and can produce both empathy and numbness through repetition. **Ethical questions** pervade the field: when does a photographer's presence alter events? Is it ethical to photograph suffering for publication? Does documentary film exploit its subjects? Can "objectivity" exist when every camera angle is a choice?

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 Origins of Documentary Film
- **Robert Flaherty, *Nanook of the North*** (1922): feature-length film depicting Inuit life — commercially successful, establishing documentary as a viable cinematic form. Flaherty developed an improvisational, observational approach — living with his subjects for extended periods — but also staged scenes (the igloo interior was a half-igloo built for camera access; hunting scenes used traditional methods Nanook no longer used)
- **Grierson and the British documentary movement** (1930s–1940s): John Grierson defined documentary and established it as a tool of democratic education — *Drifters* (1929, herring fishermen), Night Mail (1936, with W.H. Auden and Benjamin Britten); founded the National Film Board of Canada (1939)
- **Leni Riefenstahl, *Triumph of the Will*** (1935): documenting the 1934 Nuremberg Rally — technically brilliant and politically devastating; demonstrated that documentary and propaganda are not mutually exclusive; raised permanent questions about the ethics of aestheticizing politics

### 1.2 Documentary Modes (Nichols)
- **Bill Nichols** (*Introduction to Documentary*, 2001): six modes of documentary representation:
  - **Expository**: authoritative voiceover ("voice of God"), argument-driven (traditional TV documentary)
  - **Observational**: "fly on the wall" — no narration, no interviews, camera as passive witness (Frederick Wiseman, *Titicut Follies*, 1967)
  - **Participatory**: filmmaker interacts with subjects (Michael Moore, *Roger and Me*, 1989)
  - **Reflexive**: drawing attention to the filmmaking process itself (Dziga Vertov, *Man with a Movie Camera*, 1929)
  - **Performative**: subjective, emotional, personal engagement (Marlon Riggs, *Tongues Untied*, 1989)
  - **Poetic**: aesthetic experience, tone, and rhythm over argument (Godfrey Reggio, *Koyaanisqatsi*, 1982)

### 1.3 Photography as Witness
- **Photojournalism**: photography's claim to evidentiary status made it a powerful instrument of social documentation and reform:
  - Jacob Riis (*How the Other Half Lives*, 1890): flash photography of New York tenement life — instrumental in housing reform
  - Dorothea Lange (*Migrant Mother*, 1936): iconic Depression-era photograph — commissioned by the Farm Security Administration
  - Robert Capa (D-Day, Spanish Civil War): "If your pictures aren't good enough, you're not close enough" — the photojournalist as witness at risk
  - Nick Ut (*The Terror of War* / "Napalm Girl," 1972): Vietnam War image that shifted public opinion

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Sontag's Critique
- **Susan Sontag** (*On Photography*, 1977): photographs do not simply mirror reality — they appropriate it, miniaturize it, and turn experience into collectible images. Photography creates a "habit of seeing" that can produce aesthetic distance from suffering
- **Sontag (*Regarding the Pain of Others*, 2003)**: partially revised her earlier position — acknowledging that photographs of atrocity can produce genuine ethical responses, but warning against "compassion fatigue" (repeated exposure to suffering images produces numbness rather than action)

### 2.2 Ethical Dilemmas
- **Kevin Carter's Sudanese famine photograph** (1993): vulture near a malnourished child — won the Pulitzer Prize; Carter committed suicide the following year amid criticism for photographing rather than helping. The case encapsulates the documentary dilemma: the act of witnessing may conflict with the obligation to intervene
- **Staging and manipulation**: the history of documentary photography includes significant staging — Alexander Gardner repositioned bodies at Gettysburg (1863); the iconic Iwo Jima flag-raising photograph (Rosenthal, 1945) was the second flag-raising (not the first); Lewis Hine's child labor photographs, while documenting real conditions, were composed for maximum emotional impact

### 2.3 Direct Cinema and Cinéma Vérité
- **Direct cinema** (US, 1960s — Robert Drew, D.A. Pennebaker, Frederick Wiseman, Albert and David Maysles): lightweight 16mm cameras and portable sync sound enabled "observational" filmmaking — filmmakers as unobtrusive witnesses; no interviews, no narration, no staging (*Primary*, 1960; *Salesman*, 1969; *Grey Gardens*, 1975)
- **Cinéma vérité** (France — Jean Rouch, Edgar Morin): filmmaker as provocateur — *Chronique d'un été* (1961): "Do you think the presence of the camera changes behavior?" asked on camera — the film acknowledges and interrogates its own construction

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Post-Truth Documentary in the Deepfake Era
- With the proliferation of deepfakes, AI-generated imagery, and sophisticated video manipulation, photography's evidentiary status — already contested — may be fundamentally undermined. If any image can be fabricated convincingly, the epistemological foundation of documentary (the camera was there; this happened) collapses. Whether technical solutions (cryptographic image authentication, blockchain provenance) or institutional trust (verified journalism organizations) can maintain the evidentiary function of documentary in the deepfake era is an open question

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Documentary Is Purely Objective
- **[NOT SUPPORTED]** The claim that documentary film or photography provides an unmediated window on reality is contradicted by the entire history and theory of the genre. Every documentary involves choices: what to film, from what angle, how long to hold a shot, what to include in the edit, how to structure the narrative, what musical score to use. These choices are authorial, not neutral. As Grierson himself acknowledged, documentary is "the creative treatment of actuality" — not raw reality
