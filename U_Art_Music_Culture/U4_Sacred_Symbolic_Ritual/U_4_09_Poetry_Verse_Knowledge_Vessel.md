## QUICK SUMMARY

Poetry — structured, rhythmic, and densely composed language — served as humanity's primary technology of knowledge preservation for millennia before widespread literacy.
The **Epic of Gilgamesh** (c. 2100 BCE in Sumerian, c. 1200 BCE in Standard Babylonian) is the earliest surviving literary epic, composed in verse on cuneiform tablets; **Homeric epic** (*Iliad*, *Odyssey*, c. 8th century BCE) was transmitted orally in dactylic hexameter, with **Milman Parry and Albert Lord's oral-formulaic theory** (1930s–1960s) demonstrating that repeated phrases ("rosy-fingered dawn," "swift-footed Achilles") functioned as composing units for improvising bards.
The Persian **Shahnameh** (Ferdowsi, c. 1010 CE, ~50,000 couplets) preserved Iranian mythological and historical tradition; Sufi **ghazals** (Rumi, Hafez) encoded mystical experience; Sanskrit **shlokas** transmitted philosophical, scientific, and religious knowledge across millennia; and the Hebrew **Psalms** served liturgical, devotional, and political functions.
Across cultures, verse served identical purposes: mnemonic structure (meter, rhyme, parallelism), emotional resonance, authoritative formulation, and resistance to textual corruption — poetry was a survival technology for knowledge.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 The Epic of Gilgamesh — earliest literary verse

The Gilgamesh tradition represents the oldest known literary narrative:
- **Sumerian poems** about Gilgamesh (king of Uruk, c. 2800 BCE, possibly historical) date to the Ur III period (c. 2100 BCE).
- The **Standard Babylonian version** (Sîn-lēqi-unninni, c. 1200 BCE) unified earlier poems into a 12-tablet epic — ~3,000 lines of Akkadian verse.
- Verse structure: rhythmic Akkadian poetry with parallelism, repetition, and stock phrases — oral-formulaic characteristics.
- Themes: mortality, friendship, hubris, the futility of immortality — anticipated philosophical concerns of subsequent millennia.
- The Flood narrative (Tablet XI) parallels Genesis — demonstrating literary transmission across cultures and languages.

### 1.2 Homeric oral-formulaic composition

Milman Parry (1928–1935) and Albert Lord (*The Singer of Tales*, 1960) demonstrated that Homeric epic was composed orally:
- **Formulas**: repeated metrical phrases ("wine-dark sea," "swift-footed Achilles") are not decorative but structural — they fill specific metrical positions in the dactylic hexameter line.
- **Type-scenes**: arming, feasting, assembly — conventional narrative patterns that aid improvisation.
- **Parry's fieldwork** among South Slavic *guslari* (oral poets in Yugoslavia) demonstrated that illiterate singers could compose and perform epics of thousands of lines using formulaic technique.
- The *Iliad* (~15,693 lines) and *Odyssey* (~12,110 lines): composed orally over generations, written down c. 750–650 BCE.
- **Homeric Question**: whether "Homer" was one person, two, or a tradition — remains debated, but oral-formulaic composition is firmly established.

### 1.3 Sanskrit verse traditions

Sanskrit literature preserved knowledge across religious, philosophical, scientific, and artistic domains:
- **Vedas** (c. 1500–1200 BCE): the *Rigveda* contains 1,028 hymns in metrical verse — transmitted orally with extraordinary precision through elaborate mnemonic systems (*vikṛti* recitation patterns with forward, reverse, and interlocking repetitions).
- **Shloka** meter: 32-syllable verse (2 lines of 16 syllables each) — the standard meter of the *Mahabharata* (~100,000 shlokas, world's longest epic), *Ramayana*, and Puranas.
- **Panini's *Ashtadhyayi*** (c. 4th century BCE): ~4,000 grammatical rules composed in sutra form (extremely compressed verse meant for memorization) — the first formal grammar of any language.
- Verse-form scientific texts: *Surya Siddhanta* (astronomy), *Lilavati* (mathematics, Bhaskara II, 12th century) — scientific knowledge encoded in memorable verse.

### 1.4 Hebrew poetry and the Psalms

Hebrew biblical poetry employs distinctive structures:
- **Parallelism** (Robert Lowth, *De Sacra Poesi Hebraeorum*, 1753): synonymous (A restates B), antithetic (A contrasts B), and synthetic (B extends A) — the fundamental organizing principle.
- **150 Psalms**: attributed traditionally to David but composed across centuries (c. 1000–300 BCE); served liturgical, devotional, royal, penitential, and wisdom functions.
- **Acrostic poems**: Psalm 119 (176 verses, 22 sections corresponding to the Hebrew alphabet, each section's 8 verses beginning with the same letter) — mnemonic structure facilitating memorization.
- Prophetic poetry (Isaiah, Jeremiah, Amos): verse as vehicle for social criticism and theological argument.

### 1.5 The Shahnameh and Iranian verse tradition

Ferdowsi's *Shahnameh* (Book of Kings, c. 1010 CE):
- ~50,000 couplets (the world's longest poem composed by a single author) in *masnavi* form (rhyming couplets in *mutaqārib* meter).
- Preserves the mythological and historical tradition of pre-Islamic Iran — from the creation myth and legendary kings (Jamshid, Zahhak, Rostam) through the Sassanid dynasty.
- Deliberately employed "pure" Persian vocabulary to resist Arabic linguistic influence following the Islamic conquest — served as a cultural preservation project.
- Manuscript tradition: lavishly illustrated copies (e.g., Houghton/Shah Tahmasp *Shahnameh*, c. 1524–1535, 258 paintings — the most expensive illustrated manuscript ever produced).

### 1.6 African griot and oral poetry traditions

West African griots (*jeli*, *gewel*, *griot*):
- Hereditary poet-musicians who served as historians, genealogists, counselors, and praise-singers across Mande, Wolof, and other West African cultures.
- **Sundiata** epic (Mali Empire foundation narrative, 13th century): transmitted orally by griots for centuries, first transcribed by Djibril Tamsir Niane (1960).
- The *Ozidi Saga* (Ijaw, Niger Delta), *Mwindo* epic (Nyanga, Congo) — African oral epics that combine prose, verse, song, and dramatic performance.
- Griots are living libraries — the proverb "When an old man dies, a library burns" (attributed to Hampâté Bâ, 1960, UNESCO) reflects the status of oral poets as knowledge vessels.

## 2. CREDIBLE BUT DEBATED CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated)

### 2.1 Sufi mystical poetry as encoded knowledge

Persian Sufi poets (Rumi, Hafez, Attar, Saadi) employed the ghazal form:
- **Rumi** (Jalāl ad-Dīn Muhammad Rūmī, 1207–1273): *Masnavi-ye Ma'navi* (~25,000 couplets) — called "the Quran in Persian" — uses narrative, parable, and paradox to transmit mystical experience.
- **Hafez** (c. 1315–1390): 500+ ghazals whose surfaces present wine, love, and gardens but whose interiors, in Sufi interpretation, convey stages of mystical union (*fanā'*, annihilation of self).
- Debate: whether Sufi poetry is primarily mystical allegory, secular love poetry, or both simultaneously — modern scholarship (Lewisohn, 2010) argues for irreducible polysemy.

### 2.2 The mnemonic superiority of verse over prose

Cognitive science available evidence suggests:
- Meter, rhyme, and alliteration enhance memory encoding — **Rubin (1995)** showed that constraints of poetic form reduce the variation possible in recall, making texts more faithfully transmitted.
- Ballads transmitted orally for centuries show remarkable stability — far more than prose narratives.
- However, the "mnemonic advantage" applies primarily to retrieval cues and structural features, not necessarily verbatim recall of content — debate continues on the mechanism.

### 2.3 Australian Aboriginal songlines as geographical poetry

Aboriginal Australian oral tradition:
- **Songlines** (dreaming tracks) are mythological narratives encoded in song that simultaneously function as navigational maps across the Australian landscape.
- The rhythm and melodic contour of songs correspond to the terrain being traversed — the music itself encodes geographical information.
- Cultural sensitivity limits scholarly access — much knowledge is restricted, and academic descriptions (Chatwin's *The Songlines*, 1987) are viewed as oversimplified by Aboriginal communities.

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Pre-literate verse traditions extending deep into the Paleolithic

Scholars speculate that structured rhythmic recitation may have accompanied ritual activities at sites like Lascaux or Chauvet Cave (30,000+ BP), based on the acoustics of painted chambers. Evidence is circumstantial — no direct evidence of Paleolithic verse survives.

## 4. DUBIOUS OR FRINGE CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 The Vedas encode advanced scientific knowledge transmitted from a lost civilization

While Sanskrit verse traditions are genuinely sophisticated and encode real astronomical and mathematical knowledge, claims that Vedic hymns describe nuclear weapons (*brahmastra*), aircraft (*vimana*), or quantum physics are anachronistic projections of modern concepts onto ancient texts. Vedic knowledge reflects the genuine achievements of its own historical context.

### 4.2 Homer's *Odyssey* is a literal navigation guide to Atlantic locations

Various authors (e.g., Vinci, *The Baltic Origins of Homer's Epic Tales*, 2006) have proposed that Homeric geography describes real Atlantic, Baltic, or other non-Mediterranean locations. Professional classicists and archaeologists reject these claims — the Homeric world corresponds recognizably to the eastern Mediterranean, with mythological elaboration.
