## QUICK SUMMARY

A small but growing body of geophysical research has documented measurable electromagnetic and geomagnetic anomalies at several ancient megalithic sites, including the Rollright Stones (Oxfordshire, England), Carnac (Brittany, France), Avebury, and various Irish passage tombs.
Researcher Paul Devereux's "Dragon Project" (1977–1987) and subsequent work by Don Robins, Michael Persinger, and others detected localized magnetic field variations, enhanced radiation levels (within safe margins), and ultrasound emissions at specific stone monuments.
These measured anomalies may result from the natural magnetic properties of the stones themselves (many megalithic stones are granite, dolerite, or basalt with high iron content), subsurface geological faults, or piezoelectric effects from tectonic stress.
The central debate is whether ancient builders intentionally selected sites or stones for their geomagnetic properties, or whether any correlation between monuments and anomalies is coincidental.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Stones with measurable magnetic properties

Many megalithic monuments incorporate stones with inherent magnetic properties:
- **Bluestone at Stonehenge:** The spotted dolerite bluestones transported from the Preseli Hills (~250 km away) contain significant concentrations of magnetite. Darvill and Wainwright (2014) documented their enhanced magnetic susceptibility.
- **Granite at Carnac:** Many Carnac menhirs are composed of granite with measurable residual magnetism.
- **Basalt at various sites:** Basalt, used at multiple megalithic sites worldwide, has among the highest magnetic susceptibility of common rock types.
The magnetic properties of these stones are a function of their mineral composition (magnetite, hematite, ilmenite content), not anomalous or unexplained phenomena (Darvill & Wainwright, 2014; Robins, 1985).

### 1.2 Dragon Project measurements (1977–1987)

Paul Devereux organized multi-year monitoring at the Rollright Stones (Oxfordshire) and other sites using:
- Gauss magnetometers detecting localized magnetic variations up to 50% above background at specific stones.
- Geiger counters detecting slight radiation elevation (within normal safe limits) at the King Stone.
- Ultrasound detectors registering emissions from some stones at dawn around the equinoxes (seasonal correlation noted but not proven causal).
The measurements were conducted by volunteer physicists and engineers, and while not published in major peer-reviewed journals, the data collection methodology was documented and repeatable (Devereux, 1990; Robins, 1985).

### 1.3 Archaeomagnetic dating studies

Archaeomagnetism — using the thermoremanent magnetism preserved in fired archaeological materials — is a well-established dating technique.
These studies confirm that ancient fires, kilns, and heated stones preserve measurable records of the Earth's magnetic field at the time of heating.
While primarily a dating method, archaeomagnetic studies demonstrate that ancient people necessarily interacted with stones that had measurable magnetic properties, even if they may not have perceived this (Tarling, 1983; Aitken, 1990).

## 2. CREDIBLE BUT DEBATED CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated)

### 2.1 Intentional selection of magnetic stones

Research by the University of Oxford and Bournemouth University has shown:
- At some Welsh sites, stones with measurably higher magnetic susceptibility were preferentially used as standing stones, even when non-magnetic alternatives were available nearby.
- At Stonehenge, the specific bluestones transported from Wales have enhanced magnetic and acoustic properties compared to other available Preseli stone types.
This pattern suggests **some degree of intentional selection**, though whether builders selected for magnetic properties specifically (vs. color, texture, acoustic properties, or cultural significance of the source location) cannot be determined from the physical evidence alone (Darvill & Wainwright, 2014; Thomas, 2022).

### 2.2 Persinger's tectonic strain theory

Neuroscientist Michael Persinger proposed that many sacred sites are located on or near tectonic fault zones, where geological stress generates electromagnetic fields that can affect human consciousness — producing altered states, visions, or feelings of "presence."
Evidence supporting the theory:
- Statistical correlation between reported "haunting" sites and proximity to faults and aquifers.
- Laboratory experiments showing temporal lobe stimulation via weak magnetic fields can produce experiences of "presence" and altered states ("God Helmet" experiments).
Evidence against:
- The correlation between sacred sites and faults may simply reflect the fact that fault zones often produce springs, mineral deposits, and dramatic landscape features that attract settlement for practical reasons.
- The "God Helmet" findings have been disputed in replication attempts (Persinger, 1987; Granqvist et al., 2005).

### 2.3 Acoustic properties of megalithic chambers

Related research has documented acoustic anomalies at megalithic sites:
- Newgrange and Cairn L (Loughcrew) in Ireland produce infrasonic resonance when standing waves are generated inside the chambers.
- The frequency of resonance (~110 Hz) has been shown in modern neuroscience studies to activate areas of the brain associated with language, emotional processing, and altered states.
- Researchers link acoustic and geomagnetic anomalies as co-selected properties (Jahn et al., 1996; Cook et al., 2008).

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Ancient "geomagnetic awareness"

Researchers propose that ancient peoples possessed an empirical awareness of geomagnetic properties — perhaps through effects on dowsing rods (which could theoretically respond to magnetic gradients) or through direct physiological sensitivity.
Human magnetoreception research (Baker, 1989; Wang et al., 2019) has shown that some humans do exhibit unconscious brain responses to changes in the geomagnetic field, though whether this is sufficient for conscious awareness of geomagnetic anomalies at specific locations is unproven.

### 3.2 Earth energy grids and ley lines

The broader "earth energy" framework that connects geomagnetic anomalies to ley lines, geodetic grids, and global energy patterns remains scientifically unsubstantiated at the scale claimed (see O_1_01 for full ley line coverage).

## 4. DUBIOUS OR FRINGE CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Stone circles as "power generators"

Claims that stone circles function as electromagnetic generators or energy transmitters have no basis in physics. The measured magnetic anomalies are small localized variations, not coherent power generation.

### 4.2 Crystal-energy healing at ancient sites

Claims that visiting megalithic sites produces measurable health benefits through "earth energies" are unsupported by medical research. Any reported benefits likely reflect placebo effects, meditation-like relaxation, and the psychological value of pilgrimage and nature connection.
