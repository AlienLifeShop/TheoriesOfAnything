## QUICK SUMMARY

The **Aztec Empire** — more precisely the **Mexica-led Triple Alliance** (c. 1428–1521 CE) of **Tenochtitlan**, **Texcoco**, and **Tlacopan** — was the dominant political and military power in **Mesoamerica** at the time of the Spanish arrival. At its height under **Moctezuma II** (r. 1502–1520), the empire controlled a population of roughly 5–6 million people across central Mexico through a tributary system extracting tribute in goods, labor, and sacrificial victims from ~400–500 subject city-states. The capital, **Tenochtitlan** — founded on an island in Lake Texcoco c. 1325 by the semi-legendary migration of the Mexica people — had grown by 1519 into one of the largest cities in the world, with an estimated population of 200,000–250,000 (larger than any contemporary European city except possibly Constantinople and Paris) — connected to the mainland by three great causeways, supplied by an aqueduct, and sustained by **chinampas** (raised-bed artificial islands — often called "floating gardens" — one of the most productive agricultural systems ever devised). The **Templo Mayor** — the double pyramid at the center of the sacred precinct, dedicated to **Huitzilopochtli** (god of war and the sun) and **Tlaloc** (god of rain) — was the focus of an elaborate ritual cycle that included **human sacrifice** on a scale debated by scholars but unquestionably central to Aztec religion. The Aztec **cosmovision** conceived the universe as existing in the Fifth Sun (the fifth cosmic era), sustained by the blood of sacrificial victims — without which the sun would cease to move and the cosmos would end. The empire fell to **Hernán Cortés** and his Spanish-Tlaxcalan-indigenous alliance in 1521, after a devastating siege in which smallpox and other European diseases killed perhaps half the population.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 Political Structure and Territory
- The **Triple Alliance** (Excan Tlatoloyan) was formed in 1428 after the overthrow of the Tepanec/Azcapotzalco hegemony — Tenochtitlan (the Mexica), Texcoco (the Acolhua), and Tlacopan (the Tepanec) divided the tribute of subjected states, with Tenochtitlan rapidly becoming the dominant partner
- The empire was **hegemonic** rather than territorial — it did not station garrisons or administrators in most conquered territories; instead, it extracted **tribute** (cacao, cotton, feathers, jade, gold, maize, obsidian, and sacrificial captives) and punished resistance with devastating military campaigns
- At its greatest extent (c. 1519), the Triple Alliance dominated most of central Mexico from the Gulf Coast to the Pacific — but strategic unconquered enclaves persisted (notably **Tlaxcala**, whose hostility to the Aztecs made them crucial allies for Cortés)

### 1.2 Tenochtitlan
- Founded c. 1325 on a small island in Lake Texcoco — according to Mexica tradition, at the spot where an eagle was seen perched on a cactus devouring a serpent (the image on the modern Mexican flag)
- By 1519: an urban center of ~200,000–250,000 people covering ~13–14 km²; connected to the mainland by three causeways (from Tepeyac, Iztapalapa, and Tlacopan); supplied with fresh water by an aqueduct from Chapultepec; the central sacred precinct enclosed the **Templo Mayor** and ~78 other temples and structures
- **Chinampas**: artificial raised planting beds constructed in the shallow lake — created by layering mud, decaying vegetation, and aquatic plants over wooden frames anchored to the lakebed; extraordinarily productive (multiple harvests per year); archaeologically documented and still partially operative in Xochimilco

### 1.3 Templo Mayor Excavations
- The **Templo Mayor** was systematically excavated from 1978 onward (directed by Eduardo Matos Moctezuma) — in the heart of Mexico City; excavations revealed **seven construction phases** (each ruler enlarged the pyramid); thousands of offering deposits including sacrificial human remains, jade, obsidian, coral, gold, and imported objects from throughout Mesoamerica
- Ongoing excavations (2010s–2020s) discovered the **Huei Tzompantli** (Great Skull Rack) — a massive structure containing the skulls of thousands of sacrificed individuals — confirming and exceeding earlier Spanish accounts of the scale of public skull display

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Human Sacrifice — Scale and Meaning
- Human sacrifice was a central ritual practice in Aztec religion — prisoners of war, slaves, and occasionally voluntary participants were killed in ceremonies atop pyramids; the heart was extracted and offered to the gods; the body was sent down the pyramid steps
- **Scale**: the Spanish Conquistadors reported enormous numbers (Bernal Díaz del Castillo estimated 136,000 skulls on the Tenochtitlan tzompantli); modern scholars debate the exact numbers but the 2017–2020 discovery of the Huei Tzompantli (with thousands of skulls) confirmed that sacrifice operated on a large scale — though likely lower than the most extreme Spanish claims
- **Cosmological rationale**: in the Aztec cosmovision, the current world (the Fifth Sun) was sustained by the sacrifice of the gods at its creation — in return, humans were obligated to feed the sun with human blood (chalchihuatl — "precious liquid") to prevent cosmic dissolution; sacrifice was thus understood as a reciprocal cosmic obligation, not mere cruelty

### 2.2 Cosmovision and Calendar
- The Aztecs operated two interlocking **calendars**: the **tonalpohualli** (260-day ritual calendar — 20 trecenas of 13 days) and the **xiuhpohualli** (365-day solar calendar — 18 months of 20 days plus 5 unlucky days — nemontemi); every 52 years, the two calendars aligned in a **Calendar Round**, marked by the **New Fire Ceremony** (Toxiuhmolpilia), during which all fires were extinguished and re-lit from a new fire drilled on a sacrificial victim's chest
- The **Aztec Sun Stone** (Piedra del Sol — carved c. 1502–1521, ~3.6 m diameter, ~24 tonnes) is not a functional calendar but a cosmological monument depicting the Five Suns, the day-signs of the tonalpohualli, and solar imagery — one of the most famous artifacts from the pre-Columbian Americas

### 2.3 Fall of the Empire
- **Hernán Cortés** landed on the Gulf Coast in April 1519 with ~500 soldiers, 16 horses, and 14 artillery pieces; he formed critical alliances with indigenous groups hostile to Aztec dominance — especially the **Tlaxcala** confederation
- After the initial meeting with **Moctezuma II** and a period of uneasy coexistence, the Spanish were expelled from Tenochtitlan during the **Noche Triste** (June 30, 1520); Cortés regrouped, built brigantines, and besieged Tenochtitlan from August 1520 to August 13, 1521
- The siege was devastating: **smallpox** (introduced by the Spanish) killed an estimated 40–50% of the Tenochtitlan population; combined with starvation, the destruction of the aqueduct, and the overwhelming numbers of indigenous allies, the Mexica were defeated; the last tlatoani, **Cuauhtémoc**, was captured and later executed

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Moctezuma's Motivations
- Whether Moctezuma genuinely believed Cortés to be the returning god **Quetzalcoatl** — as many popular accounts claim — is debated. Scholars such as Matthew Restall and Camilla Townsend have argued that the "returning god" narrative was a post-conquest construction, shaped by Spanish and indigenous chroniclers after the fact to rationalize the defeat

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Aztec Society as Purely Barbaric
- **[REFUTED]** Colonial and later narratives that reduce Aztec civilization to "savagery" defined by human sacrifice ignore the extraordinary achievements in agriculture (chinampas), urban planning, poetry (Nahuatl poetic traditions — "flower and song"), legal systems, education (the calmecac and telpochcalli schools), botanical gardens, and astronomical observation that characterized one of the most complex civilizations in human history
