## QUICK SUMMARY

**Tiwanaku** (also spelled Tiahuanaco) was a major pre-Columbian civilization centered at the site of the same name on the **Bolivian Altiplano** (high plateau), approximately 3,850 meters above sea level and 20 km southeast of **Lake Titicaca** — the highest navigable lake in the world. Flourishing from approximately 500 BCE to 1000 CE (with its political peak around 500–900 CE), Tiwanaku developed into one of the two major pan-Andean states (alongside the contemporaneous **Wari/Huari** based in Ayacucho, Peru) — exercising cultural, religious, and political influence over a vast area spanning modern Bolivia, southern Peru, northern Chile, and northwestern Argentina. The site features extraordinary **monumental stone architecture**: the **Akapana** pyramid (a terraced, cross-shaped platform mound ~17 m tall, with internal stone drainage channels), the **Kalasasaya** (a large rectangular semi-subterranean enclosure with monolithic stone gateways), the iconic **Gateway of the Sun** (Puerta del Sol — a single block of andesite carved with the "Staff God" figure, often identified with the creator deity Viracocha), and **Pumapunku** (a complex of precisely cut and fitted stone blocks — some weighing over 100 tonnes — whose engineering precision has generated both legitimate archaeological admiration and pseudoscientific speculation). Tiwanaku's agriculture relied on an ingenious system of **raised fields (suka kollus)** — elevated planting platforms surrounded by water-filled channels that moderated frost, recycled nutrients, and created microclimates enabling agriculture at an altitude where frost events occur nearly year-round. The civilization collapsed around 1000 CE, likely due to severe prolonged **drought** documented in Quelccaya ice-core and Lake Titicaca sediment records.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 Location and Chronology
- The urban and ceremonial center of Tiwanaku is located in the southern Lake Titicaca basin at ~3,850 m elevation in modern Bolivia's La Paz Department; the monumental core of the site covers approximately 4 km²; the wider metropolitan area may have housed 10,000–40,000 people at its peak (estimates vary)
- Occupation at the site began in the Late Formative period (c. 500 BCE); Tiwanaku expanded into a regional polity by ~400 CE and became a major state during its "Classic" period (c. 500–900 CE); decline and abandonment occurred c. 1000–1100 CE
- **UNESCO World Heritage Site**: the Tiwanaku archaeological site was inscribed in 2000

### 1.2 Monumental Architecture
- **Akapana**: a terraced step-pyramid (~200 × 200 m base, ~17 m high), T-shaped in plan, built from earth fill encased in andesite and sandstone retaining walls; contained an elaborate internal **drainage system** of fitted stone channels — capable of directing water through multiple levels; interpreted as both a political and ritual structure (possibly associated with water/rain ceremonies)
- **Kalasasaya**: a large semi-subterranean/above-ground rectangular enclosure (~130 × 120 m) with monolithic stone pillars; contains the **Ponce Monolith** (a 3-m-tall anthropomorphic stone statue holding a kero — a ceremonial drinking vessel — and a snuff tablet) and the **Gateway of the Sun**
- **Gateway of the Sun**: carved from a single block of andesite (~3 m high, ~4 m wide, ~10 tonnes); the central figure — a frontal deity with radiating ray-like appendages emanating from the head, holding two staffs — is the "Staff God" (sometimes identified with Viracocha or an earlier Andean deity); flanked by rows of running winged attendant figures
- **Pumapunku**: a separate complex ~1 km southwest of the Akapana; features enormous precision-cut stone blocks (andesite and red sandstone — some blocks estimated at 130+ tonnes) with complex interlocking joints, recessed niches, and H-shaped clamps; the engineering sophistication of Pumapunku is exceptional by any standard

### 1.3 Raised-Field Agriculture (Suka Kollus)
- The Tiwanaku population depended on **raised-field agriculture** — elevated planting surfaces (typically 5–15 m wide, 10–200 m long, 1 m high) surrounded by water-filled channels — an ingenious adaptation to the extreme Altiplano environment (4,000 m elevation; frequent killing frosts; poor, saturated soils)
- **Microclimate effects**: water in the channels absorbs solar heat during the day and releases it at night, creating a warmer microclimate that protects crops from frost; decomposing aquatic plants and algae in the channels create nutrient-rich compost; channels also function as fish farms
- Archaeological and experimental evidence (notably Alan Kolata's and Clark Erickson's experiments in the 1980s–1990s) demonstrated that reconstructed raised fields significantly outperformed modern dry-farming techniques at the same elevation

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 State Organization and Expansion
- Tiwanaku's political organization is debated — models range from a **centralized state** with colonial enclaves (direct control of distant territories in the Moquegua Valley of Peru, the Atacama Desert of Chile, and the Cochabamba Valley of Bolivia) to a more **hegemonic** system of culturally and ideologically integrated but politically autonomous communities
- Tiwanaku colonies or outposts have been identified in the **lower Moquegua Valley** (Omo and Chen Chen sites — with Tiwanaku-style architecture, ceramics, and burial practices ~300 km from the capital) and in **San Pedro de Atacama** (northern Chile — presence of Tiwanaku-style textiles, snuff trays, and iconography)
- The relationship between Tiwanaku and the contemporaneous **Wari** (Huari) state (centered in Ayacucho, Peru) is debated — they shared some iconographic elements (the Staff God motif) but were politically distinct; whether they interacted as rivals, allies, or independently operating states remains unclear

### 2.2 Religion and Iconography
- The **Staff God** iconography (frontal figure with radiating head-rays, holding staffs or plants) is the central religious motif — appearing on the Gateway of the Sun, textiles, ceramics, and metalwork; this motif has Andean precedents stretching back to Chavín (c. 900 BCE) and probably represents a pan-Andean religious concept rather than a uniquely Tiwanaku creation
- **Psychoactive substance use**: snuff trays and tubes found in Tiwanaku-associated contexts (especially at San Pedro de Atacama) were used for inhaling hallucinogenic powders (likely vilca/cebil — *Anadenanthera colubrina* seeds containing bufotenine and DMT); ritual use of psychoactive substances was likely integral to Tiwanaku religious practice

### 2.3 Collapse
- Lake-sediment cores (from Lake Titicaca) and the **Quelccaya ice cap ice core** (Thompson et al. 1985, 2006) document a severe, prolonged **drought** beginning c. 1000 CE — coinciding precisely with Tiwanaku's decline and the abandonment of raised fields
- The drought reduced Lake Titicaca's water level significantly, undermining the hydrological basis for raised-field agriculture and the surplus production that sustained the urban population; political fragmentation followed

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Stone-Moving Techniques
- How the Tiwanaku builders transported and precisely shaped enormous stone blocks (some weighing 130+ tonnes) at 3,850+ meters elevation remains incompletely understood. Proposed methods include log-and-roller transport, reed-boat transport across Lake Titicaca (for andesite blocks quarried on the Copacabana peninsula), and earth ramps — but no definitive evidence for the specific techniques has been recovered

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Extraterrestrial or Lost-Civilization Origin
- **[NOT SUPPORTED]** Claims that Tiwanaku and particularly Pumapunku were built by extraterrestrials, Atlanteans, or technologically advanced "lost civilizations" (popularized by Erich von Däniken, Graham Hancock, and History Channel's *Ancient Aliens*) are unsupported by any archaeological evidence. The construction techniques — while impressive — are consistent with skilled human engineering using stone-age and copper-age tools, as demonstrated by archaeological tool finds, quarry site analysis, and experimental archaeology
