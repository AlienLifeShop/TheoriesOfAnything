## QUICK SUMMARY

The **Moche** (c. 100–700 CE) and **Chimú** (c. 900–1470 CE) civilizations flourished on the **arid north coast of Peru** — the desert strip between the Andes and the Pacific where precipitation is negligible but rivers descending from the mountains create fertile irrigated valleys. These two civilizations — separated by roughly two centuries and the intervening **Sicán/Lambayeque** culture — represent the pinnacle of pre-Inca coastal Andean achievement. The **Moche** developed a non-literate but artistically brilliant culture — producing the most naturalistic and expressive **ceramic art** in the pre-Columbian Americas (portrait vessels, erotic pottery, elaborate painted scenes depicting warfare, sacrifice, and mythology), monumental mud-brick **pyramids** (the Huaca del Sol and Huaca de la Luna near Trujillo — the Huaca del Sol, at ~50 m tall and built from an estimated 130 million adobe bricks, was the largest pre-Columbian structure in South America), and astonishing **metallurgy** (gold, silver, copper, and tumbaga alloys — depletion gilding, electrochemical replacement plating). The 1987 discovery of the **Lord of Sipán** tomb — an unlooted royal Moche burial with spectacular gold and silver artifacts — is considered one of the most important archaeological discoveries in the Americas. The **Chimú** (successors to a cultural tradition that included the Sicán/Lambayeque) built the largest **adobe city** in the world — **Chan Chan** (near Trujillo) — a sprawling complex of ~20 km² with nine monumental royal compounds (ciudadelas), elaborate irrigation canals extending up to 80 km, and a political state (the Kingdom of Chimor) controlling over 1,000 km of coastline until conquered by the Inca under Tupac Inca Yupanqui c. 1470.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 Moche Geography and Chronology
- The Moche (also called Mochica) thrived from c. 100–700 CE in the river valleys of Peru's north coast — from the Piura Valley in the north to the Huarmey Valley in the south; the term "Moche" derives from the Moche Valley near Trujillo, site of their principal ceremonial center
- The Moche were **not** a unified centralized state but rather a **system of interconnected polities** sharing iconographic themes, ceramic traditions, and ritual practices — with at least two major political centers: the **Southern Moche** (Huacas del Sol y de la Luna, Moche Valley) and the **Northern Moche** (Sipán, San José de Moro, Pampa Grande)
- **Moche ceramics**: arguably the most artistically accomplished pottery tradition in the ancient Americas — fine-line painted vessels depicting complex narrative scenes (combat, ritual sacrifice, the "Sacrifice Ceremony," supernatural beings); portrait vessels — individualized naturalistic human faces — unique in pre-Columbian art

### 1.2 Moche Metallurgy and Tombs
- **Metallurgy**: the Moche achieved sophisticated metalwork — gold, silver, copper, and tumbaga (gold-copper alloy); techniques included lost-wax casting, hammering, repoussé, soldering, **depletion gilding** (acid etching to remove copper from the surface of tumbaga, leaving a gold surface), and electrochemical replacement plating (a gold-mercury amalgam technique)
- **Lord of Sipán** (discovered 1987 by Walter Alva at the Huaca Rajada complex, Lambayeque Valley): an unlooted royal Moche tomb (c. 250 CE) — the burial contained the remains of a warrior-priest lord accompanied by rich gold-and-silver regalia (headdresses, nose ornaments, necklaces of oversized gold and silver peanuts, ear spools, chest plates, a crescent-shaped headdress), sacrificed retainers, and llamas; hailed as the "King Tut of the Americas" — one of the richest intact ancient tombs ever excavated in the Western Hemisphere

### 1.3 Chimú — Chan Chan and Estado
- The **Kingdom of Chimor** (the Chimú state, c. 900–1470 CE) was the largest pre-Inca polity in South America — controlling ~1,000 km of the Peruvian coast from Tumbes to the Chillón Valley
- **Chan Chan** (near Trujillo): the Chimú capital — the largest **adobe city** in the pre-Columbian world, covering ~20 km²; contained nine massive walled **ciudadelas** (royal compounds, each built by a successive Chimú king), with elaborate audiencias (U-shaped administrative chambers), walk-in wells, storage rooms, and burial platforms; the ciudadela walls were decorated with carved adobe **friezes** depicting fish, birds, marine mammals, and geometric patterns
- **UNESCO World Heritage Site**: Chan Chan was inscribed in 1986 (and simultaneously placed on the World Heritage in Danger list due to erosion and encroachment)

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Moche Ritual and Iconography
- Moche iconography depicts a recurring ritual narrative — the **"Sacrifice Ceremony"** (also called the "Presentation Theme"): captured warriors are stripped, bound, and brought before elaborately dressed figures who collect and drink their blood from goblets; archaeological discoveries (skeletal remains of sacrificed individuals at the Huaca de la Luna, goblets with human blood residue) confirm that the depicted rituals were actually practiced — not merely symbolic
- The Moche **Decapitator** (Ai apaec — "he who creates") is the most prevalent supernatural figure in Moche art — depicted with fanged mouth, holding a tumi (ceremonial knife) and a severed head; interpreted as a deity associated with agricultural fertility, warfare, and sacrifice

### 2.2 Moche Collapse
- The Moche system collapsed c. 550–700 CE — attributed to a combination of **El Niño-related flooding** (mega-El Niño events documented in archaeological flood deposits), **drought** (possibly linked to a 6th-century climatic downturn), and internal political fragmentation
- The **Huaca del Sol** (c. 50 m tall, ~340 × 160 m at base, built from an estimated 130 million+ adobe bricks — the largest pre-Columbian structure in South America) was partially destroyed by Spanish colonial-era treasure hunters who diverted the Moche River to wash through the pyramid in search of gold

### 2.3 Chimú Hydraulic Engineering
- The Chimú constructed one of the most ambitious **irrigation systems** in the ancient Americas — canals extending up to 80 km, interconnecting multiple river valleys; the **Intervalley Canal** connecting the Chicama and Moche valleys (over 70 km) is considered an engineering masterpiece
- The Chimú were conquered by the **Inca** under Tupac Inca Yupanqui c. 1470 — the Chimú king (the Great Chimú) was captured and taken to Cusco; Chimú metalworkers were deported to Cusco to serve Inca elite — meaning much surviving "Inca" metalwork may actually have been produced by Chimú artisans

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Moche Political Organization
- Whether the Moche constituted a **state** or a looser political integration (a series of peer polities united by shared elite ideology) remains debated; the "Southern Moche vs. Northern Moche" distinction may reflect two separate polities rather than a single civilization, with different political structures and artistic traditions
