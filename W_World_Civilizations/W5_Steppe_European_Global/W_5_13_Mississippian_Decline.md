## QUICK SUMMARY

**Cahokia** — the largest pre-Columbian city north of Mexico, located in the **American Bottom** floodplain of the Mississippi River near modern-day St. Louis, Missouri/East St. Louis, Illinois — rose rapidly around 1050 CE to become a monumental urban center with an estimated population of 10,000–20,000+ people, over 120 earthen **mounds** (including the massive **Monks Mound** — the largest pre-Columbian earthwork north of Mexico at ~30 m tall and ~6.2 hectares at its base), a grand plaza, a timber palisade, and astronomical observation posts (**Woodhenge**). Yet by approximately 1350–1400 CE, Cahokia was **completely abandoned** — its population dispersed, its monumental landscape left to erode, and its organizational system dissolved. The collapse of Cahokia and the broader **Mississippian** cultural system (which extended from the Great Lakes to the Gulf Coast and from Oklahoma to the Carolinas) is one of the most significant and debated episodes of societal decline in precolonial North America. Proposed explanations include: **environmental degradation** (deforestation, soil exhaustion from intensive maize agriculture), **climatic disruption** (a shift from the Medieval Warm Period to the Little Ice Age — bringing shorter growing seasons and drought), **flooding** (catastrophic Mississippi River floods documented in geological deposits), **warfare and social conflict** (construction of a massive defensive palisade c. 1170 CE suggests escalating violence), **political fragmentation** (the breakdown of centralized elite authority), and **disease** (possibly pre-Columbian epidemic — debated). The decline was not instantaneous but occurred as a gradual depopulation spanning at least 150 years, and the "collapse" was more likely a **dispersal** — populations reorganized into smaller, decentralized communities across the midcontinent rather than vanishing.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 Cahokia — Rise and Peak
- **Cahokia** experienced a dramatic and rapid expansion around 1050 CE — a "Big Bang" of urban growth (Pauketat 2004) — transforming from a modest agricultural settlement into the largest city in North America in a single generation; this expansion involved massive construction projects (mound building, plaza leveling), population influx from surrounding regions, and the establishment of an elite-controlled political-ritual system
- At its peak (c. 1050–1200 CE): ~120+ mounds (platform mounds, conical mounds, ridge-top mounds); **Monks Mound** (~30 m tall, ~291 × 236 m at base — larger in footprint than the Great Pyramid of Giza); the **Grand Plaza** (~19 hectares — one of the largest public spaces in the ancient Americas); **Woodhenge** (a series of timber-post circles used for astronomical observations — marking solstices and equinoxes); a timber **palisade** (~3.2 km perimeter, built c. 1170 CE with bastions — suggesting defensive concerns)
- **Population estimates**: range from 10,000 to 40,000 at peak (the higher estimates include the greater Cahokia region / American Bottom); the most commonly cited scholarly estimates are 10,000–20,000 for the urban core

### 1.2 Chronology of Decline
- Depopulation began gradually after ~1200 CE — the **Stirling phase** (c. 1100–1200) represents the peak, while the **Moorehead phase** (c. 1200–1275) shows declining population, contraction of the settled area, and changes in material culture (less elaborate ceramics, smaller residential areas)
- By the **Sand Prairie phase** (c. 1275–1350), the population had fallen dramatically — probably to a few hundred or fewer; by c. 1350–1400, the site was **effectively abandoned**
- The broader Mississippian world continued for several centuries after Cahokia's decline — other Mississippian centers (Moundville in Alabama, Etowah in Georgia, Spiro in Oklahoma) persisted into the 14th–16th centuries, with some contact-era Mississippian chiefdoms encountered by de Soto's expedition (1539–1543) — but the American Bottom itself was largely depopulated

### 1.3 Environmental Evidence
- **Paleoclimatic data**: tree-ring records and lake-sediment analysis document a shift from the **Medieval Climate Anomaly** (Medieval Warm Period — warmer, moister conditions that facilitated maize agriculture in the midcontinent) to the **Little Ice Age** (c. 1250–1550 — cooler, drier conditions) — coinciding with Cahokia's decline
- **Deforestation**: pollen and sediment records from adjacent Horseshoe Lake document extensive deforestation around 1000–1200 CE (clearing for agriculture, mound construction, fuel, and building materials); deforestation increased flooding and erosion, degraded soil fertility, and reduced wildlife habitat
- **Flooding**: geological studies (Munoz et al. 2015) document major Mississippi River flood events in the 12th–13th centuries that inundated the American Bottom — potentially destroying crops and habitations

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Political and Social Causes
- The construction of the **palisade** (c. 1170–1275, rebuilt at least four times) is widely interpreted as evidence of increasing **warfare and social conflict** — whether from external threats or internal unrest; the palisade enclosed only the central precinct (not the entire urban area), suggesting it protected the elite/ritual core
- **Political fragmentation**: scholars (Pauketat, Alt) argue that Cahokia's centralized political-ritual authority was inherently unstable — dependent on elite manipulation of cosmological narratives, surplus mobilization, and factional alliances that could not be sustained indefinitely; as environmental conditions deteriorated and the costs of maintaining the system exceeded its benefits, populations simply **opted out** — migrating to smaller, more egalitarian communities
- **Evidence of violence**: some Cahokia-area burials from the decline period show evidence of violent death (scalping, arrow wounds); the famous **Mound 72 sacrificial burials** (53 young women in a mass grave, plus executed men and elaborate high-status burials — dating to the early period, c. 1050–1100) suggest a coercive elite practice that may have generated resentment

### 2.2 Dispersal Rather Than Collapse
- The decline of Cahokia was not a demographic "collapse" in the sense of mass death — populations **dispersed** rather than disappeared; archaeological evidence shows population increases in the surrounding uplands (western Illinois, Missouri, the central Mississippi Valley) coinciding with Cahokia's decline — suggesting relocation rather than extinction
- Descendant communities likely include many of the **Dhegihan Siouan** peoples (Osage, Quapaw, Kansa, Ponca) and possibly the **Omaha** — though establishing direct lineal descent from Cahokia to specific historic tribes is methodologically challenging

### 2.3 Cahokia in Context
- Cahokia's rise and fall mirrors broader patterns in complex society dynamics — rapid centralization followed by gradual fragmentation; parallels have been drawn with **Classic Maya collapse**, Chaco Canyon abandonment, Tiwanaku, and other cases of pre-modern urban decline
- The absence of Cahokia from popular historical consciousness — despite being the largest city in precolonial North America — reflects broader patterns of the erasure of indigenous history in American education

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Pre-Columbian Disease
- Researchers have suggested that a **pre-Columbian epidemic** (perhaps a disease native to the Americas, rather than a European import) may have contributed to depopulation; however, skeletal evidence is insufficient to confirm or refute this hypothesis, and no specific pathogen has been identified

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 "Mysterious Disappearance"
- **[MISLEADING]** Popular accounts frequently describe Cahokia's inhabitants as "vanishing" or the site as an "unsolved mystery." The archaeological record clearly documents a gradual depopulation and dispersal, driven by identifiable environmental, political, and social factors — there is no mystery, only incomplete knowledge of the relative weight of contributing causes
