## QUICK SUMMARY

The **Byzantine Empire** (c. 330–1453 CE) — the continuation of the eastern half of the Roman Empire, centered on **Constantinople** (modern Istanbul, founded as Byzantium, refounded by Constantine I in 330 CE) — endured for over a millennium after the fall of Rome (476 CE), serving as the most powerful Christian state in the world for most of its existence and as the primary bulwark of Europe against successive waves of Persian, Arab, Turkic, and Mongol expansion. At its height under **Emperor Justinian I** (r. 527–565) and the empress **Theodora**, the empire reconquered much of the western Mediterranean (North Africa, Italy, southeastern Spain), codified Roman law in the **Corpus Juris Civilis** (the foundational legal code of Western civilization), and constructed **Hagia Sophia** (532–537) — the largest cathedral in the world for nearly a millennium and one of the supreme achievements of human architecture. The Byzantines preserved and transmitted **Greek learning** (philosophy, science, literature) to both the Islamic world and Renaissance Europe, developed **Orthodox Christianity** (with its distinctive theology, liturgy, and monasticism — spreading it to the Slavic world: Russia, Serbia, Bulgaria, Romania), invented **Greek fire** (an incendiary naval weapon whose exact formula remains unknown), and produced extraordinary **mosaic and icon art** that defined religious visual culture in the Eastern Mediterranean and beyond. The empire survived multiple existential crises — the Arab sieges of Constantinople (674–678, 717–718), the Iconoclast controversy (726–843), the Seljuk Turkish conquest of Anatolia after Manzikert (1071), and the catastrophic Fourth Crusade (1204, when fellow Christians sacked Constantinople) — before finally falling to the **Ottoman Turks** under Sultan **Mehmed II** on May 29, 1453.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 Constantinople
- Founded as **Byzantium** (an ancient Greek colony); refounded by **Constantine I** in 330 CE as **Nova Roma/Constantinople** — intended as a "New Rome" strategically positioned on the Bosphorus, controlling the straits between the Mediterranean and the Black Sea, and between Europe and Asia
- **Defenses**: the **Theodosian Walls** (built 413 CE, expanded repeatedly) — a triple line of walls and a moat stretching ~6.5 km across the land approach; the walls withstood every siege for over 1,000 years until 1453; the city's position on a peninsula protected by water on three sides made it virtually impregnable
- **Population**: at its peak (5th–6th centuries), Constantinople held an estimated 400,000–500,000 inhabitants — the largest city in Europe and possibly the world; it contained the **Hippodrome** (seating ~100,000), the **Great Palace**, public baths, aqueducts, cisterns (the Basilica Cistern), and the massive commercial infrastructure of a capital city

### 1.2 Justinian and the Corpus Juris Civilis
- **Justinian I** (r. 527–565): the most celebrated Byzantine emperor; alongside his empress **Theodora** (a former actress of humble origins who proved a formidable political partner), Justinian pursued three great projects:
  1. **Reconquest**: his generals Belisarius and Narses reconquered North Africa (from the Vandals, 533–534), Italy (from the Ostrogoths, 535–554 — a devastating 20-year war), and southeastern Spain
  2. **Corpus Juris Civilis** (Body of Civil Law): compiled 529–534 by the jurist Tribonian — a comprehensive codification of Roman law comprising the *Codex Justinianus*, the *Digest* (excerpts from classical jurists), the *Institutes* (a textbook), and the *Novellae* (new laws); this compilation became the foundation of legal systems throughout continental Europe and remains the basis of civil law worldwide
  3. **Hagia Sophia** (Church of the Holy Wisdom): built 532–537 by the architects Anthemius of Tralles and Isidore of Miletus — a domed basilica with a central dome ~31 m in diameter, rising ~55 m above the floor; the dome appears to "float" on a ring of windows — an engineering marvel that was the largest enclosed space in the world for nearly a millennium

### 1.3 Preservation of Greek Learning
- The Byzantine Empire preserved and copied classical **Greek manuscripts** — philosophy (Plato, Aristotle), science (Galen, Hippocrates, Ptolemy, Archimedes), literature (Homer, the tragedians), and history (Thucydides, Herodotus) — that would otherwise have been lost
- Byzantine scholars transmitted Greek texts to the Islamic world (through translation movements — especially in Abbasid Baghdad) and to Renaissance Italy (especially after 1453, when refugee scholars brought manuscripts westward — catalyzing the Italian Renaissance's recovery of classical learning)

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Orthodox Christianity and the Slavic World
- **Byzantine Christianity** developed distinctive theological emphases (the Filioque controversy — whether the Holy Spirit proceeds from the Father alone or from the Father "and the Son"; resolved differently in East and West, contributing to the **Great Schism of 1054** between Rome and Constantinople), liturgical traditions (the Divine Liturgy of St. John Chrysostom), and aesthetic expressions (icons, mosaics, church architecture)
- The **Christianization of the Slavs**: Byzantine missionaries **Cyril and Methodius** (9th century) created the **Glagolitic alphabet** (precursor to Cyrillic) to translate the Bible and liturgy into Old Church Slavonic — establishing the Orthodox Christian tradition in Bulgaria, Serbia, and eventually Russia (Christianization of Kievan Rus, 988 CE — Prince Vladimir of Kiev); the **Cyrillic alphabet** (used today by ~250 million people across Russia, Ukraine, Serbia, Bulgaria, and Mongolia) derives from this Byzantine missionary effort

### 2.2 Greek Fire
- **Greek fire** (πῦρ θαλάσσιον — "sea fire"): an incendiary naval weapon first deployed in 678 CE against the Arab fleet besieging Constantinople — a liquid substance that ignited on contact with water and could be projected through siphons mounted on Byzantine warships
- The exact formula is **unknown** — a closely guarded state secret; modern hypotheses suggest a mixture of naphtha (petroleum), quicklime, sulfur, and resin, possibly pressurized; it gave the Byzantine navy a decisive advantage for centuries and was crucial to the survival of the empire during the Arab sieges

### 2.3 The Fourth Crusade and Decline
- The **Fourth Crusade (1204)**: Venetian and French Crusaders, originally intending to campaign in Egypt, were diverted to Constantinople — they stormed the city, subjected it to three days of looting (destroying countless manuscripts, relics, and artworks), and established the **Latin Empire** (1204–1261); the Byzantines recovered Constantinople in 1261 under Michael VIII Palaiologos, but the empire never recovered its former strength
- **Fall of Constantinople (May 29, 1453)**: Sultan **Mehmed II** besieged the city with ~80,000 troops and the massive **Urban cannon** (a bombard ~8 m long); the last emperor, **Constantine XI Palaiologos**, died fighting on the walls; Mehmed converted Hagia Sophia into a mosque and made Constantinople the Ottoman capital

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Greek Fire Composition
- Despite extensive research, the exact composition and delivery mechanism of Greek fire remains uncertain — no formula survives; experimental attempts to recreate it have produced plausible but unconfirmed results; whether it contained saltpeter (making it an early form of napalm or proto-gunpowder weapon) is debated

### 4.1 Byzantium as Decadent or Stagnant
- **[REFUTED]** The long-standing Western historiographic tradition (influenced by Edward Gibbon's *Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire*) of characterizing Byzantine civilization as decadent, corrupt, and culturally stagnant is thoroughly contradicted by the evidence — the empire produced extraordinary legal, architectural, artistic, military, and theological achievements and preserved the intellectual heritage of the ancient world for a millennium
