## QUICK SUMMARY

The **Khazar Khaganate** (c. 650–1048 CE) was a major Turkic empire that dominated the steppe and steppe-forest region between the **Caspian Sea**, the **Black Sea**, the **Caucasus Mountains**, and the **Volga River** — controlling key segments of the **Silk Road** and serving as a critical buffer state between the Byzantine Empire, the Islamic Caliphate, and the emerging Slavic states. At its peak (8th–9th centuries), the Khaganate extended from the Dnieper River in the west to the Aral Sea in the east, with its capital at **Itil** (Atil) on the Volga delta (near modern Astrakhan, Russia — the site has never been definitively excavated). The Khazars are most famous for an event unique in world history: the **conversion of the ruling elite to Judaism** — an event attributed to the reign of **Bulan** (or Sabriel) c. 740–861 CE (dates debated), making the Khazar Khaganate the only major state in world history to adopt **Judaism as its ruling religion** (alongside populations practicing Islam, Christianity, and Turkic shamanism/Tengrism). This conversion is attested by multiple independent sources — Arabic (al-Mas'udi, al-Istakhri, Ibn Fadlan), Byzantine (Theophanes), and the remarkable **Khazar Correspondence** (a series of letters between **Hasdai ibn Shaprut**, the Jewish vizier of Umayyad Córdoba, and **King Joseph** of the Khazars, c. 960 CE) — though the precise extent of Judaization (limited to the elite? widespread among the populace?) and the motivations remain debated. The Khaganate collapsed in the late 10th century under attacks from the **Kievan Rus** (Sviatoslav I destroyed Itil in 965 CE) and the Pechenegs, but the Khazar legacy continues to generate intense scholarly and political debate — particularly through Arthur Koestler's controversial 1976 book *The Thirteenth Tribe*, which proposed (without adequate evidence) that Ashkenazi Jews descended predominantly from Khazar converts.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 Geography and Political Structure
- The Khazars were a **Turkic** semi-nomadic people (linguistically West Turkic, though the elite may have been ethnically diverse); they emerged as a distinct polity after the collapse of the **Western Turkic Khaganate** in the mid-7th century
- The Khaganate was governed by a **dual-kingship** system: a sacred overlord (**Khagan**) — a figurehead with ritual/legitimate authority — and a practical ruler (**Bek** or Beg) who wielded actual political and military power; this dual-leadership structure parallels patterns in other Turkic and steppe empires
- **Territory**: at its height, the Khaganate controlled the North Caucasus, the lower and middle Volga region, parts of the Don basin, Crimea, and the western Kazakh steppe — a geographically vast but sparsely populated domain centered on trade routes

### 1.2 Strategic Importance
- The Khazars served as a crucial **buffer state** between the expanding Arab/Islamic Caliphate to the south and the Byzantines and Slavic peoples to the west and north — the **Khazar-Arab wars** (642–799 CE) were among the largest military conflicts of the 7th–8th centuries, with the Khazars repelling multiple Arab invasions through the Caucasus that might otherwise have penetrated into Eastern Europe
- The Khaganate controlled key **trade routes**: Silk Road traffic between China/Central Asia and Byzantium; the **Volga trade route** connecting Scandinavia (via the Volga Bulgars and Norse/Varangian traders) with the Islamic world; trade made Itil a cosmopolitan commercial center — described by Arab geographers as a city of mosques, churches, synagogues, and markets

### 1.3 The Khazar Correspondence
- The **Khazar Correspondence**: a series of letters exchanged c. 955–961 CE between **Hasdai ibn Shaprut** (Jewish physician, diplomat, and de facto foreign minister of Caliph Abd al-Rahman III of Córdoba) and **King Joseph** of the Khazars — Joseph's letter describes the conversion of the Khazar king Bulan to Judaism, the extent of Khazar territory, and court practices
- **Authenticity**: while early scholars questioned the letters' authenticity, the discovery of the **Cambridge Document** (Schechter Text) in the Cairo Genizah — a separate Khazar Hebrew text — and corroboration from independent Arabic, Byzantine, and Slavic sources have convinced most modern scholars that the correspondence is fundamentally authentic (though possibly containing later interpolations)

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Conversion to Judaism
- Multiple independent sources attest to the conversion: Arabic geographers (al-Istakhri, al-Mas'udi — 10th century), the Byzantine chronicler Theophanes Confessor (9th century), and the Khazar correspondence itself
- The **motivation** for conversion is debated: the most common explanation is that the Khazar elite adopted Judaism as a **politically neutral religion** — positioned between the Christian Byzantine Empire (which demanded religious alignment as a condition of alliance) and the Islamic Caliphate (which likewise sought doctrinal conformity); Judaism, as a monotheistic Abrahamic religion without an associated political empire seeking to dominate the Khazars, offered religious respectability without political subordination
- The **extent** of Judaization is debated: King Joseph's letter implies widespread adoption, but Arabic sources suggest that Judaism was primarily the religion of the ruling class — with Muslim, Christian, and shamanist communities coexisting within the Khaganate; the Khaganate was remarkably **religiously tolerant** — featuring courts with judges from different religious communities

### 2.2 Decline and Fall
- The Khaganate weakened in the 10th century due to: the rise of the **Kievan Rus** (Sviatoslav I of Kiev attacked and destroyed the major Khazar cities — Sarkel in 965, Itil probably in 968–969); pressure from the **Pechenegs** (Turkic nomads) from the east; and the growing power of the Volga Bulgars to the north
- After the fall of Itil, remnant Khazar communities persisted in Crimea and the Caucasus into the 11th–12th centuries — but the Khaganate as a political entity ceased to exist
- **Sarkel** (a Khazar fortress on the Don River, built c. 833 with Byzantine engineering assistance — its name means "White House" in Turkic): excavated by M. I. Artamonov in the 1930s–1950s before being flooded by the Tsimlyansk Reservoir; the excavation confirmed Khazar material culture and fortification techniques

### 2.3 Archaeological Record
- Unlike many of its better-documented neighbors, the Khazar archaeological record is **frustratingly limited**: the capital **Itil** has never been conclusively located or excavated (the Volga delta's shifting hydrology has likely submerged or obscured the site); most knowledge of Khazar material culture comes from peripheral sites (Sarkel, Samosdelka — a candidate for Itil, Crimean fortifications)
- Russian and Soviet-era archaeological investigation of Khazar sites was complicated by political sensitivities — both Tsarist and Soviet authorities were uneasy about a Jewish Turkic empire on Russian soil

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Khazar Origin of Ashkenazi Jews (The Koestler Thesis)
- Arthur Koestler's *The Thirteenth Tribe* (1976) proposed that **Ashkenazi Jews** are primarily descended from Khazar converts rather than from the ancient Israelites — a thesis adopted for various political purposes. **Genetic studies** (Behar et al. 2006, 2013; Ostrer 2012; Atzmon et al. 2010) have consistently shown that Ashkenazi Jews share substantial genetic affinities with other Jewish populations and with Middle Eastern populations — indicating predominantly **Levantine/Middle Eastern ancestry** with varying degrees of European admixture; the Koestler thesis is not supported by genetic evidence, though some Khazar admixture at a minor level cannot be excluded
