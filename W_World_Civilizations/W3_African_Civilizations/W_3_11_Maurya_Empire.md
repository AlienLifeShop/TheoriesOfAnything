## QUICK SUMMARY

The **Maurya Empire** (c. 322–185 BCE) was the first politically unified pan-Indian state, at its height encompassing most of the Indian subcontinent — from the Hindu Kush in the northwest to the Bay of Bengal in the east, and from the Himalayas to the Deccan. Founded by **Chandragupta Maurya** (r. c. 322–298 BCE) — who overthrew the Nanda dynasty and subsequently confronted Seleucus Nicator (Alexander's successor in the east) — the empire was consolidated under his son **Bindusara** and reached its greatest extent and cultural significance under his grandson **Ashoka** (r. c. 268–232 BCE). Two foundational texts define the Mauryan legacy: the **Arthashastra** (attributed to Chandragupta's chief minister **Kautilya/Chanakya** — a treatise on statecraft, economics, military strategy, and espionage that is one of the most comprehensive political science texts from the ancient world) and Ashoka's **rock and pillar edicts** — inscriptions in Prakrit, Greek, and Aramaic proclaiming his policy of **Dhamma** (moral law — nonviolence, religious tolerance, ethical governance, welfare of all beings) after his remorseful conversion to Buddhism following the devastating **Kalinga War** (c. 261 BCE, in which an estimated 100,000 were killed). Ashoka's edicts represent the first documented attempt by a ruler to govern according to ethical principles of compassion and nonviolence, making him a figure of exceptional significance in the history of political ethics. The Mauryan capital **Pataliputra** (modern Patna, Bihar) was described by the Greek ambassador Megasthenes as one of the greatest cities in the ancient world.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 Founding and Territorial Extent
- **Chandragupta Maurya** (r. c. 322–298 BCE) established the empire by overthrowing the **Nanda dynasty** of Magadha in northeastern India; according to classical Indian and Greek sources, he was guided by the Brahmin strategist **Kautilya** (also called Chanakya or Vishnugupta)
- After Alexander the Great's withdrawal from India (326 BCE), Chandragupta defeated the Greek garrisons in the northwest and subsequently fought **Seleucus I Nicator** (Alexander's Macedonian successor in the east) c. 305 BCE — the resulting treaty ceded the eastern satrapies (parts of modern Afghanistan, Baluchistan, and Makran) to the Mauryas and established diplomatic relations (Seleucus sent the ambassador **Megasthenes** to Pataliputra)
- At its greatest extent under **Ashoka** (r. c. 268–232 BCE), the empire encompassed virtually the entire Indian subcontinent except the extreme south (the Tamil kingdoms of the Cheras, Cholas, and Pandyas); estimated population of 50–60 million — one of the largest empires in the ancient world

### 1.2 Ashoka's Edicts
- Ashoka is known primarily through his **rock and pillar edicts** — approximately 33 edicts inscribed on polished sandstone pillars, rock faces, and cave walls throughout the empire (from Kandahar in Afghanistan to Odisha in eastern India)
- Languages: most edicts are in **Prakrit** (Brahmi and Kharoshthi scripts); two edicts from Kandahar are in **Greek** and **Aramaic** — reflecting the empire's multi-ethnic character
- Content: the edicts proclaim Ashoka's policy of **Dhamma** — which included: prohibition of animal slaughter on certain days; provision of medical care for humans and animals; construction of wells, rest houses, and tree-planting along roads; religious tolerance (all sects should "dwell in all places"); appointment of **Dhamma-mahamattas** (officers of righteousness) to oversee ethical governance; and expressions of personal remorse for the violence of the **Kalinga War**

### 1.3 Pataliputra and Administration
- **Pataliputra** (modern Patna): described by Megasthenes (preserved in fragments by Strabo, Arrian, and Diodorus) as a large, well-fortified city surrounded by a deep moat and wooden palisade with 570 towers and 64 gates — rivaling Persepolis and the great Mediterranean cities
- Archaeological excavations at Kumrahar (Patna) have revealed polished sandstone pillar foundations of a massive **hypostyle hall** — possibly the assembly hall described by Megasthenes
- The Mauryan administration was sophisticated — the Arthashastra describes a centralized bureaucracy with departments for revenue, taxation, agriculture, mining, trade, espionage, and military affairs

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 The Arthashastra
- The **Arthashastra** ("Science of Material Gain" or "Treatise on Statecraft"): attributed to **Kautilya**, Chandragupta's minister — though the surviving text is likely compiled/redacted over several centuries (core composition possibly 3rd century BCE, with additions through the 3rd century CE)
- Content: a comprehensive manual covering state administration, taxation, law, diplomacy, warfare, espionage, trade regulation, and ethical duties of rulers — described as "Machiavelli before Machiavelli" for its unsentimental, pragmatic approach to power
- Key concepts: the **Mandala theory** (a king's neighbors are natural enemies; neighbors' neighbors are natural allies — a geopolitical model of concentric circles of friend and foe); the **Saptanga** (theory of the seven elements of the state — king, minister, territory, fort, treasury, army, ally); extensive discussion of **espionage** networks and **covert operations**
- The Arthashastra was rediscovered in 1905 by R. Shamasastry in a palm-leaf manuscript in Mysore — prior to this, it was known only from references in other texts

### 2.2 Ashoka and Buddhism
- Ashoka's edicts describe his conversion to Buddhism and his patronage of the Sangha (Buddhist community) — he funded the construction of monasteries, stupas (including the Great Stupa at Sanchi), and pilgrimages to Buddhist sites
- According to Buddhist tradition, Ashoka convened the **Third Buddhist Council** at Pataliputra and dispatched missionary monks to Sri Lanka, Central Asia, Southeast Asia, and the Hellenistic kingdoms — accelerating Buddhism's transformation from a regional Indian movement to a world religion
- The **Ashoka Chakra** (Wheel of Dharma) from the Sarnath Lion Capital has been adopted as the emblem on India's national flag; the Lion Capital itself is the national emblem of the Republic of India

### 2.3 Decline
- After Ashoka's death (c. 232 BCE), the empire fragmented under weak successors; the last Mauryan king, **Brihadratha**, was assassinated by his general **Pushyamitra Shunga** (c. 185 BCE), who founded the Shunga dynasty
- Causes of decline: overexpansion; the costs of Ashoka's welfare programs; diminished military readiness (possibly resulting from Ashoka's emphasis on nonviolence); and centrifugal tendencies of provincial governors

### 3.1 Chandragupta and Alexander
- Classical sources (especially Justin's *Epitome*) suggest that Chandragupta met Alexander the Great during Alexander's India campaign — and that this encounter inspired his ambitions. The historicity of this meeting is debated; no confirming Indian source exists

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Ashoka as Pacifist Absolutist
- **[OVERSIMPLIFIED]** Popular narratives sometimes portray Ashoka as having renounced all violence after Kalinga and becoming an absolute pacifist. His edicts, while promoting nonviolence, also retained capital punishment for certain crimes and maintained a standing army; his governance was reform-oriented rather than pacifist in the modern sense
