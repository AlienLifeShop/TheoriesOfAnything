## QUICK SUMMARY

**Carthage** (from Phoenician *Qart-ḥadašt* — "New City") was a Phoenician colony founded c. 814 BCE on the coast of modern-day **Tunisia** that grew into the dominant maritime and commercial power of the western Mediterranean — a thalassocracy (sea empire) controlling trade routes, colonies, and resources from the Straits of Gibraltar to Libya, including Sardinia, western Sicily, the Balearic Islands, southern Spain, and the North African coast. At its height (4th–3rd centuries BCE), Carthage rivaled Rome in power and ambition — the two civilizations fought three catastrophic **Punic Wars** (264–146 BCE) that would determine the fate of the Mediterranean world. The Carthaginian general **Hannibal Barca** (247–183 BCE) — one of history's greatest military commanders — famously crossed the Alps with war elephants (218 BCE) and devastated Roman Italy for 15 years during the Second Punic War, but Carthage ultimately lost and was **utterly destroyed** by Rome in 146 BCE (the city was razed, the population enslaved, and the territory absorbed as the Roman province of Africa). Carthage is renowned for its **maritime expertise** (Phoenician-inherited navigation and shipbuilding technology; the **Periplus of Hanno** — a 5th-century BCE account of an African coastal voyage possibly reaching the Gulf of Guinea), its circular military **Cothon** (harbor), its agricultural innovation (the treatise of **Mago** was so valued that the Roman Senate ordered its translation into Latin), and the controversial **Tophet** — a sacred precinct containing the cremated remains of infants and young children, interpreted by scholars as evidence of **child sacrifice** to the gods **Baal Hammon** and **Tanit** and by others as a cemetery for naturally deceased children. The Romans' deliberate destruction of Carthage means that we know the civilization primarily through the hostile accounts of its enemies — one of history's great cases of **victor's historiography**.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 Foundation and Geography
- Founded traditionally c. 814 BCE (archaeological evidence suggests settlement from at least the late 9th or early 8th century BCE) by **Phoenician settlers** from Tyre (in modern Lebanon) — the legendary foundress is Queen **Dido** (Elissa)
- Located on a peninsula projecting into the Gulf of Tunis (modern Tunisia) — a strategically superior site controlling the narrows between Sicily and North Africa, and thus the central Mediterranean sea lanes
- **Byrsa Hill**: the citadel of Carthage — the oldest settlement and the last stronghold during the Roman siege; archaeological excavations (UNESCO-sponsored since the 1970s) have revealed Punic-era housing, streets, workshops, and extensive infrastructure beneath Roman reconstruction

### 1.2 Commercial and Maritime Empire
- Carthage inherited and expanded the **Phoenician commercial network** — trading in purple dye, tin (from Cornwall and Brittany), silver (from Spain — the mines of Tartessos/Tartessus), gold (from West Africa via trans-Saharan routes), ivory, animal skins, pottery, glass, and agricultural products
- **Naval supremacy**: the Carthaginian fleet was the most powerful in the western Mediterranean for centuries; the **Cothon** (the circular military harbor) could accommodate ~220 warships in covered dry docks arranged around a central island admiralty building — one of the most impressive port installations in the ancient world
- **Colonies and trading posts**: Carthage controlled a network of subsidiary settlements along the North African coast, in Sardinia, western Sicily, the Balearic Islands, Ibiza, and southern Spain (Carthago Nova — modern Cartagena)

### 1.3 Agriculture
- **Mago's treatise**: a comprehensive Punic agricultural manual (28 books) — considered the most important agricultural text of the ancient Mediterranean; after the destruction of Carthage (146 BCE), the Roman Senate specifically ordered its translation from Punic into Latin (the only Carthaginian text to receive such treatment); its influence persisted through Roman agricultural writers (Columella, Varro, Pliny) — the original has not survived
- Carthaginian agriculture was highly developed — intensive cultivation of olives, grapes, figs, pomegranates, and grain in the fertile Tunisian plains; the use of enslaved labor on large estates (latifundia) may have influenced later Roman agricultural practices

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 The Punic Wars
- **First Punic War** (264–241 BCE): fought primarily over Sicily — Rome built a navy from scratch (allegedly copying a captured Carthaginian quinquereme) and ultimately prevailed; Carthage lost Sicily, and (in 238 BCE) Sardinia and Corsica
- **Second Punic War** (218–201 BCE): **Hannibal Barca** — commanding an army from Spain with African, Spanish, Gallic, and Numidian troops plus ~37 war elephants — crossed the Pyrenees and the **Alps** in late 218 BCE (one of the most audacious military operations in history); he won stunning victories at the Trebia, Lake Trasimene, and **Cannae** (216 BCE — where ~50,000–70,000 Roman soldiers were killed in a single day, the worst military defeat in Roman history); despite 15 years in Italy, Hannibal was unable to take Rome itself; the Roman general **Scipio Africanus** carried the war to Africa and defeated Hannibal at **Zama** (202 BCE)
- **Third Punic War** (149–146 BCE): Rome, driven by Cato the Elder's famous admonition ("Carthago delenda est" — "Carthage must be destroyed"), besieged and destroyed Carthage; the city was razed, the population (~500,000) was enslaved or killed; the site was ritually cursed

### 2.2 The Tophet Controversy
- The **Tophet** (a term borrowed from the Hebrew Bible) at Carthage (the Tophet of Salammbô/Tanit) is a sacred precinct containing thousands of **urns** with the cremated bones of infants, children, and small animals (lambs, kids), along with stelae dedicated to **Tanit** (the chief goddess of Carthage) and **Baal Hammon**
- **Child sacrifice interpretation**: classical sources (Diodorus Siculus, Plutarch, Tertullian) describe Carthaginian child sacrifice — children burned alive as offerings to Baal Hammon; modern archaeological evidence includes: the age distribution of remains (predominantly neonates and infants up to ~3 months), the presence of dedicatory stelae with votive inscriptions, and the proportionally high ratio of infant to animal remains. Scholars like Stager and Wolff support the sacrifice interpretation
- **Counter-interpretation**: other scholars (notably Schwartz, Fantar, and Lancel) argue that the Tophet may be a specialized **infant cemetery** (for naturally deceased children — neonatal mortality in the ancient world was very high) with accompanying dedications; this view holds that classical accounts were biased — anti-Carthaginian propaganda by Greek and Roman enemies
- The debate remains unresolved, though the sacrifice interpretation is currently the majority scholarly position

### 2.3 Navigation and Exploration
- **Periplus of Hanno** (c. 500 BCE): a Greek translation of a Punic inscription describing a Carthaginian expedition led by **Hanno** along the West African coast — possibly reaching as far as Cameroon or Gabon (encounters with "gorillas" — likely gorillas or chimpanzees — are described); the account is the earliest surviving record of European/Mediterranean contact with tropical West Africa
- **Himilco** (c. 500 BCE): reportedly sailed northward along the Atlantic coast, reaching Britain and possibly exploring tin trade routes — known only fragmentarily through Pliny and Avienus

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Extent of Hanno's Voyage
- Whether Hanno reached as far as the Gulf of Guinea, Cameroon, or even further south remains debated — identifications of places mentioned in the Periplus are uncertain; the "gorillas" encountered in the text may be apes or possibly misidentified local peoples (the word "gorilla" in modern zoological usage derives from this text)

### 4.1 Carthage as More "Barbaric" than Rome
- **[MISLEADING]** Roman and Greek portrayals of Carthage as uniquely cruel or uncivilized reflect **victor's bias** — Rome itself practiced mass enslavement, crucifixion, arena executions, and the destruction of entire cities (Carthage, Corinth, Jerusalem); describing Carthaginian sacrifice (if it occurred) as exceptionally monstrous while ignoring comparable Roman practices is historically asymmetric

## Counter-Arguments & Criticisms

- **Child sacrifice evidence disputed**: M'hamed Hassine Fantar (*Carthage: Approche d'une civilisation*, Alif, 1993) and Sergio Ribichini argue that Tophet burials may represent a sacred cemetery for stillborn or naturally deceased infants rather than evidence of systematic child sacrifice — the skeletal evidence is ambiguous, and Roman accounts (Diodorus Siculus, Plutarch) may reflect hostile propaganda from a civilization that destroyed Carthage
- **Roman bias in primary sources**: Richard Miles (*Carthage Must Be Destroyed*, Allen Lane, 2010) emphasizes that virtually all surviving literary sources about Carthage were written by its Greek and Roman enemies — no Punic literature survives, creating an inherently one-sided historical record that scholars must critically evaluate rather than accept at face value
- **Carthaginian navigation claims exaggerated**: Duane Roller (*Through the Pillars of Herakles*, Routledge, 2006) cautions that Hanno's Periplus, the primary evidence for Carthaginian exploration of West Africa, survives only in a single late Greek manuscript of uncertain provenance — identifications of places mentioned in the text remain speculative, and claims of voyages reaching Cameroon or beyond lack corroborating evidence
- **Tophet interpretation remains contested**: Paolo Xella et al. ("Phoenician Bones of Contention," *Antiquity* 87, 2013) present osteological evidence supporting the sacrifice interpretation, while Patricia Smith et al. ("Age Estimations Attest to Infant Sacrifice at the Carthage Tophet," *Antiquity* 87, 2013) counter-argue that the age distribution of remains is consistent with natural infant mortality patterns in pre-modern societies
- **Carthage as Mediterranean peer, not exotic "other"**: Josephine Crawley Quinn (*In Search of the Phoenicians*, Princeton University Press, 2017) argues that modern scholarship has over-emphasized Carthaginian distinctiveness — Carthage shared fundamental political, economic, and cultural structures with other Mediterranean city-states, and framing it as uniquely barbaric or uniquely sophisticated both distort the historical record
