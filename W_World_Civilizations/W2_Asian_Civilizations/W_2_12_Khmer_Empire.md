## QUICK SUMMARY

The **Khmer Empire** (c. 802–1431 CE) — centered in present-day Cambodia and extending across much of mainland Southeast Asia — was one of the most powerful and sophisticated civilizations in world history, yet its true scale was only fully revealed by 21st-century **LiDAR** (Light Detection and Ranging) surveys. These airborne surveys (Damian Evans *et al.*, 2012, 2016) penetrated dense tropical forest canopy to reveal that **Greater Angkor** was the **largest pre-industrial city in the world** — covering approximately **1,000 km²** with a population estimated at 750,000 to 1 million — far exceeding contemporary European cities. The empire's power rested on an extraordinary **hydraulic infrastructure**: massive artificial reservoirs (**barays**) — the West Baray alone measures 8 km × 2.1 km and held 56 million m³ of water — canals, dikes, and rice-paddy irrigation systems that enabled multiple annual harvests, supporting the dense population needed to build Angkor Wat (c. 1113–1150, under **Suryavarman II**) and the Bayon temple complex (c. 1181–1220, under **Jayavarman VII**). The Khmer kings ruled as **devarajas** (god-kings), fusing Hindu and Buddhist cosmology with political authority; state temples represented Mount Meru, the cosmic axis. **Jayavarman VII** (r. 1181–c. 1218) — the empire's most ambitious builder — constructed Angkor Thom, the Bayon (with its 200+ enigmatic smiling stone faces), Ta Prohm, Preah Khan, and an empire-wide road-and-hospital system, while shifting state religion from Hinduism to Mahayana Buddhism. The empire's decline and the abandonment of Angkor (traditionally dated to the Thai sack of 1431, but LiDAR and paleoclimate evidence now suggest a more complex, gradual process) involved hydraulic system failure, prolonged drought (14th–15th centuries, corroborated by Southeast Asian tree-ring data), overextension under Jayavarman VII, and shifting trade networks favoring the coast.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 Founding and Political Structure
- **Jayavarman II** (r. c. 802–835 CE): traditionally credited with founding the Khmer Empire by declaring independence from Javanese overlordship and establishing the **devaraja** (god-king) cult on Phnom Kulen — uniting the Khmer polities under a single divine monarchy
- **Devaraja cult**: the king as the earthly embodiment or servant of a Hindu deity (typically Shiva or Vishnu); state temples housed the royal linga (Shiva) or Vishnu image representing the king's divine essence; political legitimacy was cosmological — the king maintained the order of the universe through ritual
- **Empire at its height** (12th–13th centuries): controlled much of modern Cambodia, Thailand (Isan and parts of central Thailand), southern Laos, and southern Vietnam — the dominant power in mainland Southeast Asia

### 1.2 LiDAR Revelations
- **Damian Evans *et al.*** (2012 — *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*; 2016 — *Journal of Archaeological Science*): airborne LiDAR surveys of the Greater Angkor region revealed:
  - An urban sprawl of ~1,000 km² surrounding the temple complexes — the world's largest pre-industrial urban landscape
  - Extensive hydraulic engineering: canals, embankments, ponds, and rice-field grids extending far beyond previously known boundaries
  - Previously unknown medieval cities beneath forest cover in the Phnom Kulen hills and at Mahendraparvata (Jayavarman II's early capital)
  - Grid-pattern urban planning with residential mounds, water management features, and connecting roads — undermining the idea of Angkor as merely a temple complex surrounded by forest

### 1.3 Hydraulic Engineering
- **Barays** (artificial reservoirs): the West Baray (~8 km × 2.1 km), East Baray (~7.5 km × 1.8 km), and smaller barays stored monsoon water for dry-season irrigation — enabling wet-rice cultivation sufficient to support a massive population
- **Canal networks**: connected barays, rivers, and rice paddies; managed water flow across a relatively flat landscape; the system's engineering sophistication reflects centuries of accumulated hydraulic knowledge
- The **Tonle Sap** (Great Lake) — the largest freshwater lake in Southeast Asia — and its annual flood-pulse ecosystem provided critical protein (fish) to the Angkor population

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Jayavarman VII and the Buddhist Transformation
- **Jayavarman VII** (r. 1181–c. 1218): the last great Khmer builder-king; assumed power after defeating the Cham invasion of Angkor (1177 — Cham forces sacked the capital by naval assault via the Tonle Sap); his reign saw the most intense building program in Angkor's history:
  - **Angkor Thom** (12 km² walled city — the final Khmer capital at Angkor; five monumental gates with four-faced towers; the Terrace of the Elephants and Terrace of the Leper King)
  - **The Bayon**: state temple at the center of Angkor Thom — 54 towers with ~200 serene, enigmatic smiling faces (probably representing the bodhisattva Avalokiteshvara, or a fusion of the bodhisattva with Jayavarman VII's own likeness)
  - **Ta Prohm**: Buddhist monastery-university complex (12,640 inhabitants recorded in inscription); later famous for its tree-engulfed ruins
  - **Empire-wide infrastructure**: inscriptions record 102 hospitals along major roads; 121 rest-houses for travelers; a road network connecting the capital to provincial centers
- **Religious shift**: Jayavarman VII shifted state patronage from Hinduism (specifically Shaivism, the dominant Khmer court religion for four centuries) to **Mahayana Buddhism** — interpreting kingship through the bodhisattva ideal of compassion rather than the Shaiva devaraja model

### 2.2 Collapse Theories
- **Traditional narrative**: the Thai kingdom of Ayutthaya sacked Angkor in 1431, leading to abandonment and the shift of the Khmer capital to Phnom Penh. However, modern scholarship sees this as a final event in a prolonged decline rather than a single catastrophic collapse
- **Hydraulic failure hypothesis** (Roland Fletcher, Christophe Pottier): the elaborate water management system — designed for specific hydrological conditions — became increasingly difficult to maintain as the infrastructure aged, was modified, and faced novel stresses; failure cascaded through the agricultural system
- **Paleoclimate evidence** (Brendan Buckley *et al.*, 2010 — *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*): Vietnamese cypress tree-ring data reveal prolonged droughts in the 14th and 15th centuries, interspersed with unusually intense monsoons — alternating stress on the hydraulic system; this climate instability may have exceeded the system's capacity for adaptation
- **Overextension**: Jayavarman VII's unprecedented building program may have strained labor, resources, and social cohesion; a Hindu reaction (evidenced by systematic defacement of Buddhist images) following his death suggests internal religious and political conflict
- **Trade network shift**: the rise of maritime trade in the South China Sea favored coastal polities over inland agrarian empires — the Khmer economy became less competitive relative to emerging Tai and Vietnamese coastal centers

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Population Scale and Urbanization Debates
- Population estimates for Greater Angkor range from ~750,000 to over 1 million at its peak — based on LiDAR-derived settlement density and agricultural capacity models. These estimates, while supported by spatial analysis, remain debated; scholars suggest lower figures based on different assumptions about household size, agricultural productivity, and the proportion of the LiDAR-visible landscape that was simultaneously occupied

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Angkor Built by Non-Khmer Outsiders
- **[DEBUNKED]** Various fringe claims attribute Angkor's construction to Egyptian, Indian, or extraterrestrial builders. The archaeological, epigraphic, art-historical, and architectural evidence overwhelmingly demonstrates indigenous Khmer construction, with documented influences from Indian religious and artistic traditions that were adopted, adapted, and transformed by Khmer architects and artisans over centuries. Thousands of Sanskrit and Khmer inscriptions document the builders, patrons, and religious foundations of Angkor's monuments
