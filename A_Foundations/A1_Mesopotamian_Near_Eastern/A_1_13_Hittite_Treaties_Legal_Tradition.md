## QUICK SUMMARY

The Hittite Empire (c. 1650–1178 BCE), based at Hattusa (modern Boğazköy, Turkey), produced one of the richest legal and diplomatic archives of the ancient world. Over 30,000 cuneiform tablet fragments recovered from the Hattusa archives document treaties, law codes, religious rituals, and diplomatic correspondence. The Egyptian-Hittite peace treaty of 1259 BCE (Treaty of Kadesh) is the oldest surviving international peace treaty, with copies preserved in both Akkadian cuneiform and Egyptian hieroglyphs. The Hittite vassal treaty form — featuring preamble, historical prologue, stipulations, divine witnesses, and blessings/curses — has been identified by scholars (Mendenhall, Kitchen) as the structural model for biblical covenant theology, making these texts foundational for understanding both ancient international law and the development of Judeo-Christian religious concepts (Tier 1–2).

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Discovery of the Hattusa Archives
- Hugo Winckler excavated Boğazköy (ancient Hattusa) in 1906 and discovered approximately 10,000 cuneiform tablet fragments in the first season alone
- Subsequent excavations by Kurt Bittel and the German Archaeological Institute (1931–present) recovered over 30,000 fragments total
- Tablets written in at least eight languages: Hittite (Nesite), Akkadian, Sumerian, Hurrian, Hattic, Luwian, Palaic, and even a few lines of an unknown language
- Bedřich Hrozný deciphered Hittite as an Indo-European language in 1915–1917, revolutionizing linguistics — Hittite became the oldest attested Indo-European language
- **Primary Source:** Hrozný, B. "Die Lösung des hethitischen Problems." *Mitteilungen der Deutschen Orient-Gesellschaft* 56 (1915): 17–50.

### 1.2 The Egyptian-Hittite Peace Treaty (Treaty of Kadesh, ~1259 BCE)
- Concluded between Hattusili III of Hatti and Ramesses II of Egypt after the Battle of Kadesh (~1274 BCE) and years of subsequent conflict
- **Akkadian version** found at Boğazköy (now in Istanbul Archaeological Museum); **Egyptian version** inscribed on the walls of the Temple of Amun at Karnak and the Ramesseum
- Both versions are substantially harmonious but differ in emphasis — the Egyptian text credits Ramesses with the initiative for peace; the Hittite version credits Hattusili
- Treaty provisions include: mutual non-aggression, defensive alliance, extradition of fugitives, succession guarantees, and divine witnesses from both Egyptian and Hittite pantheons
- A replica of the cuneiform version hangs in the United Nations headquarters in New York as a symbol of international peace
- **Primary Source:** Beckman, G. *Hittite Diplomatic Texts*. 2nd ed. Scholars Press, 1999. Text no. 15.
- **Counter-Argument:** None — the treaty's existence and content are universally accepted. Only minor dating controversies exist (1259 BCE vs. 1258 BCE depending on the chronological framework used).

### 1.3 Hittite Vassal Treaty Form
- Hittite treaties with vassal states follow a consistent structure:
  1. **Preamble** — identification of the Hittite Great King
  2. **Historical prologue** — recounting past relations and benefits provided by the overlord
  3. **Stipulations** — obligations of the vassal
  4. **Provision for deposit and public reading**
  5. **Divine witnesses** — list of gods from both parties
  6. **Blessings and curses** — consequences of compliance or violation
- This six-element structure is remarkably consistent across multiple treaties spanning 200+ years
- Key examples: Treaty with Shattiwaza of Mitanni (CTH 51/52), Treaty with Duppi-Teshub of Amurru (CTH 62), Treaty with Alaksandu of Wilusa (CTH 76, possibly Homeric "Alexander/Paris of Troy")
- **Primary Source:** Beckman 1999; see also Kitchen, K.A. & Lawrence, P.J.N. *Treaty, Law and Covenant in the Ancient Near East*. 3 vols. Harrassowitz, 2012.

### 1.4 The Hittite Law Code
- Two tablets (approximately 200 paragraphs) recovered from Hattusa, dating to ~1650–1500 BCE with later copies/revisions
- Distinct from Mesopotamian law codes (Hammurabi, Ur-Nammu) in several ways: emphasizes restitution over retribution; includes revision clauses showing legal evolution ("formerly... but now..."); treats offenses pragmatically rather than morally
- Covers: assault and homicide, theft, property damage, sexual offenses, slavery, agriculture, livestock, sorcery, and ritual impurity
- Notably less harsh than contemporary Mesopotamian or later biblical codes — many capital offenses in Hammurabi's Code are penalized by fines in Hittite law
- **Primary Source:** Hoffner, H.A. *The Laws of the Hittites: A Critical Edition*. Brill, 1997.
- **Counter-Argument:** The "reforming" character of Hittite law may be overstated — some "formerly" clauses may represent scholarly scribal commentary rather than actual historical legal changes.

### 1.5 Hittite Religious and Ritual Texts
- The Hattusa archive includes hundreds of ritual texts — the largest surviving corpus of Bronze Age ritual practice
- The *Illuyanka* myth (dragon-slaying) and *Kumarbi* cycle (Hurrian theogony adopted by Hittites) provide important comparative mythology parallels — Kumarbi's castration of the sky god parallels Hesiod's *Theogony* (Kronos/Ouranos)
- Festival texts (AN.TAḪ.ŠUM festival, KI.LAM festival, *purulli* festival) document state religious ceremonies lasting weeks
- **Primary Source:** Beckman, G. "Hittite Religion." In *Religions of the Ancient World*, edited by S.I. Johnston. Harvard University Press, 2004.

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Hittite Treaties as Model for Biblical Covenant
- George Mendenhall (1954) and later Kenneth Kitchen argued that the biblical covenant between Yahweh and Israel (especially as presented in Deuteronomy and Joshua 24) follows the structure of Hittite second-millennium vassal treaties — not later Assyrian treaty forms
- The parallels include: divine self-identification ("I am the LORD your God"), historical prologue ("who brought you out of Egypt"), stipulations (the commandments), and blessings/curses (Deuteronomy 27–28)
- This argument, if correct, supports a second-millennium BCE dating for core covenant traditions — earlier than most critical scholars propose
- **Counter-Argument:** Dennis McCarthy and others countered that similar treaty elements also appear in first-millennium Assyrian treaties (Esarhaddon's Succession Treaty, ~672 BCE) and that the parallels are not uniquely Hittite. The debate remains active.
- **Primary Source:** Mendenhall, G.E. "Covenant Forms in Israelite Tradition." *The Biblical Archaeologist* 17/3 (1954): 50–76.

### 2.2 Treaty of Alaksandu and Homeric Troy Connection
- Hittite treaty with Alaksandu of Wilusa (CTH 76) — Wilusa is widely identified with Greek *Wilios/Ilios* (Troy), and Alaksandu may correspond to Greek Alexandros (Paris of Troy)
- The treaty references previous conflict between Hatti and Ahhiyawa (plausibly = Mycenaean Achaeans/Greeks)
- If correct, this constitutes the earliest historical evidence behind Homeric epic traditions
- **Counter-Argument:** The identifications (Wilusa = Troy, Ahhiyawa = Achaeans) are broadly accepted but not absolutely proven. Bryce (2005) and Hawkins (1998) support them; Steiner (2007) raises methodological cautions.
- **Primary Source:** Bryce, T. "The Hittites and the Trojan War." In *The Oxford Handbook of the Bronze Age Aegean*, 2010.

### 2.3 Hittite Influence on Greek Mythology via Hurrian Intermediary
- The *Kumarbi Cycle* (Song of Kumarbi, Song of Ullikummi) — a Hurrian mythology adapted by Hittite scribes — shows striking parallels to Hesiod's *Theogony*:
  - Succession of sky gods through violent overthrow (castration)
  - A stone or monster used as a weapon/substitute
  - A final triumphant storm god (Teshub / Zeus)
- These parallels suggest a route of mythological transmission from Anatolia/Syria to Greece, possibly through coastal contact during the Late Bronze Age
- **Primary Source:** López-Ruiz, C. *When the Gods Were Born: Greek Cosmogonies and the Near East*. Harvard University Press, 2010.

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Hittite Medicine and Plague Prayers as Evidence for Epidemic
- The "Plague Prayers of Mursili II" (~1321–1295 BCE) describe a devastating plague that lasted 20+ years and killed Mursili's father (Suppiluliuma I) and brother (Arnuwanda II)
- Scholars have connected this to a historical epidemic — possibly introduced from Egypt via prisoners of war taken during campaigns in Syria
- The plague prayers show remarkably modern-seeming causative reasoning: Mursili asks the gods to reveal the cause, acknowledges his father's treaty violations as potential divine punishment, and attempts systematic ritual remedy
- **Counter-Argument:** The "plague" may be a literary/theological topos rather than a specific historical epidemic; ancient Near Eastern plague descriptions were common in religious literature.

### 3.2 Hattusa's Destruction and the End of the Hittite Empire
- Hattusa was destroyed by fire around 1178 BCE during the broader Bronze Age Collapse
- The destroyers remain unidentified — candidates include Kaskian tribes from the Pontic region, "Sea Peoples," internal rebellion, or a combination of factors
- The empire fragmented into Neo-Hittite successor states (Carchemish, Aleppo, Hamath) that preserved Hittite cultural elements for centuries
- **Counter-Argument:** Archaeological evidence suggests the city may have been partially abandoned BEFORE destruction, complicating the "conquest" narrative.

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 **[DEBUNKED]** Hittites as "Missing" from History, Discovered by the Bible
- A popular apologetic claim asserts that the Hittites were known only from the Bible until archaeology confirmed their existence, proving biblical accuracy
- In reality, Hittite monuments were known to European travelers since the 18th century, and cuneiform references to Hatti were identified before the Boğazköy excavation
- While the Bible does mention "Hittites," the biblical usage likely refers to Neo-Hittite states of the first millennium rather than the Bronze Age Hittite Empire
- **Debunking Source:** Bryce, T. *The Kingdom of the Hittites*. Oxford University Press, 2005. Chapter 1.

---

## COUNTER-ARGUMENTS

- **Against the Mendenhall/Kitchen treaty-covenant thesis:** The structural parallels between Hittite treaties and biblical covenant may reflect a broader Near Eastern legal convention rather than direct Hittite influence. First-millennium Assyrian treaties also share structural elements.
- **Against Hittite exceptionalism:** While Hittite law appears more "humane" than Mesopotamian parallels, this may reflect the state of preservation rather than actual practice — we have far more Hittite "revision" texts than for other cultures.
