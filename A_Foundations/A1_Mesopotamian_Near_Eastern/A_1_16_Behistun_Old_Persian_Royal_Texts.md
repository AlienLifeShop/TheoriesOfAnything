## QUICK SUMMARY

The Behistun Inscription (also spelled Bisotun, located on a cliff face in western Iran) is the most important Old Persian royal text and one of the most significant epigraphic monuments in the history of scholarship — it was the key that enabled the decipherment of cuneiform writing systems, unlocking the entire literary and historical record of ancient Mesopotamia. Commissioned by Darius I (r. 522–486 BCE) to commemorate his seizure of power from a usurper (Gaumata) and his suppression of rebellions across the vast Achaemenid Empire, the inscription is carved into a limestone cliff 100 meters above the ground, accompanied by a monumental relief panel showing Darius triumphant over defeated rebel kings beneath the winged symbol of Ahura Mazda. The text appears in three cuneiform scripts — Old Persian, Elamite, and Babylonian Akkadian — creating the trilingual parallel that (like the Rosetta Stone for Egyptian hieroglyphs) enabled systematic decipherment. Henry Rawlinson's perilous copying of the inscription between 1835 and 1847 (climbing the cliff face with ladders and ropes) and subsequent decipherment work, alongside contributions by Georg Friedrich Grotefend, Edward Hincks, and Jules Oppert, opened the entire cuneiform world — Sumerian, Akkadian, Hittite, Elamite — to modern scholarship. Beyond decipherment, Achaemenid royal inscriptions (at Persepolis, Naqsh-e Rostam, Susa, Pasargadae) constitute a vital primary source for understanding the ideology, religion, and administrative structure of the first "world empire."

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 The Behistun Inscription: Physical Monument
- The **Behistun Inscription** is carved into a cliff face of Mount Behistun (Bisotun), ~30 km east of Kermanshah, western Iran, at approximately 100 meters above the ancient road below — the Royal Road connecting Babylon to Ecbatana passed directly beneath, ensuring maximum visibility
- The relief panel (5.5 m × 3 m) shows Darius I standing with his foot on the prostrate Gaumata, his left hand raised toward the winged figure of Ahura Mazda, while nine captured rebel kings stand bound before him in a line, with a tenth (Skunkha the Scythian) added later
- The text consists of five columns of Old Persian cuneiform (~515 lines), plus versions in Elamite (c. 650 lines) and Babylonian Akkadian (c. 112 lines) — the three versions convey the same content with minor variations, creating the trilingual key to decipherment
- Inscribed on UNESCO World Heritage Site list in 2006

### 1.2 Content: Darius's Narrative
- Darius narrates how the legitimate Achaemenid king Cambyses II died while campaigning in Egypt, after which a usurper (identified as Gaumata the Magian, claiming to be Cambyses' brother Bardiya) seized the throne — Darius, as a member of a collateral branch of the Achaemenid royal family, killed Gaumata and claimed the throne "by the will of Ahura Mazda"
- The inscription then recounts **19 battles** against rebels in 9 countries within roughly one year (522–521 BCE) — including revolts in Babylon (twice), Media, Elam, Parthia, Margiana, Persia, Arachosia, and among the Scythians — demonstrating that Darius faced a genuine empire-wide crisis of legitimacy upon taking power
- The text repeatedly attributes Darius's success to Ahura Mazda ("By the will of Ahura Mazda I am king; Ahura Mazda bestowed the kingdom upon me") — the earliest extensive royal inscription invoking Zoroastrian/Mazdean religious authority
- **Counter-Argument:** Darius's account is obviously self-serving propaganda — the claim that Gaumata was a false pretender may have been a cover for Darius's usurpation of the legitimate Bardiya; several Greek historians (Ctesias, Justin) preserve alternative versions of events, and modern scholars (Pierre Briant, *From Cyrus to Alexander*) treat Darius's narrative with appropriate skepticism

### 1.3 Decipherment of Cuneiform
- **Georg Friedrich Grotefend** (1802) made the first breakthrough in Old Persian decipherment by comparing the Persepolis inscriptions of Darius and Xerxes — identifying royal names and titles by their structural position without knowing the language or the values of the signs
- **Henry Rawlinson** independently and more thoroughly deciphered Old Persian between 1835–1847 using the Behistun text, physically copying the inscription from scaffolding and ladders at extreme height — by 1847 he could read most of the Old Persian text
- **Edward Hincks** made crucial contributions to both Old Persian and Babylonian Akkadian decipherment — he recognized the syllabic nature of cuneiform and identified many sign values
- The "1857 test": the Royal Asiatic Society sent identical previously unread Akkadian inscriptions to Rawlinson, Hincks, Oppert, and Talbot independently — all four produced substantially similar translations, confirming that Akkadian cuneiform had been legitimately deciphered

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Achaemenid Imperial Ideology
- Achaemenid royal inscriptions (Persepolis, Naqsh-e Rostam, Susa) present a consistent imperial ideology: the king rules "by the will of Ahura Mazda," maintains arta (cosmic truth/order) against drauga (the Lie), protects all peoples of the empire, and punishes liars — inscriptions at Persepolis list 23–30 subject peoples, emphasizing the multinational scope of the empire
- The **Cyrus Cylinder** (539 BCE, British Museum) — often claimed as the "first declaration of human rights" — presents Cyrus the Great's conquest of Babylon as a liberation, emphasizing his restoration of deported peoples and their gods to their homelands; while not a universal human rights charter in the modern sense, it demonstrates a distinctive Achaemenid ideology of tolerant imperial rule
- Xerxes' **Daiva Inscription** (XPh, Persepolis) — in which Xerxes describes destroying a sanctuary of the daivas (false gods) and establishing the worship of Ahura Mazda — suggests a more assertive Mazdean religious policy under Xerxes than under Cyrus or Darius
- **Counter-Argument:** Royal inscriptions are propaganda — they present the idealized self-image of the rulers, not necessarily the reality of imperial governance; Achaemenid "tolerance" was pragmatic imperial administration, not modern multiculturalism

### 2.2 Relationship to Zoroastrianism
- Darius's Behistun inscription and other Achaemenid royal texts invoke Ahura Mazda but do not mention Zarathustra (Zoroaster) by name — their theology (dualism of truth/lie, invocation of Ahura Mazda, emphasis on arta) is consistent with Zoroastrian concepts but the relationship between Achaemenid royal religion and the Zoroastrian tradition of the Avesta is debated
- Scholars argue the Achaemenids were Zoroastrian; others argue they followed a pre-Zoroastrian or para-Zoroastrian Mazdean tradition; still others view the question as unanswerable given the limited evidence
- The later Achaemenids (Artaxerxes II) explicitly invoked Mithra and Anahita alongside Ahura Mazda, suggesting increasing syncretism or the formal incorporation of these deities into the royal cult

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 The Real Identity of Gaumata/Bardiya
- Whether the man Darius killed was truly a pretender (Gaumata the Magian impersonating the dead Bardiya) or the legitimate Bardiya himself is one of the most debated questions in Achaemenid history — Darius's narrative is the primary account, and he had obvious motivation to delegitimize his predecessor
- Modern reconstructions range from accepting Darius's account at face value, to proposing that Bardiya was genuine and Darius was the usurper, to suggesting that the truth is unrecoverable

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 "The Cyrus Cylinder Is a Universal Human Rights Declaration"
- **[DEBUNKED]** While the Cyrus Cylinder expresses a form of political tolerance, calling it a "human rights" document imposes anachronistic categories — it follows the genre of Mesopotamian royal legitimation inscriptions, and Cyrus's "liberations" were strategic acts consolidating his conquest of Babylon, not expressions of universal rights; the "first charter of human rights" claim, promoted by the Shah of Iran in the 1970s, is not supported by scholarly analysis of the text
