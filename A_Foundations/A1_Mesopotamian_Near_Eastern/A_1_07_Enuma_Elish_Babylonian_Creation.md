## QUICK SUMMARY

The **Enuma Elish** ("When on high…") is the **Babylonian creation epic** — a cosmogonic poem of approximately 1,100 lines inscribed on **seven clay tablets**, composed ca. **1100 BCE** (though likely drawing on older traditions back to the Old Babylonian period, ~1800 BCE). It tells how **Marduk**, the patron god of Babylon, rose from junior deity to **king of the gods** by defeating the primordial chaos dragon **Tiamat** — then split her body to create heaven and earth, established cosmic order, and created humanity from the blood of **Kingu** (Tiamat's consort). The text is simultaneously a **cosmogony** (creation story), a **theogony** (divine genealogy), and a **political charter** — it justifies Babylon's supremacy and Marduk's position at the head of the divine assembly (→ [A_1_05](A_1_05_Divine_Council_Assembly_of_Gods.md)). It was ritually recited every year at the **Akitu (New Year) festival** in Babylon. The Enuma Elish has profound connections to **Genesis 1** (→ [A_2_01](../A2_Biblical_Gnostic_Western_Esoteric/A_2_01_Bible_Serpent_References.md)); the Hebrew *tehom* ("the deep") appears to derive from *Tiamat*, and the seven-day structure of creation may echo the seven tablets. It also provides the archetypal **chaoskampf** (chaos-combat) pattern that recurs in the Baal Cycle (→ [A_1_06](A_1_06_Ugaritic_Literature_Baal_Cycle.md)), Hittite myths (→ [C_5_07](../../C_Global_Traditions/C5_Regional_Analytical_Traditions/C_5_07_Hittite_Hurrian_Mythology_Kumarbi.md)), Greek Titanomachy, and Norse Ragnarök.

## 1. THE TEXT AND ITS DISCOVERY

### 1.1 Archaeological Recovery

| Detail | Information |
|--------|-------------|
| **Discovery** | Henry Layard (1849) and Hormuzd Rassam (1852–53) recovered fragments from Ashurbanipal's library at Nineveh |
| **First publication** | George Smith, 1876 — presented to the newly founded Society of Biblical Archaeology |
| **Tablets** | Seven numbered tablets; most substantially preserved; some lacunae remain |
| **Provenance** | ~7th century BCE copies from Nineveh, but composition dates to ~1100 BCE (Nebuchadnezzar I era); older antecedents possible |
| **Language** | Standard Babylonian (literary Akkadian dialect) |
| **Other copies** | Fragments from Ashur, Kish, Sultantepe, and Uruk confirm wide distribution |
| **Current location** | British Museum (primary tablets); fragments in various collections |

### 1.2 Title and Structure

- **Title:** Named from its incipit (opening words): *enūma eliš* = "When on high…" (Mesopotamian convention)
- **Length:** ~1,100 lines across 7 tablets (~150-170 lines per tablet)
- **Genre:** Cosmogonic epic combined with aretalogy (hymn of praise to Marduk's 50 names)
- **Colophon:** Some copies note they were for the Akitu festival

## 2. THE NARRATIVE

### 2.1 Tablets I–VII Summary

| Tablet | Content |
|--------|---------|
| **I** | In the beginning, only **Apsu** (fresh water, male) and **Tiamat** (salt water, female) exist. Their mingling generates successive pairs of gods. The noise of younger gods disturbs Apsu, who plots to destroy them. **Ea** (Enki) learns of the plot, kills Apsu, and builds his temple on the corpse. Ea's son **Marduk** is born — magnificent, with four eyes and four ears. |
| **II** | Tiamat, enraged by Apsu's death, creates 11 **monster-creatures** (serpent-dragons, lion-demons, scorpion-men, fish-men). She elevates **Kingu** as her consort and gives him the **Tablet of Destinies** (→ [A_1_02](A_1_02_Sumerian_ME_Divine_Programs.md)). The gods panic. Ea and Anu fail to face her. |
| **III** | The gods assemble (→ [A_1_05](A_1_05_Divine_Council_Assembly_of_Gods.md)). **Anshar** summons the divine council. Marduk volunteers to fight Tiamat — but demands supreme kingship as payment. |
| **IV** | The gods test Marduk's power by having him destroy and recreate a constellation. Satisfied, they grant him kingship. Marduk arms himself with winds, storms, and a net. He confronts Tiamat, drives an evil wind into her mouth, and slays her with an arrow through her heart. He captures Kingu and takes the Tablet of Destinies. |
| **V** | Marduk **splits Tiamat's body**: her upper half becomes the sky, her lower half the earth. He establishes the stars, constellations, calendar, moon phases. He creates the **Tigris and Euphrates** from her eyes. |
| **VI** | Marduk creates **humanity** from the blood of Kingu — humans are made to serve the gods, freeing them from labor. He builds **Babylon** and **Esagila** (Marduk's temple). The gods praise him with **50 names**. |
| **VII** | Continuation of the 50 names of Marduk, each encoding a theological attribute. Final instruction that the poem should be handed down, taught to future generations. |

## 3. THEOLOGICAL AND POLITICAL FUNCTION

### 3.1 Marduk's Rise as Political Theology

| Level | Function |
|-------|----------|
| **Cosmic** | Establishes the origin of cosmos from primordial chaos |
| **Theogonic** | Explains divine hierarchy — why Marduk reigns supreme over older gods |
| **Political** | Justifies Babylon's supremacy — other cities' gods willingly submitted to Marduk |
| **Liturgical** | Provides the text for annual Akitu recitation, renewing cosmic and political order |
| **Anthropological** | Explains human purpose — created from defeated rebel's blood to serve the gods |

### 3.2 Humans Created from Blood of a Rebel

The creation of humanity carries profound implications:
- Humans are made from **Kingu's blood** — the rebel consort of Tiamat
- This means humanity has both **divine substance** (blood of a god) and **rebellious origin** (blood of the defeated)
- Humans are created for **labor** — to free gods from toil
- This contrasts with Genesis, where humans are made in God's image for relationship
- But parallels Sumerian accounts where humans are created as workers (→ [A_1_01](A_1_01_Sumerian_Texts_and_Tablets.md), A_1_02)

## 4. CONNECTIONS TO GENESIS AND OTHER TRADITIONS

### 4.1 Enuma Elish and Genesis 1

| Enuma Elish | Genesis 1 | Relationship |
|-------------|-----------|-------------|
| Tiamat (salt water) | *Tehom* ("the deep") — Gen 1:2 | *Tehom* likely derives from *Tiamat* linguistically |
| Creation from splitting Tiamat | Separation of waters above/below (Day 2) | Structural parallel — dividing primordial waters |
| Seven tablets | Seven days of creation | Possible structural echo |
| Divine rest after creation | God rests on Day 7 | Both end with rest/celebration |
| Humans from clay + divine blood | Humans from dust + divine breath | Comparable but theologically distinct |
| Multiple gods in assembly | "Let US make man" (Gen 1:26) — plural | Possible echo of divine council (→ [A_1_05](A_1_05_Divine_Council_Assembly_of_Gods.md)) |
| Combat cosmogony | No combat — creation by word | Genesis DELIBERATELY removes combat (de-mythologization) |

## 5. THE AKITU FESTIVAL

### 5.1 Annual Re-Enactment

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Timing** | Nisannu (first month, spring equinox) — 12-day festival |
| **Day 4** | Enuma Elish recited in its entirety before Marduk's statue |
| **Day 5** | King's humiliation — priest strips king of regalia, slaps his face; king weeps before Marduk |
| **Day 8-10** | Determination of destinies — king "takes Marduk's hand," renewing his right to rule |
| **Day 11** | Sacred marriage (possible — debated) |
| **Purpose** | Annual renewal of cosmic order; king's authority re-legitimated; new year established |
| **Duration** | Attested from Old Babylonian period through Seleucid era (~1800 BCE – 200 BCE) — 1,600+ years |
