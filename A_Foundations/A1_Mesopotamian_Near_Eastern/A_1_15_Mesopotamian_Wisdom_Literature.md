## QUICK SUMMARY

Mesopotamian wisdom literature — spanning over 2,000 years from Sumerian proverb collections (c. 2500 BCE) to late Babylonian philosophical dialogues (c. 500 BCE) — represents humanity's earliest sustained written engagement with questions of justice, suffering, mortality, divine caprice, and the meaning of human existence. These cuneiform texts, preserved on clay tablets from Nippur, Babylon, Nineveh, and other sites, include proverbs, fables, debate poems, philosophical dialogues, and extended meditations on theodicy that prefigure and directly parallel biblical wisdom traditions (Job, Ecclesiastes, Proverbs). The *Instruction of Shuruppak* (c. 2500 BCE) — advice from a father to his son on practical ethics — is among the oldest literary texts in human history. *Ludlul Bēl Nēmeqi* ("I Will Praise the Lord of Wisdom," c. 1700–1200 BCE), often called the "Babylonian Job," addresses a righteous man's inexplicable suffering and eventual divine rescue. The *Babylonian Theodicy* (c. 1000 BCE) is a structured acrostic dialogue between a sufferer and a friend debating why the gods allow the wicked to prosper. The *Dialogue of Pessimism* (c. 1000 BCE) — a darkly comic exchange between a master and slave who find reasons both for and against every possible human action — has been called the earliest expression of philosophical nihilism or absurdism.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Instruction of Shuruppak
- The **Instruction of Shuruppak** (c. 2600–2500 BCE, with later copies from c. 1800 BCE) is attributed to Shuruppak, father of Ziusudra (the Sumerian flood hero), and consists of practical and ethical advice: "Do not buy a donkey that brays; do not locate a field on a road; do not make a well in a field — people will cause you damage; do not place a garden next to a highway"
- The text is one of the oldest known literary compositions, predating even the earliest written Gilgamesh narratives — copies have been found at Nippur, Ur, and Abu Salabikh
- The genre of father-to-son instruction connects to Egyptian wisdom literature (Instruction of Ptahhotep, c. 2400 BCE) and biblical Proverbs, suggesting a widespread ancient Near Eastern pattern

### 1.2 Sumerian Proverb Collections
- Over **1,500 Sumerian proverbs** have been recovered from scribal school (edubba) tablets, primarily from Nippur — organized thematically by subject and compiled as school exercises for apprentice scribes
- Proverbs range from practical observation ("A restless woman in the house adds ache to pain") to philosophical reflection ("Man — his days are numbered, whatever he may do, he is but wind") to humor ("The fox, having urinated into the sea, said: 'The depths of the sea are my urine!'")
- These collections represent the world's earliest known proverb literature, predating biblical Proverbs by over a millennium

### 1.3 Ludlul Bēl Nēmeqi ("Babylonian Job")
- *Ludlul Bēl Nēmeqi* ("I Will Praise the Lord of Wisdom") — preserved in multiple copies from Nineveh, Babylon, and Sippar — describes the suffering of Shubshi-meshre-Shakkan, a formerly prosperous and pious nobleman who loses wealth, health, social standing, and family support despite his devotion to the gods
- Over four tablets, the sufferer describes his inexplicable affliction (fevers, paralysis, social abandonment), his baffled questioning of divine justice ("What is good in one's sight is evil for a god; what is bad in one's mind is good for his god. Who can understand the counsel of the gods in the midst of heaven?"), and his eventual restoration through Marduk's intervention via dream-messengers
- The text predates the biblical Book of Job by several centuries and treats the identical theodicy problem — why does a righteous person suffer? — though the resolution differs (Marduk physically heals the sufferer; the biblical God offers no direct explanation)

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 The Babylonian Theodicy
- The **Babylonian Theodicy** (c. 1000 BCE) — a 27-stanza acrostic poem (the first letters of each stanza spell out the author's name and profession) — presents a structured dialogue between a sufferer and a pious friend debating divine justice
- The sufferer argues that the gods created humanity flawed ("Narru [the god Enlil], king of the gods, who created mankind, majestic Ea, who pinched off their clay, the queen who fashioned them, lady Mami, gave twisted speech to the human race; with lies and not truth they endowed them forever") — making injustice intrinsic to the human condition rather than a deviation from it
- The friend offers traditional piety (the gods reward virtue eventually), but the dialogue ends ambiguously — neither position is fully endorsed, leaving the theodicy problem unresolved
- **Counter-Argument:** Whether the text represents genuine philosophical skepticism or is itself a pious exercise (demonstrating faith's endurance through questioning) is debated among Assyriologists

### 2.2 The Dialogue of Pessimism
- The **Dialogue of Pessimism** (Arad-mitanguranni, c. 1000 BCE) — a comic-philosophical dialogue between a master and his slave — consists of ten episodes in which the master proposes an action ("Slave, obey me!" "I will go to the palace"), the slave enthusiastically supports it with reasons, then the master reverses course ("I will not go to the palace"), and the slave equally enthusiastically supports the opposite position
- Actions considered and rejected include: visiting the palace, eating, hunting, marrying, litigation, rebellion, religious sacrifice, lending money, performing charity, and (in the chilling final exchange) killing oneself
- Interpretations range from nihilistic philosophy (nothing has intrinsic value; all arguments are equally valid and invalid) to social satire (mocking sycophantic servants) to absurdist humor — the text has been compared to modern existentialist and absurdist literature (Camus, Beckett)
- **Counter-Argument:** Reading the text as "ancient nihilism" may impose modern philosophical categories on a culture with very different intellectual frameworks — it may instead be a scribal exercise in rhetorical argumentation or a parody of wisdom literature

### 2.3 Connection to Biblical Wisdom Literature
- Structural and thematic parallels between Mesopotamian and biblical wisdom are well-documented: *Ludlul* ↔ Job (righteous suffering, divine inscrutability); Babylonian Theodicy ↔ Job's dialogues (debate format, divine justice questions); Sumerian proverbs ↔ Proverbs (practical wisdom, father-son instruction); Dialogue of Pessimism ↔ Ecclesiastes (vanity of human endeavor)
- Direct literary dependence is debated — scholars propose that Israelite wisdom writers knew Mesopotamian texts (given centuries of cultural contact and the exile in Babylon), while others argue for common thematic responses to universal human problems rather than direct borrowing
- The Sumerian composition *A Man and His God* (c. 2000 BCE) — an even earlier "righteous sufferer" text — extends the tradition's antiquity well beyond *Ludlul*

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Mesopotamian Proto-Philosophy
- Scholars (Van De Mieroop, Bottéro) argue that Mesopotamian wisdom literature constitutes a genuine philosophical tradition — systematic reflection on ethics, epistemology, and theodicy — that should be recognized alongside Greek philosophy rather than dismissed as "pre-philosophical" mythology
- Whether the Babylonian Theodicy's systematic argumentation and the Dialogue of Pessimism's radical skepticism qualify as "philosophy" depends on how philosophy is defined — if systematic reason-giving about fundamental questions counts, Mesopotamia has a strong claim; if the Greek tradition of abstract conceptual analysis is required, the label may not fit

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 "Biblical Wisdom Literature Is Wholly Original"
- **[DEBUNKED]** Claims that biblical wisdom traditions (Job, Proverbs, Ecclesiastes) arose independently without any connection to earlier Near Eastern wisdom literature are untenable given the extensive textual, structural, and thematic parallels documented by over a century of comparative scholarship — while direct dependence on specific texts is debated, the broader cultural tradition is clearly continuous
