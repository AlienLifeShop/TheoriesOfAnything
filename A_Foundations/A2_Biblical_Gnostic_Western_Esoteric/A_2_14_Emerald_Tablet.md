## QUICK SUMMARY

The **Emerald Tablet** (*Tabula Smaragdina*) is a short cryptic text — ranging from 12 to 15 lines depending on the version — attributed to the legendary **Hermes Trismegistus** ("Thrice-Great Hermes") that became the foundational document of Western alchemy and Hermetic philosophy. Its most famous dictum, **"That which is above is like that which is below, and that which is below is like that which is above"** ("as above, so below"), encapsulates the principle of **macrocosm-microcosm correspondence** — the idea that the universe's structure is mirrored at every scale from the cosmic to the atomic. The earliest known version appears in Arabic in the works attributed to **Jābir ibn Hayyān** (Geber, 8th century CE) and in the *Kitāb Sirr al-Khalīqa* ("Book of the Secret of Creation") attributed to **Balīnās** (Apollonius of Tyana, though likely a 9th-century pseudepigraphon). The text was translated into Latin by Hugo of Santalla (12th century), and its influence extended through medieval alchemy, Renaissance Hermeticism, Rosicrucian thought, and modern esotericism. Isaac Newton produced his own translation c. 1680. Despite its brevity, the Emerald Tablet has been called "the most important single document in the history of Western alchemy."

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Textual History
- **No ancient original exists** — the Emerald Tablet is not found in the Corpus Hermeticum, the Greek Hermetica, or any Greco-Egyptian source
- The earliest known text appears in Arabic:
  - The *Kitāb Sirr al-Khalīqa wa Ṣan'at al-Ṭabī'a* ("Book of the Secret of Creation and the Art of Nature"), attributed to Balīnās (Apollonius of Tyana), likely composed in the 8th–9th century CE — includes the Tablet as an inset narrative (Weisser 1979)
  - Works attributed to **Jābir ibn Hayyān** (Geber, c. 721–815 CE) reference and quote the Tablet — making Jābirian corpus the earliest datable context (Kraus 1942)
- The Latin translation by **Hugo of Santalla** (c. 1140 CE) became the standard version circulating in medieval Europe — found in numerous alchemical manuscripts
- **Isaac Newton** produced an English translation (c. 1680) preserved among his alchemical papers at King's College, Cambridge — Newton's engagement with alchemy was extensive, comprising over a million words of manuscript (Dobbs 1975)

### 1.2 The Text Itself
- The full text is remarkably brief — the standard Latin version:
  - "Verum, sine mendacio, certum et verissimum: Quod est inferius est sicut quod est superius, et quod est superius est sicut quod est inferius, ad perpetranda miracula rei unius..."
  - ("True, without falsehood, certain and most true: That which is below is like that which is above, and that which is above is like that which is below, to accomplish the miracles of the One Thing...")
- The text describes:
  - The unity of all things originating from a single source (the "One Thing")
  - The principle of correspondence — above mirrors below
  - The alchemical process: separation and recombination (the father = Sun, the mother = Moon, carried by the Wind, nursed by the Earth)
  - The claim that the author (Hermes) possesses "the three parts of the wisdom of the whole world"

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 The As-Above-So-Below Principle
- The macrocosm-microcosm correspondence principle articulated in the Tablet has deep roots in multiple traditions:
  - **Platonic tradition**: The *Timaeus* describes the cosmos as a living being whose structure is reflected in the human body
  - **Stoic physics**: The concept of *sympatheia* — universal interconnection between all parts of the cosmos
  - **Hindu philosophy**: *Yathā piṇḍe tathā brahmāṇḍe* ("as in the microcosm, so in the macrocosm")
  - **Chinese thought**: The human body as a microcosm of heaven and earth in traditional Chinese medicine
- The Emerald Tablet crystallizes this widespread philosophical idea into its most concentrated, influential formulation (Principe 2013)

### 2.2 Influence on Western Alchemy
- The Tablet served as the theoretical cornerstone of Western alchemy from the 12th through 17th centuries:
  - The "One Thing" was identified with the *prima materia* (first matter) from which all substances derive
  - The process described (separation, purification, recombination) was read as the formula for the *opus magnum* (Great Work) — the transmutation of base metals into gold and/or the creation of the Philosopher's Stone
  - European alchemists from Roger Bacon to Albertus Magnus to Paracelsus referenced the Tablet as authoritative teaching (Holmyard 1957; Principe 2013)

### 2.3 The "Hermes Trismegistus" Attribution
- Hermes Trismegistus is a syncretic figure combining the Greek god **Hermes** (messenger, guide of souls, patron of writing) with the Egyptian god **Thoth** (ibis-headed god of wisdom, writing, magic, and the moon)
- The historical reality: "Hermes Trismegistus" is a literary persona under whose name a vast body of philosophical, magical, astrological, and alchemical texts were composed by multiple anonymous authors between c. 100 BCE and 300 CE (and later in Arabic)
- The Emerald Tablet's attribution to Hermes is pseudepigraphic — the text was likely composed in the Islamic golden age but draws on genuine Greco-Egyptian philosophical concepts (Copenhaver 1992)

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Egyptian Temple Origin
- The Arabic *Kitāb Sirr al-Khalīqa* frames the discovery narratively: Balīnās (Apollonius) finds the Tablet in a vault beneath a statue of Hermes, held in the hands of a corpse seated on a golden throne
- This literary framing has led some to speculate that the Tablet preserves genuine ancient Egyptian temple teaching transmitted through Hellenistic intermediaries into Arabic
- No Egyptian-language precursor has been found, and the literary framing is a standard Arabic-era genre convention (Holmyard 1923; Ruska 1926)

### 3.2 Encoded Chemical Knowledge
- Historians of science have argued that the Tablet's language encodes genuine chemical processes (distillation, sublimation, calcination) in allegorical form
- While medieval alchemists certainly read it this way, whether the original author(s) intended chemical specificity or purely philosophical/spiritual teaching is debated

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 The Tablet as Literal Emerald Artifact
- **[LEGENDARY]** There is no evidence that an actual emerald tablet ever existed — the name refers to the text's content (the "emerald" symbolizing the Green Lion or viriditas of alchemical transformation), not a physical object
- Claims of a literal gem-stone tablet are post-medieval embellishments
