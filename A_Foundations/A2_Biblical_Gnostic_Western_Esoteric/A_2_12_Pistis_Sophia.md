## QUICK SUMMARY

The **Pistis Sophia** ("Faith Wisdom") is a major Gnostic text preserved in the **Askew Codex** (British Library, Add. MS 5114), a 4th–5th century CE Coptic manuscript containing four books of post-resurrection teachings attributed to the risen Jesus to his disciples. Unlike the brief, enigmatic sayings of the Gospel of Thomas, Pistis Sophia presents an elaborate cosmic drama: the aeon **Sophia** (Wisdom) falls from her place in the heavens through deception by a false light, is trapped in the chaos below the 13th aeon, and is progressively rescued by the risen Christ through 13 "repentances" (hymnic prayers modeled on the Psalms). The text features the most prominent role for **Mary Magdalene** in any ancient Christian document — she asks 39 of the 46 recorded questions and is praised by Jesus as superior in understanding to the male disciples. Books 1–3 detail the cosmological geography of aeons, archons, and the Treasury of Light; Book 4 (likely an earlier, separate composition) describes post-mortem punishments and mercies, functioning as a Gnostic eschatological manual.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Manuscript and Textual History
- The sole surviving manuscript is the **Askew Codex**, a parchment codex purchased by the British Museum from the heirs of Dr. Anthony Askew in 1785 — making it one of the earliest Gnostic texts to reach European scholarship
- The codex is written in **Sahidic Coptic** with some Subachmimic dialect features, dated paleographically to the late 4th or early 5th century CE
- The text was first translated into Latin by M.G. Schwartze (published posthumously 1851) and into English by G.R.S. Mead (1896, revised 1921)
- The original language of composition was almost certainly **Greek**, translated into Coptic — the Greek original is lost
- Carl Schmidt's critical edition (1925, revised by MacDermot 1978) remains the standard scholarly text

### 1.2 Structure and Content
- **Books 1–3** (the "Pistis Sophia" proper, c. 250–300 CE):
  - Set 11 years after the resurrection, Jesus teaches disciples on the Mount of Olives
  - He describes how Sophia fell from the 13th aeon by following a false light created by the archon **Authades** (Self-Willed)
  - Sophia utters 13 repentances (penitential hymns closely modeled on the Psalms of David)
  - Jesus, as the "First Mystery," descends through the aeons to rescue Sophia and restore her to her place
  - The text includes detailed cosmological geography: the 24 invisibles, the 12 aeons, the 13th aeon, the regions of chaos, the Treasury of Light, and the Ineffable
- **Book 4** (likely an earlier, separate text, c. 200–250 CE):
  - Post-mortem fates of righteous and unrighteous souls
  - Detailed descriptions of punishments administered by archonic demons and the conditions for mercy
  - Instructions for ritual practices including baptisms of fire, water, and the Holy Spirit

### 1.3 Mary Magdalene's Prominence
- Mary Magdalene dominates the question-and-answer format: she asks **39 of 46 questions** and provides interpretations of Sophia's hymns
- Jesus explicitly praises her: "Mariam, thou blessed one, whom I will complete in all the mysteries of the height, speak openly, thou art she whose heart is more directed to the Kingdom of Heaven than all thy brothers" (Chapter 17)
- The male disciple Peter protests: "My Lord, we are not able to bear with this woman, for she takes our opportunity and does not allow any of us to speak" (Chapter 36) — mirroring the canonical tension and suggesting awareness of gender politics in early Christian communities
- King (2003) and De Boer (2004) analyze this as evidence for traditions honoring Mary Magdalene's authority in Gnostic communities

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Cosmological System
- Pistis Sophia presents one of the most detailed Gnostic cosmological maps:
  - The **Ineffable** (ultimate divine source) → the **First Mystery** (Jesus in cosmic role) → the **Treasury of Light** (24 invisibles, 12 aeons)
  - Below the 12 aeons: the **13th aeon** (Sophia's original place, ruled by the 24 archons)
  - Below: the regions of **chaos** where Sophia falls — populated by hostile archonic forces
  - The "Outer Darkness" as ultimate punishment zone, divided into dragon-ruled compartments
- This system is partially compatible with but distinct from the Valentinian and Sethian Gnostic systems found at Nag Hammadi — Pistis Sophia may represent an independent Gnostic tradition or a late synthesis

### 2.2 Psalm Reinterpretation
- Sophia's 13 repentances are structured as Gnostic reinterpretations of the Hebrew Psalms — each Psalm cited by the text is recontextualized as Sophia's prayer from within the chaos:
  - Psalm 69 ("Save me, O God, for the waters are come in unto my soul") becomes Sophia's cry from the watery chaos below the aeons
  - Psalm 30 ("I will extol thee, O Lord, for thou hast lifted me up") becomes Sophia's thanksgiving after each stage of rescue
- This exegetical method reveals how Gnostic communities appropriated and reinterpreted existing Jewish scripture (Franzmann 1996)

### 2.3 Sophia Mythology and the Feminine Divine
- The Sophia figure unifies several strands of ancient thought:
  - Jewish Wisdom tradition (Proverbs 8, Wisdom of Solomon) where Sophia is personified as a feminine divine attribute
  - Gnostic cosmological drama where Sophia's fall explains the creation of the material world
  - The "fallen and redeemed feminine" archetype analyzed by feminist scholars of Gnosticism (King 2003)
- Pistis Sophia's treatment is distinctively optimistic — Sophia is ultimately restored, unlike some Gnostic systems where her fall is more permanent

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Ritual Context
- The detailed descriptions of baptisms, seals, and mystery rituals in Book 4 suggest that Pistis Sophia may have served as a **liturgical manual** for an active Gnostic community
- Whether the described rituals were actually performed or are purely literary constructs cannot be determined from the text alone
- The "mystery of the Ineffable," "baptism of fire," and successive initiations parallel aspects of later mystery traditions and have been compared to the initiatory structures described in Iamblichus's *De Mysteriis*

### 3.2 Egyptian Influence
- The cosmological geography (multiple underworld compartments, weighing of souls, dragon guardians) has been compared to Egyptian afterlife literature — the Books of the Netherworld, the judgment scene in the Book of the Dead
- A direct compositional link to Egyptian temple theology is possible given the text's likely Egyptian provenance (Coptic-speaking community, possibly Theban region)

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Pistis Sophia as Direct Teaching of Jesus
- **[THEOLOGICAL CLAIM]** The text's claim to record actual post-resurrection teachings of Jesus is a pseudepigraphic convention — the composition dates to 200–300 CE, approximately 170–270 years after the events described
