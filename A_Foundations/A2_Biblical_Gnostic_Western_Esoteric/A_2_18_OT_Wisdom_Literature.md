## QUICK SUMMARY

**Old Testament Wisdom Literature** (*Ḥokmah*) encompasses three canonical books — **Proverbs**, **Job**, and **Ecclesiastes** (Qoheleth) — and, in the broader canon, **Sirach** (Ecclesiasticus) and the **Wisdom of Solomon**. These texts represent a distinctive tradition within Israelite religion focused not on covenant history or prophetic revelation but on **practical observation, moral reasoning, and philosophical reflection** about the created order, human experience, and the relationship between righteousness and prosperity. **Proverbs** (c. 10th–5th century BCE composition range) presents collections of maxims attributed to Solomon on virtuous living and introduces the figure of **personified Wisdom** (*Ḥokmah* / Sophia) as God's companion at creation (Proverbs 8:22–31). **Job** (c. 6th–4th century BCE) is the ancient world's most sophisticated **theodicy** — a philosophical drama challenging the doctrine that suffering always results from sin. **Ecclesiastes** (c. 3rd century BCE) is a radical philosophical meditation on the futility (*hevel*) of human striving and the inscrutability of divine purpose. Together these works connect Israelite thought to the broader **Ancient Near Eastern wisdom tradition** (Egyptian *maat*, Mesopotamian dispute poems, Aramaic wisdom) and anticipate philosophical questions central to later Greek, Christian, and Islamic thought.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Proverbs: Structure and Date
- **Proverbs** (*Mishlei*) is a composite text containing at least seven distinct collections:
  1. Chapters 1–9: Extended discourses on wisdom and folly (including personified Wisdom), likely the **latest** section (post-exilic, 6th–5th century BCE)
  2. Chapters 10:1–22:16: "Proverbs of Solomon" — short couplets, likely the **oldest** core (pre-exilic, possibly 10th–8th century BCE)
  3. 22:17–24:22: "Sayings of the Wise" — closely paralleling the **Egyptian Instruction of Amenemope** (c. 1200 BCE), demonstrating cross-cultural wisdom exchange
  4. 24:23–34: Additional "Sayings of the Wise"
  5. Chapters 25–29: "Proverbs of Solomon copied by the men of Hezekiah" (late 8th century BCE setting)
  6. 30:1–33: "Words of Agur"
  7. 31:1–9: "Words of King Lemuel" (possibly of Massa/Arabian origin); 31:10–31: Acrostic poem on the "Woman of Valor"
- The parallel between Proverbs 22:17–23:11 and the **Instruction of Amenemope** was identified by Budge (1923) and is now universally accepted — it demonstrates direct Egyptian literary influence on Israelite wisdom

### 1.2 Job: Structure and Literary Form
- **Job** comprises a **prose frame** (chapters 1–2, 42:7–17) — likely an older folk tale — surrounding an elaborate **poetic dialogue** (3:1–42:6) of disputed date (most scholars favor 6th–4th century BCE)
- Structure of the poetic body:
  - Job's opening lament (ch. 3)
  - Three cycles of dialogue with three friends — Eliphaz, Bildad, Zophar (chs. 4–27)
  - Wisdom poem (ch. 28 — possibly an independent insertion)
  - Job's final oath of innocence (chs. 29–31)
  - Elihu speeches (chs. 32–37 — widely considered a later addition)
  - **Yahweh's speeches from the whirlwind** (chs. 38–41) — the divine response
  - Job's submission (42:1–6)
  - Prose epilogue with restoration (42:7–17)
- The central theological question: **Is there disinterested righteousness?** Can a person serve God without expectation of reward?

### 1.3 Ecclesiastes: Authorship and Date
- **Ecclesiastes** (*Qoheleth*, "The Assembler/Preacher") presents itself as the words of a king in Jerusalem (1:1, 1:12) — traditionally identified with Solomon
- **Actual date**: Linguistic evidence (Aramaisms, late Hebrew features, possible Greek loanwords) places composition in the **3rd century BCE**, making it one of the latest OT books — possibly Hellenistic-period Jerusalem (Seow 1997)
- The book's key term is ***hevel*** (הֶבֶל) — traditionally "vanity," more accurately **"breath," "vapor," "absurdity," "transience"** — applied repeatedly to all human endeavor: "hevel havalim, all is hevel" (1:2)

### 1.4 Ancient Near Eastern Parallels
- Israelite wisdom traditions participate in a broader **Ancient Near Eastern wisdom network**:
  - **Egypt**: Instruction of Ptahhotep, Instruction of Amenemope, Instruction of Ani — moral maxims embedded in an *maat* (cosmic order) framework
  - **Mesopotamia**: Sumerian Proverbs, Babylonian *Ludlul Bēl Nēmeqi* ("I Will Praise the Lord of Wisdom" — a "Babylonian Job"), *Dialogue of Pessimism* (paralleling Ecclesiastes)
  - **Aramaic**: *Words of Ahiqar* (7th–5th century BCE) — wisdom tale with close parallels to Proverbs
- These parallels demonstrate that **wisdom literature was an international genre** shared across the ancient Near East, not unique to Israel

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Personified Wisdom (Proverbs 8)
- In Proverbs 8:22–31, Wisdom (*Ḥokmah*) speaks as a **cosmic figure** present at creation:
  - "Yahweh created/possessed me at the beginning of his way" (8:22 — the verb *qānānī* is debated: "created," "acquired," or "possessed")
  - "Before the mountains were settled… I was brought forth" (8:25)
  - "I was beside him as a master craftsman (*ʾāmôn*)" (8:30 — also debated: "craftsman," "nursling," or "faithful one")
  - "Rejoicing in his inhabited world, and delighting in the children of men" (8:31)
- This passage became foundational for:
  - **Jewish Sophia theology** (Sirach 24, Wisdom of Solomon 7–8)
  - **Christian Logos theology** — John 1:1–3 draws on wisdom-creation language; the Arian controversy centered on whether Proverbs 8:22 proved Christ was "created"
  - **Kabbalistic Ḥokhmah** — the second sefirah in the Kabbalistic tree

### 2.2 Job's Challenge to Retribution Theology
- Job directly challenges the **Deuteronomistic theology of retribution** (righteousness → blessing, sin → punishment) that dominates much of the Hebrew Bible
- The friends (especially Eliphaz) represent orthodox retribution theology; Job insists his suffering is unjust
- Yahweh's speeches from the whirlwind (chs. 38–41) **neither affirm retribution theology nor explain Job's suffering** — instead, they display the radical otherness and incomprehensibility of divine creative power
- This theological move has been described as the Hebrew Bible's most radical statement of **negative theology** (Newsom 2003)

### 2.3 Qoheleth's Intellectual World
- Debate continues concerning whether Ecclesiastes reflects **Greek philosophical influence** (particularly Epicureanism) or represents an indigenous development of Israelite skeptical wisdom
- The *carpe diem* passages (2:24, 3:12–13, 5:18, 8:15, 9:7–9) — "eat, drink, and find enjoyment in your toil" — parallel both Epicurean ethics and the **Gilgamesh tavern-keeper's advice** (Tablet X Old Babylonian version)
- Most scholars now see Qoheleth as working within the ANE wisdom tradition with possible but not necessary Greek influence (Fox 1999)

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Pre-Israelite Origins of Job
- The land of **Uz** (Job 1:1) and the non-Israelite names of Job and his friends have led scholars to propose that the Job story originated in an **Edomite** or **Transjordanian** wisdom tradition, adopted and adapted by Israelite scribes
- This would explain the text's unusual vocabulary and theological perspective, but definitive evidence is lacking

### 3.2 Wisdom as Goddess Figure
- Scholars (e.g., Lang 1986) argue that personified Wisdom in Proverbs 1–9 preserves traces of a **goddess figure** — possibly related to the Canaanite Asherah or Egyptian Maat/Isis
- The evidence is suggestive but inconclusive — personification may be a literary device rather than evidence of suppressed goddess worship

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Solomonic Authorship of All Three Books
- **[LEGENDARY]** Traditional attribution to Solomon (Proverbs, Ecclesiastes, Song of Songs) is a literary convention — Ecclesiastes's language is demonstrably post-exilic (3rd century BCE), and Job shows no Solomonic connection
