## QUICK SUMMARY

The **Jain Agamas** (*Āgama*, "tradition/scripture") are the canonical scriptures of **Jainism**, one of the world's oldest continuously practiced religions. The teachings are attributed to **Mahāvīra** (*Vardhamāna*, c. 599–527 BCE or c. 540–468 BCE), the 24th and final *Tīrthaṅkara* ("ford-maker," spiritual teacher who has crossed the ocean of rebirth), though Jain tradition holds that the teachings are eternal and have been rediscovered by each Tīrthaṅkara in cyclical time. The canonical collections differ between the **Śvetāmbara** ("white-clad") and **Digambara** ("sky-clad") sects: the Śvetāmbara recognize a canon of **45 texts** organized into several groups (11 *Aṅgas*, 12 *Upāṅgas*, prakirnakas, cheda-sūtras, and mūla-sūtras), while the Digambara reject this canon as corrupt, relying instead on later compilations such as the *Ṣaṭkhaṇḍāgama* and *Kasāyapāhuda*. The most important texts include the **Ācārāṅga Sūtra** (on ascetic conduct and Mahāvīra's biography), the **Sūtrakṛtāṅga** (on doctrinal positions and refutation of rival philosophies), and the **Tattvārtha Sūtra** (a systematic treatise accepted by both sects). Central doctrines include **ahiṃsā** (radical non-violence extended to all living beings), **anekāntavāda** (many-sidedness of reality), **aparigraha** (non-possessiveness), and a sophisticated **karma theory** in which karma is a subtle material substance adhering to the soul (*jīva*).

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Canonical Structure (Śvetāmbara)
- The Śvetāmbara canon was formally compiled at the **Council of Valabhi** (c. 454 or 514 CE) in Gujarat under Devardhi Gaṇin
- The canon is organized into:
  - **11 Aṅgas** ("Limbs"): The primary canonical texts, said to derive from the 14-*pūrva* teachings of Mahāvīra. The original 12th Aṅga (*Dṛṣṭivāda*) is regarded as lost by all traditions
  - **12 Upāṅgas** ("Sub-limbs"): Supplementary cosmological and narrative texts
  - **10 Prakirṇakas**: Miscellaneous texts on monastic practice and devotion
  - **6 Chedasūtras**: Texts on monastic discipline and penance
  - **4 Mūlasūtras**: Foundational texts for monastic life, including the *Uttarādhyayana Sūtra*
  - **2 Cūlikasūtras**: Appendix texts on logic and cosmography
- Language: **Ardhamāgadhī Prākrit** — the canonical language of Śvetāmbara Jain scriptures, related to but distinct from the Pali of Buddhist texts

### 1.2 Key Texts
- **Ācārāṅga Sūtra** (Āyāraṃga Sutta): The first Aṅga — contains Mahāvīra's biography, ascetic practices, and the foundational teaching on non-violence: "All beings are fond of life; they like pleasure and hate pain, they shun destruction and like to live, they long to live. To all, life is dear" (1.4.1)
- **Sūtrakṛtāṅga** (Sūyagaḍaṃga): The second Aṅga — surveys and critiques rival philosophical schools (Buddhism, Ājīvikism, materialism, Brahmanism), providing valuable evidence for the intellectual landscape of ancient India
- **Tattvārtha Sūtra** (c. 2nd–5th century CE, attributed to **Umāsvāti/Umāsvāmī**): The only Jain text accepted by both Śvetāmbara and Digambara — a systematic treatise in 10 chapters covering 7 fundamental categories (*tattvas*): soul (*jīva*), non-soul (*ajīva*), influx of karma (*āsrava*), bondage (*bandha*), stoppage (*saṃvara*), shedding of karma (*nirjarā*), and liberation (*mokṣa*)

### 1.3 Core Doctrines
- **Ahiṃsā** (non-violence): The supreme ethical principle — extended to all forms of life including microorganisms, plants, and elemental beings. Jain monks sweep the path before walking, strain drinking water, and wear mouth-cloths to avoid harming insects
- **Anekāntavāda** (many-sidedness): Reality is complex and multi-faceted — no single perspective can capture the whole truth. Formalized as **syādvāda** (conditional predication): any statement about reality must be qualified with "in some respect" (*syāt*)
- **Karma as material substance**: Unlike Hindu and Buddhist conceptions of karma as a moral law or intentional force, Jain doctrine describes karma as **fine, subtle matter** (*karma-pudgala*) that physically adheres to the soul, weighing it down and preventing liberation
- **Aparigraha** (non-possessiveness): Attachment to material things binds karma to the soul — monks practice total renunciation; laypeople practice proportional restraint

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Dating and Historical Context
- Mahāvīra's dates are debated: Jain tradition places his death (*nirvāṇa*) at **527 BCE**; modern scholarship generally prefers **c. 468 BCE** (Dundas 2002)
- The Ācārāṅga's oldest portions may date to the **4th–3rd century BCE** — among the earliest Indian religious texts after the Vedas (Bronkhorst 2007)
- The relationship between Jainism and Buddhism is significant — both emerged from the **Śramaṇa movement** of the 6th–5th century BCE, challenging Brahmanical orthodoxy with emphasis on asceticism, karma, and liberation through individual effort

### 2.2 Digambara Canon
- The Digambara tradition holds that the **original Āgama texts were lost** during a great famine approximately 300 years after Mahāvīra — they reject the Śvetāmbara canon as a later compilation
- Digambara canonical authority rests on:
  - **Ṣaṭkhaṇḍāgama** (6 parts) by Dharasena → Puṣpadanta and Bhūtabali (c. 2nd century CE)
  - **Kasāyapāhuda** (Treatise on Passions) — another early Digambara text
  - **Tattvārtha Sūtra** — accepted by both sects

### 2.3 Cosmology
- The Jain universe (*loka*) has a fixed, eternal structure:
  - **Upper world** (*ūrdhva-loka*): Heavens inhabited by celestial beings
  - **Middle world** (*madhya-loka*): Earth and surrounding concentric continents and oceans — includes **2.5 continents** where humans live
  - **Lower world** (*adho-loka*): Hells of increasing torment
  - **Siddha-śilā**: At the apex — the realm of liberated souls, permanently free from rebirth
- The universe is **uncreated and eternal** — there is no creator god in Jainism. The cosmos operates through natural laws of karma and cosmic cycles
- Time moves in **avasarpiṇī** (descending) and **utsarpiṇī** (ascending) half-cycles, each containing six *aras* (ages) of declining/improving quality

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Antiquity Beyond Mahāvīra
- Jain tradition holds that 23 Tīrthaṅkaras preceded Mahāvīra — the first, **Ṛṣabhadeva** (Ādinātha), lived millions of years ago in mythic time
- The historical Pārśvanātha (23rd Tīrthaṅkara, c. 8th century BCE) may have been a real historical figure whose ascetic community Mahāvīra reformed — this would place Jain origins before Buddhism and the śramaṇa movement's conventional dating (Jaini 1979)

### 3.2 Influence on Indian Philosophical Methods
- Jain contributions to Indian logic (*anekāntavāda* and *syādvāda*) may have influenced the development of **Mādhyamaka** Buddhist logic and later **Nyāya** analytical methods — but the direction and extent of influence remains debated
