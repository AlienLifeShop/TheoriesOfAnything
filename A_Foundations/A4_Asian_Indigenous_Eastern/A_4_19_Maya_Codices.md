## QUICK SUMMARY

The **Maya codices** are the only surviving pre-Columbian books from the Maya civilization — folding-screen manuscripts made of bark paper (*huun*) covered in lime plaster and painted with hieroglyphic texts and illustrations by trained scribes. Of potentially thousands that once existed, only **four** survived the systematic destruction during the Spanish Conquest, most infamously Bishop Diego de Landa's auto-da-fé at Maní in 1562. The **Dresden Codex** (39 leaves, c. 11th–12th century CE) is the finest and most important, containing Venus and eclipse tables of extraordinary astronomical precision; the **Madrid Codex** (56 leaves, the longest surviving) focuses on ritual almanacs and divination; the **Paris Codex** (11 surviving leaves) contains prophecy cycles and zodiacal material; and the **Grolier Codex** (now Mexico Codex, 10 surviving leaves, authenticated 2016) contains a Venus almanac. These manuscripts represent the pinnacle of pre-Columbian intellectual achievement in astronomy, mathematics, and writing, and their near-total destruction constitutes one of history's greatest losses of knowledge.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 The Dresden Codex
- Housed at the Sächsische Landesbibliothek (SLUB) in Dresden, Germany — 39 leaves (78 pages), accordion-folded, measuring approximately 3.56 meters unfolded
- Acquired by the Royal Library of Dresden in 1739 from a private collection in Vienna — provenance before that is unknown but likely involves 16th-century Spanish collection
- Content includes:
  - **Venus tables** (pages 46–50): Tracking Venus's synodic cycle of 584 days with corrections accumulated over centuries — accurate to within 2 hours per 481 years (Aveni 2001)
  - **Eclipse tables** (pages 51–58): Predicting lunar eclipse possibilities using a 405-lunation (11,960-day) cycle
  - **Mars tables** (pages 43a–45): Only recently recognized as tracking Mars's 780-day synodic period (Bricker & Bricker 2011)
  - Ritual almanacs, deity sequences, agricultural calendars, and the New Year ceremonies
- Radiocarbon dating of the bark paper places manufacture at c. 1210–1250 CE, though the content likely copies much older material (Landa 2013)

### 1.2 The Madrid Codex
- Housed at the Museo de América in Madrid — 56 leaves (112 pages), the longest surviving Maya codex at approximately 6.82 meters
- Also called the **Tro-Cortesianus Codex** (originally thought to be two separate manuscripts reunited in 1888)
- Content is predominantly **ritual-divinatory**: almanacs for beekeeping, hunting, weaving, planting, rain ceremonies, and new year rituals — a practical priestly handbook
- Stylistically less refined than the Dresden Codex; dated to the Late Postclassic period (c. 1250–1450 CE)
- Provenanced through isotopic analysis to the Petén lowlands of Guatemala (Coe & Kerr 1998)

### 1.3 The Paris Codex
- Housed at the Bibliothèque nationale de France — 11 surviving leaves in deteriorated condition (edges heavily damaged)
- Contains:
  - **Katun prophecies**: Cyclical historical-prophetic texts associated with 7,200-day periods
  - A possible **zodiac or constellation band** (pages 23–24): 13 animal figures hanging from a sky-band, interpreted as a Maya zodiacal system corresponding to constellations along the ecliptic (Love 1994)
  - New Year ceremonies and deity almanacs
- The zodiacal section, if confirmed, would represent the only known indigenous Mesoamerican zodiac system

### 1.4 The Grolier/Mexico Codex
- **Authenticated in 2016** after decades of controversy — a panel of scholars at Brown University confirmed it as the oldest surviving Maya codex (manufactured c. 1021–1154 CE based on radiocarbon dating) (Coe et al. 2016)
- Contains a 104-year Venus almanac on 10 surviving leaves
- Originally surfaced on the antiquities market in the 1960s (provenance unclear), leading to decades of forgery accusations now resolved by multidisciplinary analysis

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 The Scale of Destruction
- Bishop Diego de Landa ordered the burning of Maya books at Maní, Yucatán, on **July 12, 1562**, writing: "We found a large number of books in these characters, and as they contained nothing in which there were not to be seen superstition and lies of the devil, we burned them all"
- Estimates of the number of codices destroyed range from dozens to thousands — given that every major Maya city likely had scribal libraries, the loss may represent the bulk of an entire civilization's accumulated written knowledge
- Ironically, Landa's own *Relación de las cosas de Yucatán* (written c. 1566) preserved crucial information about Maya writing, calendar, and customs — including the "Landa alphabet" that later proved essential for decipherment (Coe 1992)

### 2.2 Astronomical Precision
- The Venus tables in the Dresden Codex demonstrate that Maya astronomers tracked Venus's 584-day synodic period and accumulated correction factors over centuries:
  - The tables cover 5 × 584 = 2,920 days (= 8 × 365 solar years = 8 haab), after which Venus returns to approximately the same position relative to the sun
  - The Maya correction mechanism (subtracting 8 days after 61 Venus cycles) achieves a cumulative error of only about 1 day per 6,000 years — comparable to or exceeding contemporary Old World astronomical tables
- This precision implies sustained multi-generational astronomical observation programs, likely managed by priestly specialists

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Additional Surviving Codices
- Persistent rumors and occasional claims suggest that additional Maya codices may survive in private collections, church archives (particularly in Spain and the Vatican), or undiscovered archaeological contexts
- The Grolier Codex's emergence from the antiquities market in the 1960s demonstrates that unknown manuscripts can surface, but no new codex has been authenticated since

### 3.2 Encoded Historical Records
- While the surviving codices are primarily astronomical-ritual in nature, Classic Maya inscriptions on monuments include extensive historical records — suggesting that destroyed codices may have contained dynastic histories, military accounts, and detailed narrative literature now irretrievably lost
