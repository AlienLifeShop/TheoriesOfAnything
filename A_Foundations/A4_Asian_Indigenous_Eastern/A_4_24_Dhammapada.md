## QUICK SUMMARY

The **Dhammapada** ("Verses of the Dharma/Teaching" or "Path of Dharma") is the most widely read and translated text of **Theravada Buddhism** — a collection of **423 verses** in 26 chapters (*vagga*), presenting the core ethical and psychological teachings attributed to the historical **Buddha Shakyamuni** (c. 5th century BCE). Preserved in the **Pali Canon** as part of the *Khuddaka Nikāya* (Collection of Minor Texts), the Dhammapada distills Buddhist doctrine into memorable, aphoristic poetry covering the nature of mind, karma, impermanence (*anicca*), suffering (*dukkha*), non-self (*anattā*), ethical conduct, mental discipline, and the path to liberation (*nibbāna*). The text exists in multiple recensions across Buddhist traditions: the **Pali Dhammapada**, the **Gāndhārī Dharmapada** (Khotan, c. 1st century CE), the **Patna Dharmapada** (hybrid Sanskrit), and the Chinese **Fa Ju Jing** (法句經, translated c. 224 CE). The existence of these independent versions demonstrates that the collection's core was transmitted from a very early period, likely within a century or two of the Buddha's death. The Dhammapada's influence extends across all Buddhist cultures and has been one of the primary vehicles through which Buddhist thought has engaged the modern Western world.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Textual History and Versions
- **Pali Dhammapada**: 423 verses in 26 chapters (*vagga*), preserved in the Pali Canon (Tipiṭaka) — the standard Theravada recension. The commentary (*Dhammapadaṭṭhakathā*) attributed to Buddhaghosa (5th century CE) provides narrative frames for each verse
- **Gāndhārī Dharmapada**: Discovered in the **Khotan** region (Central Asia), written in Gāndhārī Prakrit in Kharoṣṭhī script — dated paleographically to the **1st century CE**, making it one of the oldest surviving Buddhist manuscripts (Brough 1962)
  - Contains approximately 500 verses with significant overlap but different arrangement from the Pali version
- **Patna Dharmapada**: Discovered among manuscripts at the K.P. Jayaswal Research Institute in Patna — written in a hybrid Buddhist Sanskrit
- **Chinese Fa Ju Jing** (法句經): Translated by Vighna and Zhu Jiangyan in **224 CE** — contains 752 verses in 39 chapters, a substantially expanded version
- **Sanskrit Udānavarga**: An enlarged collection attributed to Dharmatrāta (c. 1st–2nd century CE), preserved in the Sarvāstivāda tradition — 1,000+ verses in 33 chapters
- The existence of multiple independent recensions demonstrates the text's antiquity and widespread use across early Buddhist schools

### 1.2 Structure
- The 26 chapters of the Pali Dhammapada are organized thematically:
  - Ch. 1 (Yamakavagga): Twin Verses — mind determines reality
  - Ch. 2 (Appamādavagga): Heedfulness (*appamāda*)
  - Ch. 3 (Cittavagga): The Mind
  - Ch. 11 (Jarāvagga): Old Age
  - Ch. 14 (Buddhavagga): The Buddha
  - Ch. 20 (Maggavagga): The Path
  - Ch. 24 (Taṇhāvagga): Craving
  - Ch. 26 (Brāhmaṇavagga): The True Brahmin (redefining spiritual attainment beyond caste)
- The verses are in **Pali gāthā meter** (primarily anuṣṭubh / śloka), designed for memorization and oral transmission

### 1.3 Central Teachings
- **Mind as primary** (1.1–2): "Mind is the forerunner of all actions. All deeds are led by mind, created by mind. If one speaks or acts with a corrupt mind, suffering follows as the wheel follows the hoof of the ox. If one speaks or acts with a pure mind, happiness follows like a shadow that never departs."
- **Impermanence** (*anicca*): All conditioned phenomena arise and pass away — recognizing this is the path to dispassion and liberation (chs. 11, 20)
- **Karma and ethical responsibility**: Actions have consequences that shape future experience — intentional action (*cetanā*) is karma (ch. 1, 9, 17)
- **The Middle Way**: Avoidance of extreme asceticism and extreme indulgence (implicit throughout, explicit in commentary)
- **Non-self** (*anattā*): The constructed nature of the person — "all phenomena are not-self" (v. 279)
- **Nibbāna**: The unconditioned state beyond suffering, craving, and rebirth — "nibbāna is the supreme happiness" (v. 203–204)

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Dating and Authenticity
- The Dhammapada is generally regarded as containing **some of the oldest Buddhist material**, though disentangling "original" Buddha-sayings from later additions is methodologically challenging
- K.R. Norman (1997) argues that many verses reflect very early linguistic layers of Pali and may preserve formulations from **within a century of the Buddha's death** (c. 400 BCE)
- The Gāndhārī version's 1st-century-CE paleographic date provides a firm *terminus ante quem* for the collection's main body — the collection must predate this by a considerable margin

### 2.2 Relationship to Other Early Buddhist Texts
- Many Dhammapada verses also appear in the **Sutta Nipāta**, **Theragāthā/Therīgāthā**, and **Saṃyutta Nikāya** — suggesting a common pool of early Buddhist gāthā material from which multiple collections were assembled
- The relationship between these parallel collections illuminates the process of **canon formation** in early Buddhism

### 2.3 Cross-Cultural Wisdom Parallels
- The Dhammapada has been compared to:
  - **Confucian Analects** (A_4_18): Aphoristic ethical wisdom attributed to a historical sage
  - **Proverbs** (A_2_18): Proverbial moral instruction within a religious framework
  - **Stoic philosophy**: Self-mastery, acceptance of impermanence, ethical self-discipline
- These parallels reflect common features of **axial-age wisdom traditions** (Jaspers 1953) rather than direct borrowing

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Pre-Buddhist Elements
- Some verses may incorporate pre-Buddhist **Śramaṇa** (ascetic wanderer) or even **Brahmanical** proverbial material, adapted to Buddhist doctrinal purposes
- Chapter 26 (Brāhmaṇavagga) explicitly redefines "Brahmin" as one who attains spiritual realization regardless of birth — this polemical redefinition suggests engagement with existing Brahmanical concepts

### 3.2 Greek Philosophical Contact
- Scholars have noted parallels between Dhammapada verses and Greek gnomological collections (Democritus, Epicurus) — raising the question of exchange via the Hellenistic kingdoms of Bactria and Gandhāra
- Evidence for direct literary transmission remains inconclusive
