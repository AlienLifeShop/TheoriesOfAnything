## QUICK SUMMARY

The *I Ching* (易經, *Yìjīng*, "Classic of Changes") is one of the oldest continuously used texts in human history, originating from Shang dynasty oracle bone divination (~1200 BCE) and formalized during the Western Zhou period (~1046–771 BCE). Its system of 64 hexagrams — each composed of six stacked lines, either solid (yang ☰) or broken (yin ☷) — constitutes a binary symbolic framework that predates Leibniz's binary arithmetic by over two millennia. The text functions simultaneously as a divination manual, a cosmological model, and a philosophical guide to change and decision-making. Its layered composition includes the base hexagram figures (attributed to Fu Xi and King Wen), the line statements (Duke of Zhou), and extensive commentaries known as the *Ten Wings* (traditionally attributed to Confucius). The *I Ching* remains foundational to Chinese philosophy, medicine, martial arts, and cosmology.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Origins in Shang Dynasty Divination
- **Oracle bone divination** (卜辞, *bǔcí*) at Yinxu (Anyang, Henan province) dates to ~1200 BCE and represents the earliest Chinese writing
- Shang diviners applied heat to turtle plastrons and ox scapulae, interpreting the resulting cracks as answers from ancestors and spirits
- Over **150,000 oracle bone fragments** have been recovered, with approximately 50,000 bearing inscriptions
- The binary crack patterns (single vs. double lines) likely influenced the development of the yin-yang line system used in the hexagrams
- David Keightley's *Sources of Shang History* (1978) remains the definitive archaeological study of oracle bone divination

### 1.2 Textual Layers and Composition
- The *I Ching* is a **composite text** assembled over approximately 800 years:

| Layer | Content | Traditional Attribution | Scholarly Dating |
|-------|---------|------------------------|-----------------|
| **Hexagram figures** | 64 hexagrams (6-line figures) | Fu Xi (mythical) / King Wen | ~1100–1000 BCE |
| **Guaci** (卦辞) | Hexagram judgments | King Wen (~1100 BCE) | ~1050–950 BCE |
| **Yaoci** (爻辞) | Line statements (384 total) | Duke of Zhou (~1040 BCE) | ~1000–800 BCE |
| **Ten Wings** (十翼) | Commentaries and appendices | Confucius (traditional) | ~400–200 BCE |

- The oldest textual stratum (hexagram and line statements) contains archaic Zhou-dynasty language, referencing historical events and rulers
- Edward Shaughnessy's translation of the Mawangdui silk manuscript *Yijing* (c. 168 BCE) reveals a substantially different hexagram order from the received King Wen sequence

### 1.3 The Hexagram System — Structure
- **Trigrams** (八卦, *bāguà*): 8 three-line figures representing fundamental natural phenomena:

| Trigram | Name | Chinese | Natural Image | Quality |
|---------|------|---------|---------------|---------|
| ☰ | Qian | 乾 | Heaven | Creative |
| ☷ | Kun | 坤 | Earth | Receptive |
| ☳ | Zhen | 震 | Thunder | Arousing |
| ☵ | Kan | 坎 | Water | Abysmal |
| ☶ | Gen | 艮 | Mountain | Stillness |
| ☴ | Xun | 巽 | Wind | Gentle |
| ☲ | Li | 離 | Fire | Clinging |
| ☱ | Dui | 兌 | Lake | Joyous |

- **Hexagrams**: 64 six-line figures (8 × 8 combinations of trigrams), representing all possible situations of change
- Each hexagram line can be **stable** or **changing** (old yang → young yin, old yin → young yang), creating a dynamic system that models transformation over time
- Total possible readings with changing lines: 4,096 (64 × 64) paired hexagram transitions

### 1.4 Divination Methods — Yarrow Stalk and Coin
- The **yarrow stalk method** (蓍草, *shīcǎo*): original procedure using 50 stalks, with a complex sorting algorithm producing unequal probabilities for each line type
- The **three-coin method**: later simplification (~2nd century CE) using three coins, yielding equal (25%) probabilities for the four line types
- The yarrow algorithm produces different probability distributions than the coin method:

| Line Type | Yarrow Probability | Coin Probability |
|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Old Yang (changing) | 3/16 (~18.75%) | 2/16 (12.5%) |
| Young Yang (stable) | 5/16 (~31.25%) | 6/16 (37.5%) |
| Young Yin (stable) | 7/16 (~43.75%) | 6/16 (37.5%) |
| Old Yin (changing) | 1/16 (~6.25%) | 2/16 (12.5%) |

- The asymmetry in yarrow stalk probabilities has been interpreted as encoding a cosmological bias toward stability and gradual change

### 1.5 Archaeological Confirmation — Mawangdui and Shanghai Museum Texts
- **Mawangdui silk manuscripts** (1973, tomb sealed 168 BCE): contain a version of the *Yijing* with a **different hexagram sequence** and variant line statements
- **Shanghai Museum bamboo strips** (acquired 1994, ~300 BCE): contain commentary on 43 hexagrams, predating the Ten Wings
- The Mawangdui hexagram order is arranged by upper trigram groupings, not the received King Wen sequence, indicating that the "original" ordering was not fixed in the Warring States period
- These discoveries confirm the text's fluidity prior to Han dynasty canonization

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Leibniz and the Binary Connection
- Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (1646–1716) developed **binary arithmetic** independently in 1679
- In 1703, the Jesuit missionary Joachim Bouvet sent Leibniz the Fu Xi hexagram arrangement (先天八卦, *Xiantiān bāguà*), organized by binary counting order
- Leibniz published "Explication de l'Arithmétique Binaire" (1703), recognizing that the hexagrams **correspond exactly to binary numbers 0–63** when broken = 0 and solid = 1
- Whether the ancient Chinese recognized binary mathematics as such, or whether the correspondence is structural rather than intentional, remains debated among sinologists
- The connection is structurally valid but does **not imply** the Chinese invented binary computation

### 2.2 The Ten Wings as Confucian Appropriation
- The *Ten Wings* (十翼, *Shíyì*) — including the *Xici zhuan* (Great Commentary), *Shuogua zhuan* (Discussion of Trigrams), and *Xugua zhuan* (Sequence of Hexagrams) — transformed the *Yijing* from an oracular manual into a philosophical cosmology
- Scholarly consensus holds that they are **not by Confucius** but by Confucian scholars of the Warring States period (~400–200 BCE)
- The *Xici zhuan* introduces key concepts: taiji (太極, "Supreme Ultimate"), the yin-yang cosmology of alternation, and the metaphysical notion that change itself is the fundamental principle of reality
- This Confucian overlay shaped how the text was read for two millennia, sometimes obscuring the earlier oracular layer

### 2.3 The King Wen Sequence — Structural Principles
- The 64 hexagrams in the received text follow the **King Wen sequence** (文王序, *Wénwáng xù*), which pairs hexagrams by inversion or complement
- Each pair of hexagrams (1–2, 3–4, 5–6, etc.) is related by turning the hexagram upside down; if the inversion produces the same hexagram, the complement (all lines switched) is used instead
- Only **8 palindromic hexagrams** are their own inversions; these are paired by complementation
- Whether the sequence encodes a deliberate mathematical or cosmological ordering beyond the inversion principle is contested

### 2.4 The Yijing in Traditional Chinese Medicine and Feng Shui
- The trigram-organ correspondences form part of the theoretical foundation of TCM, mapping onto the five-phase (wuxing) system
- Feng shui (風水) uses the bagua arrangement to orient spatial relationships
- While these applications are well-documented historically, their therapeutic efficacy claims belong to the domain of alternative medicine rather than evidence-based practice

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 The I Ching as a Model of DNA Codons
- In the 1970s, Martin Schönberger (*The I Ching and the Genetic Code*, 1973) and Katya Walter proposed that the 64 hexagrams map onto the 64 codons of the genetic code (4³ = 64)
- The structural isomorphism is real (both are 6-bit quaternary systems: 4 bases × 3 positions = 64 codons; 2 line types × 6 lines = 64 hexagrams)
- There is **no credible mechanism** by which ancient Chinese could have encoded knowledge of DNA, and the numerical coincidence does not constitute evidence of intentional correspondence
- The claim is structurally interesting but epistemologically unfounded

### 3.2 Terence McKenna's Timewave Zero
- Terence McKenna (1946–2000) developed "Timewave Zero," a mathematical transformation of the King Wen sequence that he claimed modeled the **fractal structure of temporal novelty** across history
- McKenna interpreted the resulting wave as predicting increasing "novelty" (complexity, interconnection) culminating at a singularity point (originally December 2012)
- The mathematical basis has been criticized by mathematicians as arbitrary; the 2012 endpoint did not produce any verifiable singularity
- The theory remains culturally significant in psychedelic and counterculture circles but lacks scientific support

### 3.3 Fu Xi's Trigrams as Pre-Literate Knowledge System
- The attribution of the trigrams to the mythical emperor Fu Xi (伏羲) has been interpreted as reflecting a genuine **pre-literate mnemonic system**
- Scholars (Sarah Allan, *The Way of Water and Sprouts of Virtue*, 1997) argue that the trigram system encodes early Chinese observations of natural cycles in a systematic, transmissible format that predates writing
- The claim is plausible but unverifiable, as no pre-Shang evidence of trigram use has been archaeologically confirmed
