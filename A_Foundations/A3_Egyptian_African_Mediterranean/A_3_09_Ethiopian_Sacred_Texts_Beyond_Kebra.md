## QUICK SUMMARY

The Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church preserves the most expansive biblical canon in Christendom — 81 books, compared to 66 in the Protestant canon and 73 in the Roman Catholic canon — including texts considered apocryphal or lost in all other Christian traditions. Written in Ge'ez (classical Ethiopic), a South Semitic liturgical language no longer spoken as a vernacular, these texts include the complete text of 1 Enoch (known elsewhere only from fragments until the Ge'ez manuscripts were brought to Europe in 1773 by James Bruce), the Book of Jubilees ("Little Genesis," a retelling of Genesis–Exodus with precise jubilee-based chronology and expanded angelology), and uniquely Ethiopian compositions such as the *Mashafa Berhan* (Book of Light) and *Mashafa Milad* (Book of Nativity). Beyond the biblical canon, Ethiopian sacred literature encompasses a vast hagiographic tradition (*Gadla* lives of saints, with detailed accounts of indigenous Ethiopian saints alongside universal Christian figures), liturgical poetry (*Qene*, a sophisticated Ge'ez verse form used in worship), and the *Fetha Nagast* (Law of Kings) — a comprehensive legal code governing both ecclesiastical and civil matters that served as Ethiopia's supreme law until 1930. The Ethiopian textual tradition is of unique importance to biblical scholarship because several "lost" Second Temple Jewish texts survived only in Ethiopia, having been transmitted by the Ethiopian church when they were lost or actively suppressed in all other traditions.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 The Ethiopian Biblical Canon
- The Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo canon includes **81 books** — all 66 of the Protestant canon, plus deuterocanonical books shared with Catholic and Orthodox traditions (Sirach, Wisdom, Tobit, Judith, 1–2 Maccabees), and additional texts unique to the Ethiopian canon:
  - **1 Enoch** (Henok): the complete 108-chapter text of 1 Enoch, including the Book of the Watchers, Similitudes of Enoch, the Astronomical Book, the Book of Dream Visions, and the Epistle of Enoch
  - **Jubilees** (Kufale): 50 chapters retelling Genesis–Exodus with expanded angelology, precise sacred chronology based on 49-year jubilee cycles, and laws attributed to Moses via angelic dictation
  - **4 Baruch** (Paraleipomena Jeremiou)
  - The broader canon also includes texts like the *Sinodos*, *Mashafa Tefut* (Book of Covenant), and parts of the *Octateuch*
- These texts were transmitted continuously in Ge'ez manuscripts from at least the 5th–6th centuries CE, when Christianity became the state religion of Aksum — the manuscript tradition extends unbroken to the present

### 1.2 Preservation of 1 Enoch
- **1 Enoch** was known to early Church Fathers (Tertullian, Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria) and quoted in the biblical Epistle of Jude (1:14–15), but was progressively excluded from Western and most Eastern Christian canons — by the 4th–5th century CE, the text had effectively disappeared from European Christianity
- James Bruce brought three Ge'ez manuscripts of 1 Enoch to Europe from Ethiopia in 1773 — these remained the only complete witness to the text until Aramaic fragments were discovered at Qumran (Dead Sea Scrolls) in the 1940s–1950s, confirming that the Ge'ez text was a faithful translation of the ancient Aramaic original
- The Ethiopian preservation of 1 Enoch for over 1,500 years when all other traditions lost it demonstrates the unique archival function of the Ethiopian church
- **Counter-Argument:** The Ge'ez translation of 1 Enoch shows some textual variants and expansions compared to the Qumran Aramaic fragments — it is a faithful but not identical witness; critical editions must compare both traditions

### 1.3 The Book of Jubilees
- **Jubilees** (c. 160–150 BCE) was widely read in Second Temple Judaism — 15 fragmentary manuscripts were found at Qumran, suggesting it was nearly as popular as the biblical books themselves in certain Jewish communities
- The complete text survives only in Ge'ez — Latin fragments and a few Syriac citations provide partial corroboration, but the Ethiopian text is the sole complete witness
- Jubilees prescribes a 364-day solar calendar, details the origin and hierarchy of angels (including their creation on the first day), provides elaborate expansions of patriarchal narratives, and contains strict halakhic rulings — making it a vital source for understanding pre-rabbinic Judaism

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Fetha Nagast (Law of Kings)
- The **Fetha Nagast** — compiled in the 13th century from earlier Coptic, Arabic, and Byzantine sources, translated into Ge'ez in the 15th century — served as Ethiopia's supreme legal code for religious and civil matters until Emperor Haile Selassie adopted a modern constitution in 1930
- The text combines canon law (church governance, ordination, marriage, fasting, liturgical regulations) with civil law (property, contracts, criminal penalties, inheritance, slavery regulations) — creating a comprehensive legal framework grounded in Christian theology
- The Fetha Nagast was cited in the 2005 Ethiopian constitution drafting process and remains symbolically important in Ethiopian legal culture
- **Counter-Argument:** The Fetha Nagast was not exclusively indigenous — its core derives from the *Nomocanon* of the Coptic patriarch Cyril ibn Laqlaq (1235) and other non-Ethiopian sources, adapted for Ethiopian conditions

### 2.2 Hagiographic Tradition (Gadla Literature)
- Ethiopian **Gadla** (acts/struggle of a saint) literature comprises hundreds of saint biographies — combining historical information, miraculous narratives, theological instruction, and local cultural detail
- The *Gadla Lalibela* (Life of King Lalibela) recounts the construction of the famous rock-hewn churches of Lalibela (12th–13th century CE) with angelic assistance — while the miraculous elements are hagiographic convention, the text provides valuable historical context for the actual construction project
- The *Gadla Tekle Haymanot* (Life of Tekle Haymanot) — the most important indigenous Ethiopian saint — exists in multiple recensions reflecting regional monastic traditions competing for prestige

### 2.3 Beta Israel (Ethiopian Jewish) Traditions
- The **Beta Israel** (Ethiopian Jews, formerly called Falasha) preserved a distinct Jewish tradition based on the *Orit* (Torah in Ge'ez), supplemented by other Ge'ez texts including 1 Enoch and Jubilees, with practices reflecting pre-rabbinic Judaism (no Talmud, animal sacrifice until relatively recently, purity laws more stringent than rabbinic norm)
- Their sacred texts overlap significantly with but are distinct from the Ethiopian Christian canon — suggesting a shared early transmission history that diverged before the rabbinic/Christian split of the early centuries CE
- **Counter-Argument:** The origin of Beta Israel — whether they represent an ancient Jewish community predating rabbinic Judaism, Christian converts to a form of Judaism, or an indigenous movement — remains debated; DNA published findings demonstrate primarily Ethiopian genetic background rather than Levantine origin

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Other "Lost" Texts in Ethiopian Monasteries
- Scholars have periodically speculated that Ethiopian monastic libraries — many in remote, inaccessible locations — may contain yet-undiscovered texts preserved only in Ge'ez
- The Ethiopian Manuscript Microfilming Library (EMML) project catalogued thousands of manuscripts, and the Hill Museum and Manuscript Library continues digitization — but many monasteries have not been fully catalogued, and the possibility of further discoveries remains
- Claims of specific "lost" texts (e.g., the lost ending of the *Shepherd of Hermas*, or alternate versions of other pseudepigrapha) in uncatalogued monasteries remain unverified

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 "The Ethiopian Canon Is the 'Original' Bible"
- **[DEBUNKED]** Claims that the Ethiopian 81-book canon represents the "original" or "true" Bible are anachronistic — there was no fixed biblical canon in the earliest period of Christianity; the Ethiopian canon reflects decisions made by the Ethiopian church over centuries, incorporating texts that other traditions excluded or lost, but it is a tradition-specific canonical decision, not a recovery of a single "original" list
