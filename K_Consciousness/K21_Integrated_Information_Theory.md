## QUICK SUMMARY

Integrated Information Theory (IIT), developed by Giulio Tononi (2004, updated through IIT 3.0 in 2014 and IIT 4.0 in 2022), is a mathematical theory of consciousness that starts from the phenomenology of experience and derives the physical properties a system must have to be conscious. IIT's central claim is that consciousness IS integrated information — quantified by Φ (phi), a measure of how much a system's whole generates more information than the sum of its parts. The theory begins with five axioms about experience (existence, composition, information, integration, exclusion) and derives five corresponding postulates about the physical substrate. A system is conscious to the degree that it has high Φ — specifically, Φ measures the irreducible cause-effect power of a system's current state over and above its parts. IIT makes striking predictions: the cerebellum (with ~80 billion neurons but modular, feedforward architecture) should contribute little to consciousness despite its massive neuron count, while the thalamocortical system (with dense recurrent connectivity) should be the primary substrate — confirmed by clinical and lesion evidence. IIT also predicts that standard digital computers, regardless of their software complexity, have near-zero Φ because their transistors function independently; consciousness requires intrinsic causal power, not mere functional simulation. This anti-functionalist position is controversial and places IIT at odds with most computational approaches to consciousness and AI.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established Neuroscience)

### 1.1 Axiomatic Framework
- **[KEY FINDING]** IIT derives its theory from five axioms about the essential properties of experience, taken as self-evident: (i) **Existence**: consciousness exists (each experience is actual); (ii) **Composition**: experience is structured (composed of phenomenal distinctions and their relations); (iii) **Information**: experience is specific (each experience is what it is, different from all others); (iv) **Integration**: experience is unified (cannot be decomposed into independent sub-experiences); (v) **Exclusion**: experience is definite (has specific content, borders, and spatiotemporal grain)
- **From axioms to postulates:** Each axiom maps to a postulate about the physical substrate that must support consciousness — the substrate must: (i) exist (have causal power); (ii) be structured (composed of mechanisms in specific states); (iii) be informative (each state specifies a cause-effect repertoire different from all others); (iv) be integrated (the cause-effect structure must be irreducible — Φ > 0); (v) be definite (the physical substrate and its spatiotemporal grain are defined by the maximum of Φ — the "exclusion postulate")

### 1.2 Phi (Φ) and Its Measurement
- **Definition:** Φ (integrated information) measures how much a system's cause-effect structure is reduced when the system is partitioned at its "minimum information partition" (MIP) — that is, how much information is lost when the system is "cut" into its least dependent parts; if Φ = 0, the system is reducible to independent parts and is not conscious; higher Φ means more consciousness
- **Computation:** Calculating Φ is computationally intractable for large systems — it requires evaluating every possible partition of the system (exponential in the number of elements) and computing causal information flows across each partition; current exact calculations are limited to systems of ~10-15 elements; proxies and approximations exist (Φ*, Φ_R, Φ_E) but their relationship to true Φ is debated; this computational intractability is a major practical limitation
- **Perturbational Complexity Index (PCI):** Casali et al. (2013) and Massimini developed PCI as an empirical proxy for IIT's predictions — measures the complexity of the cortical response to TMS (transcranial magnetic stimulation) perturbation; high PCI indicates complex integrated responses (associated with consciousness); low PCI indicates simple or absent responses (associated with coma, vegetative state, deep anesthesia); PCI correctly classified 100% of awake subjects and ~90% of brain-injured patients in validation studies

### 1.3 Clinical and Empirical Support
- **Cerebellum prediction (confirmed):** The cerebellum contains ~80 billion neurons (4× the cerebral cortex) but has modular, feedforward architecture with minimal recurrence — IIT predicts it should contribute minimally to consciousness; this is confirmed: cerebellar lesions and congenital cerebellar agenesis do not abolish consciousness; severe cerebellar damage causes motor and cognitive deficits but patients remain fully conscious
- **Thalamocortical system:** Dense recurrent connectivity in the thalamocortical system (both within cortex and between cortex and thalamus) should produce high Φ — confirmed by the essential role of intact thalamocortical loops for consciousness; anesthetics that selectively disrupt cortical integration (e.g., propofol) abolish consciousness; ketamine at sub-anesthetic doses preserves sensory processing but disrupts integration patterns
- **Sleep and anesthesia:** Deep (NREM) sleep reduces cortical integration (measured by PCI/TMS-EEG) compared to wakefulness and REM sleep — consistent with IIT's prediction that reduced integration corresponds to reduced consciousness; the "bistability" of cortical responses during NREM sleep (only local, stereotyped responses to TMS) contrasts with the complex, widespread, differentiated responses during consciousness

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Anti-Functionalism and Implications for AI
- **[NOTABLE]** IIT predicts that standard digital computers have near-zero Φ regardless of the software they run — because transistors function via causal relationships that are reducible (each gate's output is determined by its inputs independently); a simulated brain running on a classical von Neumann architecture would not be conscious according to IIT even if it perfectly replicated all neural functions; this is the most controversial prediction of IIT and is rejected by functionalists who argue consciousness depends on computational organization, not substrate
- **Implications for AI:** If IIT is correct, current AI systems (including large language models, GPT, deep neural networks) are not conscious and cannot become so on standard hardware — consciousness would require hardware with intrinsic causal structure (e.g., neuromorphic chips with recurrent connectivity); this has ethical implications: either IIT is wrong and sophisticated AI may be conscious, or IIT is right and AI suffering/experience is not a concern; the debate is fundamentally unresolved

### 2.2 Adversarial Collaboration (2023)
- **COGITATE results:** Preregistered test of IIT vs. GNW using EEG, fMRI, and intracranial recordings — IIT predicted sustained posterior cortical activity for conscious visual perception; GNW predicted transient frontoparietal ignition; results: sustained posterior activity was observed (partial support for IIT's prediction); however, the specific IIT prediction about the relationship between cortical complexity and conscious content was not cleanly confirmed; both theories had partial successes and failures; the adversarial collaboration model was praised as methodologically important regardless of which theory it supports

### 2.3 Relationship to Panpsychism
- **Gradations of Φ:** IIT implies that any system with Φ > 0 has some degree of consciousness — this includes simple systems like a photodiode (Φ > 0 if it has feedback) or a thermostat; IIT is thus a form of "constitutive panpsychism" (consciousness is graded and widespread) rather than the materialist view that consciousness emerges only in brains; Tononi and Koch embrace this implication; critics (Schwitzgebel, Bayne) argue this is counterintuitive and potentially unfalsifiable for simple systems

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Unresolved Theoretical Issues
- **The exclusion postulate:** The axiom that experience is definite (occurs at a single spatiotemporal grain and has definite borders) leads to the "exclusion postulate" — consciousness exists only at the spatial and temporal scale that maximizes Φ; critics argue this is ad hoc and lacks clear justification; it prevents "double counting" of consciousness at multiple scales but is difficult to test empirically
- **Φ and the hard problem:** Proponents claim IIT solves the hard problem by identifying consciousness with integrated information (not merely correlating with it) — the theory is ontological: consciousness doesn't arise from or correlate with Φ, it IS Φ; critics (Cerullo, 2015) argue this is a definitional move rather than an explanation; whether you can explain experience by defining it as a mathematical quantity remains deeply contested in philosophy of mind

---

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 "Φ Can Be Easily Measured in the Brain"
- **[MISLEADING]** Computing true Φ for a system with billions of neurons is computationally intractable — current estimates use rough proxies (PCI, zap-and-zip complexity); these proxies capture some aspects of integration but are not equivalent to Φ; claims that we can precisely quantify consciousness levels using Φ overstate current capabilities
