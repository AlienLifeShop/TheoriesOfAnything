## QUICK SUMMARY

Troy (modern Hisarlik, northwestern Turkey) is one of the most famous archaeological sites in the world, identified with the legendary city of Homer's *Iliad*. The mound contains at least nine major stratigraphic layers spanning from approximately 3000 BCE to the Roman period (~500 CE). Heinrich Schliemann's sensational excavations in the 1870s—including his dubious claim of discovering "Priam's Treasure"—brought the site to global attention but also caused enormous stratigraphic damage. Subsequent excavators Wilhelm Dörpfeld, Carl Blegen (1930s), and Manfred Korfmann (1988–2005) progressively refined the chronology, identified Troy VIIa (c. 1190–1180 BCE) as the most likely candidate for a Homeric-era destruction, and discovered an extensive lower town that expanded the known settlement area tenfold. Hittite texts referencing "Wilusa" provide independent textual evidence for a significant Bronze Age polity at or near this location, making Troy a paradigm case for the intersection of mythology, textual history, and archaeological verification.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Stratigraphic Sequence: Nine Major Occupation Levels
- The site of Hisarlik contains a minimum of nine major occupation phases (Troy I through Troy IX), refined into over 46 sub-phases.
- Troy I–V: Early Bronze Age (~3000–1750 BCE). Troy VI–VIIa: Late Bronze Age (~1700–1180 BCE). Troy VIIb–IX: Iron Age through Roman period.
- The mound rises approximately 16 meters above the surrounding Troad plain.
- Troy II (Schliemann's "Burnt City") dates to approximately 2550–2300 BCE—over a millennium too early for any Trojan War.
- **Primary Source:** Blegen, C.W. *Troy and the Trojans.* Thames and Hudson, 1963.
- **Counter-Argument:** None significant regarding the basic stratigraphy; debates concern which level corresponds to Homer's Troy.

### 1.2 Schliemann's Excavations and "Priam's Treasure" (1870s)
- Heinrich Schliemann conducted major excavations at Hisarlik between 1870 and 1890, driven by his conviction that the site was Homer's Troy.
- In May 1873, Schliemann announced the discovery of a cache of gold objects he called "Priam's Treasure," which he assigned to Troy II.
- Schliemann's excavation methods were destructive: he drove a large trench through the center of the mound, destroying significant portions of later (and more relevant) occupation layers.
- The treasure was smuggled out of the Ottoman Empire to Germany; it was seized by the Soviet Army in 1945 and is now held at the Pushkin Museum, Moscow.
- **Primary Source:** Traill, D.A. *Schliemann of Troy: Treasure and Deceit.* St. Martin's Press, 1995.
- **Counter-Argument:** Traill and others have demonstrated that Schliemann fabricated aspects of the discovery circumstances (e.g., his wife Sophia's presence) and that several objects from different contexts may have been grouped together.

### 1.3 Wilhelm Dörpfeld's Stratigraphic Corrections
- Dörpfeld, Schliemann's assistant and a trained architect, excavated at Troy in 1893–1894 after Schliemann's death (1890).
- He established that Troy VI, not Troy II, was the major Late Bronze Age citadel, with its impressive limestone fortification walls, large freestanding houses, and cultural connections to the Mycenaean world.
- Dörpfeld argued Troy VI was Homer's Troy, destroyed by earthquake around 1300 BCE.
- **Primary Source:** Dörpfeld, W. *Troja und Ilion.* Beck and Barth, 1902.
- **Counter-Argument:** Blegen later argued the earthquake destruction of Troy VI did not match an enemy siege narrative; Troy VIIa became the preferred candidate.

### 1.4 Carl Blegen's Systematic Excavations (1932–1938)
- Blegen, from the University of Cincinnati, conducted the most systematic pre-war excavation of Troy using controlled stratigraphic methods.
- He established the refined sub-phase chronology still largely in use and identified Troy VIIa as the best candidate for Homer's Troy based on evidence of violent destruction by fire, hastily constructed interior partition walls (suggesting a besieged population), and storage jars sunk into house floors.
- The destruction of Troy VIIa is dated to approximately 1190–1180 BCE, broadly contemporaneous with the traditional Greek date for the Trojan War (~1184 BCE per Eratosthenes).
- **Primary Source:** Blegen, C.W. et al. *Troy: Excavations Conducted by the University of Cincinnati, 1932–1938.* 4 vols. Princeton University Press, 1950–1958.
- **Counter-Argument:** The "siege" indicators are ambiguous—storage jars and partitions could reflect economic stress or population crowding unrelated to warfare.

### 1.5 Korfmann's Renewed Excavations and the Lower Town (1988–2005)
- Manfred Korfmann (University of Tübingen) directed new excavations from 1988 until his death in 2005, applying modern methods including magnetometry, surface survey, and systematic flotation.
- Korfmann discovered a substantial lower town south of the citadel mound, extending the known settlement area from approximately 2 hectares (citadel alone) to 20–30 hectares.
- A defensive ditch system surrounded the lower town, indicating it was recognized as part of the settlement requiring protection.
- He argued Troy was a major Bronze Age trading center controlling access to the Dardanelles (Hellespont).
- **Primary Source:** Korfmann, M. "Troia/Wilusa: General Background and a Summary of the 1987–2002 Campaign Results." In Wagner, G.A. et al. (eds.), *Troia and the Troad.* Springer, 2003.
- **Counter-Argument:** Frank Kolb (2004) vigorously contested Korfmann's claims about the lower town's size and significance, calling them exaggerated; the "Troia-Debatte" became one of the most heated academic controversies in German archaeology.

### 1.6 Hittite References to Wilusa
- Hittite diplomatic texts from the 13th century BCE reference a vassal state called "Wilusa" in northwestern Anatolia, which linguists widely identify with Greek "(W)Ilion" (= Ilios/Troy).
- The "Alaksandu Treaty" (c. 1280 BCE) between Hittite king Muwatalli II and Alaksandu of Wilusa may parallel the name "Alexandros" (Paris of Troy's alternate name in Homer).
- A Hittite text (the Manapa-Tarhunta letter) mentions conflict involving Wilusa.
- **Primary Source:** Hawkins, J.D. "Tarkasnawa King of Mira: 'Tarkondemos,' Boğazköy Sealings and Karabel." *Anatolian Studies* 48 (1998): 1–31.
- **Counter-Argument:** The Wilusa/Ilion equation, while widely accepted, is not universally agreed upon; some scholars place Wilusa elsewhere in western Anatolia.

### 1.7 Horse Remains at Troy VI
- Bones of domesticated horses have been found in Troy VI contexts, consistent with the Homeric epithet "horse-taming Trojans."
- These represent some of the earliest horse remains in western Anatolia.
- **Primary Source:** Jablonka, P. and Rose, C.B. "Late Bronze Age Troy: A Response to Frank Kolb." *American Journal of Archaeology* 108.4 (2004): 615–630.
- **Counter-Argument:** Horse bones alone do not confirm the Homeric narrative; horses were widespread in Late Bronze Age Anatolia.

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Troy VIIa Destruction Reflects Warfare
- The destruction layer of Troy VIIa includes extensive burning, collapsed structures, and skeletal remains of individuals who appear to have died violently (including an arrowhead embedded in a human spine, found by Blegen).
- The pattern is consistent with sack and burning of a settlement following a military assault.
- Slingshot pellets and arrowheads found in the destruction debris support a military event.
- **Primary Source:** Blegen, C.W. *Troy and the Trojans.* Thames and Hudson, 1963.
- **Counter-Argument:** Some scholars argue the destruction could reflect internal conflict, accidental fire, or a combination of earthquake and opportunistic attack rather than a formal siege.

### 2.2 Bronze Age Maritime Trade Hub
- Troy's strategic position at the southern entrance to the Dardanelles, where wind and current patterns frequently forced ancient ships to wait for favorable conditions, made it a natural trade emporium.
- Material culture from Troy includes Mycenaean pottery, Cypriot imports, Anatolian metalwork, and connections to Central European bronze traditions.
- The site may have levied tolls or provisioned ships waiting to pass the straits.
- **Primary Source:** Korfmann, M. "Was There a Trojan War?" *Archaeology* 57.3 (2004): 36–41.
- **Counter-Argument:** Direct evidence for toll-collection or port infrastructure is limited.

### 2.3 Homeric Oral Tradition Preserving Historical Memory
- The convergence of Late Bronze Age destruction at Troy VIIa, Hittite references to Wilusa, the Mycenaean material presence, and Greek oral tradition has led many scholars to accept that a historical conflict at Troy underlies the *Iliad*.
- This does not imply the *Iliad* is a factual account—rather that the epic may preserve a kernel of Late Bronze Age geopolitical reality transmitted through centuries of oral poetic tradition.
- **Primary Source:** Latacz, J. *Troy and Homer: Towards a Solution of an Old Mystery.* Oxford University Press, 2004.
- **Counter-Argument:** Moses Finley (*The World of Odysseus*, 1954) famously argued that the Homeric poems reflect early Iron Age society, not Bronze Age realities, and that searching for a "real" Trojan War is methodologically flawed.

### 2.4 Priam's Treasure as Mixed Assemblage
- Modern analysis suggests the "Treasure of Priam" is a composite assemblage, possibly collected from multiple archaeological contexts or augmented with subsequently acquired objects.
- Metallurgical analysis of some gold objects is consistent with Early Bronze Age dating (Troy II period), but provenance questions persist.
- **Primary Source:** Easton, D.F. "Priam's Treasure." *Anatolian Studies* 34 (1984): 141–169.
- **Counter-Argument:** Despite integrity questions, the core of the assemblage likely does originate from Hisarlik's Early Bronze Age levels.

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Large-Scale Military Coalition Against Troy
- The Homeric tradition describes a pan-Achaean expedition involving contingents from across the Mycenaean world. Some scholars speculate that Late Bronze Age Hittite-Ahhiyawa (Mycenaean?) tensions in western Anatolia could provide a geopolitical context for such a coalition.
- The "Tawagalawa Letter" references conflict between the Hittite king and the king of Ahhiyawa over influence in western Anatolian territories.
- **Primary Source:** Bryce, T. *The Trojans and Their Neighbours.* Routledge, 2006.
- **Counter-Argument:** Extrapolating a ten-year siege from fragmentary Hittite diplomacy goes well beyond the evidence.

### 3.2 Troy as "Gateway City" Between East and West
- Korfmann proposed that Troy functioned as a pivotal node connecting Aegean maritime trade with inland Anatolian caravan routes, making it a "gateway community."
- This elevated status could explain why it became a target for Mycenaean expansionism.
- **Primary Source:** Korfmann, M. "Was There a Trojan War?" *Archaeology* 57.3 (2004): 36–41.
- **Counter-Argument:** Kolb (2004) argued that Korfmann inflated Troy's importance and that the lower town evidence does not support a major urban center.

### 3.3 Trojan Horse as Memory of Siege Warfare Technology
- Some scholars have proposed the Trojan Horse myth may encode a memory of a siege engine, an earthquake (Poseidon, associated with horses, also "earth-shaker"), or a ship with a horse figurehead.
- **Primary Source:** Schofield, L. *The Mycenaeans.* British Museum Press, 2007.
- **Counter-Argument:** These rationalizations are unprovable; the Trojan Horse may be a purely literary invention with no historical correlate.

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 **[DEBUNKED]** Schliemann's Account of Finding the Treasure
- Schliemann claimed he and his wife Sophia found Priam's Treasure together on May 31, 1873, dismissing workers to secretly excavate it. Documentary evidence (including Sophia's letters) proves she was in Athens at the time.
- His diary entries and later accounts contain contradictory descriptions of the find circumstances.
- **Primary Source:** Traill, D.A. *Schliemann of Troy: Treasure and Deceit.* St. Martin's Press, 1995.

### 4.2 **[DEBUNKED]** Troy II as Homer's Troy
- Schliemann's original identification of Troy II (c. 2550–2300 BCE) with the city of the *Iliad* is archaeologically untenable—it predates the Mycenaean civilization by a millennium and the conventional Trojan War date by over a thousand years.
- **Primary Source:** Blegen, C.W. *Troy and the Trojans.* Thames and Hudson, 1963.

### 4.3 **[DEBUNKED]** Troy as Purely Mythological with No Historical Basis
- The extreme skeptical position that Troy/Hisarlik has no connection to any historical conflict is no longer tenable given the Hittite Wilusa evidence, the Troy VIIa destruction layer, and Mycenaean material presence.
- **Primary Source:** Latacz, J. *Troy and Homer.* Oxford University Press, 2004.

---

## COUNTER-ARGUMENTS

- **Against the Trojan War's historicity:** Finley's critique remains influential—the *Iliad* may be a literary composition reflecting Iron Age values projected onto a legendary past, with no recoverable historical core. Archaeological destruction layers cannot be linked to specific named conflicts.
- **Against Korfmann's lower town claims:** Kolb argued the lower town was sparsely occupied, largely agricultural, and did not justify calling Troy a major city. The "Troia-Debatte" exposed methodological tensions between maximalist and minimalist interpretations of survey data.
- **Against Wilusa = Ilion:** While linguistically plausible, alternative locations for Wilusa have been proposed, and the identification depends on phonological reconstructions that are not universally accepted.
- **Against oral tradition as historical evidence:** The degree to which 500+ years of oral transmission can preserve accurate historical details about a specific conflict is inherently uncertain.
