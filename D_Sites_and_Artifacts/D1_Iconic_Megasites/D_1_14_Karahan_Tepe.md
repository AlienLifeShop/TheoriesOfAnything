## QUICK SUMMARY

Karahan Tepe is a Pre-Pottery Neolithic (PPN) site in southeastern Turkey (Şanlıurfa Province), approximately 46 km southeast of Göbekli Tepe, dating to c. 9400–8200 BCE. Discovered during surface surveys in 1997 and systematically excavated since 2019 under the Turkish Ministry of Culture's **Taş Tepeler** ("Stone Hills") research program directed by Prof. Necmi Karul, Karahan Tepe has emerged as one of the most significant archaeological finds of the 21st century. The site features monumental T-shaped pillars reminiscent of but stylistically distinct from Göbekli Tepe, elaborately carved 3D relief sculptures (serpents, human heads, a leopard), and the extraordinary **Structure AB** — a subterranean chamber with carved phallic pillars and an enigmatic carved human head emerging from the wall. The site challenges the traditional narrative that monumental architecture required agricultural surplus by demonstrating that **pre-agricultural hunter-gatherer communities** could organize large-scale construction projects, and it reveals that Göbekli Tepe was not a unique anomaly but part of a **regional network of PPN ritual sites** across the Şanlıurfa plateau.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Discovery and Excavation History
- **Discovery:** Identified during a 1997 surface survey by Bahattin Çelik (Istanbul University), who noted concentrations of T-shaped pillar fragments, stone tools, and carved stone fragments on the surface of a low hilltop.
- **Survey and initial documentation:** Çelik (2000, 2011) published surface finds and classified Karahan Tepe as a Göbekli Tepe–type PPN ritual site. Limited test trenches conducted 2000–2014.
- **Systematic excavation:** Full-scale excavations commenced in **September 2019** under the **Taş Tepeler Project**, directed by **Prof. Necmi Karul** (Istanbul University) and sponsored by the Turkish Ministry of Culture and Tourism. The Taş Tepeler ("Stone Hills") program is a large-scale research initiative encompassing at least 12 PPN sites in the Şanlıurfa region, including Göbekli Tepe, Karahan Tepe, Sayburç, Harbetsuvan Tepesi, Gürcü Tepe, Ayanlar Höyük, Yeni Mahalle, Çakmak Tepe, Kurt Tepesi, Taslı Tepe, Sefertepe, and Hamzan Tepe. Karahan Tepe is the second-most intensively excavated site after Göbekli Tepe.
- **Ongoing status:** Excavation is active — approximately 5% of the estimated site area has been uncovered as of early 2026.

### 1.2 Dating
- **Radiocarbon dates (2019–2024 campaigns):** c. **9400–8200 BCE** (PPNA–Early PPNB transition) — broadly contemporary with and possibly slightly later than Göbekli Tepe's earliest phases (c. 9600–8000 BCE).
- **Cultural period:** Pre-Pottery Neolithic A (PPNA) to Early Pre-Pottery Neolithic B (EPPNB). No pottery at the site. Subsistence economy based on **wild plant gathering and hunting** (primarily gazelle, aurochs, equids) — no evidence of domesticated cereals or animals at Karahan Tepe. This firmly places monumental construction **before the development of agriculture** in this region.

### 1.3 Architecture and Layout
- **Site extent:** Covers approximately **1 hectare** of exposed remains, with geophysical survey (GPR) indicating substantial unexcavated structures extending further.
- **T-shaped pillars:** Like Göbekli Tepe, the site features carved limestone **T-shaped pillars**:
  - Monolithic stones with a broad horizontal "head" atop a narrower vertical "body"
  - Interpreted as highly stylized anthropomorphic figures (the T-shape representing a human torso and head in profile)
  - Karahan Tepe's pillars are generally **smaller** than Göbekli Tepe's largest (which reach 5.5 m and 16 tonnes)
  - The largest at Karahan Tepe are ~3–4 m tall
  - Some pillars bear carved relief decoration on their surfaces; others are plain
- **Key difference from Göbekli Tepe:** While Göbekli Tepe's enclosures are predominantly **circular**, Karahan Tepe displays a wider range of architectural forms:
  - Oval structures
  - Sub-circular structures
  - **Rectangular** structures
  - These diverse forms suggest experimentation with spatial organization or different functional requirements
- **Pillar quarries:** A partially quarried, unfinished T-shaped pillar has been found **in situ** in the bedrock quarry adjacent to the site, demonstrating local production (pillars were not transported long distances). Channel-cutting and wedge-splitting marks in the quarry document the extraction process.
- **Bedrock modification:** Unlike Göbekli Tepe, where structures sit on the natural hilltop surface, much of Karahan Tepe's monumental architecture has been **carved into the bedrock itself** — floors, benches, and even some pillars emerge directly from the living rock, blurring the boundary between architecture and sculpture.
- **Total site extent:** The site covers approximately **11 hectares** of a limestone hilltop; geophysical survey (georadar and magnetic) has revealed at least **250 T-shaped pillars** (mostly still in situ or visible in survey data), making Karahan Tepe potentially larger than Göbekli Tepe in total pillar count (Göbekli Tepe has ~200 pillars in ~20 enclosures, most unexcavated)
- **Hybrid construction technique:** The architecture combines both **free-standing construction** (T-shaped pillars erected in sockets) and **rock-cut excavation** (chambers carved directly into the bedrock) — a hybrid technique not prominently seen at Göbekli Tepe
- **"Head Pillar":** A T-shaped pillar with a **realistically carved human face** (eyes, nose, jaw) — the most explicit anthropomorphic rendering of the T-pillar concept found to date, confirming and exceeding the interpretation of T-pillars as stylized human figures
- **Structure AD:** A smaller chamber (separate from Structure AB) containing **phallus-shaped pillars** carved from the bedrock itself (not free-standing) — one such sculpture stands approximately 2.3 m tall, integrated with the surrounding rock floor

### 1.4 Structure AB — The "Phallus Room"
- **Location:** A semi-subterranean, oval chamber carved partially into the bedrock — the most iconic space at Karahan Tepe.
- **Description:**
  - The room contains at least **11 carved limestone pillars** arranged in a roughly oval configuration.
  - Several pillars are carved as naturalistic **phallic forms** — tapered, rounded pillars of varying heights rising from the bedrock floor, with anatomically explicit sculpted heads. This is unlike anything at Göbekli Tepe.
  - At the north wall of the chamber, a **carved human head** emerges from the bedrock wall — a naturalistic face with defined eyes, nose, mouth, and chin, sculpted in 3D relief as if a figure is pushing through the stone from behind. The head is positioned directly above a carved serpent.
  - **Bench seating:** A continuous rock-cut bench runs along the chamber's circumference, suggesting an audience or congregation space.
  - **Interpretation (Karul 2020, 2021):** The combination of phallic pillars, human head, serpent iconography, and bench seating has been interpreted as a **ritual space** connected to themes of fertility, ancestor veneration, death-and-rebirth symbolism, or clan initiation. No consensus on specific function exists — excavation is ongoing.

### 1.5 Sculptural Program
- **Carved human heads:** Multiple carved 3D human heads have been found at Karahan Tepe — sculpted from bedrock or as portable objects. Features are naturalistic with detailed eyes, noses, mouths, and jaw lines. One head from Structure AB appears to depict an older individual with defined wrinkles.
- **Animal reliefs:** T-shaped pillars and walls bear carved reliefs of **serpents** (prominent — recurring motif across multiple structures), **a leopard/large cat** (carved in high relief on a pillar), and geometric patterns.
- **Comparative iconography:** While Göbekli Tepe emphasizes foxes, wild boar, snakes, and vultures (interpreted by Schmidt as totemistic), Karahan Tepe's iconographic emphasis on human heads, phallic forms, and serpents may indicate a **distinct ritual program** — potentially focused on human ancestry, fertility, and chthonic symbolism rather than (or in addition to) totemic animal associations.
- **Portable objects:** Ground-stone objects including stone bowls, pestles, and carved miniature T-shaped pillar replicas have been recovered — suggesting both domestic/utilitarian and symbolic/miniature-reproduction activities at the site.
- **Pigment traces:** Microscopic analysis of some pillar surfaces has revealed faint traces of **red ochre**, raising the possibility that carved reliefs and sculptures were originally painted — a feature that would dramatically alter the visual experience of the ritual spaces.
- **Stone vessel technology:** The ground-stone bowls demonstrate sophisticated manufacturing techniques including rotary grinding and internal polishing, indicating specialized craft production beyond simple expedient tool-making.

### 1.6 Lithic Technology
- **Stone tool assemblage:** Dominated by **flint and obsidian** tools — pressure-flaked points (projectile points for hunting), bladelets, scrapers, and micro-drills. The obsidian was imported from sources in eastern Anatolia (Bingöl, Nemrut Dağ — distances of 250–400 km), demonstrating long-distance exchange networks even among pre-agricultural groups.
- **Groundstone technology:** Limestone and basalt ground-stone tools (mortars, pestles, querns) for processing wild plant foods. The energy investment in stone grinding equipment suggests intensified wild cereal processing — a possible precursor to domestication.
- **Debitage analysis:** Large quantities of limestone flaking debris around pillars and within structures document on-site carving — the monumental sculptures were produced *in situ* rather than imported finished.

### 1.7 Faunal and Botanical Evidence
- **Animal remains:** Preliminary zooarchaeological analysis identifies **gazelle** (*Gazella* sp.) as the dominant faunal taxon, followed by aurochs (*Bos primigenius*), equids (*Equus* sp.), wild boar, and smaller mammals. All species are **wild** — no morphological indicators of domestication are present.
- **Bird remains:** Avian bones include large raptors (eagles, vultures) — consistent with Göbekli Tepe where vulture iconography is prominent.
- **Botanical evidence:** Charred wild plant remains include einkorn wheat (*Triticum boeoticum*), wild barley (*Hordeum spontaneum*), and various pulses — all in their **wild morphological forms** (non-shattering rachises absent). This demonstrates intensive wild plant exploitation but not cultivation.
- **Feasting debris:** Concentrations of animal bone, grinding equipment, and fire-cracked stone in association with monumental structures are consistent with **communal feasting events** — ritual meals involving large quantities of prepared food. Dietrich et al. (2012) have documented similar feasting assemblages at Göbekli Tepe, interpreting them as evidence that large-scale ritual gatherings motivated surplus food production and ultimately contributed to the domestication process.

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Taş Tepeler as a Regional Ritual Network
- The identification of 12+ PPN sites with T-shaped pillar architecture across the Şanlıurfa plateau demonstrates that Göbekli Tepe was **not unique** — it was the largest node in a regional network of ritual/ceremonial sites. Karul's Taş Tepeler framework proposes a **coordinated or interrelated social system** connecting these sites — possibly involving seasonal aggregation events where dispersed hunter-gatherer bands converged for ritual, feasting, exchange, and social reproduction.
- **Sayburç:** A PPN site excavated since 2021, located ~50 km NW of Karahan Tepe, containing an extraordinary relief panel depicting two human figures flanking wild animals (bulls, leopards) — the first known Neolithic narrative scene. The discovery of sites like Sayburç confirms the regional pattern extends beyond Karahan and Göbekli.
- **Known Taş Tepeler sites include:**
  - Göbekli Tepe (largest, most intensively excavated)
  - Karahan Tepe (second-largest; Structure AB)
  - Sayburç (narrative relief panel)
  - Harbetsuvan Tepesi (pillar fragments, surface finds)
  - Gürcü Tepe (small-scale pillar site)
  - Ayanlar Höyük (residential and ritual evidence)
  - Yeni Mahalle (urban development threat)
  - Çakmak Tepe, Kurt Tepesi, Taslı Tepe, Sefertepe, Hamzan Tepe (survey-documented, limited excavation)
- The geographical distribution spans approximately **200 km east-west** across the northern Harran Plain, suggesting a coherent cultural zone rather than isolated occurrences.

### 2.2 Pre-Agricultural Complexity Debate
- The traditional archaeological model (Childe's "Neolithic Revolution") posits that **surplus agriculture** was a prerequisite for monumental architecture, social stratification, and organized religion. Göbekli Tepe (Schmidt 2006, 2012) challenged this model; Karahan Tepe and the broader Taş Tepeler network now **reinforce the challenge** — demonstrating that monumental construction, elaborate ritual, long-distance exchange, and probable social coordination occurred among communities with **no evidence of food production**.
- Revised models propose that **ritual and social aggregation drove sedentism and agriculture**, not the reverse — monumental sites may have been the catalyst for the transition to farming rather than its product. Significant debate continues.

### 2.3 Deliberate Backfilling
- Like Göbekli Tepe, there is evidence that some structures at Karahan Tepe were **deliberately buried** after a period of use — filled with rubble, soil, and refuse. At Göbekli Tepe, this practice preserved the enclosures for 11,000 years and is interpreted as intentional decommissioning (possibly following a cycle of construction, use, and ritual "burial" of the structure). Karahan Tepe's evidence for this practice is still being assessed.

### 2.4 Social Organization Implications
- Constructing monumental pillar structures required **coordinated labor** — quarrying heavy stones, transporting them (even short distances), carving elaborate reliefs, and maintaining the structure. Estimates for Göbekli Tepe suggest 500+ workers; Karahan Tepe's smaller scale may require fewer but still demands organizational capacity beyond a single family band. This implies **nascent social hierarchy** or at least temporary leadership structures (possibly religious specialists or ritual leaders) among PPN populations — a radical revision of hunter-gatherer social models.

### 2.5 Comparative Site Analysis — Karahan vs. Göbekli
- **Scale:** Göbekli Tepe is significantly larger — its enclosures contain pillars up to 5.5 m and 16 tonnes; Karahan Tepe's pillars are typically 3–4 m and lighter. Göbekli Tepe's enclosures are more uniformly circular with paired central pillars.
- **Iconographic emphasis:** Göbekli Tepe's relief program emphasizes **animals** — foxes, boars, snakes, vultures, lions, spiders, scorpions — composing a rich bestiary. Karahan Tepe places greater emphasis on the **human form** — carved heads, phallic pillars, human figures — possibly suggesting a shift from animal-centered to human-centered symbolic systems.
- **Domestic vs. ritual:** Both sites show minimal evidence of residential occupation — no hearths, cooking areas, or sleeping quarters have been securely identified within the monumental structures. However, recent work at Göbekli Tepe has challenged the purely "temple" interpretation, with researchers identifying possible domestic components. At Karahan Tepe, the relationship between ritual structures and possible habitation areas remains under investigation.
- **Chronological overlap:** The two sites were likely occupied **simultaneously** (at least partially overlapping in the PPNA/EPPNB), raising questions about whether they served different communities, different functions within a single social network, or different seasons of a mobile group's annual cycle.

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Astronomical Alignments
- Proposals that T-shaped pillar enclosures encode astronomical alignments (stellar, solar, or precessional) have been made for Göbekli Tepe (Sweatman & Tsikritsis 2017 — contested). Similar claims have been extended to Karahan Tepe, but with no published archaeoastronomical study specific to the site.

### 3.2 Shamanic/Altered-State Interpretation
- The Structure AB assemblage (phallic pillars, serpent imagery, carved head emerging from stone) has been compared to cross-cultural **shamanic cosmologies** — the head-through-wall motif interpreted as a "portal" between worlds. Lewis-Williams's neuropsychological model of cave art (entoptic phenomena and trance states) has been speculatively applied. No direct evidence (e.g., psychoactive plant residues) supports this interpretation at Karahan Tepe.
- However, the head-emerging-from-stone motif is **without direct parallel** in any other PPN site — making cross-cultural shamanic analogies particularly speculative for this unique iconographic element.

### 3.3 Connection to Younger Dryas
- The PPN sites of Upper Mesopotamia emerge in the immediate aftermath of the **Younger Dryas** cold event (c. 10,900–9700 BCE).
- Researchers hypothesize a causal link — the climatic stress of the Younger Dryas may have forced:
  - Social reorganization of previously mobile hunter-gatherer bands
  - Ritual innovation as a response to ecological crisis
  - Intensification of wild cereal processing (documented in groundstone technology)
  - Eventually, subsistence transformation toward agriculture
- The chronological coincidence is suggestive but causation is undemonstrated.
- Alternative models propose that the post-Younger Dryas climatic **amelioration** (improved conditions) provided the ecological opportunity for population growth and social complexity rather than the stress itself being the driver.

### 3.4 Undiscovered Sites
- The Taş Tepeler survey has identified 12+ sites; systematic satellite imagery analysis and GPR survey suggest potentially dozens more PPN sites across the Harran Plain and surrounding uplands. The full extent of the PPN monumental network remains unknown.

### 3.5 Symbolic "Death" of Structures
- The deliberate backfilling of pillar enclosures at both Göbekli and Karahan Tepe has been interpreted by researchers as a form of **ritual "killing" or "burial"** of the structure — analogous to the intentional breaking of objects ("killing" ceramics, grinding stones) documented in Neolithic contexts. If correct, the structures had finite ritual lifespans — built, used, ceremonially decommissioned, and then replaced with new construction. This cycle-of-renewal model would explain the multiple overlapping phases at both sites.
- Others interpret backfilling pragmatically — as site management to prepare a stable surface for new construction above, without attributing ceremonial significance to the act.

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source)

- Claims that Karahan Tepe represents technology from a "lost advanced civilization" or extraterrestrial intervention have no support; the lithic technology (stone-tool-based), construction methods (bedrock quarrying with stone and antler tools), and iconographic repertoire are fully consistent with documented PPN material culture.
- Proposals dating the site to 50,000+ years ago or naming it the "oldest temple in the world" (superseding Göbekli Tepe) misrepresent radiometric evidence.
- Assertions that Structure AB's human head is a "portal" or "stargate" are modern fantasy projections onto Neolithic iconography.
- Popular claims about ancient "knowledge of advanced mathematics" encoded in pillar arrangements lack any documented measurement or peer-reviewed analysis.

While the scale and precision remain impressive, they do not necessarily require invoking unknown technologies.

Measurement uncertainty and selective reporting of favorable data points are ongoing concerns.
