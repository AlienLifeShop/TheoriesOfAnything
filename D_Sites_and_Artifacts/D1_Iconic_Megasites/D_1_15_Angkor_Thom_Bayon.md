## QUICK SUMMARY

**Angkor Thom** ("Great City") — the last and most enduring capital city of the Khmer Empire — was built by **Jayavarman VII** (r. c. 1181–1218 CE) as a walled, moated urban complex of approximately **9 square kilometers** in present-day Cambodia. At its center stands the **Bayon**, one of the most visually stunning and iconographically complex temples in world architecture: a three-level pyramidal structure topped by **54 towers**, each bearing four colossal **serene stone faces** — approximately 216 faces in total (the exact count varies by survey) — gazing outward to the four cardinal directions. These enigmatic faces, slightly smiling and heavy-lidded, have been variously interpreted as representations of the **bodhisattva Avalokiteshvara** (the compassionate Buddha-to-be who delays his own enlightenment to help all sentient beings), of **Jayavarman VII himself** in his identity as the *devaraja* (god-king) merged with the bodhisattva, or of **Brahma** (the four-faced Hindu creator god, reflecting the temple's syncretic Hindu-Buddhist character). Angkor Thom represents a deliberate shift from the **Hindu** temple-mountain tradition of Angkor Wat (dedicated to Vishnu by Suryavarman II, c. 1150) to a **Mahayana Buddhist** cosmological vision — though the Bayon's iconography blends both traditions freely. The Bayon's 1.2 km of **bas-relief galleries** depict an extraordinary combination of military campaigns (Jayavarman VII's victory over the Cham invaders who sacked Angkor in 1177), scenes of **everyday life** (markets, cooking, fishing, cockfighting, childbirth — a vivid ethnographic record unparalleled in medieval Southeast Asian art), and mythological narratives (the Churning of the Ocean of Milk). Angkor Thom's **hydraulic infrastructure** — the massive moats, reservoirs (*baray*), and canal systems that sustained a population possibly exceeding 750,000 — has reshaped understanding of pre-industrial urbanism.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Angkor Thom — Urban Layout
- **Walled city**: square plan approximately **3 km per side** (9 km²), enclosed by an **8-meter-high laterite wall** and a **100-meter-wide moat** (possibly stocked with crocodiles according to Zhou Daguan's 1296 account)
- **Five monumental gates**: four axial gates plus the Victory Gate (east) — each gate is a 23-meter-high tower topped by the four-faced motif, flanked by causeways lined with 54 stone figures (gods/asuras) on each side, pulling a nāga (serpent) — representing the **Churning of the Ocean of Milk** narrative
- **Central features**: the Bayon (state temple, at the exact center), the Baphuon (pre-existing Hindu temple incorporated into the complex), the Terrace of the Elephants (300-meter ceremonial platform), the Terrace of the Leper King (carved underworld scenes), the Royal Palace enclosure, and Phimeanakas (the royal chapel with its legendary serpent-woman encounter described by Zhou Daguan)

### 1.2 The Bayon — Architecture and Iconography
- **Three-level structure**: outer gallery (rectangular, 156 × 141 m), inner gallery, and central massif with the 54 face-towers rising to a central tower (~43 m high)
- **Face towers**: each carrying **four faces** oriented to the cardinal directions — carved in low relief on massive sandstone blocks; the faces share a consistent style: broad forehead, lowered eyelids, thick lips with slight upward curve ("the Bayon smile"), wide nose; total face count estimated at **200–216** depending on survey
- **Bas-reliefs**: the outer gallery contains approximately **11,000 figures** in its 1.2 km of carved panels — divided into:
  - **Historical scenes**: naval battles against the Cham on the Tonle Sap (depicting the Khmer-Cham war of 1177–1181), military processions, Khmer and Cham soldiers distinguished by hairstyle and equipment
  - **Everyday life scenes**: the most vivid and detailed genre scenes in all of Angkorian art — market commerce, fishing, food preparation, wrestling matches, chess players, women giving birth attended by midwives, a man climbing a tree to escape a tiger, cockfights with spectators betting
  - **Mythological panels**: the inner gallery depicts Hindu deities and the Churning of the Ocean of Milk

### 1.3 Jayavarman VII and the Buddhist Turn
- Jayavarman VII is one of the most **prolific builders** in history — in addition to Angkor Thom and the Bayon, he constructed **Ta Prohm** (a Buddhist monastery with 12,640 dependents), **Preah Khan** (a Buddhist university and temple complex), **Banteay Kdei**, **Neak Pean** (an island healing temple), and an extensive network of roads, bridges, and hospitals (*ārogyaśālā*)
- His adoption of **Mahayana Buddhism** as state religion (replacing the Shaivite Hinduism of predecessors) was revolutionary in Khmer context — inscriptions describe him as "suffering the illnesses of his subjects more than his own" and modeling himself on the bodhisattva ideal of universal compassion
- Subsequent Hindu revivalists (beginning under Jayavarman VIII, late 13th century) systematically **defaced Buddhist imagery** throughout Angkor — thousands of Buddha images were chiseled into Hindu deities or hermits, though the Bayon's face-towers were too monumental to destroy

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Identity of the Faces
- The scholarly debate over the identity of the Bayon faces remains unresolved:
  - **Avalokiteshvara hypothesis** (most widely accepted): the faces represent the bodhisattva of compassion gazing compassionately in all directions — consistent with Jayavarman VII's Mahayana Buddhist program; supported by inscriptional evidence linking Jayavarman VII to Avalokiteshvara and by the four-directional orientation matching Buddhist cosmological symbolism
  - **Jayavarman VII as devaraja**: the faces may represent the king himself — an assertion of royal omniscience and omnipresence, the king's face gazing over his entire realm; the *devaraja* concept (god-king) could apply in Buddhist as well as Hindu framework
  - **Brahma hypothesis**: the four faces recall the four-faced Hindu creator god Brahma — reflecting the Buddhist-Hindu syncretism that characterized Angkorian religion
  - Most scholars now favor a **blended interpretation**: Jayavarman VII as living embodiment of Avalokiteshvara, his royal face identical to the bodhisattva's face

### 2.2 Hydraulic Urbanism
- Recent LiDAR surveys (Damian Evans, 2012–2015) have revealed that **Greater Angkor** was the **largest pre-industrial urban complex** in the world — extending over approximately **1,000 km²** with a sprawling network of reservoirs, canals, rice paddies, roads, and residential mounds
  - The system's vulnerability: over-engineering of the hydraulic network may have contributed to Angkor's eventual decline — environmental degradation, siltation, and the inability to maintain an increasingly complex infrastructure under resource stress

### 2.3 Zhou Daguan's Account
- **Zhou Daguan** (1296–1297): a Chinese diplomat who spent approximately one year at Angkor Thom and wrote a detailed account (*The Customs of Cambodia*) — the only surviving eyewitness description of the living capital; describes the Bayon, the royal court, social structure, trade, festivals, religion, and daily life; invaluable for interpreting the archaeological remains

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Population Estimates
- Population estimates for Greater Angkor range from **500,000 to over 1,000,000** — LiDAR data supports the higher end of this range based on residential density, but precise demographic reconstruction is difficult

### 3.2 Climate Change and Collapse
- The role of **prolonged drought** (documented in tree-ring data from Southeast Asian dendrochronology — Buckley et al. 2010) in Angkor's decline in the 14th–15th centuries is credible but the relative weight of climate, warfare (Thai invasions), overexploitation, and the shift of trade routes to coastal ports remains debated
