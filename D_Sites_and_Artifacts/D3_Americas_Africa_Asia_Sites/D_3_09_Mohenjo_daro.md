## QUICK SUMMARY

**Mohenjo-daro** ("Mound of the Dead" in Sindhi) — located in present-day Sindh province, Pakistan — is the largest and best-preserved urban center of the **Indus Valley Civilization (IVC)**, one of the three great Bronze Age civilizations alongside Mesopotamia and Egypt. At its peak (c. 2500–1900 BCE), Mohenjo-daro covered approximately **250 hectares** and housed an estimated **30,000–40,000 inhabitants**, making it one of the largest cities in the ancient world. Together with **Harappa** (in Punjab, Pakistan), **Dholavira** (Gujarat, India), and **Rakhigarhi** (Haryana, India), Mohenjo-daro exemplifies the IVC's remarkable achievements in **urban planning, hydraulic engineering, and standardization** — achievements all the more striking because the civilization apparently lacked the monumental royal or religious architecture, militaristic iconography, and royal propaganda that characterize contemporary Mesopotamian and Egyptian civilizations. The city's **grid-planned streets**, **standardized baked-brick construction** (bricks in a consistent 1:2:4 ratio), **sophisticated drainage system** (every house connected to covered street drains — the most advanced sanitation system in the ancient world), the enigmatic **Great Bath** (the earliest known public water tank/ritual bathing structure), and the **uniformity of weights and measures** across the entire IVC zone (stretching from Shortugai in Afghanistan to Daimabad in Maharashtra) suggest a level of **administrative coordination** unprecedented for the 3rd millennium BCE — yet achieved apparently without a centralized monarchy, standing army, or decipherable written language.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Site Layout and Urban Design
- **Two major sectors**: the elevated "Citadel" (western mound, ~12 m above the plain, approximately 200 × 400 m) and the "Lower City" (eastern area, much larger, residential and commercial)
- **Grid street plan**: major streets oriented roughly north-south and east-west, intersecting at right angles; main streets up to ~10 m wide; residential blocks divided by narrower lanes; houses entered from side lanes rather than main streets (suggesting concern for privacy and noise)
- **Standardized baked bricks**: fired mudbricks in the ratio **1:2:4** (approximately 7 × 14 × 28 cm for standard size) used consistently throughout the city and across the entire IVC zone — a remarkable feat of standardization suggesting centralized quality control
- **Multi-story houses**: homes typically two stories, with interior courtyards, private wells (over 700 wells identified at Mohenjo-daro alone), private bathrooms with paved floors, and seated latrines draining into the street system

### 1.2 Drainage and Water Management
- **Street-level drainage**: nearly every house had a connection to **covered brick drains** running beneath the streets — settling pits (soak pits/cesspits) at regular intervals for maintenance access; drains eventually fed into larger channels leading outside the city
  - This system has no parallel in the contemporary ancient world — Mesopotamian cities had open gutters at best; Roman sewer systems (cloaca maxima, etc.) are 2,000 years later
- **The Great Bath** (Citadel mound): a waterproofed, brick-lined tank approximately **12 × 7 × 2.4 m deep**, with steps descending on two sides, surrounded by a corridor and small chambers; the floor and walls sealed with **bitumen** to prevent leakage; a large drain for emptying; no contemporary artifact explains its function, but ritual purification bathing (analogous to later Hindu water rituals) is the most common interpretation
- **Private wells**: unusually abundant — over 700 identified (one per ~3 houses), suggesting that clean water access was a priority; some wells were public, accessible from the street

### 1.3 Material Culture
- **Seals**: thousands of **steatite stamp seals** bearing Indus script characters and animal motifs (unicorn bull, elephant, rhinoceros, tiger, zebu, water buffalo) — used for administrative/commercial purposes (sealing bales of goods); the "unicorn" motif (a single-horned bovine in profile) is the most common
- **Standardized weights**: cubical chert weights in a **binary-decimal** system (ratios 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64... then shifting to decimal: 160, 320, 640...) — identical weights found across the entire IVC territory from Afghanistan to Gujarat, suggesting integrated trade and administration
- **Indus Script**: approximately 4,000 inscriptions known, typically very short (average ~5 signs), found on seals, pottery, copper tablets, and other objects — **undeciphered** despite over 100 attempts; the brevity of texts has led scholars (Farmer, Sproat, Witzel 2004) to question whether it represents a true writing system (i.e., encoding spoken language) or a non-linguistic symbol system

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Political Organization
- The IVC's political structure remains one of its greatest mysteries — unlike Mesopotamia and Egypt, there are:
  - **No palaces** identified with certainty (no structure clearly identifiable as a royal residence)
  - **No royal tombs** (burials are comparatively simple and egalitarian)
  - **No monumental royal art** (no images of kings, conquest scenes, or propaganda)
  - **No evidence of warfare** (no weapons caches, defensive fortifications are ambiguous, no depictions of battles)
- Interpretations range from: **corporate governance** (Kenoyer 2008 — rule by mercantile/priestly elite without centralized monarchy), to **heterarchy** (decentralized, council-based leadership), to the suggestion that governing structures existed but used perishable materials (wood, textiles) that have not survived

### 2.2 Decline and Abandonment
- Mohenjo-daro was gradually abandoned over the period c. **1900–1700 BCE** — the decline of the IVC is now understood as a **multi-causal process**:
  - **River changes**: shifts in the course of the Ghaggar-Hakra river system (the now-dry "Sarasvati") and possibly the Indus itself, disrupting agriculture and trade
  - **Climate change**: weakening of the Indian Summer Monsoon documented in paleoclimate records
  - **Trade disruption**: collapse of the Mesopotamian trade network (the IVC's principal export market)
  - The earlier theory of **Aryan invasion** destroying the IVC is largely abandoned — there is no archaeological evidence of invasion or military destruction at Mohenjo-daro; the famous "massacre" skeletons (Wheeler 1947) have been reinterpreted as scattered unburied dead from the late occupation, not evidence of a single violent event

### 2.3 Religious Life
- In the absence of deciphered texts, IVC religion must be reconstructed from material culture alone — the **"Priest-King"** (a small soapstone bust from Mohenjo-daro showing a bearded man with a trefoil-patterned cloak and headband) is the most famous figurine but its actual function is unknown
- A seal showing a seated figure in a cross-legged posture surrounded by animals has been interpreted as a "Proto-Shiva" or "Proto-Pashupati" (Marshall 1931) — suggesting continuity with later Hindu traditions, though this identification is heavily debated

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Population of Greater IVC
- Population estimates for the entire IVC at its peak range from **1–5 million** — it was the most extensive of the three Bronze Age civilizations in terms of geographic spread (c. 1.25 million km²), though individual cities were smaller than contemporary Ur or Memphis

### 3.2 Connection to Vedic Sarasvati
- The identification of the Ghaggar-Hakra paleochannel with the Rig Vedic **Sarasvati** river remains debated — if correct, it would link IVC sites to the geographical setting of the earliest Vedic texts, with significant implications for the Indo-Aryan migration/homeland debate

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Nuclear Destruction
- **[DEBUNKED]** Claims (originating in David Davenport's *Atomic Destruction 2000 B.C.*, 1979) that Mohenjo-daro was destroyed by an **ancient nuclear explosion** (citing "vitrified" bricks and radioactive skeletons) are entirely false — no vitrified materials or anomalous radioactivity have been found at the site by any credentialed excavation team
