## QUICK SUMMARY

**Sigiriya** ("Lion Rock") — a massive column of volcanic rock rising approximately **200 meters (660 feet)** above the surrounding plains in the central Matale District of Sri Lanka — is one of the most dramatic archaeological sites in South Asia. Transformed by King **Kashyapa I** (r. 477–495 CE) into a fortified royal palace complex of extraordinary ambition, Sigiriya integrates a **summit palace** (on a plateau of approximately 1.6 hectares at the rock's top), a series of **terraced gardens** cascading down the western face, an elaborate system of **water gardens** at the base (featuring some of the oldest known landscaped garden designs, with symmetric pools, islands, gravity-fed fountains, and underground conduits), and the famous **frescoes** — the "Sigiriya Maidens" or "Cloud Maidens" (*Apsaras*), painted in a sheltered pocket of the rock face approximately 100 meters up. The monumental entrance to the summit was through a colossal **lion gateway** — visitors passed between the paws of an enormous brick-and-plaster lion sculpted over the natural rock; only the paws survive today, but the lion's jaws originally framed the stairway. According to the *Cūḷavaṁsa* (the continuation of the Sri Lankan Pali chronicle *Mahāvaṁsa*), Kashyapa seized the throne by patricide (killing his father King Dhatusena, who had built the Kalawewa reservoir), then moved from the traditional capital Anuradhapura to Sigiriya to defend against his exiled brother Moggallana; after Moggallana's return with an army, Kashyapa was defeated and the site was abandoned as a royal residence, becoming a **Buddhist monastery** from the 5th century until approximately the 14th century. UNESCO inscribed Sigiriya as a World Heritage Site in 1982, recognizing it as a masterwork combining urban planning, hydraulic engineering, garden design, and artistic achievement.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 The Rock and Summit Palace
- **Geological formation**: Sigiriya is a **magma plug** from an extinct volcano — a column of harder rock exposed by the erosion of the surrounding softer material; the nearly vertical sides made it a natural fortress
- **Summit area**: approximately **1.6 hectares** (roughly 370 × 170 m) — excavations by H.C.P. Bell (1898) and the Cultural Triangle Project (1982 onward) revealed:
  - A **royal palace complex** with brick and timber structures on stone foundations
  - A **swimming pool/cistern** cut into the living rock at the summit, still holding water during the rainy season
  - Audience halls, gardens, and defensive walls along the summit perimeter
  - Evidence of sophisticated **water distribution**: cisterns and channels carved into the rock surface collected rainwater and distributed it through the summit complex

### 1.2 Water Gardens
- The symmetrically planned water gardens at Sigiriya's western base are the **best-preserved ancient gardens in Asia**:
  - **Three tiers**: (1) the outer moated enclosure with earth ramparts, (2) a middle zone of geometrically planned pools and islands, (3) the inner terraced gardens ascending the boulder-strewn base of the rock
  - **Symmetrical pools**: interconnected rectangular pools with limestone-lined channels, serpentine brick-border channels, and underground conduits
  - **Gravity-fed fountains**: the oldest known fountains in South Asia — pipes beneath the gardens used hydraulic pressure from higher pools to create **jet fountains** that still function during the rainy season when sufficient water pressure builds up; the engineering demonstrates understanding of hydrostatic principles
  - **Design symmetry**: the entire western garden complex is organized on a central east-west axis aligned with the rock summit — one of the earliest known examples of fully planned axial garden design

### 1.3 Frescoes
- Painted in a sheltered gallery approximately **100 m above the base** on the western face — originally perhaps 500+ figures spread across an area approximately 140 m long and 40 m high; **22 figures survive today** (damage from weather, vandalism, and a deliberate attack in 1967)
- The surviving frescoes depict beautiful women from the waist up, bare-breasted, adorned with elaborate jewelry and headdresses, some holding flowers and trays of offerings — painted in the "Sigiriya style" using a technique combining wet and dry fresco application
- **Identity debated**: variously interpreted as (a) apsaras (celestial nymphs), (b) court ladies, (c) lightning goddesses, (d) queens, or (e) representations of clouds surrounding the mountain (Paranavitana's "cloud maiden" hypothesis) — no inscription definitively identifies them

### 1.4 Mirror Wall and Graffiti
- Below the fresco gallery, a polished plaster wall (the **"Mirror Wall"**) was once so highly burnished that the king could see his reflection — it retains traces of its original polish
- The wall bears approximately **1,800 inscriptions** (*Sigiri Graffiti*) dating from the 6th to the 14th century CE — visitors' comments, often poetic verses inspired by the frescoes; these constitute some of the earliest known examples of **Sinhalese poetry** and provide a unique record of medieval Sri Lankan literary culture

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Kashyapa's Motivations
- The *Cūḷavaṁsa* portrays Kashyapa as a usurper and patricide — but scholars question whether the chronicle (written by Buddhist monks who opposed Kashyapa) may be **politically biased**:
  - An alternative interpretation suggests Kashyapa may have had a legitimate claim to the throne and chose Sigiriya not solely for defense but to create a new symbolic center — the rock transformed into a representation of **Mount Meru** (the cosmic mountain at the center of Buddhist-Hindu cosmology), with the lion gate and summit palace representing the divine realm
  - The water gardens may represent the pleasure gardens of the celestial realm, and the frescoes may depict divine beings — making Sigiriya a **cosmological landscape** rather than merely a military fortress

### 2.2 Post-Kashyapa Monastic Use
- After Kashyapa's defeat (495 CE), Sigiriya was converted to a Buddhist monastery that continued functioning for approximately **800 years** — caves at the base and on the rock face contain meditation cells, drip-ledge rock shelters, and Buddhist inscriptions; the site's transformation from royal sinfulness to monastic piety may be deliberate, with the Buddhist community reinterpreting the site's meaning

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Pre-Kashyapa Use
- The rock shelters at Sigiriya's base show evidence of **prehistoric habitation** (possibly 3000 BCE onward) — Buddhist monastic occupation may have preceded Kashyapa's royal transformation by centuries; scholars suggest Kashyapa expanded an existing monastic complex rather than building from scratch

### 3.2 Holistic Design Vision
- The proposal that the entire Sigiriya complex (water gardens, boulder gardens, terraced gardens, frescoes, mirror wall, lion gate, and summit palace) was conceived as a single integrated design — a **gesamtkunstwerk** expressing a coherent cosmological-political vision — is compelling but difficult to confirm archaeologically without evidence of a master plan
