## QUICK SUMMARY

**Sanchi** — a hilltop complex near the town of Sanchi Nagar in **Madhya Pradesh, central India** — is the finest surviving ensemble of **early Buddhist monumental architecture** and one of the most important Buddhist pilgrimage and art-historical sites in the world. The Great Stupa (**Stupa 1**) at Sanchi is the oldest known **stone structure** in India with a continuous construction history traceable from the **3rd century BCE** (founded by Emperor **Ashoka**, r. c. 268–232 BCE) through multiple enlargements and embellishments reaching their final form under the **Sunga** (2nd–1st centuries BCE) and **Satavahana** dynasties (1st centuries BCE–CE). The Great Stupa is a hemispherical dome (*anda*) of brick and stone approximately **16.5 m (54 ft) in height** and **36.6 m (120 ft) in diameter**, surrounded by a stone railing (*vedika*) with four monumental gateways (*toranas*) at the cardinal points — these gateways are among the finest examples of **early Indian sculpture**, covered with elaborate bas-relief carvings depicting scenes from the life of the Buddha, Jataka tales (stories of the Buddha's previous lives), and representations of plants, animals, celestial beings (*yakshas* and *yakshinis*), and symbolic Buddhist motifs (the wheel, the Bodhi tree, footprints — notably, the Buddha himself is **never depicted in human form** in these earliest reliefs, represented instead only through **aniconic symbols**). The complex includes several smaller stupas (Stupas 2 and 3), monasteries (*viharas*), temples, and the famous **Ashoka Pillar** (a polished sandstone column originally crowned by the four-lion capital that became the emblem of the Indian Republic, though the capital at Sanchi is now lost — the surviving Sarnath lion capital is the one adopted as the national emblem). Sanchi was abandoned after the decline of Buddhism in India (c. 12th–13th centuries CE), rediscovered in 1818, and became a **UNESCO World Heritage Site** in 1989.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Historical Development
- **Ashokan foundation** (c. 3rd century BCE): Emperor Ashoka (Maurya dynasty) is credited with founding the original stupa — a simple brick hemispherical mound constructed over Buddhist relics; Ashoka's connection to Sanchi may derive from his having served as governor of adjacent Vidisha (Besnagar) before becoming emperor; his queen Devi was reportedly from the Vidisha area
- **Sunga enlargement** (c. 2nd century BCE): the original brick stupa was **doubled in diameter** by encasing it in stone-faced masonry; the stone railing (*vedika*) and the processional path (*pradakshina patha*) around the base were added during this period
- **Torana gateways** (c. 1st century BCE – early 1st century CE): the four ornamental stone gateways were erected during the Sunga and Satavahana periods — these are the most celebrated features of Sanchi, each gateway consisting of two square pillars supporting three superimposed horizontal architraves (crossbars), all densely carved with narrative and decorative reliefs

### 1.2 Stupa Architecture
- The Great Stupa (Stupa 1) demonstrates the canonical elements of early Buddhist stupa design:
  - **Anda** (dome): hemispherical solid masonry dome approximately 16.5 m high, 36.6 m in diameter — originally covered with a thick coat of plaster, which was likely whitewashed or painted
  - **Harmika**: a square stone railing/platform at the summit of the dome, symbolizing the abode of the gods or the boundary between the earthly and celestial realms
  - **Chattra** (umbrella): a three-tiered stone parasol rising from the center of the harmika — symbolizing the three jewels of Buddhism (Buddha, Dharma, Sangha) and representing royalty/spiritual authority
  - **Vedika** (railing): a stone circumambulatory fence surrounding the base — worshippers would walk clockwise (*pradakshina*) around the stupa as a meditative and devotional practice
  - **Medhi** (terrace): a raised platform at the stupa's base providing a processional walkway

### 1.3 The Toranas (Gateways)
- Each of the four gateways is approximately **10–11 m tall** and is covered on all surfaces with high-relief and bas-relief carving:
  - **Narrative panels**: scenes from the Jataka tales (the Shyama Jataka, Vessantara Jataka, and others), events from the Buddha's life (the Great Departure, the Enlightenment, the First Sermon, the Miracle at Shravasti), and episodes from Ashoka's reign
  - **Aniconic representation**: in these earliest reliefs, the Buddha is never shown in human form — his presence is indicated exclusively through symbols: the **Bodhi tree** (the Enlightenment), **footprints** (his presence/journey), the **wheel** (*dharmachakra*, the First Sermon), an **empty throne** (his sovereignty), the **riderless horse** (the Great Departure), and a **parasol** (his dignity)
  - **Bracket figures**: sensual *salabhanjika* (tree-spirit) figures of women grasping tree branches serve as bracket supports on the gateway pillars — among the most admired sculptures of ancient India
  - **Inscriptions**: donor inscriptions on the gateways record the names and occupations of individuals and guilds who funded specific carvings — providing valuable sociological data about early Buddhist patronage

### 1.4 Other Structures
- **Stupa 2** (c. 2nd century BCE): located at the base of the hill — notable for its railing medallions with some of the earliest narrative carvings at Sanchi; contained relics of several early Buddhist teachers
- **Stupa 3** (c. 2nd–1st century BCE): smaller stupa with a single torana gateway; relic deposits attributed to Shariputra and Maudgalyayana (two chief disciples of the Buddha)
- **Ashoka Pillar**: a polished Chunar sandstone column (the Sanchi capital is now fragmentary — preserved portions are in the Sanchi museum); an inscription in Brahmi script records Ashoka's edict against schism in the Buddhist community
- **Monasteries and temples**: several later structures (Gupta period, c. 5th century CE) including Temple 17 — a flat-roofed, four-columned temple considered one of the earliest structural (non-rock-cut) stone temples in India

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Aniconic Phase Debate
- The absence of anthropomorphic Buddha images at Sanchi and other early Buddhist sites (Bharhut, Amaravati in their earliest phases) has been the subject of debate:
  - **Traditional view** (Foucher, Coomaraswamy): early Buddhists observed a **prohibition against depicting the Buddha in human form** — the aniconic symbols were not merely conventional but reflected a deliberate theological/aesthetic choice
  - **Revisionist view** (Huntington, 1990): the scenes at Sanchi may not depict the historical Buddha at all but rather rituals, festivals, and devotional practices — the "absence" of the Buddha may be an interpretive error
  - **Current consensus** leans toward the traditional aniconic interpretation but acknowledges the complexity of early Buddhist art's relationship to text and practice

### 2.2 Patronage and Social History
- The gateway **donor inscriptions** (over 600) reveal that Sanchi was supported by a broad base of donors — monks, nuns, laymen, laywomen, guilds (ivory workers, merchants), and officials — not exclusively by royal patronage
- This pattern demonstrates that **communal donation** was central to Buddhist monumental construction — individual panels and sculptures were sponsored by specific donors whose names are permanently recorded

### 2.3 Relic Significance
- The relics deposited within the stupas (bone fragments, small vessels, inscribed relic caskets) are identified by inscription as belonging to specific early Buddhist teachers:
  - Stupa 3 contained relic caskets of **Shariputra** and **Maudgalyayana** — two of the Buddha's foremost disciples
  - These identifications, based on contemporary inscriptions, are generally accepted as authentic records of early Buddhist reliquary practice (the relics were distributed to multiple sites as a devotional practice)

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Pre-Buddhist Sacred Site
- The Sanchi hilltop may have been a **pre-existing sacred site** (possibly associated with yakshas/tree-spirit worship) before Ashoka's Buddhist foundation — the presence of *yaksha* and *yakshini* imagery on the gateways and the integration of tree-spirit symbolism could reflect continuity with earlier religious practices

### 3.2 Astronomical Orientation
- The cardinal orientation of the four toranas has suggested to researchers a possible **astronomical alignment** function — but the cardinal orientation is a standard feature of stupa design prescribed by Buddhist cosmological symbolism (the four directions corresponding to specific aspects of the Buddha's life), and specific astronomical functions have not been demonstrated

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Greek Origin of Indian Sculpture
- **[MISLEADING]** Claims that Indian sculptural art (including Sanchi) was introduced by Greeks (via the Gandhara school) are contradicted by the evidence — Sanchi's earliest carvings (2nd century BCE) predate or are contemporary with Gandharan Buddhist art, and their style is distinctly Indian (not Hellenistic); the Gandhara school represents a separate, parallel development in the northwest

### 4.2 Hindu Origin
- **[UNSUPPORTED]** Claims that Sanchi was originally a Hindu site converted to Buddhism have no archaeological support — the site is Buddhist from its earliest identified phase
