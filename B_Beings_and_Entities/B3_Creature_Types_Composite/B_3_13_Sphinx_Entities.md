## QUICK SUMMARY

The **Sphinx** — a composite creature with a lion's body and a human (or divine) head — appears as a guardian being across multiple civilizations of the ancient world, functioning as a **liminal protector** stationed at thresholds between the sacred and profane, the living and the dead, the known and the unknown. The Egyptian sphinx (*shesep-ankh*, "living image") is predominantly a **benevolent guardian**, represented in hundreds of statues from the Great Sphinx of Giza (c. 2500 BCE) to the ram-headed sphinxes of Karnak. The Greek sphinx (*Σφίγξ*) is a **dangerous, riddling creature** — female, winged, and deadly — most famous from the Oedipus myth where she devours those who cannot answer her riddle. Mesopotamia produced analogous figures: the **lamassu** (human-headed winged bull) and **shedu** (human-headed winged lion) guarding palace entrances. Hittite, Persian, and South Asian traditions feature their own sphinx-type guardians at temple and palace gateways. This document treats the sphinx as an **entity class** — analyzing its roles as guardian, threshold-keeper, riddler, and symbol of royal/divine power across cultures — distinguished from [D_3_15](../../D_Sites_and_Artifacts/D3_Americas_Africa_Asia_Sites/D_3_15_Great_Zimbabwe.md) which focuses on the Great Sphinx as a specific site.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Egyptian Sphinxes
- The Egyptian sphinx is attested from the **Old Kingdom** (c. 2600 BCE) onward — the Great Sphinx at Giza is the oldest and largest (details in D_4_01)
- **Types**: (a) Androsphinx — human head, lion body (most common, representing the pharaoh); (b) Criosphinx — ram head, lion body (associated with Amun, as in the avenue at Karnak); (c) Hieracosphinx — falcon head, lion body (associated with Horus)
- Function: **Apotropaic guardian** — sphinxes guard temple entrances, necropoli, and processional ways, embodying royal power merged with leonine strength
- The Egyptian sphinx is almost always **male** (representing the king) and **benevolent** — a protector, not a threat
- Notable examples: Avenue of Sphinxes at Luxor/Karnak (~1,350 sphinxes), Hatshepsut's granite sphinx at the Met, Amenhotep III's sphinxes

### 1.2 Greek Sphinx
- The Greek sphinx (*Σφίγξ*, from *σφίγγω*, "to squeeze/strangle") is **female**, **winged**, with a lion's body, woman's head, and sometimes a serpent's tail
- **The Oedipus myth**: The Sphinx sits outside Thebes, posing a riddle — "What walks on four legs in the morning, two at noon, and three in the evening?" Oedipus answers correctly ("Man"), and the Sphinx destroys herself
  - First attested in literary form in Sophocles' *Oedipus Tyrannus* (c. 429 BCE); the riddle appears in later scholiasts and the pseudo-Apollodoran *Bibliotheca*
- Artistic representations appear from the **Late Geometric period** (c. 750 BCE) on pottery, sculpture, and architectural relief
- The **Naxian Sphinx** at Delphi (c. 560 BCE): A monumental sphinx atop a tall Ionic column dedicated by the people of Naxos — guarding the oracle site
- Greek sphinxes frequently appear on **funerary monuments** as grave-guardians in Attica (6th–5th century BCE)

### 1.3 Mesopotamian Lamassu and Shedu
- **Lamassu**: Human-headed winged bulls flanking Neo-Assyrian palace gateways (Nineveh, Nimrud, Khorsabad, c. 900–612 BCE)
  - Typically shown with five legs (appearing to stand still from the front and walk from the side)
  - Inscribed with protective texts
- **Shedu**: Human-headed winged lions — functionally equivalent to lamassu
- Both serve as **supernatural palace guardians** — their colossal scale (up to 16 feet tall, 40 tons) projects divine-royal authority

### 1.4 Hittite and Persian Sphinxes
- **Hittite sphinxes**: Gate guardians at Hattusa (Yerkapı/Sphinx Gate, c. 14th century BCE) and Alaca Höyük — female-headed, sometimes with wings
- **Persian/Achaemenid sphinxes**: Appear on column capitals and glazed-brick reliefs at Susa and Persepolis — winged, crowned, combining Egyptian and Mesopotamian elements and reflecting the empire's multicultural synthesis

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Transmission and Transformation
- The sphinx concept likely **originated in Egypt** (earliest examples, 3rd millennium BCE) and was transmitted to the eastern Mediterranean during the **Bronze Age** through trade and diplomatic contact (Dessenne 1957)
- The transformation from benevolent Egyptian guardian to dangerous Greek riddler represents a fundamental cultural reinterpretation:
  - **Egypt**: Sphinx = royal authority incarnate (king's head on lion's body = pharaoh as living god)
  - **Greece**: Sphinx = dangerous otherworldly knowledge-keeper (feminine, wild, fatal) — the riddle theme may reflect Greek anxieties about encounters with the monstrous "other" (Moret 1902; Demisch 1977)

### 2.2 Liminal Symbolism
- Across all traditions, sphinxes are **threshold beings** — positioned at gates, entrances, bridges between worlds
- They embody the fusion of **human intelligence** with **animal power** — the guard at the boundary who requires proof of belonging before granting passage
- This liminal function connects sphinxes to broader guardian-entity patterns: cherubim at Eden's gate, Janus at Roman doorways, temple door-guardians in Hindu-Buddhist architecture

### 2.3 South and Southeast Asian Parallels
- **Purushamriga/Purushamirugam** (India): Human-headed lion figures in Hindu and Buddhist temple architecture — gate guardians at South Indian temples
- **Manussiha** (Myanmar/Burma): Human-faced lion (*manuthiha*) — guardian figure in Burmese Buddhist cosmology
- Whether these represent independent development or diffusion through trade networks is debated

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Initiatory Function
- The Greek sphinx's riddle has been interpreted as encoding an **initiatory test** — the hero must demonstrate wisdom (knowledge of human nature) to pass from one stage of life to another
- Whether this reflects actual ritual practice or is a narrative-philosophical motif is unknown

### 3.2 Astronomical Readings
- Researchers have associated the sphinx form (lion body) with the zodiacal sign **Leo**, suggesting an astral dimension — particularly for the Great Sphinx at Giza
- Evidence for deliberate Leo-symbolism in Egyptian sphinx creation remains circumstantial
