## QUICK SUMMARY

The modern era of official U.S. government UAP investigation began in **2007** when the **Defense Intelligence Agency (DIA)** established the **Advanced Aerospace Weapon System Applications Program (AAWSAP)**, later reorganized as the **Advanced Aerospace Threat Identification Program (AATIP)**. The program's existence was revealed publicly in December 2017 through coordinated reporting by the *New York Times*, *Washington Post*, and *Politico* — accompanied by the release of three U.S. Navy gun-camera videos (FLIR1/Gimbal/GoFast). This disclosure triggered a cascade of institutional developments: the **Unidentified Aerial Phenomena Task Force (UAPTF)** was established under the Office of Naval Intelligence in 2020; Congress mandated a UAP assessment from the **Director of National Intelligence (DNI)** in its 2021 Intelligence Authorization Act; the **All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office (AARO)** was established in July 2022 under the Department of Defense; and the **2023 National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA)** created the most sweeping UAP transparency provisions in U.S. history, including whistleblower protections, requests for a UAP records review board, and congressional hearings. This document traces the institutional genealogy of U.S. government UAP programs from AAWSAP through AARO, examining how each program was established, funded, staffed, and how its findings have shaped policy.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 AAWSAP / AATIP (2007–2012)
- **AAWSAP** was established in 2007 at the DIA, with **$22 million** in funding secured by Senator Harry Reid (D-NV) through a Special Access Program (SAP) budget line:
  - The program was directed by **James Lacatski** (DIA) and contracted primarily to **Bigelow Aerospace Advanced Space Studies (BAASS)**, owned by Robert Bigelow — a defense contractor and UAP researcher
  - AAWSAP contracted 38 **Defense Intelligence Reference Documents (DIRDs)** — scientific/technical papers on advanced physics topics including warp drive, metamaterials, invisibility cloaking, traversable wormholes, and other frontiers — authored by academic physicists including Hal Puthoff, Eric Davis, and others
  - **Luis Elizondo** has stated he served as AATIP director from approximately 2010 until his resignation in October 2017, when he left the Pentagon in protest over what he described as excessive secrecy and lack of attention to UAP as a potential threat
  - The Pentagon has given inconsistent statements about Elizondo's role — initially denying, then partially acknowledging his involvement with the program

### 1.2 The December 2017 Disclosure
- On December 16, 2017, the *New York Times* (Helene Cooper, Ralph Blumenthal, Leslie Kean), *Washington Post*, and *Politico* simultaneously published stories revealing AATIP's existence:
  - Three U.S. Navy infrared videos were released: **FLIR1** (2004 Nimitz/Tic Tac encounter), **Gimbal** (2015 Roosevelt encounter), and **GoFast** (2015 Roosevelt encounter)
  - The videos showed objects exhibiting anomalous flight characteristics — no visible propulsion, rapid acceleration, apparent transmedium movement
  - The DoD officially confirmed the videos' authenticity in **April 2020**, stating they depicted "unidentified aerial phenomena"

### 1.3 UAPTF (2020–2022)
- In August 2020, Deputy Secretary of Defense David Norquist approved the establishment of the **Unidentified Aerial Phenomena Task Force (UAPTF)** under the Office of Naval Intelligence:
  - The UAPTF was tasked with investigating UAP encountered by U.S. military personnel
  - In June 2021, the **ODNI Preliminary Assessment** (mandated by Congress) was released — documenting **144 UAP reports** from U.S. government sources between 2004 and 2021, with only **1 resolved** (a deflating balloon). The report identified five potential explanatory categories: airborne clutter, natural atmospheric phenomena, USG or industry developmental programs, foreign adversary systems, and an "other" bin
  - The assessment explicitly stated that UAP "clearly pose a safety of flight issue and may pose a challenge to U.S. national security"

### 1.4 AARO (2022–Present)
- In July 2022, the **All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office (AARO)** was established under the Office of the Under Secretary of Defense for Intelligence & Security:
  - Directed initially by **Dr. Sean Kirkpatrick** (physicist, intelligence community veteran)
  - AARO's mandate expanded beyond UAP to include **transmedium objects** and "anomalous, unidentified space, airborne, submerged, and transmedium objects"
  - AARO released a **Historical Record Report** (Volume I, March 2024) reviewing 80+ years of U.S. government involvement with UAP — concluding that most historical reports were misidentifications and that no evidence of extraterrestrial technology had been confirmed. **Volume II** is pending
  - Kirkpatrick resigned in December 2023, citing personal reasons — he was replaced by **Tim Phillips** as acting director

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 David Grusch Whistleblower Testimony (2023)
- **David Charles Grusch**, a former intelligence officer (NGA, NRO) and UAPTF/AARO briefer, filed a whistleblower complaint with the **Intelligence Community Inspector General (ICIG)** in 2023:
  - Grusch publicly stated that the U.S. government possesses **retrieved non-human origin materials and craft**, and has been running a decades-long **reverse-engineering program** outside congressional oversight
  - The ICIG found Grusch's complaint **"credible and urgent"** in July 2022 — but this determination relates to whistleblower process, not the truth of specific claims
  - Grusch testified under oath before the **House Oversight Committee** on July 26, 2023, alongside Lt. Commander Ryan Graves and retired Commander David Fravor
  - DoD and AARO have stated they have found no evidence substantiating claims of a crash-retrieval program — the factual dispute is ongoing

### 2.2 Congressional Engagement
- Congressional interest in UAP has intensified since 2017:
  - Classified briefings have been provided to members of the Senate Intelligence, Armed Services, and Appropriations committees
  - Senators Rubio, Gillibrand, Rounds, and Heinrich — and Representatives Burchett, Luna, and Moskowitz — have been particularly active in pushing for transparency
  - The bipartisan nature of UAP interest — spanning both parties and both chambers — is historically unusual and reflects the national-security framing of the issue
