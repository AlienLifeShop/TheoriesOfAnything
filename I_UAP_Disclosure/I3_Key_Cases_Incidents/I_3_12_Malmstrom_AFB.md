## QUICK SUMMARY

On **March 16, 1967**, at **Malmstrom Air Force Base** in Montana, ten Minuteman I intercontinental ballistic missiles (ICBMs) at **Echo Flight** went offline in rapid succession — their guidance and control systems registering "No-Go" faults almost simultaneously. A similar incident reportedly occurred approximately one week later at **Oscar Flight**, where an additional ten missiles went offline while base security personnel reported a glowing red UAP hovering near the launch control facility. These events — involving the temporary disabling of nuclear-armed strategic weapons during the Cold War — represent one of the most consequential alleged UAP-nuclear interactions on record. **Captain Robert Salas**, the deputy missile combat crew commander on duty at Oscar Flight, has become the most prominent public witness — testifying before multiple forums, including the National Press Club (2010) and congressional briefings. Official USAF records confirm the Echo Flight missile shutdown on March 16, 1967, and attribute it to an "electronic noise pulse" of unknown origin that simultaneously affected the logic coupler units of all ten missiles — an event Boeing engineers determined had an extremely low probability of occurring by chance. The cause remains officially unexplained. The incident is significant because it connects UAP activity to nuclear weapons security — a pattern reported across multiple nuclear facilities in the United States, Soviet Union, and United Kingdom.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Echo Flight Shutdown — March 16, 1967
- Official USAF records confirm the Echo Flight incident:
  - On the morning of March 16, 1967, all ten **Minuteman I missiles** at Echo Flight (341st Strategic Missile Wing, Malmstrom AFB) went to "No-Go" status in rapid sequence — within approximately 10 seconds
  - The shutdown was caused by "electronic noise pulses" affecting the logic coupler units in each launch facility — the couplers are the interface between the launch control center and the missiles
  - **Boeing** engineers investigated the failure and were unable to identify a conventional cause — tests replicated the failure mode (noise pulse injection → coupler shutdown) but could not explain what generated the pulses simultaneously across ten geographically dispersed launch facilities connected by hardened underground cables
  - The USAF Strategic Missile Wings' operational history records and unit histories confirm the incident
  - The missiles were restored to operational status within approximately one day

### 1.2 Official Documentation
- Multiple official documents reference the Echo Flight shutdown:
  - **341st Strategic Missile Wing unit history**, March 1967 — records the incident and investigation
  - Boeing's engineering analysis — concluded that simultaneous failure across ten dispersed sites connected by shielded cables was extremely improbable under any known electromagnetic interference scenario
  - The incident was reported through the chain of command as a significant anomaly — temporary loss of ten nuclear-armed ICBMs during the Cold War was a serious national security event
  - FOIA releases have made portions of the official documentation publicly available

### 1.3 Pattern — UAP and Nuclear Sites
- The Malmstrom incident is part of a broader pattern of reported UAP activity near nuclear facilities:
  - **U.S. nuclear weapons sites**: UAP sightings at nuclear facilities have been reported at multiple installations including: Minot AFB (1968), F.E. Warren AFB, Vandenberg AFB, Los Alamos National Laboratory, and Oak Ridge
  - **Soviet Union**: Soviet military records (partially disclosed after the Cold War) describe UAP encounters at nuclear missile sites in Ukraine and Kazakhstan — including a 1982 incident at Byelokoroviche where a UAP allegedly triggered a launch sequence initiation (subsequently overridden)
  - **United Kingdom**: the Rendlesham Forest incident (1980) occurred adjacent to RAF Bentwaters/Woodbridge — a base widely reported to have housed U.S. nuclear weapons
  - Robert Hastings (*UFOs and Nukes*, 2008) compiled testimony from over 150 former U.S. military personnel describing UAP encounters at nuclear weapons sites

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Oscar Flight Incident
- Captain **Robert Salas** reports that approximately one week after Echo Flight, a similar incident occurred at **Oscar Flight** (same wing):
  - Salas states he was the deputy missile combat crew commander on duty when security guards reported a glowing red oval object hovering above the front gate of the launch control facility
  - Shortly after the UAP report, missiles at Oscar Flight began going to "No-Go" status
  - The Oscar Flight incident is less fully documented than Echo Flight — researchers debate the exact timing and details
  - Salas has maintained his account consistently over decades, testified under oath, and obtained partial corroboration from other personnel — but the Oscar Flight UAP-shutdown connection has been questioned by researchers who argue the missile failures may have been unrelated to UAP reports

### 2.2 Salas and Other Witness Testimony
- Multiple military witnesses have come forward regarding Malmstrom and similar incidents:
  - **Robert Salas**: testified at the National Press Club (2010, 2013), submitted testimony to congressional offices, and published *Faded Giant* (2005) and *Unidentified* (2014)
  - **Robert Jamison**: former targeting team commander at Malmstrom, confirmed being dispatched to restart missiles after the Echo Flight incident
  - **David Schuur**: former missile crew member, confirmed discussions of the incident within the wing
  - The accumulation of witness testimony from multiple independent former officers strengthens the case, though no witness to both the UAP and the shutdown simultaneously is available for Echo Flight
