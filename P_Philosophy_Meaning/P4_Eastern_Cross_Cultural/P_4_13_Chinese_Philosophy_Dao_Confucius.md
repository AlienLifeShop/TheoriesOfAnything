## QUICK SUMMARY

**Chinese philosophy** encompasses one of the world's richest and longest-continuous intellectual traditions, spanning from the **Zhou dynasty** (~1046–256 BCE) to the present. The foundational period — the **Hundred Schools of Thought** (百家, *bǎijiā*) during the Spring and Autumn (771–476 BCE) and **Warring States** (475–221 BCE) periods — produced a remarkable proliferation of competing philosophical schools in circumstances analogous to classical Greece. **Confucianism** (Confucius/Kǒngzǐ, 551–479 BCE) emphasized moral self-cultivation, social harmony, ritual propriety (*lǐ*), humaneness (*rén*), and the ideal of the **junzi** (exemplary person/gentleman) — a virtue ethics grounded in family relationships and extended to the cosmos. **Daoism** (Laozi and Zhuangzi, ~4th century BCE) rejected Confucian social conventionalism in favor of alignment with the **Dao** (Way) — the ultimate, ineffable pattern of cosmic process — through **wu wei** (non-coercive action, effortless spontaneity) and return to natural simplicity. **Mohism** (Mozi, ~470–391 BCE) advocated **universal love** (*jiān ài*) and consequentialist reasoning — critiquing Confucian ritual and hierarchical love as wasteful and partial. **Legalism** (Han Feizi, Shang Yang) rejected moral cultivation entirely in favor of strict laws, punishments, and statecraft — arguing that human nature is selfish and only institutional compulsion produces social order. The tradition was transformed by the arrival of **Buddhism** (1st–3rd centuries CE), which introduced concepts of emptiness, mind-only, and karma, and eventually by the great synthesis of **Neo-Confucianism** (Zhu Xi, 1130–1200; Wang Yangming, 1472–1529), which integrated Confucian ethics, Daoist cosmology, and Buddhist psychological insights into a comprehensive metaphysical and moral system that dominated East Asian thought for nearly a millennium.

## 1. THE HUNDRED SCHOOLS PERIOD

### 1.1 Historical Context

- The breakdown of Zhou dynasty centralized authority created a political situation of competing states, social upheaval, and intellectual ferment — analogous to the Greek city-states; philosophical thinkers emerged as advisors to rulers, educators, and social critics
- The **Hundred Schools** label (from Sima Tan/Sima Qian, ~100 BCE) includes: Confucianism (Rújiā), Daoism (Dàojiā), Mohism (Mòjiā), Legalism (Fǎjiā), School of Names (Míngjiā — logicians), Yin-Yang School (Yīnyángjiā), Agriculturalists (Nóngjiā), Military Strategists (Bīngjiā), and others
- Key characteristic of Chinese philosophy: deeply **practical** — concerned with governance, moral self-cultivation, and social harmony rather than abstract metaphysics for its own sake (though metaphysics is present); philosophy is not separable from political theory and moral practice

## 2. CONFUCIUS AND EARLY CONFUCIANISM

### 2.1 Confucius (Kǒngzǐ, 551–479 BCE)

- Our main source is the **Analects** (*Lúnyǔ*) — a collection of sayings, dialogues, and anecdotes compiled by disciples after his death; Confucius was a teacher, political advisor, and moral philosopher from the state of Lu
- **Core concepts:**
  - **Rén** (仁, humaneness, benevolence, goodness): the supreme virtue — loving and caring for others; "a person of rén, wishing to establish themselves, also establishes others; wishing to develop themselves, also develops others" (*Analects* 6.30)
  - **Lǐ** (禮, ritual propriety, ceremony): the system of traditional rituals, customs, and social norms that structure harmonious human interaction; not mere external form but the embodiment of respect, care, and social consciousness
  - **Yì** (義, righteousness, appropriateness): moral rightness — doing what is appropriate and just in particular circumstances
  - **Junzi** (君子, exemplary person): the ethical ideal — one who cultivates virtue, practices ritual, treats others with humaneness, and leads by moral example rather than coercion
  - **Xiào** (孝, filial piety): devotion and respect toward parents and ancestors — the root of all virtue; "filial piety and brotherly respect are the root of humaneness" (*Analects* 1.2)

### 2.2 Governance by Moral Example

- Confucius opposed governance by punishment and force; the ruler should lead by moral example and virtue: "If you lead the people by means of regulations and keep order among them by punishments, they will flee from punishment but have no sense of shame. If you lead them by means of virtue and keep order among them by ritual, they will have a sense of shame and will also correct themselves" (*Analects* 2.3)
- The **rectification of names** (zhèngmíng): When names and reality diverge — when a prince is not princely, a father is not fatherly — social disorder results; restoring the correct relationship between names and their referents restores social harmony

## 3. MENCIUS AND XUNZI — HUMAN NATURE DEBATE

### 3.1 Mencius (Mèngzǐ, ~372–289 BCE)

- Mencius defended and developed Confucianism, arguing that human nature is **innately good** (*xìng shàn*): we are born with four "sprouts" (rudimentary moral feelings) — (1) compassion (sprout of rén), (2) shame/dislike (sprout of yì), (3) modesty/deference (sprout of lǐ), (4) right/wrong (sprout of zhì/wisdom)
- **The child-by-the-well:** Everyone who sees a child about to fall into a well feels alarm and distress — not to gain favor from the parents, not to win praise, not to avoid the sound of crying — but spontaneously; this proves the universality of innate moral feeling
- Moral cultivation is nurturing these innate sprouts — like tending plants; vice comes from neglecting or distorting them, not from their absence

### 3.2 Xunzi (Xúnzǐ, ~310–235 BCE)

- Xunzi argued the opposite: human nature is **bad** (*xìng è*) — our natural inclinations (desire, aggression, self-interest) tend toward disorder; goodness is the result of **conscious effort** (wéi) — education, ritual practice, and institutional constraints
- "Human nature is bad; its goodness is the result of conscious activity" — without cultivation, humans are selfish and destructive; the sages created rituals, music, and laws precisely because human nature is not naturally virtuous
- Despite disagreeing about human nature, Mencius and Xunzi agree that moral self-cultivation is essential; they disagree about whether it involves drawing out innate goodness (Mencius) or restraining and reshaping natural badness (Xunzi)

## 4. DAOISM — LAOZI AND ZHUANGZI

### 4.1 The Dao De Jing (Dào Dé Jīng)

- Attributed to **Laozi** (老子, "Old Master") — probably a composite text rather than the work of a single author; ~5,000 characters; composed ~4th–3rd century BCE
- **Dao** (道, the Way): The ultimate reality, the source and pattern of all things; it cannot be named, defined, or conceptualized: "The Dao that can be spoken of is not the enduring Dao; the name that can be named is not the enduring name" (Ch. 1); the Dao is prior to all distinctions (being/non-being, good/bad, self/other); it is the formless process from which all forms emerge and to which they return
- **De** (德, virtue/power/efficacy): The Dao manifest in particular things — each thing's natural potency or way of being; good governance and good living align with de by following natural tendencies rather than forcing artificial order
- **Wu wei** (無為, non-action/non-coercive action): NOT passivity but effortless, spontaneous action aligned with the Dao — like water, which is soft and yielding yet carves through stone; the sage ruler governs without coercion, so that people feel they have achieved harmony by themselves; "The best rulers — the people do not know they exist" (Ch. 17)
- **Return to naturalness:** The *Dao De Jing* critiques Confucian virtues (rén, yì, lǐ) as symptoms of alienation from the Dao: "When the Great Dao is abandoned, humaneness and righteousness appear; when knowledge and cleverness arise, great hypocrisy appears" (Ch. 18); the further society departs from natural simplicity, the more it needs artificial moral rules

### 4.2 Zhuangzi

- **Zhuangzi** (莊子, ~369–286 BCE): The most philosophically sophisticated Daoist text; playful, paradoxical, and subversive; uses humor, stories, and absurdist dialogues to undermine fixed categories of thought
- **The butterfly dream:** "Once Zhuangzi dreamed he was a butterfly, fluttering about joyfully. He didn't know he was Zhuangzi. Suddenly he awoke, and there he was — Zhuangzi. But he didn't know if he was Zhuangzi who had dreamed he was a butterfly, or a butterfly dreaming he was Zhuangzi" — radical skepticism about the stability of identity, the reliability of experience, and the distinction between dreaming and waking
- **Perspectivism and transformation:** All judgments are relative to standpoints; what seems right from one perspective seems wrong from another; the sage doesn't eliminate perspectives but transcends fixed attachment to any single perspective; "this" and "that" mutually produce each other; the sage dwells in **the pivot of the Dao** — the center where all perspectives are equalized
- **Skill stories:** Cook Ding (carving an ox with effortless precision), Wheelwright Bian (wordless craft knowledge), Woodcarver Qing — illustrate wu wei as mastery beyond conscious control; the highest skill transcends deliberation and operates from a deep attunement with the nature of things

## 5. MOHISM AND LEGALISM

### 5.1 Mozi and Universal Love

- **Mozi** (墨子, ~470–391 BCE): Critiqued Confucian **partial love** (loving one's family more than strangers) as the root of social conflict; advocated **jiān ài** (universal/impartial love) — treating all people with equal care regardless of relationship; early form of utilitarianism/consequentialism
- **Against fatalism:** Human well-being depends on human effort, not heaven's decree
- **Against ritual:** Confucian elaborate rituals and music are wasteful; resources should go to the welfare of the people
- Mohist logic: the School of Names developed sophisticated logical and semantic analysis; the Mohist *Canons* contain early Chinese logic, epistemology, and philosophy of language

### 5.2 Legalism (Fǎjiā)

- **Han Feizi** (~280–233 BCE), **Shang Yang**: Rejected moral cultivation entirely; human nature is selfish — only institutional incentives (rewards and punishments) and clear laws can maintain social order
- Influenced the **Qin unification** of China (221 BCE) under Emperor Qin Shi Huang — centralized bureaucracy, standardized laws, harsh punishments, book burning (including Confucian texts); effective at creating order but brutal; the Qin dynasty collapsed within 15 years; Legalism was subsequently discredited but its institutional innovations (bureaucracy, legal codification) persisted

## 6. NEO-CONFUCIANISM

### 6.1 Zhu Xi (1130–1200)

- **Neo-Confucianism** (理學, lǐxué, "study of principle"): The great synthesis that dominated East Asian thought from the 12th to 19th centuries; integrated Confucian ethics with cosmological and metaphysical frameworks influenced by Daoism and Buddhism
- **Zhu Xi's system:** Reality has two aspects: **lǐ** (理, principle — the rational pattern or order of things, the "what it ought to be") and **qì** (氣, vital energy/material force — the concrete stuff of which things are made); every thing has its specific lǐ (essence, nature); the Great Ultimate (*tàijí*) is the totality of all principles; moral cultivation involves "investigating things" (*gé wù*) — studying the world to discern the principles of things, thereby extending knowledge and perfecting character
- Zhu Xi's commentaries on the Four Books (Analects, Mencius, Great Learning, Doctrine of the Mean) became the basis for the Chinese civil service examinations from 1315 to 1905

### 6.2 Wang Yangming (1472–1529)

- **Wang Yangming** (心學, xīnxué, "study of mind"): Challenged Zhu Xi's emphasis on external investigation; argued that **the mind IS principle** — moral knowledge is innate (良知, *liángzhī*, innate knowing of the good) and does not need to be acquired through external study; "knowledge and action are one" (*zhī xíng hé yī*) — genuine knowledge necessarily manifests in action
- Influence: Wang Yangming's philosophy influenced Meiji Japan, Korean Confucianism, and modern Chinese thought

## 7. KEY CONCEPTS ACROSS TRADITIONS

### 7.1 Shared Framework

| Concept | Meaning | Significance |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| **Dao** (道) | Way, path, pattern, process | Central to all Chinese philosophy — the way things are and ought to be |
| **Tian** (天) | Heaven, nature, natural order | The cosmic moral order (Confucianism) or natural process (Daoism) |
| **Qi** (氣) | Vital energy, material force | The stuff of the universe — neither purely material nor purely spiritual |
| **Yin-Yang** (陰陽) | Complementary opposites | Dynamic interaction of passive/active, dark/light, receptive/creative |
| **De** (德) | Virtue, power, efficacy | Moral excellence (Confucianism) or natural potency (Daoism) |

## 8. COUNTER-ARGUMENTS AND CRITICAL ASSESSMENT

### 8.1 Is Chinese Philosophy "Philosophy"?
- Some Western philosophers have questioned whether Chinese thought constitutes "philosophy" (academic gatekeeping — P_4_12 raises the same issue for Mesoamerican thought); this objection reflects Eurocentric definitions; Chinese thinkers addressed metaphysics, epistemology, ethics, logic, and philosophy of language with sophistication; excluding them from "philosophy" is arbitrary

### 8.2 Gender and Hierarchy
- Confucianism has been criticized for patriarchal hierarchy — the "three bonds" (ruler-subject, father-son, husband-wife) have historically reinforced gender inequality; contemporary Confucian feminists (Li-Hsiang Rosenlee, Chenyang Li) argue that Confucian resources can support gender equality through rén and relational ethics

### 8.3 Orientalism Risks
- Western discussions of Chinese philosophy risk exoticizing, oversimplifying, or romanticizing — treating it as "mystical wisdom" rather than rigorous philosophical argument; careful engagement requires reading primary sources and attending to internal debates, not superficial East-West comparisons
