## QUICK SUMMARY

**The philosophy of time** addresses some of the deepest questions in metaphysics: Is time **real** or an illusion? Does the present moment have a special ontological status, or are past, present, and future equally real? Does time genuinely **flow**, or is temporal passage a subjective experience projected onto a static reality? These questions have engaged thinkers from **Augustine** (*Confessions* XI, ca. 400 CE: "What, then, is time? If no one asks me, I know; if I wish to explain to one who asks, I know not") through **J.M.E. McTaggart** (1908: argued that time is unreal because his A-series and B-series descriptions are either contradictory or incomplete) to contemporary debates between **presentists** (only the present exists), **eternalists** (past, present, and future are equally real — the "block universe"), and **growing block** theorists (the past and present exist but the future does not yet). Modern physics has profoundly influenced this debate: Einstein's **special relativity** (1905) eliminated absolute simultaneity — what counts as "now" depends on the observer's motion — which many philosophers interpret as supporting eternalism (no observer-independent present) while undermining presentism. The **arrow of time** — the asymmetry between past and future — is explained in physics by the second law of thermodynamics (entropy increases toward the future) and cosmological boundary conditions, but why the universe started in a low-entropy state remains unexplained. The philosophy of time intersects with consciousness (does the subjective experience of temporal flow require explanation beyond physics?), with free will (if the future already exists, is agency an illusion?), and with ancient traditions (cyclical time in Hindu-Buddhist cosmology vs. linear time in Abrahamic traditions).

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 McTaggart's A-Series and B-Series
- **J.M.E. McTaggart** (1908, *Mind* 17: 457–474, "The Unreality of Time"):
  - **A-series**: events are ordered as past, present, or future — these properties are temporal positions that change (yesterday was future, then present, now past)
  - **B-series**: events are ordered by the permanent relations "earlier than" and "later than" — these never change
  - McTaggart argued: (1) genuine time requires the A-series (the B-series alone is insufficient because it contains no change), but (2) the A-series is contradictory (every event has all three A-properties — past, present, future — which are incompatible), therefore (3) time is unreal
  - This argument has generated over a century of debate: most philosophers reject McTaggart's conclusion but disagree about which premise to deny

### 1.2 Special Relativity and Simultaneity
- **Einstein's special relativity** (1905) and **Minkowski's spacetime** formulation (1908):
  - There is no absolute, observer-independent "now" — simultaneity is relative to the observer's state of motion (two events simultaneous for one observer may occur at different times for another)
  - **Rietdijk-Putnam argument** (1966/1967): if simultaneity is relative, then events in your future are already present for some other observer — implying those events are real, supporting **eternalism** (the block universe)
  - **Counter-Argument:** Some philosophers (Stein 1968, *Journal of Philosophy*; Maudlin 2002) argue that relativity does not compel eternalism — it only shows that "now" cannot be defined as a global hyperplane; local temporal relations and the causal structure of spacetime preserve a meaningful distinction between past and future

### 1.3 Presentism vs. Eternalism
- **Presentism**: only the present exists; past events have happened and no longer exist, future events have not yet happened and do not yet exist:
  - Aligns with common-sense temporal experience
  - Faces challenges from special relativity (no absolute present), truthmaker theory (what makes past-tensed statements true if the past doesn't exist?), and cross-temporal relations
  - Defended by Prior (1967, *Past, Present, and Future*), Zimmerman (2008), Bourne (2006)
- **Eternalism** (block universe): past, present, and future events all exist equally — the universe is a four-dimensional block of spacetime; temporal passage is a subjective illusion:
  - Supported by the natural interpretation of Minkowski spacetime
  - Challenged by the **manifest image** — our pervasive experience of temporal flow and becoming
  - Defended by Sider (2001), Mellor (1998), Quine
- **Growing block** (Broad 1923; Tooley 1997): past and present events exist, but the future does not — the block of reality grows as time passes; faces its own challenges (why does the present edge feel special?)

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 The Arrow of Time
- The **thermodynamic arrow of time**: entropy increases toward the future (second law of thermodynamics) — this is the physical basis for the asymmetry between past and future:
  - The fundamental laws of physics are (mostly) **time-symmetric** — they work equally well run forward or backward
  - The arrow of time arises from **boundary conditions**: the universe began in a state of extraordinarily low entropy (the "Past Hypothesis," Albert 2000; Carroll 2010)
  - Why the Big Bang had low entropy remains an open cosmological question — some propose inflationary cosmology, others anthropic reasoning
- **Psychological arrow of time**: we remember the past but not the future; we feel time flowing from past to future — this is hypothesized to be a consequence of the thermodynamic arrow (memory formation requires entropy increase)

### 2.2 Bergson: Duration and Lived Time
- **Henri Bergson** (*Time and Free Will*, 1889; *Creative Evolution*, 1907):
  - Distinguished between **scientific time** (spatialized, measured by clocks, represented as a line of discrete instants) and **duration** (*durée*) — lived temporal experience, which is continuous, indivisible, and irreducible to spatial representation
  - Bergson argued that the intellect spatializes time for practical purposes but thereby distorts its true nature; only intuition grasps real duration
  - Influential on Proust, Deleuze, and process philosophy; largely dismissed by analytic philosophers but experiencing revival
  - **Einstein-Bergson debate** (1922, Paris): Einstein argued that Bergson's "philosopher's time" has no physical reality; Bergson argued that Einstein's physics presupposes lived temporal experience — the debate echoes today in consciousness studies

### 2.3 Ancient and Cross-Cultural Concepts of Time
- **Cyclical time**: Hindu-Buddhist cosmology (kalpas, yugas, samsara — vast cycles of creation, destruction, and recreation), Maya long count, Aztec "Five Suns," eternal return (Nietzsche, Stoic ekpyrosis)
- **Linear time**: Judeo-Christian-Islamic tradition (creation, history, eschaton — a unique temporal narrative with a beginning and end)
- **Aboriginal Australian "Dreamtime"**: not a past era but an ongoing dimension of reality that interpenetrates the present — a concept that does not map onto either linear or cyclical Western categories
- These diverse frameworks challenge the assumption that any single philosophical account of time is universal

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Time as Emergent
- Several approaches in quantum gravity suggest that time may not be fundamental but **emergent** from a more basic timeless structure:
  - Wheeler-DeWitt equation (quantum gravity): the wave function of the universe satisfies an equation with no time variable — the "problem of time" in quantum gravity
  - Loop quantum gravity and some string theory approaches suggest time emerges from more fundamental relational structures
  - Barbour (*The End of Time*, 1999): proposed that the universe is a collection of "Nows" (configurations) with no intrinsic temporal ordering — time is an illusion generated by the correlations between configurations

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Time Travel to the Past is Physically Realized
- **[DEBUNKED]** While general relativity permits closed timelike curves in certain exotic spacetimes (Gödel universe, Kerr black hole interior, Tipler cylinder), there is no evidence that any of these configurations exist in the actual universe, and the **chronology protection conjecture** (Hawking 1992) proposes that quantum effects prevent their formation; popular claims of successful time travel to the past lack any scientific basis
