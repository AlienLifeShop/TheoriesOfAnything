## QUICK SUMMARY

The **philosophy of information** (PI) is a relatively young branch of philosophy that investigates the conceptual nature and basic principles of information, including its dynamics (computation, information flow), its utilization (epistemic, ethical, social), and its relationship to reality, knowledge, truth, and meaning. The field was given its most systematic articulation by **Luciano Floridi** (*The Philosophy of Information*, 2011; *The Ethics of Information*, 2013), who defines PI as the philosophical field concerned with: (a) the **critical investigation of the conceptual nature and basic principles of information**, including its dynamics, utilization, and sciences; and (b) the **elaboration and application of information-theoretic and computational methodologies** to philosophical problems. Floridi's central metaphysical thesis — **Informational Structural Realism (ISR)** — holds that the ultimate nature of reality is informational: the world consists of informational structures, and what we call objects, processes, and events are patterns of information at various levels of abstraction. His ethical framework — **information ethics (IE)** — extends moral consideration to all informational entities (not just sentient beings), treating the destruction or corruption of information as a form of harm to the **infosphere** (the totality of informational entities and their relations — including but extending beyond the digital environment). The practical urgency of PI has exploded with the digital revolution: questions of **data privacy**, **algorithmic bias**, **AI ethics**, **surveillance capitalism**, **digital identity**, and the transformation of human experience by information and communication technologies (ICTs) — what Floridi calls the **"Fourth Revolution"** (after Copernicus, Darwin, and Freud) and **"onlife"** (the blurring of the boundary between online and offline existence) — place the philosophy of information at the center of contemporary ethical and political debate.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 What Is Information?
- The concept of **information** is used in multiple, technically distinct senses across disciplines:
  - **Shannon information** (Claude Shannon, 1948): a quantitative measure of uncertainty reduction — the information content of a message is a function of its probability, measured in bits. Shannon's theory is deliberately syntactic — it quantifies information without regard to meaning or truth
  - **Semantic information**: information as meaningful content — what a message is about, whether it is true or false. Floridi defines semantic information as "well-formed, meaningful, and truthful data" — the **veridicality thesis**: false information is not information but misinformation
  - **Naturalized information**: information as a feature of the natural world — DNA carries genetic information, neural signals carry information about the environment, etc. (Dretske, 1981)
  - The relationship between these senses — and whether a unified concept of information is possible — remains debated

### 1.2 Floridi's Philosophy of Information
- **Levels of abstraction (LoA)**: a core methodological concept — every description of a system operates at a particular level of abstraction, selecting certain features (observables) and ignoring others. Different LoAs yield different descriptions of the same system, and apparent philosophical disagreements sometimes dissolve when the relevant LoA is specified
- **Informational Structural Realism (ISR)**: reality is ultimately **informational** — physical objects are patterns of information, and the properties we attribute to them are features of informational structures at particular LoAs:
  - This is a form of **structural realism**: what science describes is not the intrinsic nature of things but their structural/relational properties — and these structures are informational
  - ISR is compatible with (and inspired by) the convergence of physics and information theory (e.g., Wheeler's "it from bit," Landauer's principle — information is physical)
- **The Fourth Revolution**: just as Copernicus displaced Earth from the center of the universe, Darwin displaced humans from the center of biology, and Freud displaced the conscious ego from the center of the mind — information and communication technologies are displacing humans from the center of the **infosphere**, making us "informational organisms" (inforgs) embedded in an informational environment

### 1.3 Information Ethics
- Floridi's **information ethics (IE)**: extends moral consideration beyond sentient beings to all informational entities — every informational object (a database, a forest, a cultural artifact, a digital identity) has a minimal moral standing:
  - **Entropy** (in the informational sense — the destruction, corruption, or degradation of informational objects) is the basic form of evil in IE
  - IE does not replace human-centered or animal-centered ethics but provides a broader framework within which to locate them
  - Practical applications: data privacy, digital rights, the ethics of deletion (is deleting data destroying something of value?), the ethics of documentation

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Digital Ethics: Privacy, Algorithms, and AI
- The philosophy of information provides conceptual tools for addressing urgent contemporary issues:
  - **Privacy**: informational privacy — the right to control access to one's personal information — is increasingly threatened by surveillance technologies, data mining, and the "datification" of everyday life
  - **Algorithmic fairness and bias**: algorithms used in hiring, criminal justice, healthcare, and finance can encode and amplify biases. PI asks: what constitutes algorithmic fairness? Who is morally responsible for algorithmic harms? How should we govern automated decision-making?
  - **AI ethics**: questions of machine autonomy, moral responsibility for AI actions, the ethics of autonomous weapons, the impact of automation on labor and human dignity
  - **Surveillance capitalism** (Zuboff, 2019): the extraction and commodification of personal data by technology companies as a new form of economic and social power

### 2.2 Epistemological Implications
- The **epistemic status of digital information**: the internet and social media have transformed the production, distribution, and consumption of knowledge, raising questions about: truth and post-truth, epistemic bubbles and echo chambers, the authority of expertise, the nature of testimony in digital contexts
