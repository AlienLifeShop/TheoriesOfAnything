## QUICK SUMMARY

**Skepticism** — the philosophical position that knowledge is uncertain, limited, or impossible — is one of the oldest and most persistent currents in philosophy. Ancient **Pyrrhonian skepticism** (Pyrrho, ~360–270 BCE; Sextus Empiricus, ~160–210 CE) practiced **epoché** (suspension of judgment): for any philosophical claim, equally strong arguments can be offered on both sides (**isosthenia**, equipollence); since the arguments balance, the rational response is to suspend judgment — neither affirm nor deny; the result is **ataraxia** (tranquility, freedom from disturbance) — the skeptic achieves peace of mind by abandoning the futile search for certain truth. **Academic skepticism** (Arcesilaus, ~316–241 BCE; Carneades, ~214–129 BCE) — associated with Plato's Academy — argued that nothing can be known with certainty, but some beliefs are more **probable** (pithanon) than others, allowing practical action without certainty. In the modern period, **Descartes** (1641) used radical skepticism as a **method** — doubting everything in order to find what cannot be doubted (the *cogito*: "I think, therefore I am") — and thereby attempted to establish indubitable foundations for knowledge. **Hume** (1748) argued that induction (reasoning from past experience to future events) cannot be rationally justified — the "problem of induction" — and that many fundamental beliefs (causation, the external world, personal identity) rest on **custom and habit**, not rational demonstration. Contemporary skeptical arguments include **brain-in-a-vat** scenarios (Putnam, 1981), the **closure principle** challenge, and the **underdetermination of theory by evidence**. Skepticism has never been a popular position — few philosophers are consistent skeptics — but it functions as the most powerful tool for testing the foundations of knowledge, catalyzing epistemological progress by raising challenges that every theory of knowledge must address.

## 1. WHAT IS SKEPTICISM?

### 1.1 Types of Skepticism

| Type | Scope | Claim |
|------|-------|-------|
| **Global skepticism** | All knowledge | No knowledge of any kind is possible |
| **Local skepticism** | Specific domains | Knowledge of the external world / other minds / morality / the future is impossible |
| **Methodological skepticism** | Heuristic | Doubt as a tool for discovering what can withstand scrutiny (Descartes) |
| **Academic skepticism** | Theoretical | Nothing can be known with certainty; probability is the best we can achieve |
| **Pyrrhonian skepticism** | Therapeutic | Suspension of judgment on all theoretical matters leads to tranquility |

### 1.2 Skepticism vs. Denial

- Skepticism is NOT the same as denial: the skeptic does not claim "knowledge is impossible" (that would be a positive claim requiring justification); the Pyrrhonist suspends judgment — neither affirming nor denying; the state is one of **inquiry** (the Greek *skepsis* means investigation), not settled conviction about the impossibility of knowledge

## 2. PYRRHONIAN SKEPTICISM

### 2.1 Pyrrho of Elis (~360–270 BCE)

- Pyrrho left no writings; his thought is known through his student Timon of Phlius and the later systematic exposition by **Sextus Empiricus** (~160–210 CE)
- According to Aristocles' testimony: Pyrrho held that (1) things are equally indifferent, unmeasurable, and undecidable; (2) our sensations and opinions are neither true nor false; (3) we should therefore be without opinion, uncommitted, and unwavering — and this will produce **ataraxia** (tranquility)
- Legend: Pyrrho traveled with Alexander's army to India and was influenced by Indian ascetics (gymnosophists); the connection to Buddhist or Jain skepticism has been debated but remains speculative

### 2.2 Sextus Empiricus — Systematic Pyrrhonism

- **Sextus Empiricus** (*Outlines of Pyrrhonism*, *Against the Professors*): the most complete surviving account of ancient skepticism; Sextus defined the skeptic as one who continues to investigate (unlike the dogmatist who has settled on answers and unlike the Academic skeptic who has settled on impossibility)
- **The skeptical method:** For any philosophical claim (e.g., "the honey is sweet"), the skeptic assembles arguments and appearances on both sides until they reach **isosthenia** (equal strength); unable to decide, the skeptic **suspends judgment** (epoché); tranquility follows "as a shadow follows a body"
- **Living without belief:** The skeptic lives by **appearances** (phainomena) — following custom, natural inclination, bodily needs, and craft expertise without committing to theoretical beliefs about the underlying nature of things; "I do not affirm that the honey IS sweet — I merely report that it APPEARS sweet to me"

## 3. ACADEMIC SKEPTICISM

### 3.1 Arcesilaus and Carneades

- **Arcesilaus** (~316–241 BCE): Head of Plato's Academy; argued against the Stoic criterion of truth (the "cataleptic impression" — an impression so clear and distinct it must be true); Arcesilaus argued that for any cataleptic impression, an indistinguishable non-cataleptic impression is possible — therefore no impression is certainly true; conclusion: **suspend judgment on everything** ("nothing can be known — not even this")
- **Carneades** (~214–129 BCE): The greatest Academic skeptic; developed the theory of **to pithanon** (the probable/persuasive) — while certainty is impossible, some impressions are more probable than others; we can act on probable beliefs without claiming certainty; three grades of probability: (1) probable, (2) probable and uncontradicted, (3) probable, uncontradicted, and thoroughly examined; this allowed practical action within a skeptical framework
- **Academic vs. Pyrrhonian:** Key difference — Academics made a positive claim ("nothing can be known") and offered probability as a guide; Pyrrhonists refused even this claim, suspending judgment about whether knowledge is possible

## 4. THE TROPES — EQUIPMENT FOR DOUBT

### 4.1 The Ten Modes of Aenesidemus

- **Aenesidemus** (1st century BCE) compiled **ten modes (tropes)** for generating suspension of judgment — systematic arguments showing that appearances conflict and we cannot determine which is correct:
  1. Different animals perceive things differently
  2. Different people perceive things differently
  3. Different senses give different information about the same object
  4. Circumstances (health, waking/sleeping, age) alter perception
  5. Position, distance, and place affect appearance
  6. Things are never perceived in isolation but always mixed with other things
  7. Quantities and compositions alter properties
  8. All things are relative
  9. Frequency/rarity affects judgment (a comet vs. the sun)
  10. Different customs, laws, beliefs, and dogmas conflict

### 4.2 Agrippa's Five Modes

- **Agrippa** (1st century CE?) distilled the skeptical challenge into five fundamental modes — the **Agrippan trilemma** (actually pentad):
  1. **Disagreement:** Unresolvable disagreement exists among philosophers and ordinary people
  2. **Infinite regress:** Every justification requires a further justification, ad infinitum
  3. **Relativity:** Things appear differently in different relations/contexts
  4. **Hypothesis:** Dogmatists resort to unjustified assumptions
  5. **Circularity:** Arguments used to establish a point presuppose the point to be established
- The combination of regress, hypothesis, and circularity creates the **Agrippan trilemma** (Münchhausen trilemma): any attempt to justify a belief leads to (a) infinite regress, (b) arbitrary stopping point, or (c) circularity — none is satisfactory → justification is impossible → suspend judgment

## 5. DESCARTES AND MODERN SKEPTICISM

### 5.1 Methodological Doubt

- **René Descartes** (*Meditations on First Philosophy*, 1641): Used radical doubt as a **method** to find what is indubitable — doubting everything to discover what survives doubt; three waves of doubt: (1) the senses sometimes deceive (perceptual illusions); (2) the dreaming argument (I cannot distinguish dreaming from waking); (3) the **evil demon** (an all-powerful deceiver could make all my beliefs false, including beliefs about mathematics and logic)
- **The cogito:** "I think, therefore I am" (*cogito ergo sum*) — even if everything else can be doubted, the act of doubting proves the existence of the doubter; this is the Archimedean point from which Descartes rebuilds knowledge
- Descartes is NOT a skeptic — he uses skepticism as a tool to overcome skepticism by establishing certain foundations; but his evil demon hypothesis became the template for modern skeptical scenarios

## 6. HUME'S SKEPTICISM

### 6.1 The Problem of Induction

- **David Hume** (*An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding*, 1748): Cannot rationally justify the inference from observed to unobserved — we have seen the sun rise every morning, but this does not PROVE it will rise tomorrow; the assumption that nature is uniform (the future will resemble the past) cannot itself be justified without circularity (using past experience to justify reliance on past experience)
- Hume's conclusion: Inductive reasoning is based on **custom and habit**, not rational demonstration; we cannot help believing that the future will resemble the past — but this is a psychological fact about human nature, not a rational justification

### 6.2 Mitigated Skepticism

- Hume distinguished destructive/excessive skepticism (paralysis of belief and action) from **mitigated skepticism** — a healthy modesty about the limits of reason, awareness of cognitive biases, and willingness to proportion belief to evidence; Hume is a practical person who thinks and acts, but does so with the recognition that certainty is unavailable
- Hume's skepticism about causation (we never perceive the "necessary connection" between cause and effect — only constant conjunction and habitual expectation), personal identity ("a bundle or collection of different perceptions"), and the external world was enormously influential

## 7. CONTEMPORARY SKEPTICAL ARGUMENTS

### 7.1 Brain-in-a-Vat

- **The modern evil demon:** Imagine you are a brain in a vat, fed artificial sensory inputs by a supercomputer; all your experiences are indistinguishable from normal experience, but none correspond to an external world; how can you know you are NOT a brain in a vat?
- **Putnam's refutation (1981):** Used his own externalist semantics (P_5_05) to argue that a brain in a vat cannot formulate the thought "I am a brain in a vat" — because its words "brain" and "vat" would refer to simulated brains and vats, not real ones; the sentence would be self-refuting; this refutation is controversial — many philosophers find it unconvincing

### 7.2 Closure-Based Skepticism

- **Closure principle:** If you know P, and you know that P implies Q, then you know Q; applied skeptically: You claim to know you have hands; having hands implies you are NOT a handless brain in a vat; but you do NOT know you are not a brain in a vat; therefore (by contraposition) you do NOT know you have hands
- **Responses:** Nozick's tracking theory (knowledge doesn't require closure), contextualism (standards for "knowledge" vary with context), and pragmatic rejection of the scenario

### 7.3 Moral Skepticism

- **Moral skepticism:** Doubts about the existence of moral knowledge, moral facts, or moral truth; distinct from amoralism (not caring about morality); forms include: error theory (moral judgments are all false — Mackie, 1977), non-cognitivism (moral statements are not truth-apt), and moral epistemic skepticism (we cannot know which moral beliefs are true)

## 8. COUNTER-ARGUMENTS AND CRITICAL ASSESSMENT

### 8.1 The Self-Refutation Objection
- **Objection:** Skepticism is self-refuting — "I know that I know nothing" is a knowledge-claim; "No belief is justified" is itself a belief requiring justification
- **Response:** Pyrrhonists anticipated this — they do NOT assert "nothing can be known"; they report their own state of mind ("I am unable to decide"); their skepticism is an activity (investigating, suspending), not a doctrine; Sextus: "The skeptic does not dogmatize — not even about the impossibility of dogmatism"

### 8.2 The Impracticality Objection
- **Objection (the *apraxia* charge):** If you suspend judgment on everything, you cannot act — you cannot even decide to eat or avoid cliffs
- **Response:** Pyrrhonists follow appearances, custom, natural drives, and craft skills — they live practically while suspending judgment on theoretical matters; the charge confuses theoretical commitment with practical engagement

### 8.3 Skepticism's Productive Role
- Skepticism has been historically productive: it forced the development of better epistemological theories (foundationalism, coherentism, reliabilism, contextualism); it motivated the scientific method (empirical testing as response to theoretical uncertainty); it remains the most powerful tool for exposing unjustified assumptions
