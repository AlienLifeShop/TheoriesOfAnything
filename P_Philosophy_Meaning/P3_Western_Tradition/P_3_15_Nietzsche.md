## QUICK SUMMARY

**Friedrich Wilhelm Nietzsche** (1844-1900) was a German philosopher, classical philologist, and cultural critic whose radical questioning of morality, religion, truth, and human meaning has made him one of the most influential — and controversial — thinkers in modern philosophy. Writing in an aphoristic, literary, and deliberately provocative style, Nietzsche attacked the foundations of Western moral and metaphysical thought, particularly its Christian and Platonic roots. His core ideas include: (1) the **death of God** — not a metaphysical claim about God's non-existence but a diagnosis of the cultural crisis created by the collapse of the Christian-Platonic framework that had provided meaning and moral order for two millennia; (2) the threat of **nihilism** — the danger that, without transcendent values, life becomes meaningless; (3) the **revaluation of all values** (*Umwertung aller Werte*) — the project of replacing "slave morality" (rooted in resentment, guilt, and otherworldly hope) with life-affirming values; (4) the **Übermensch** (Overman/Superman) — a future type of human who creates their own values and affirms life fully; (5) **eternal recurrence** — the thought experiment of living one's life exactly as it has been, repeated infinitely, as the ultimate test of life-affirmation; and (6) the **will to power** — the fundamental drive of all living things, not merely political domination but the creative, self-overcoming impulse to growth, mastery, and expression.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 Biographical Context
- Born October 15, 1844, in Röcken, Saxony, the son of a Lutheran pastor who died when Nietzsche was four
- Appointed **professor of classical philology** at the University of Basel at age 24 (1869) — an extraordinarily young appointment — largely on the recommendation of his teacher Friedrich Ritschl
- Published *The Birth of Tragedy* (1872) — combining philology with philosophy, arguing that Greek tragedy emerged from the tension between **Apollonian** (rational, ordered) and **Dionysian** (ecstatic, chaotic) impulses
- Resigned his Basel professorship in 1879 due to chronic illness — spent the next decade as a peripatetic writer, living in boarding houses across Switzerland, Italy, and southern France, producing the works that would make his reputation
- Mental collapse in Turin, January 3, 1889 — spent the last 11 years of his life in the care of his mother and sister, unable to work. Died August 25, 1900
- His sister **Elisabeth Förster-Nietzsche** edited and distorted his posthumous writings (especially *The Will to Power*, a compilation she assembled from his notebooks) to align with her own nationalist and antisemitic agenda — leading to the infamous (and utterly false) appropriation of Nietzsche by the Nazis

### 1.2 The Death of God and Nihilism
- Proclaimed in *The Gay Science* (1882), §125 — the parable of the **Madman** who runs into the marketplace crying "God is dead! And we have killed him!":
  - Not a statement of atheism but a **cultural diagnosis**: the metaphysical framework that had grounded Western morality, meaning, and truth (Platonism and Christianity) has lost its hold on modern people
  - The Madman realizes he has "come too early" — people do not yet understand the enormity of what has happened
- **Nihilism** — the consequence of God's death: if there are no objective values, life threatens to seem purposeless:
  - **Passive nihilism**: the "last man" who seeks only comfort and avoids suffering — the worst outcome
  - **Active nihilism**: a transitional, destructive stage that clears the ground for new value-creation

### 1.3 The Revaluation of Values
- *On the Genealogy of Morals* (1887): Nietzsche's most systematic work, tracing the historical origins of moral concepts:
  - **First Essay**: the distinction between **master morality** (originating among nobles — values: strength, pride, excellence, affirming life as "good") and **slave morality** (originating among the powerless — revalues weakness as virtue, labels the strong as "evil," and valorizes humility, pity, and suffering through **ressentiment**)
  - **Second Essay**: the origins of guilt and bad conscience in the internalization of aggressive instincts by humans constrained by society
  - **Third Essay**: the "ascetic ideal" — why humanity has preferred to will nothingness (religious self-denial, scholarly objectivity) rather than not will at all

### 1.4 Eternal Recurrence
- Presented in *The Gay Science* (§341) and *Thus Spoke Zarathustra*:
  - The thought experiment: "What if a demon said to you: 'This life as you now live it and have lived it, you will have to live once more and innumerable times more; and there will be nothing new in it...'" — Would you rejoice or despair?
  - Functions as an existential test: the ability to affirm eternal recurrence is the ultimate measure of life-affirmation (**amor fati** — "love of fate")
  - Whether Nietzsche intended this as a literal cosmological claim or purely a psychological/ethical test is debated among scholars

### 1.5 Perspectivism
- There are no "facts," only **interpretations** — all knowledge is perspectival, shaped by the interests, drives, and position of the knower:
  - Not a straightforward relativism: Nietzsche valued intellectual honesty, rigor, and the multiplication of perspectives as a path to fuller understanding

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Will to Power
- The **will to power** (*Wille zur Macht*) is Nietzsche's proposed fundamental drive:
  - Not mere political domination but the impulse to growth, self-overcoming, creative expression, and the imposition of form on chaos
  - Intended as a replacement for Schopenhauer's "will to live" and Darwin's survival-centered natural selection
  - The published notebook fragments compiled by Elisabeth Förster-Nietzsche as *The Will to Power* (1901) are not a book Nietzsche wrote — they must be treated with caution, though many individual notes are genuine

### 2.2 Influence
- Nietzsche's influence spans existentialism (Heidegger, Sartre, Camus), postmodernism (Foucault, Derrida, Deleuze), psychoanalysis (Freud acknowledged Nietzsche as a precursor), literature (Mann, Rilke, Yeats), and music (Strauss, Mahler)

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Nietzsche's Mental Collapse
- The cause of his January 1889 breakdown remains debated: traditionally attributed to **tertiary syphilis**, alternative diagnoses include brain tumor (meningioma), CADASIL (a hereditary vascular condition), or frontotemporal dementia. Definitive diagnosis is impossible given the available evidence

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Nietzsche as Nazi Philosopher
- **[REFUTED]** Nietzsche explicitly condemned German nationalism, antisemitism, and racism in numerous passages. The Nazi appropriation was based on Elisabeth's editorial distortions and selective quotation
