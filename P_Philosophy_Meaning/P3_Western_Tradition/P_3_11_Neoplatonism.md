## QUICK SUMMARY

**Neoplatonism** is the philosophical and spiritual system founded by **Plotinus** (c. 204-270 CE) and elaborated by his successors — notably **Porphyry** (c. 234-305), **Iamblichus** (c. 245-325), and **Proclus** (412-485) — which reinterpreted and systematized Plato's philosophy into a comprehensive metaphysical framework centered on the concept of **emanation** from a single, ineffable, transcendent principle: **the One** (*to hen*). In the Neoplatonic scheme, all of reality proceeds from the One through successive levels of being — from **Nous** (Intellect/Mind, containing the Forms) to the **World Soul** (*Psychē*) to the material cosmos — in a process of necessary, eternal "overflow" (*aporrhoia*). Crucially, this is not creation in time (as in Judaism, Christianity, or Islam) but an eternal, atemporal procession and return. The human soul, in this system, is a fallen fragment of the World Soul entrapped in matter, and the philosophical-spiritual goal is **henosis** — mystical union or return to the One through contemplation, asceticism, and (in later Neoplatonism) ritual practices called **theurgy**. Neoplatonism became the dominant philosophical school of late antiquity (3rd-6th centuries CE) and profoundly influenced Christian theology (Augustine, Pseudo-Dionysius), Islamic philosophy (al-Fārābī, Avicenna), Jewish Kabbalah, and Renaissance thought.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 Plotinus and the Enneads
- **Plotinus** (c. 204-270 CE) was born in Roman Egypt, studied in Alexandria under **Ammonius Saccas** for eleven years, and established his own school in Rome from c. 244 CE:
  - His writings, compiled and edited by his student **Porphyry** into six groups of nine treatises each (hence **Enneads**, from Greek *ennea* = nine), constitute the foundational texts of Neoplatonism
- The Three **Hypostases** (fundamental levels of reality):
  1. **The One** (*to hen*): the absolutely simple, infinite, ineffable first principle — beyond being, thought, and even existence. It is not a "thing" and cannot be adequately described in language
  2. **Nous** (Intellect/Mind): the first emanation from the One — the realm of pure thought thinking itself, containing the Platonic **Forms**. Nous is simultaneously thinker and thought
  3. **Psychē** (Soul/World Soul): emanates from Nous — the principle of life, motion, and time. The World Soul generates and animates the physical cosmos

### 1.2 Emanation
- The process by which reality proceeds from the One is **emanation** — a metaphor drawn from the sun's emission of light:
  - The One does not "choose" to create; its perfection necessarily "overflows" into lower levels of being, as light radiates from a source without diminishing it
  - Each level of reality is a **diminished reflection** of the one above it — matter, at the lowest level, is described as near-nothingness, the furthest from the One
  - This process is **eternal** — there is no temporal beginning; the cosmos always was and always will be
- Plotinus's emanation scheme was explicitly contrasted with Gnostic cosmologies (see his treatise *Against the Gnostics*, Enneads II.9), which he rejected for their vilification of the natural world

### 1.3 Later Neoplatonists
- **Porphyry** (c. 234-305): Plotinus's student and editor; wrote the *Isagoge*, a commentary on Aristotle's *Categories* that became the standard logic textbook for over a millennium; introduced systematic classification of universals (genus, species, etc.)
- **Iamblichus** (c. 245-325): Syrian philosopher who emphasized **theurgy** (ritual practices invoking divine powers) as necessary for the soul's ascent — arguing that philosophical contemplation alone was insufficient for lower souls to achieve union with the One
- **Proclus** (412-485): the most systematic Neoplatonist, head of the Platonic Academy in Athens; authored the *Elements of Theology* (a formal axiomatic presentation of Neoplatonic metaphysics) and the *Platonic Theology*; multiplied the hierarchical levels between the One and the material world into an elaborate system of henads, intelligible triads, and divine orders

### 1.4 End of the Pagan Schools
- The Neoplatonic Academy in Athens was closed by Emperor **Justinian I** in **529 CE** as part of the suppression of pagan intellectual institutions — the last head was **Damascius**
  - Several Neoplatonic philosophers migrated to Persia, contributing to the transmission of Greek philosophy to the Islamic world

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Influence on Christianity
- Neoplatonism profoundly shaped Christian theology:
  - **Augustine of Hippo** (354-430): explicitly acknowledged his debt to "the books of the Platonists" (likely Plotinus and Porphyry in Latin translation) in his *Confessions* — his concepts of God as immaterial, evil as privation of good, and the soul's ascent through inwardness are deeply Neoplatonic
  - **Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite** (c. 5th-6th century): anonymous Christian author whose works (*Divine Names*, *Mystical Theology*, *Celestial Hierarchy*) directly adapted Proclean metaphysics into a Christian framework of angelic hierarchies and apophatic (negative) theology — enormously influential in both East and West
  - The **Cappadocian Fathers** (Basil, Gregory of Nyssa, Gregory of Nazianzus) engaged substantially with Neoplatonic concepts

### 2.2 Influence on Islamic and Jewish Thought
- The so-called *Theology of Aristotle* — actually a paraphrase of portions of Plotinus's Enneads — was widely read in the Islamic world and attributed to Aristotle, blending Aristotelian and Neoplatonic metaphysics:
  - **Al-Fārābī** and **Avicenna** (Ibn Sīnā) developed emanation schemes influenced by this synthesis
- Jewish **Kabbalah** adopted emanation concepts: the ten **Sefirot** (divine attributes) proceeding from Ein Sof (the Infinite) structurally parallel the Neoplatonic hypostases

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Plotinus's Mystical Experiences
- Porphyry reports that Plotinus achieved mystical union with the One **four times** during his life. The phenomenology of these experiences — and whether they parallel those described in other traditions (Hindu *samādhi*, Buddhist *nirvāṇa*) — remains debated among scholars of comparative mysticism
