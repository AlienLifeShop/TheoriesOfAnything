## QUICK SUMMARY

**Aristotle** (384–322 BCE) was a Greek philosopher and polymath whose works constitute the single most influential body of thought in the history of Western and Islamic intellectual tradition. A student of **Plato** for twenty years at the Academy in Athens, he founded his own school, the **Lyceum**, in 335 BCE. Unlike Plato's emphasis on transcendent Forms, Aristotle's philosophy is empirical and taxonomic — he insisted that knowledge begins with observation of the natural world and proceeds through logical analysis. His contributions span **logic** (the invention of formal syllogistic reasoning), **metaphysics** (the four causes, substance theory, the Unmoved Mover), **natural philosophy** (physics, cosmology, meteorology), **biology** (classification of ~540 animal species), **psychology** (*De Anima* — the soul as form of the body), **ethics** (*eudaimonia* — human flourishing through virtue), and **political theory**. His geocentric cosmology of nested crystalline spheres dominated Western and Islamic science for nearly **2,000 years** until Copernicus and Galileo. Aristotle's works were transmitted to medieval Europe primarily through **Arabic translations** and commentaries (especially **Averroes/Ibn Rushd**), then integrated into Christian theology by **Thomas Aquinas**, creating the Scholastic synthesis that defined European thought until the Scientific Revolution.

## 1. HISTORICAL ARISTOTLE — LIFE AND WORKS

### 1.1 Biography

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| 384 BCE | Born in Stagira, Chalcidice (northern Greece); father Nicomachus was physician to King Amyntas III of Macedon |
| 367 BCE | Enters Plato's Academy in Athens, age 17; remains for 20 years |
| 347 BCE | Plato dies; Aristotle leaves Athens (possibly due to anti-Macedonian sentiment) |
| 343 BCE | Tutors the young **Alexander** (later Alexander the Great) at the Macedonian court |
| 335 BCE | Returns to Athens; founds the **Lyceum** (also called the Peripatetic school) |
| 323 BCE | Alexander dies; Aristotle leaves Athens ("lest Athens sin twice against philosophy" — alluding to Socrates) |
| 322 BCE | Dies in Chalcis, Euboea, age 62 |

### 1.2 Surviving Works

- Of Aristotle's estimated ~200 works, only about **31 survive** — these are primarily his **esoteric** (internal/lecture) works, not the polished **exoteric** (public) dialogues that ancient critics praised for their style
- The surviving corpus was compiled by **Andronicus of Rhodes** (~60 BCE) and consists of lecture notes and treatises organized by topic
- Major surviving works include: *Categories, De Interpretatione, Prior Analytics, Posterior Analytics, Topics, Sophistical Refutations* (the Organon/Logic), *Physics, On the Heavens (De Caelo), Meteorology, On Generation and Corruption, Metaphysics, Nicomachean Ethics, Politics, Poetics, Rhetoric, De Anima (On the Soul), History of Animals, Parts of Animals, Generation of Animals*
- **Tier 1** — The corpus is physically extant and critically edited

## 2. LOGIC — THE ORGANON

### 2.1 The Invention of Formal Logic

- Aristotle is the **inventor of formal logic** — no systematic deductive logic existed before his *Prior Analytics*
- The **syllogism** is Aristotle's central logical form: two premises yielding a necessary conclusion
  - Example: "All men are mortal. Socrates is a man. Therefore, Socrates is mortal."
- He identified the **valid forms** of syllogism (14 valid forms in three figures, later expanded to 4 figures and 19+ forms by medieval logicians)
- The *Posterior Analytics* establishes the theory of **demonstration** (*apodeixis*) — scientific knowledge requires deduction from first principles that are true, primary, and better known than the conclusion
- **Tier 1** — Aristotelian logic is the historical foundation of Western logical tradition

### 2.2 Categories and Predication

- The *Categories* identifies **ten categories** of being: Substance, Quantity, Quality, Relation, Place, Time, Position, State, Action, Passion
- These are the fundamental ways things can be said to "be" — the ontological skeleton of reality
- Aristotle's categorical framework dominated logic and metaphysics for over 2,000 years (Kant: "Aristotle has not omitted any category")
- **Tier 1** — The text and its influence are well-documented

## 3. NATURAL PHILOSOPHY — PHYSICS AND COSMOLOGY

### 3.1 Physics — Motion and Causation

- Aristotle's *Physics* is not physics in the modern sense — it is the philosophical study of **nature** (*physis*) and **change** (*kinesis*)
- Key doctrines:
  - **No void exists** — nature is a plenum; vacuum is impossible (later overturned by Torricelli, Pascal, and modern physics)
  - **Natural motion vs. violent motion:** Heavy objects naturally move downward (toward the center of the cosmos); light objects move upward. Any other motion requires an external mover
  - **The impossibility of actual infinity:** Infinite extension, infinite speed, and actual infinite quantities are impossible (potential infinity is allowed)
  - **Time and eternity:** Time is the measure of motion; the universe is eternal (no creation *ex nihilo* — major conflict with later Abrahamic theology)
- **Tier 1** — These doctrines are in the text; their historical impact is documented

### 3.2 Cosmology — The Geocentric Universe

- **De Caelo** (*On the Heavens*): Aristotle's cosmological system
- **Structure:**
  - Earth is a sphere at the center of the cosmos (**geocentric model**)
  - The cosmos is divided into the **sublunary** realm (below the moon — composed of four elements: earth, water, air, fire; subject to generation and corruption) and the **superlunary** realm (above the moon — composed of a fifth element, **aether**; perfect, eternal, unchanging)
  - The celestial bodies (Moon, Sun, planets, fixed stars) are embedded in **concentric crystalline spheres** that rotate around the Earth
  - The outermost sphere is the sphere of the **fixed stars** — beyond it, nothing exists (no space, no time)
- This cosmology was refined by **Ptolemy** (2nd c. CE, *Almagest*) into the elaborate epicyclic system that dominated astronomy until **Copernicus** (1543)
- **Tier 1** — Aristotelian-Ptolemaic cosmology is one of the best-documented scientific paradigms in history

### 3.3 The Five Elements

| Element | Quality Pair | Natural Place | Realm |
|---------|-------------|---------------|-------|
| Earth | Cold + Dry | Center (lowest) | Sublunary |
| Water | Cold + Wet | Below air | Sublunary |
| Air | Hot + Wet | Above water | Sublunary |
| Fire | Hot + Dry | Below moon | Sublunary |
| **Aether** | None (perfect) | Above moon | Superlunary |

## 4. METAPHYSICS — THE UNMOVED MOVER AND FOUR CAUSES

### 4.1 The Four Causes

- Aristotle argues that full understanding of anything requires knowing its **four causes** (*aitia*):

| Cause | Meaning | Example (a statue) |
|-------|---------|-------------------|
| **Material** | What it's made of | Bronze |
| **Formal** | What form/pattern it has | The design (shape of a person) |
| **Efficient** | What made it / who made it | The sculptor |
| **Final** | What it's for (purpose/*telos*) | To honor the depicted person |

- The **final cause** (*telos*) is the most distinctive and controversial: Aristotle holds that **everything in nature has a purpose** — the acorn exists *for the sake of* becoming an oak tree. This **teleological** worldview was fundamental to his biology and ethics and was later integrated into theistic natural theology
- **Tier 1** — The four causes doctrine is core Aristotle

### 4.2 The Unmoved Mover

- *Metaphysics* XII (Lambda): Aristotle argues for an **Unmoved Mover** (Prime Mover) — a being that is:
  - **Eternal** and **unchanging** (since it is the cause of all motion, it cannot itself be in motion)
  - **Pure actuality** — no potentiality, no matter, no becoming (fully realized being)
  - **Thought thinking itself** — the Unmoved Mover's activity is self-contemplation (*noesis noeseos*)
  - **The cause of all cosmic motion** — not by pushing (efficient causation) but by **being the object of desire** — the celestial spheres move because they "desire" to emulate the perfection of the Unmoved Mover (final causation)
- There are **47 or 55** unmoved movers in the *Metaphysics* (one for each celestial sphere) — but the **primary** Unmoved Mover is single and supreme
- This concept became the philosophical foundation for the **God of the philosophers** in Islamic, Jewish, and Christian theology (Avicenna, Maimonides, Aquinas)
- **Tier 1** — Text is explicit; the theological reception is extensively documented

### 4.3 Substance, Form, and Matter (Hylomorphism)

- **Hylomorphism** (from Greek *hyle* = matter, *morphe* = form): Every physical object is a **compound of matter and form**
- **Matter** (hyle): The underlying stuff — bronze, wood, flesh — that is potentially something but not yet actually anything specific
- **Form** (morphe/eidos): The structure, pattern, or essence that makes matter into a specific thing — what makes bronze into a statue or flesh into a human being
- Unlike Plato, who separated Forms into a transcendent realm, Aristotle held that **forms exist only in matter** — there is no Form of Horse floating in a separate reality; "horseness" exists only in actual horses
- **Tier 1** — Core Aristotelian metaphysics, extensively studied

## 5. PSYCHOLOGY AND BIOLOGY

### 5.1 De Anima — The Soul

- **De Anima** (*On the Soul*): The soul (*psyche*) is the **form of the body** — not a separate substance trapped in matter (contra Plato's dualism)
- Three levels of soul:

| Level | Type | Functions | Found in |
|-------|------|-----------|----------|
| 1 | **Nutritive** | Growth, nutrition, reproduction | Plants + animals + humans |
| 2 | **Sensitive** | Sensation, desire, locomotion | Animals + humans |
| 3 | **Rational** | Thought, reasoning, deliberation | Humans only |

- The **Active Intellect** (*nous poietikos*, De Anima III.5) — an obscure passage that generated millennia of debate: Aristotle says there is an intellect that is "separable, impassible, and unmixed" — some interpreted this as the individual human soul's immortal part; others (Averroes) as a single cosmic intellect shared by all humans; Aquinas as proof of individual soul immortality
- **Tier 1–2** — Text is clear; interpretation of Active Intellect is genuinely ambiguous

### 5.2 Biology

- Aristotle was the **first systematic biologist** — his *History of Animals*, *Parts of Animals*, and *Generation of Animals* describe and classify approximately **540 species**
- He conducted **dissections** of marine organisms (cuttlefish, octopus, etc.) and made observations still considered accurate (e.g., the hectocotylus arm of the octopus, not confirmed until the 19th century)
- His classification system (animals with blood / animals without blood ≈ vertebrates/invertebrates) remained the basis of taxonomy until Linnaeus (18th century)
- Darwin: "Linnaeus and Cuvier have been my two gods... but they were mere schoolboys to old Aristotle"
- **Tier 1** — Aristotle's biological works are his most empirically impressive achievement

## 6. ETHICS AND POLITICAL PHILOSOPHY

### 6.1 Nicomachean Ethics — Eudaimonia

- The highest human good is **eudaimonia** (εὐδαιμονία) — often translated "happiness" but better rendered "flourishing" or "living well"
- Eudaimonia is achieved through the practice of **virtue** (*arete*) — excellent character traits developed through habit
- **Virtue as the mean:** Each virtue lies between two vices (excess and deficiency): courage is the mean between cowardice and rashness; generosity between stinginess and prodigality
- **The contemplative life** (*bios theoretikos*) is the highest form of human activity — since it exercises reason (our unique faculty) on the highest objects (eternal truths)
- **Tier 1** — The Nicomachean Ethics is among the most studied philosophical texts

### 6.2 Politics

- "Man is by nature a political animal" (*Politics* 1253a) — humans are fulfilled only in community
- Aristotle classifies constitutions into six types (three good, three corrupt): Monarchy/Tyranny, Aristocracy/Oligarchy, Polity/Democracy
- He advocated for a **mixed constitution** (polity) as the most stable — anticipating later republican theory
- His defense of "natural slavery" (some people are naturally suited to be slaves — *Politics* I.5) is the most criticized element of his political philosophy
- **Tier 1** — The text is explicit; the moral critique is modern

## 7. ISLAMIC TRANSMISSION AND MEDIEVAL RECEPTION

### 7.1 The Arabic Aristotle

| Period | Key Development |
|--------|----------------|
| ~8th–9th c. | Syriac Christians (Nestorians) in Baghdad translate Aristotle from Greek → Syriac → Arabic under Abbasid patronage (House of Wisdom / *Bayt al-Hikma*) |
| 9th–10th c. | **Al-Kindi** and **Al-Farabi** integrate Aristotle with Islamic theology — the "First Teacher" (*al-mu'allim al-awwal*) |
| 11th c. | **Avicenna (Ibn Sina)** produces the most comprehensive Islamic Aristotelianism; his *Shifa* (Healing) covers the entire Aristotelian corpus |
| 12th c. | **Averroes (Ibn Rushd)** in Córdoba writes the "Great Commentary" on Aristotle — defending pure Aristotelianism against theological compromise; argues for the eternity of the world and a single Active Intellect |
| 12th–13th c. | Arabic texts translated into Latin (Toledo School of Translators) → Aristotle enters Christian Europe |

### 7.2 Thomas Aquinas and Scholasticism

- **Thomas Aquinas** (1225–1274) achieved the decisive synthesis of Aristotle and Christianity:
  - The Unmoved Mover = God (the First Way of the Five Ways)
  - Hylomorphism applied to the Eucharist → **transubstantiation** (form changes, matter remains)
  - The soul as form of the body → reconciled with personal immortality
  - Natural law ethics based on teleology → Catholic moral theology
  - The four causes → integrated into theology of creation and providence
- This Aristotelian-Thomistic synthesis became the **official philosophy of the Catholic Church** and remained so until Vatican II (and arguably still)
- **Tier 1** — The Scholastic reception of Aristotle is one of the most documented intellectual events in history

### 7.3 The Condemnation of 1277

- **Bishop Étienne Tempier** (Paris, 1277) condemned **219 propositions** drawn from Aristotelian philosophy — including the eternity of the world, determinism, and the denial of divine omnipotence
- Historians of science (Pierre Duhem, Edward Grant) have argued that these condemnations **inadvertently opened the door to modern science** — by denying Aristotle's authority, they allowed alternative physical theories (impetus theory, void, infinity) to be explored
- **Tier 2** — The Duhem-Grant thesis is influential but debated

## 8. COUNTER-ARGUMENTS AND CRITICAL ASSESSMENT

### 8.1 What Aristotle Got Wrong

- **Geocentric cosmology** — overturned by Copernicus, Kepler, Galileo, Newton
- **No void/vacuum** — disproved by Torricelli (1643) and Robert Boyle
- **Natural slavery** — morally indefensible; rejected since at least the 18th century
- **Female inferiority** — Aristotle argued women are "deformed males" (*Generation of Animals*); factually and morally wrong
- **The impossibility of actual infinity** — modern mathematics (Cantor, set theory) operates with actual infinities
- **Projectile motion** — Aristotle's explanation (the medium pushes the projectile) was replaced by impetus theory and then Newtonian mechanics
- **Tier 1** — These errors are well-established

### 8.2 What Aristotle Got Right

- **Formal logic** — the syllogism remained the foundation of logic until Frege (1879)
- **Biology** — his observational zoology is genuinely impressive (530+ species, many accurately described)
- **Virtue ethics** — experiencing a modern revival (MacIntyre, Foot, Hursthouse)
- **Teleological reasoning** — while mechanistic science rejected final causes, modern biology's concept of "function" is arguably quasi-teleological (Wright, 1973; Millikan, 1984)
- **Tier 1** — Modern scholarship documenting Aristotle's lasting contributions
