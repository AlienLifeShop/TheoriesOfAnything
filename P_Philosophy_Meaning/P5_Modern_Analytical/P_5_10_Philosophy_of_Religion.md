## QUICK SUMMARY

The **philosophy of religion** is the branch of philosophy that critically examines the concepts, arguments, and experiences at the heart of religious belief and practice — not from within any particular faith tradition but from the standpoint of **rational inquiry**. Its central questions include: Does God exist, and can God's existence be **demonstrated** (natural theology) or only accepted on **faith** (fideism)? What is the nature of God — omnipotent, omniscient, perfectly good, necessary, personal (classical theism), or something else (deism, pantheism, panentheism)? How does the existence of **evil and suffering** square with an all-powerful, all-good God (the problem of evil / theodicy)? Can **religious experience** — mystical states, visions, conversion experiences — provide evidence for the existence of God? Is there **one true religion**, or do all religions contain partial truths about the same ultimate reality (religious pluralism)? And what is the relationship between **faith and reason** — are they complementary, independent, or incompatible? The field engages with arguments developed over millennia — from Plato and Aristotle through Anselm, Aquinas, Pascal, Hume, and Kant to modern figures such as **Alvin Plantinga** (reformed epistemology), **Richard Swinburne** (inductive natural theology), **John Hick** (religious pluralism), and their critics.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 Arguments for God's Existence
- **Cosmological Arguments**: reasoning from the existence of the universe to a First Cause or Necessary Being:
  - **Aquinas's Five Ways** (1265-1274): arguments from motion, efficient causation, contingency, degrees of perfection, and teleology (see P_3_12)
  - **Leibniz's Principle of Sufficient Reason**: everything that exists must have a reason for its existence; the universe as a whole requires a reason, which must be a Necessary Being
  - **Kalām Cosmological Argument** (al-Ghazālī, revived by William Lane Craig): the universe began to exist; whatever begins to exist has a cause; therefore the universe has a cause
- **Ontological Arguments**: reasoning from the **concept** of God to God's existence:
  - **Anselm** (1078): God is "that than which nothing greater can be conceived" — if God existed only in the understanding and not in reality, a greater being (one existing in reality) could be conceived, which is a contradiction
  - **Kant's objection**: existence is not a real predicate (it adds nothing to the concept of a thing)
  - **Plantinga's modal version**: if it is possible that a maximally great being exists, then (by S5 modal logic) such a being exists in all possible worlds, including the actual one
- **Teleological (Design) Arguments**: the universe exhibits apparent purpose, order, or fine-tuning:
  - **Paley's Watchmaker** (1802): the complexity of biological organisms implies a designer
  - **Fine-Tuning Argument**: the physical constants of the universe appear finely calibrated for life — taken by some (e.g., Robin Collins, Swinburne) as evidence of design, by others as explained by the multiverse hypothesis or anthropic selection

### 1.2 The Problem of Evil
- The most formidable challenge to classical theism:
  - **Logical Problem of Evil** (J.L. Mackie, 1955): the propositions "God is omnipotent," "God is perfectly good," and "evil exists" appear logically inconsistent
  - **Plantinga's Free Will Defense** (1974): it is logically possible that God could not create a world with free creatures who never choose evil — this blocks the logical problem
  - **Evidential Problem of Evil** (William Rowe, 1979): even if logically compatible, the sheer quantity and distribution of suffering makes God's existence improbable
  - **Theodicies**: soul-making (Hick — suffering enables moral and spiritual growth), free will (Augustine), greater goods, and divine hiddenness

### 1.3 Religious Experience
- **William James** (*The Varieties of Religious Experience*, 1902): classic study of mysticism, conversion, saintliness, and the healthy-minded vs. sick-souled types — argued that religious experiences, while not conclusive proof, are legitimate data
- **Rudolf Otto** (*The Idea of the Holy*, 1917): the **numinous** — the distinctive quality of religious experience as *mysterium tremendum et fascinans* (awe-inspiring mystery)
- **Alston** (1991): religious experience can function as a form of perception of the divine (mystical perception) — analogous in structure to sense perception

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Religious Pluralism
- **John Hick** (*An Interpretation of Religion*, 1989): the Real (ultimate reality) is experienced differently through different religious and cultural lenses — no single tradition has exclusive access to truth. This "pluralist hypothesis" challenges exclusivism and inclusivism but is criticized for implying that all religions are equally adequate descriptions of the Real

### 2.2 Reformed Epistemology
- **Alvin Plantinga** (*Warranted Christian Belief*, 2000): belief in God can be **properly basic** — rationally justified without being based on evidence or argument, just as belief in the reality of the external world or other minds is properly basic. This challenges the **evidentialist objection** (the claim that belief without sufficient evidence is irrational)

### 2.3 Faith and Reason
- Three classical positions:
  1. **Compatibility** (Aquinas): reason and faith address the same reality; some truths about God are accessible to reason, others require revelation
  2. **Fideism** (Kierkegaard, Pascal): faith transcends or even opposes reason — the "leap of faith"; Pascal's Wager (it is pragmatically rational to believe in God)
  3. **Conflict** (Hume, Russell, the New Atheists): religious claims are vulnerable to rational criticism and cannot withstand scrutiny
