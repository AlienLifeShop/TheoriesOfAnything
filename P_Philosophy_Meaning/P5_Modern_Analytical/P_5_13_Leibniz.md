## QUICK SUMMARY

**Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz** (1646–1716) was among the most versatile intellects in Western history — a mathematician, philosopher, logician, diplomat, jurist, historian, and engineer who co-invented the infinitesimal calculus (independently of Newton), laid foundations for formal logic and computational theory, and constructed one of the most ambitious metaphysical systems ever conceived. His philosophy centers on the **monad** — the ultimate simple substance, without parts, windows, or spatial extension, yet mirroring the entire universe from its unique perspective. Every monad is a self-contained "living mirror of the universe" whose internal states unfold according to a program implanted by God. The apparent interaction between substances (mind and body, one body and another) is not real causation but **pre-established harmony** — God coordinated all monads at creation so their independent unfoldings correspond perfectly, like perfectly synchronized clocks. Leibniz is also famous for his **theodicy** — the argument that this world, despite containing evil, is the **"best of all possible worlds"** because an omniscient, omnipotent, omnibenevolent God would necessarily create the world with the greatest compossible perfection. Satirized by Voltaire in *Candide* (through the character Dr. Pangloss), Leibniz's optimism is actually a sophisticated logical argument grounded in his **principle of sufficient reason** (nothing exists without a reason why it exists rather than not) and the notion of **compossibility** (not all possibilities are jointly realizable; God selects the maximally harmonious combination).

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 Life and Works
- Born in Leipzig, Saxony; earned his doctorate in law at age 20. Served as diplomat and advisor to multiple German courts, spending much of his career in Hanover
- Co-invented the infinitesimal calculus (published 1684, using the notation — dx, ∫ — that became standard), independently of Newton (who developed his version earlier but published later), leading to a bitter priority dispute
- Major philosophical works: *Discourse on Metaphysics* (1686), *New Essays on Human Understanding* (completed 1704, published posthumously 1765 — a detailed response to Locke), *Theodicy* (1710), *Monadology* (1714)
- Also contributed to: binary arithmetic, combinatorics, the concept of kinetic energy (*vis viva*), geology, formal logic, the dream of a universal symbolic language (*characteristica universalis*), and practical inventions (calculating machine, mining technology)

### 1.2 The Monadology
- **Monads** are the ultimate simple substances — they have no parts, no extension, no shape, and no windows through which anything could enter or leave:
  - Every monad has **perceptions** (internal representations of the universe) and **appetitions** (tendencies toward new perceptions) — a monad is thus like a tiny mind or soul
  - Monads differ from each other by their degree of clarity of perception: bare monads (inorganic matter — confused, unconscious perceptions), animal souls (sensation, memory), rational souls (self-consciousness, reason)
  - **Petites perceptions**: each monad has an infinity of minute, unconscious perceptions — anticipating later ideas about the unconscious mind
  - **No inter-monadic causation**: monads do not interact with each other. All apparent causal interaction is the result of pre-established harmony

### 1.3 Pre-Established Harmony
- God, in creating the world, programmed each monad's internal sequence of states so that all monads unfold in perfect coordination — the mind and body appear to interact, but each follows its own law:
  - Resolves the **mind-body problem** without Descartes's interactionism or Malebranche's occasionalism
  - The "clock analogy": two perfectly synchronized clocks keep the same time not because one causes the other but because they were set in coordination by the clockmaker

### 1.4 Principle of Sufficient Reason
- **Nothing exists without a sufficient reason why it exists rather than not, and why it is so rather than otherwise** — a foundational principle of Leibniz's metaphysics:
  - Applied to God's choice of this world: God had a sufficient reason (namely, it is the best) for creating this particular world
  - Applied to the **identity of indiscernibles**: if two things were qualitatively identical in every respect, there would be no sufficient reason for them to be distinct — therefore no two things are perfectly alike

### 1.5 Theodicy: The Best of All Possible Worlds
- **Theodicy** (a term Leibniz coined): the justification of God's goodness in the face of evil:
  - God, being omniscient, surveys all possible worlds; being omnibenevolent, selects the best; being omnipotent, creates it
  - Evil exists because no finite world can be absolutely perfect — some imperfection is necessary for the greatest overall good (e.g., shadows make a painting more beautiful)
  - Three kinds of evil: **metaphysical** (finitude/imperfection inherent in being a creature), **physical** (suffering), **moral** (sin — arising from free will)
  - **Compossibility**: not all good things are jointly realizable; the best possible world is the one with the greatest compossible perfection — the maximum variety of phenomena with the maximum simplicity of laws

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Leibniz and Logic
- Leibniz envisioned a **characteristica universalis** — a universal formal language in which all knowledge could be expressed — and a **calculus ratiocinator** — a mechanical procedure for resolving disputes by calculation ("Let us calculate!"):
  - This vision anticipated formal logic (Frege, Russell), computer science (Turing), and artificial intelligence
  - Leibniz also developed a sophisticated theory of possible worlds that was revived in 20th-century modal logic (Kripke)

### 2.2 Debate Over the Status of Monads
- Scholars debate whether Leibniz's monads are best understood as: (a) substance pluralism (there really are infinitely many independent substances), (b) phenomenalism/idealism (only minds/perceptions are real; matter is "well-founded phenomenon"), or (c) a precursor to information-theoretic ontology

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Leibniz's Optimism Was Naïve
- **[OVERSIMPLIFIED]** Voltaire's *Candide* (1759) brilliantly satirized Leibniz's optimism, but the satire should not be confused with a refutation. Leibniz did not claim that this world is free of evil or suffering — only that no better compossible world was available. His argument is a subtle logical claim about the optimization constraints on divine creation
