## QUICK SUMMARY

**African philosophy** extends far beyond the Ubuntu concept most familiar to Western audiences. It is a diverse, complex, frequently contested field encompassing multiple traditions, methods, and debates. The "Great Debate" in African philosophy concern whether a distinctly African philosophy exists and what form it takes. **Ethnophilosophy** (Placide Tempels's *Bantu Philosophy*, 1945; Alexis Kagame; John Mbiti) treats the collective worldview, cosmology, and value systems of traditional African cultures as philosophy — an approach criticized by **Paulin Hountondji** (*African Philosophy: Myth and Reality*, 1976) as reducing philosophy to ethnography and denying Africans individual critical thinking. **Sage philosophy** (Henry Odera Oruka) identified indigenous thinkers — individual sages within traditional communities — who engaged in genuine philosophical reflection (critical, systematic reasoning about fundamental questions) independent of Western academic training. **Négritude** (Léopold Sédar Senghor, Aimé Césaire) was a literary and philosophical movement asserting the value and distinctiveness of Black African culture and experience against colonial denigration — emphasizing emotion, rhythm, and communal consciousness, though later criticized (by Wole Soyinka, among others) for essentializing African identity. **Professional/academic African philosophy** (Wiredu, Gyekye, Appiah, Masolo, Menkiti) engages directly with global philosophical problems — personal identity, communalism vs. individualism, truth, justice — drawing on African concepts and intellectual resources while meeting international standards of philosophical rigor.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 The Great Debate: What Counts as African Philosophy?
- Four major trends identified by **Henry Odera Oruka** (1990):
  1. **Ethnophilosophy**: the collective worldviews, myths, proverbs, and value systems of traditional African societies as philosophy (Tempels, Mbiti, Kagame)
  2. **Philosophic sagacity (sage philosophy)**: individual traditional thinkers who engage in critical, reflective reasoning — Oruka conducted interviews with Kenyan sages demonstrating that philosophical thinking is not confined to literate or university-trained individuals
  3. **Nationalist-ideological philosophy**: political philosophies of African leaders (e.g., Nkrumah's Consciencism, Nyerere's Ujamaa, Senghor's Négritude)
  4. **Professional philosophy**: rigorous, critical philosophical work by African philosophers trained in both African and Western traditions (Hountondji, Wiredu, Gyekye, Appiah)

### 1.2 Ethnophilosophy and Its Critics
- **Placide Tempels**, *Bantu Philosophy* (1945): attempted to reconstruct a coherent metaphysical system from Bantu worldview — centered on the concept of "vital force" as the fundamental reality
  - **Criticism (Hountondji, 1976)**: ethnophilosophy is not genuine philosophy because it: (a) attributes beliefs to entire ethnic groups rather than individuals; (b) treats mythology and custom as philosophy without critical examination; (c) was often constructed by non-African scholars projecting Western categories onto African thought
  - Hountondji insisted on a **universalist** conception: philosophy is critical, individual, written inquiry that can be done by Africans without being about specifically "African" themes

### 1.3 Sage Philosophy
- **Oruka's project**: traveled to rural Kenya and recorded extended philosophical dialogues with traditional sages, distinguishing between "folk sages" (who simply transmit traditional wisdom) and "philosophic sages" (who critically examine, challenge, and revise received wisdom)
- Demonstrated that philosophical thinking — abstract, critical, reflective — occurs in oral cultures and is not dependent on literacy or Western education

### 1.4 Négritude
- A literary and intellectual movement founded in 1930s Paris by **Aimé Césaire** (Martinique), **Léopold Sédar Senghor** (Senegal), and **Léon-Gontran Damas** (French Guiana):
  - Affirmed the value, beauty, and distinctiveness of Black African civilization against European colonial claims of African cultural inferiority
  - Senghor contrasted "African reason" (intuitive, participatory, rhythmic) with "European reason" (analytical, objectifying, mechanistic) — a contrast widely debated
  - **Wole Soyinka** criticized Négritude: "A tiger does not proclaim its tigritude" — suggesting the movement reinforced the very essentialism it opposed

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Communalism and Personhood
- **Kwame Gyekye** (*An Essay on African Philosophical Thought*, 1987): defended a "moderate communitarianism" — the individual is embedded in community but retains autonomy, rights, and capacity for critical reflection
- **Ifeanyi Menkiti** ("Person and Community in African Traditional Thought," 1984): in many African traditions, personhood is not given at birth but **achieved** through moral development, social integration, and fulfillment of communal duties
- **Kwasi Wiredu** advocated **conceptual decolonization** — identifying and eliminating distortions introduced into African thought by colonial languages and categories

### 2.2 African Contributions to Global Philosophy
- Wiredu's work on truth, democracy, and consensus; Gyekye on rights and communalism; Appiah (*In My Father's House*, 1992) on race, identity, and cosmopolitanism; Mudimbe (*The Invention of Africa*, 1988) on the epistemology of colonial knowledge — these demonstrate that African philosophy contributes to universal philosophical problems, not only parochial ones

### 3.1 Reconstructive Potential of Oral Philosophy
- Scholars argue that substantial philosophical systems are embedded in African oral traditions (proverbs, myths, ritual practices) that remain largely unrecovered — suggesting that the full scope of African philosophical thought is far wider than current scholarship has mapped

### 4.1 Africa Had No Philosophy Before European Contact
- **[INCORRECT]** This claim, once commonplace in Western academia, has been thoroughly refuted. Pre-colonial Africa produced complex cosmological, ethical, and metaphysical thought systems — both in oral traditions and in literate traditions (e.g., Ethiopian philosophy, Timbuktu manuscripts)
