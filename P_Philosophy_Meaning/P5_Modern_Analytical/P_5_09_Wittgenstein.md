## QUICK SUMMARY

**Ludwig Josef Johann Wittgenstein** (1889-1951) is unique in the history of philosophy for having produced two profoundly influential but largely incompatible philosophical systems. His first major work, the *Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus* (1921) — a compressed, crystalline treatise of 75 pages structured as numbered propositions — advanced a **picture theory of meaning** according to which language mirrors the logical structure of the world: meaningful propositions are logical pictures of possible states of affairs, and what cannot be pictured (ethics, aesthetics, the meaning of life, the mystical) cannot be meaningfully said — leading to the famous concluding proposition: "Whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent" (*Wovon man nicht sprechen kann, darüber muß man schweigen*). The *Tractatus* profoundly influenced the **Vienna Circle** and logical positivism. Having believed he had solved all philosophical problems, Wittgenstein left academic philosophy — but returned in 1929, and over the next two decades developed a radically different approach, published posthumously as the *Philosophical Investigations* (1953). In this "later" philosophy, Wittgenstein abandoned the picture theory, arguing instead that the meaning of a word is its **use** in a particular "**language game**" (*Sprachspiel*) — a rule-governed social activity embedded in a "**form of life**" (*Lebensform*). General concepts are united not by shared essences but by overlapping **family resemblances** (*Familienähnlichkeiten*). The **private language argument** — the claim that a language understandable only by a single individual is impossible — challenged foundational assumptions about consciousness, inner experience, and the epistemology of mind.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 Biographical Context
- Born January 26, 1889, in Vienna, into one of the wealthiest families in Europe (the Wittgensteins were a major industrial dynasty; three of Ludwig's four brothers died by suicide)
- Studied mechanical engineering in Berlin and Manchester, then moved to Cambridge (1911) to study logic with **Bertrand Russell**, who recognized his extraordinary philosophical talent
- Served in the Austro-Hungarian army during World War I — wrote much of the *Tractatus* in notebooks while in the trenches and as a prisoner of war in Italy
- After the *Tractatus*, gave away his inherited fortune and worked as a village schoolteacher in Austria (1920-1926), designed a house for his sister in Vienna, and returned to Cambridge in 1929
- Became British professor of philosophy at Cambridge (1939-1947); died of prostate cancer, April 29, 1951, in Cambridge. His last words: "Tell them I've had a wonderful life"

### 1.2 Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus (1921)
- Structure: seven main propositions (numbered 1-7) with hierarchical sub-propositions:
  1. "The world is everything that is the case"
  2. "What is the case, the fact, is the existence of atomic facts"
  3-4. Propositions are logical pictures of facts; a proposition shows its sense
  5-6. Logic and mathematics are tautologies; ethical and metaphysical propositions are nonsensical (they try to say what can only be shown)
  7. "Whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent"
- **Picture Theory**: a meaningful proposition shares a logical form (structure of relations) with the state of affairs it depicts — like a model in a courtroom representing a traffic accident
- **The Mystical**: Wittgenstein was not dismissing ethics, aesthetics, or religion as unimportant — on the contrary, he wrote to a publisher that the book's point was ethical, and the unwritten part (what it leaves unsaid) was the more important half
- Influence on the **Vienna Circle**: members (Schlick, Carnap, Neurath) adopted the *Tractatus*'s distinction between meaningful (empirically verifiable) and meaningless propositions as the basis for **logical positivism** — though Wittgenstein himself disagreed with their interpretation

### 1.3 Philosophical Investigations (Published 1953)
- Wittgenstein's later masterwork, written over 1936-1949, published posthumously:
  - **Language Games** (*Sprachspiele*): language is not a single, unified logical system (as the *Tractatus* assumed) but a vast collection of diverse, overlapping activities — giving orders, describing objects, telling jokes, asking questions, praying, guessing riddles, etc. The meaning of a word is determined by how it is used within a particular language game
  - **Meaning as Use**: "For a large class of cases...the meaning of a word is its use in the language" (§43)
  - **Family Resemblance** (*Familienähnlichkeiten*): general concepts (e.g., "game") are not defined by a single set of necessary and sufficient conditions but by a network of overlapping similarities — "a complicated network of similarities overlapping and criss-crossing" (§66)
  - **Rule Following**: the paradox that no rule can determine its own application — an infinite number of continuations could be consistent with any finite set of examples. This leads to the insight that rule-following is fundamentally a **social practice**, not a private mental act
  - **Private Language Argument** (§§243-315): a language whose terms refer to the speaker's private sensations (known only to the speaker) is impossible — because there would be no criterion for correctness (no distinction between following a rule and merely thinking one is following it). This undermines the Cartesian picture of the mind as a private theater

### 1.4 Forms of Life
- Language games are embedded in "**forms of life**" (*Lebensformen*) — the broader social, cultural, and biological context of human activity:
  - "If a lion could talk, we could not understand him" — because his form of life would be too different from ours
  - Philosophy's task is not to discover hidden essences but to **describe** the ordinary workings of language and dissolve philosophical confusions caused by language's bewitchment

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Therapeutic Conception of Philosophy
- Later Wittgenstein saw philosophy not as a body of doctrine but as an **activity** — a therapy for conceptual confusion:
  - "Philosophy is a battle against the bewitchment of our intelligence by means of language" (§109)
  - The goal is not to solve philosophical problems but to **dissolve** them by showing they arise from misunderstandings of language

### 2.2 Influence
- Wittgenstein's later philosophy influenced **ordinary language philosophy** (Austin, Ryle), philosophy of mind and psychology (Anscombe, Malcolm), the philosophy of social science (Winch), and postmodern thought

### 4.1 Wittgenstein "Proved" Philosophy Is Impossible
- **[OVERSIMPLIFIED]** Wittgenstein did not claim philosophy was meaningless — he argued that traditional philosophical problems arise from linguistic confusion and can be dissolved through careful attention to language. He practiced philosophy intensively throughout his life
