## QUICK SUMMARY

**Environmental ethics** — the branch of philosophy that extends moral consideration beyond humans to include animals, ecosystems, species, and the natural world — emerged as a distinct field in the 1970s in response to the ecological crisis, though its roots trace to **Aldo Leopold's** *A Sand County Almanac* (1949: "A thing is right when it tends to preserve the integrity, stability, and beauty of the biotic community"). The field spans a spectrum from **anthropocentrism** (nature has value only as a resource for humans), through **biocentrism** (all living things have intrinsic value — Paul Taylor, *Respect for Nature*, 1986), to **ecocentrism** (ecosystems, species, and ecological processes have value independent of individual organisms). **Arne Naess** (1973) coined **deep ecology** — distinguishing it from "shallow ecology" (which merely manages environmental problems for human benefit) — as a philosophical platform asserting the intrinsic value of all life, rejecting human supremacism, and demanding radical reduction of human impact on the biosphere. **Peter Singer** (*Animal Liberation*, 1975) extended utilitarian ethics to sentient animals based on their capacity to suffer. The field now addresses **climate ethics** (who bears responsibility for climate change? What do we owe future generations?), **environmental justice** (disproportionate environmental burdens on marginalized communities), and **indigenous environmental philosophies** (which often assume human-nature kinship rather than separation). Environmental ethics challenges the dominant Western assumption that nature exists primarily as a resource for human use — an assumption deeply relevant to this project's themes of ancient knowledge, lost connections to natural systems, and the consequences of ecological disruption.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Leopold and the Land Ethic
- **Aldo Leopold** (*A Sand County Almanac*, 1949):
  - Proposed the **land ethic**: extend moral consideration to the **biotic community** — soils, waters, plants, and animals collectively
  - "A thing is right when it tends to preserve the integrity, stability, and beauty of the biotic community. It is wrong when it tends otherwise"
  - Leopold argued that ethical development has historically expanded the circle of moral concern (from family to tribe to nation to humanity) and that the next extension is to the land itself
  - The land ethic remains the foundational text of ecocentrist environmental ethics

### 1.2 Singer and Animal Liberation
- **Peter Singer** (*Animal Liberation*, 1975):
  - Extended utilitarian ethics to all **sentient** beings (those capable of suffering): the relevant moral criterion is not species membership but capacity for suffering
  - **Speciesism** — discrimination based solely on species membership — is morally analogous to racism or sexism
  - Practical implications: opposition to factory farming, animal experimentation, and other practices that cause unnecessary animal suffering
- **Tom Regan** (*The Case for Animal Rights*, 1983): argued from a rights-based (deontological, not utilitarian) framework that animals are "subjects of a life" possessing inherent value and rights not to be treated as mere means

### 1.3 Deep Ecology
- **Arne Naess** (1973, *Inquiry* 16: 95–100, "The Shallow and the Deep, Long-Range Ecology Movement"):
  - **Shallow ecology**: fights pollution and resource depletion primarily to protect human welfare within existing social structures
  - **Deep ecology**: questions the foundational assumptions of anthropocentrism; principles include:
    - The well-being of non-human life has value independent of its usefulness to humans
    - The richness and diversity of life are values in themselves
    - Humans have no right to reduce this richness except to satisfy vital needs
    - Present human interference with the non-human world is excessive
  - Later formalized with Sessions as the **Deep Ecology Platform** (8 principles, 1984)
  - **Counter-Argument:** Critics (Guha 1989; Bookchin 1987) argue deep ecology is politically naive, ignores class and colonial dynamics of environmental destruction, reflects First World privilege, and risks misanthropy

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Climate Ethics
- The ethics of climate change involves several unresolved questions:
  - **Intergenerational justice**: What obligations do present generations have to future generations who will bear the costs of current emissions? (Parfit, *Reasons and Persons*, 1984; Gardiner, *A Perfect Moral Storm*, 2011)
  - **Historical responsibility**: Should nations that contributed most to cumulative emissions bear more of the mitigation burden? (Shue 1993; Caney 2005)
  - **Non-identity problem** (Parfit): future people's very existence depends on present choices, making it paradoxical to claim our actions "harm" them
  - **Discount rates**: How much should future welfare be discounted relative to present welfare? (Stern Review 2006 vs. Nordhaus — a difference in ethical assumptions about discounting produces dramatically different policy recommendations)

### 2.2 Ecofeminism
- **Ecofeminism** (Warren 1990; Plumwood 1993; Merchant 1980):
  - Identifies structural parallels between the domination of nature and the domination of women — both rooted in patriarchal dualistic thinking (culture/nature, mind/body, male/female, rational/emotional)
  - Argues that environmental ethics must address both ecological and gender oppression simultaneously
  - **Counter-Argument:** Critics worry about essentialism — equating women with nature may reinforce rather than challenge patriarchal stereotypes

### 2.3 Environmental Justice
- The **environmental justice** movement (beginning with Warren County, North Carolina protests 1982; Bullard, *Dumping in Dixie*, 1990):
  - Documents that marginalized communities (low-income, communities of color, indigenous peoples) disproportionately bear environmental harms (toxic waste sites, polluting industries, climate vulnerability)
  - This challenges purely ecological environmentalism by insisting that environmental ethics must address social justice and power relations
  - Recognized by the EPA (Executive Order 12898, 1994) and now a standard consideration in environmental policy

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Rights of Nature and Legal Personhood
- Several jurisdictions have granted **legal personhood** or rights to natural entities:
  - Ecuador (2008 constitution): rights of Pachamama (Mother Earth)
  - New Zealand (2017): Whanganui River granted legal personhood
  - Bolivia (2010): Law of the Rights of Mother Earth
  - Whether these legal innovations produce meaningful environmental protection or remain symbolic is still being assessed — the philosophical and practical implications are unresolved

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Nature Is an Infallible Guide to Human Ethics
- **[DEBUNKED]** The **naturalistic fallacy** (deriving "ought" from "is") applies to both directions: claims that "natural" = "good" (justifying practices as ethical because they are "natural") or claims that ecological principles directly dictate human social ethics ignore the gap between descriptive facts about nature and prescriptive moral norms — parasitism, predation, and extinction are natural but not therefore morally praiseworthy
