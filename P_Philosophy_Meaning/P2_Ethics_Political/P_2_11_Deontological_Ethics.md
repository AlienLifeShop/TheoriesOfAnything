## QUICK SUMMARY

**Deontological ethics** (from Greek *deon*, "duty" or "obligation") is the family of moral theories holding that the rightness or wrongness of an action depends on the action's conformity to **moral rules, duties, or rights** — not on its consequences (as utilitarianism holds) or on the character of the agent (as virtue ethics holds). The foundational deontological system is **Immanuel Kant's** moral philosophy, centered on the **categorical imperative** — the supreme moral law that binds all rational beings unconditionally. Kant formulated this law in several complementary ways: (1) the **universalizability formula** — "Act only according to that maxim through which you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law"; (2) the **humanity formula** — "Act so that you treat humanity, whether in your own person or in that of any other, always as an end and never merely as a means"; and (3) the **kingdom of ends formula** — "Act according to maxims of a universally legislating member of a merely possible kingdom of ends." Kantian deontology has been developed in the 20th-21st centuries by **Christine Korsgaard** (constructivism — moral authority derives from the activity of practical reason itself), **Onora O'Neill** (emphasizing the requirement of non-deception and non-coercion), and **Thomas Scanlon** (contractualism — morality consists of principles no one could reasonably reject). An important alternative deontological framework is **W.D. Ross's** theory of **prima facie duties** (1930) — plural, competing moral obligations (fidelity, reparation, gratitude, justice, beneficence, self-improvement, non-maleficence) that must be weighed against each other in particular situations.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 Kantian Foundations
- Kant's moral philosophy is developed primarily in three works:
  - *Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals* (1785): the foundational text — derives the categorical imperative from the concept of a good will and the requirements of practical reason
  - *Critique of Practical Reason* (1788): demonstrates the reality of moral freedom and the moral law's authority
  - *Metaphysics of Morals* (1797): applies the categorical imperative to specific duties (of justice and virtue)

### 1.2 The Categorical Imperative
- The categorical imperative is **categorical** (binding unconditionally, regardless of desires or goals) as opposed to hypothetical imperatives ("If you want X, do Y"):
  - **Formula of Universal Law** (FUL): "Act only according to that maxim through which you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law"
    - Test: universalize the maxim of your action — if universalization leads to a logical contradiction (a "contradiction in conception," e.g., false promising — if everyone lied, promises would cease to exist) or a practical impossibility (a "contradiction in the will," e.g., refusing all help to others while rationally needing help yourself), the action is morally impermissible
  - **Formula of Humanity** (FH): "So act that you use humanity, whether in your own person or in the person of any other, always at the same time as an end, never merely as a means"
    - Rational beings possess **dignity** (*Würde*) — unconditional, incomparable worth — not a market price. Treating someone merely as a means (using them as a tool without regard for their own rational agency) violates their dignity
  - **Formula of the Kingdom of Ends** (FKE): act as a **legislative member** of an ideal community of rational beings, each respecting the other's autonomy — a moral republic where laws are self-imposed by reason

### 1.3 Autonomy
- **Autonomy** (*Autonomie*) is the central concept of Kantian ethics:
  - Moral agents are **self-legislating** — they give themselves the moral law through their own rational agency, rather than receiving it from external authority (God, nature, society, or desire)
  - Freedom = autonomy = the ability to act according to the moral law one gives oneself
  - **Heteronomy**: acting from external motives (inclination, habit, fear of punishment, desire for reward) — such actions lack moral worth in the distinctive Kantian sense, even if they happen to conform to duty

### 1.4 W.D. Ross's Prima Facie Duties
- **Sir William David Ross** (*The Right and the Good*, 1930): proposed a pluralist deontology as an alternative to Kantian monism:
  - **Prima facie duties** (*pro tanto* duties): moral obligations that are binding unless overridden by a stronger competing duty in specific circumstances:
    1. **Fidelity**: keeping promises and being truthful
    2. **Reparation**: making amends for past wrongs
    3. **Gratitude**: returning benefits received
    4. **Justice**: distributing goods according to merit
    5. **Beneficence**: helping others
    6. **Self-improvement**: developing one's own knowledge and virtue
    7. **Non-maleficence**: not harming others (Ross considered this the strongest prima facie duty)
  - When duties conflict (e.g., a promise conflicts with preventing harm), the agent must exercise **judgment** (not apply a mechanical rule) to determine which duty is overriding in the particular situation

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Contemporary Kantians
- **Christine Korsgaard** (*The Sources of Normativity*, 1996): moral obligations arise from the **reflective structure of human consciousness** — when we reflect on our impulses and ask whether to act on them, we must endorse principles that can withstand reflective scrutiny. This is a form of **constructivism**
- **Onora O'Neill** (*Constructions of Reason*, 1989): emphasizes that the categorical imperative requires **transparency** (non-deception) and **respect for agency** (non-coercion) — principles with direct implications for informed consent, political legitimacy, and international justice
- **Thomas Scanlon** (*What We Owe to Each Other*, 1998): **contractualism** — "An act is wrong if its performance under the circumstances would be disallowed by any set of principles for the general regulation of behavior that no one could reasonably reject." This is deontological (focused on the nature of the principles, not consequences) but grounded in reasonable agreement rather than the categorical imperative

### 2.2 Rights-Based Deontology
- **Rights-based** deontological theories (Nozick, Gewirth, Dworkin) hold that individuals possess **rights** (to life, liberty, property, bodily integrity) that constrain what may be done to them, even in pursuit of the greater good — "rights as trumps" (Dworkin) or "side constraints" (Nozick)

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Deontology Is Rigid and Ignores Consequences
- **[OVERSIMPLIFIED]** While deontological theories prioritize duties and rights over consequences, sophisticated versions (Ross, O'Neill, Scanlon) allow for contextual judgment and acknowledge that consequences matter — they simply deny that consequences are the *only* thing that matters
