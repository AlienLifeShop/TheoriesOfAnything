## QUICK SUMMARY

**Utilitarianism** is the ethical theory that the morally right action in any situation is the one that produces the **greatest overall happiness** (or well-being, or preference satisfaction) for the greatest number of those affected — a form of **consequentialism** (the view that the moral value of an action is determined solely by its consequences). Founded in its modern form by **Jeremy Bentham** (1748-1832), who proposed a systematic "**felicific calculus**" for quantifying pleasure and pain, and refined by **John Stuart Mill** (1806-1873), who introduced the distinction between **higher and lower pleasures** and defended individual liberty, utilitarianism became the dominant framework in Anglo-American moral philosophy and public policy. In the 20th and 21st centuries, **Peter Singer** (b. 1946) has championed a preference-based utilitarianism with radical implications for **animal rights** (since animals can suffer, their interests must be weighed equally) and global poverty (the obligation to give substantially to those in extreme need), helping to inspire the **effective altruism** movement. Key variants include **act utilitarianism** (judge each individual action by its consequences), **rule utilitarianism** (follow rules whose general adoption would produce the best consequences), and **preference utilitarianism** (maximize the satisfaction of preferences rather than subjective pleasure).

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 Jeremy Bentham (1748-1832)
- English philosopher, jurist, and social reformer — the founder of modern utilitarianism:
  - **The principle of utility** (*An Introduction to the Principles of Morals and Legislation*, 1789): "Nature has placed mankind under the governance of two sovereign masters, pain and pleasure. It is for them alone to point out what we ought to do"
  - **Felicific calculus**: a method for calculating the total quantity of pleasure and pain produced by an action, considering seven dimensions: intensity, duration, certainty/uncertainty, propinquity (nearness in time), fecundity (likelihood of producing further pleasure/pain), purity, and extent (number of persons affected)
  - **Egalitarian implication**: "everybody to count for one, nobody for more than one" — each person's happiness counts equally
  - Bentham was also a radical **legal reformer**: he advocated for the codification of law, the abolition of slavery and capital punishment, animal welfare legislation, the decriminalization of homosexuality, equal rights for women, and prison reform (designing the **Panopticon** — a prison architecture enabling constant surveillance)
  - His preserved body (auto-icon) is displayed at University College London to this day

### 1.2 John Stuart Mill (1806-1873)
- English philosopher, economist, and politician:
  - **Higher and lower pleasures** (*Utilitarianism*, 1863): Mill rejected Bentham's purely quantitative approach, arguing that some pleasures are qualitatively superior — "It is better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied"
  - **Competent judges**: the distinction between higher and lower pleasures is determined by those who have experienced both
  - **The harm principle** (*On Liberty*, 1859): the only legitimate reason for restricting individual liberty is to prevent harm to others — one of the foundational texts of liberal political theory
  - Also made major contributions to logic (*A System of Logic*), political economy (*Principles of Political Economy*), and the advancement of women's rights (*The Subjection of Women*, 1869)

### 1.3 Varieties of Utilitarianism
- **Act utilitarianism**: each individual action should be judged by whether it maximizes utility (the "direct" form)
- **Rule utilitarianism**: actions should conform to rules whose general adoption would maximize utility (addresses the objection that act utilitarianism might justify individual injustices)
- **Preference utilitarianism** (Hare, Singer): the right action maximizes the satisfaction of people's **preferences** rather than subjective pleasure — avoids the problem of defining and comparing subjective experiences
- **Negative utilitarianism**: focuses on minimizing suffering rather than maximizing happiness
- **Two-level utilitarianism** (R.M. Hare): everyday moral decisions use simple rules (intuitive level), but in cases of conflict, direct utilitarian calculation applies (critical level)

### 1.4 Peter Singer (b. 1946)
- Australian moral philosopher, currently at Princeton University:
  - *Animal Liberation* (1975): extended the utilitarian principle of equal consideration of interests to **all sentient beings** — if animals can suffer, their suffering must be weighed equally with comparable human suffering. This work is foundational to the modern **animal rights movement**
  - *Famine, Affluence, and Morality* (1972): argued that the distinction between letting someone die (through inaction) and killing them is morally insignificant — affluent individuals have a strong moral obligation to donate to those in extreme poverty
  - Influenced the **effective altruism** (EA) movement — the practice of using evidence and reason to determine the most effective ways to benefit others

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Major Objections
- **The problem of justice**: utilitarianism could in principle justify punishing the innocent or violating rights if doing so maximized overall happiness — critics (Rawls, Nozick) argue this shows it fails to respect individual rights
- **Demandingness objection**: if we are obligated to maximize total happiness, virtually all of our time and resources should be devoted to helping others — an unrealistically demanding standard
- **The problem of measurement**: comparing and aggregating happiness or preferences across different individuals is extremely difficult, possibly impossible
- **Bernard Williams's objection** ("integrity"): utilitarianism alienates agents from their own projects, commitments, and relationships by requiring them to view everything from an impersonal, aggregate standpoint

### 2.2 Utilitarianism in Public Policy
- Despite philosophical objections, utilitarian reasoning (particularly **cost-benefit analysis**) remains the dominant framework in public policy, economics, and healthcare resource allocation (e.g., QALYs — quality-adjusted life years)

### 3.1 Effective Altruism's Long-Term Future
- Some effective altruists (influenced by utilitarian reasoning) argue for **longtermism** — the view that the most important moral consideration is the effect of our actions on the long-term future of humanity (billions of potential future persons). This is philosophically interesting but practically controversial

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Utilitarianism Is "Cold" or Amoral
- **[OVERSIMPLIFIED]** Utilitarianism is a deeply morally engaged theory — it demands concern for the suffering of all sentient beings. Mill explicitly argued that it promotes sympathy, altruism, and social reform. The claim that it is amoral confuses its methodology with its motivation
