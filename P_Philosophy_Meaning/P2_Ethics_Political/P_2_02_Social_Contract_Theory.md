## QUICK SUMMARY

**Social contract theory** — the philosophical tradition that grounds political authority, law, and moral obligation in a (real or hypothetical) agreement among rational individuals — is one of the most influential frameworks in Western political philosophy and continues to shape contemporary debates about justice, governance, and human rights. The tradition's major architects are **Thomas Hobbes** (*Leviathan*, 1651: absent sovereign authority, life is "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short" — rational self-interest drives people to surrender liberty to an absolute sovereign in exchange for security), **John Locke** (*Two Treatises of Government*, 1689: people possess natural rights to life, liberty, and property; government exists to protect these rights and may be overthrown when it fails to do so — the theoretical foundation of liberal democracy and the American Declaration of Independence), and **Jean-Jacques Rousseau** (*The Social Contract*, 1762: legitimate political authority rests on a social compact in which individuals surrender particular freedoms to the "general will" of the community, which alone can make them truly free). In the 20th century, **John Rawls** (*A Theory of Justice*, 1971) revitalized the tradition with his thought experiment of the **original position** and **veil of ignorance** — imagining what principles of justice rational people would choose if they did not know their position in society — arguing they would choose (1) equal basic liberties for all and (2) arrangements where social and economic inequalities benefit the least-advantaged members (the difference principle). The social contract tradition has been critiqued by feminists (Pateman), race theorists (Mills), communitarian philosophers (MacIntyre, Sandel), and capability theorists (Sen, Nussbaum) for its individualistic assumptions, its reliance on idealized rational agents, and its historical exclusion of women, non-white peoples, and non-Western perspectives from the contracting parties.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Hobbes: The Leviathan
- **Hobbes** (*Leviathan*, 1651): the **state of nature** — life without government — is a "war of all against all" because humans are roughly equal in ability, meaning anyone can be killed by anyone else:
  - In this condition, there is no industry, agriculture, or culture because nothing is secure
  - Rational self-interest drives people to establish a sovereign authority (the "Leviathan") through mutual covenant: all surrender their natural freedom in exchange for security and order
  - The sovereign's authority is absolute and indivisible; rebellion is never justified because the alternative (return to the state of nature) is always worse
  - **Historical context:** Written during the English Civil War, Hobbes's argument was for monarchical authority, but the logic of his social contract applies to any sovereign power, including democratic states

### 1.2 Locke: Natural Rights and Limited Government
- **Locke** (*Two Treatises*, 1689): the state of nature is not Hobbesian war but a condition of **freedom and equality** governed by natural law — individuals possess natural rights to **life, liberty, and property**:
  - Government is established by consent to protect these pre-existing rights; its authority is limited and conditional
  - Government that violates natural rights forfeits its legitimacy; the people retain the right of **revolution**
  - Locke's property theory: labor mixed with nature creates property rights ("labor theory of property") — influential but criticized for justifying colonial appropriation of indigenous lands
  - Foundational influence on the American Declaration of Independence (1776), the U.S. Constitution, and liberal democratic theory globally

### 1.3 Rousseau: The General Will
- **Rousseau** (*The Social Contract*, 1762; *Discourse on Inequality*, 1755):
  - Distinguished between the original "state of nature" (where humans are naturally good, compassionate, and free) and civilized society (which corrupts through inequality and dependence on others)
  - The social contract creates a political community in which each person, "uniting with all, nevertheless obeys only himself and remains as free as before" — through submission to the **general will** (the common good, not the sum of individual preferences)
  - The general will can err; Rousseau's solution — "whoever refuses to obey the general will shall be forced to be free" — has been both praised as democratic foundationalism and criticized as a justification for totalitarianism (Berlin 1969)

### 1.4 Rawls: Justice as Fairness
- **Rawls** (*A Theory of Justice*, 1971):
  - The **original position**: imagine choosing principles of justice from behind a **veil of ignorance** — you don't know your race, sex, wealth, talents, intelligence, or conception of the good
  - Rawls argued rational people in this position would choose two principles:
    1. **Equal liberty principle**: each person has equal right to the most extensive basic liberties compatible with similar liberties for all
    2. **Difference principle**: social and economic inequalities are permissible only if they benefit the least-advantaged members of society
  - This framework revitalized political philosophy and generated vast literature in response (Nozick's libertarian critique, 1974; Sen's capability critique, 1980; Sandel's communitarian critique, 1982)

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Feminist and Racial Critiques
- **Carole Pateman** (*The Sexual Contract*, 1988): argued that the "original" social contract was simultaneously a **sexual contract** — establishing patriarchal right over women; women were not included as contracting parties but as objects of the contract
- **Charles Mills** (*The Racial Contract*, 1997): argued that the social contract has historically been a **racial contract** — an agreement among white people to exploit non-white peoples, with the "state of nature" serving as a category applied to colonized peoples to deny them political standing
- These critiques do not necessarily reject contractualism but demand its radical expansion to include those historically excluded

### 2.2 Contractualism vs. Communitarianism
- **Communitarian critique** (MacIntyre, *After Virtue*, 1981; Sandel, *Liberalism and the Limits of Justice*, 1982; Taylor, *Sources of the Self*, 1989):
  - Social contract theory assumes an "unencumbered self" — an autonomous individual capable of choosing values and commitments from a neutral standpoint
  - Communitarians argue this is a fiction: people are always embedded in communities, traditions, and relationships that constitute their identities; moral reasoning begins from within a tradition, not from behind a veil of ignorance
  - **Counter-Argument (from Rawlsians):** Rawls's later work (*Political Liberalism*, 1993) explicitly acknowledged reasonable pluralism and argued that the original position is a device of representation, not a claim about metaphysical selfhood

### 2.3 Non-Western Parallels
- Social contract-like reasoning exists outside the Western tradition:
  - The Mauryan political theorist **Kautilya** (*Arthashastra*, ca. 3rd century BCE): argued that kingship originated when people, suffering from "the law of the fishes" (the strong devouring the weak — analogous to Hobbes's state of nature), elected a ruler and agreed to pay taxes in exchange for protection
  - **Mozi** (Mo Tzu, 5th century BCE China): argued that government arose from the chaos of the state of nature and that the ruler's authority derives from the collective agreement of the people to follow a unified standard of right
  - These parallels suggest that contractualist reasoning may reflect a broader cross-cultural philosophical instinct, not merely a Western invention

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Social Contract and Artificial Intelligence
- Contemporary philosophers (Floridi 2013; Wallach & Allen 2009) have begun exploring whether social contract theory can be extended to human-AI relations:
  - If AI systems become sufficiently autonomous, should they be included as contracting parties? Under what conditions?
  - The question parallels historical expansions of the contract (to include women, non-white peoples, future generations)
  - No consensus exists; the debate is in early stages

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Historical Social Contract Events
- **[DEBUNKED]** Claims that actual historical societies were founded through explicit social contracts (as described by Locke or Rousseau) confuse philosophical thought experiments with historical descriptions — no society was literally founded by unanimous consent of all members; the social contract is a normative framework for evaluating political legitimacy, not a historical claim about origins
