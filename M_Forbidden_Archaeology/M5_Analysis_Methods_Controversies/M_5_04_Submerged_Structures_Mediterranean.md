## QUICK SUMMARY

The **Mediterranean Sea** contains some of the world's best-documented and most archaeologically significant **submerged settlements and structures** — sites that were built on dry land and subsequently inundated by combinations of post-glacial sea-level rise, tectonic subsidence, volcanic bradyseism (cyclical ground uplift and subsidence associated with volcanic activity), and local geological processes. Two sites exemplify the range: **Pavlopetri** (Laconia, southern Greece), submerged in approximately **3–4 meters** of water, is the **oldest known submerged city in the world**, with occupation spanning from the Late Neolithic through the Mycenaean period (c. **3500–1100 BCE**) — its street plan (at least 15 buildings, streets, and an open plaza) is visible on the seabed and has been mapped by sonar, photogrammetry, and diver survey; and **Baiae** (Bay of Naples, Italy), a luxurious Roman resort town that sank beneath the sea due to **volcanic bradyseism** associated with the Campi Flegrei caldera — its remains include marble-floored villas, mosaic floors, sculptures, thermal baths (natatio), and a nymphaeum (ornamental fountain), preserved in remarkable condition beneath 5–15 meters of water and now protected as an **underwater archaeological park** (Parco Archeologico Sommerso di Baia, established 2002). Between these two chronological extremes lie dozens of other submerged Mediterranean sites, including: **Dwejra** (Malta), **Apollonia** (Libya), **Epidaurus Limera** and **Elaphonissos** (Greece), **Caesarea Maritima** harbor (Israel, partially submerged due to subsidence), the submerged harbor of **Leptis Magna** (Libya), the Ptolemaic harbor of **Alexandria** including the site of the **Pharos Lighthouse** and the royal quarter (investigated by Franck Goddio since 1992), and **Heracleion/Thonis** (Abu Qir Bay, Egypt) — a major Egyptian-Greek port city that sank due to soil liquefaction, rediscovered by Goddio in 2000. These sites cumulatively demonstrate that significant portions of the ancient Mediterranean built environment are now underwater, and that underwater archaeology has become an essential discipline for reconstructing ancient coastal societies, trade networks, and engineering capabilities.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Pavlopetri — World's Oldest Submerged City
- Located off the southern coast of Laconia (Peloponnese, Greece), Pavlopetri lies in **3–4 meters of water**, approximately 1 m below the current sea floor in places — the site was first identified by **Nicholas Flemming** in 1967 and surveyed by the Cambridge/Nottingham team (Henderson et al. 2011–2012)
- **Chronology**: ceramic evidence spans from the Late Neolithic (c. 3500 BCE) through the Early, Middle, and Late Bronze Age (Mycenaean period) up to approximately **1100 BCE** — making it one of the longest-occupied coastal settlements in the Aegean
- The site covers approximately **9 hectares** of seabed: at least **15 buildings** with preserved wall foundations (stone socles originally supporting mudbrick upper walls), clearly defined streets, a probable central square, and shaft graves have been mapped
- **3D photogrammetric survey** (Henderson et al. 2013): created a complete digital model of the submerged architecture using thousands of photographs mosaicked together — this technique allowed recording at centimeter precision without disturbing the site
- The city's submergence was caused primarily by **tectonic subsidence** along the Hellenic Arc: the Laconian coast has subsided approximately 3–4 m since the Bronze Age — combined with post-glacial sea-level rise, this explains the site's current position

### 1.2 Baiae — Roman Luxury Submerged
- **Baiae** (modern Bacoli, Bay of Naples) was an elite Roman resort from the 2nd century BCE through the 4th century CE — frequented by Julius Caesar, Nero, Hadrian, Cicero, and other luminaries; Seneca called it "a resort of all the vices"
- The town sank beneath the sea due to **bradyseism** ("gradual ground movement") associated with the **Campi Flegrei** caldera system — the volcanic magma chamber beneath the region causes cyclical uplift and subsidence; the Baiae area experienced significant subsidence beginning in the late Roman period and continuing through the medieval period
- **Submerged remains** (5–15 m depth) include: marble-floored villas with mosaic pavements, thermal bath complexes (including the Nymphaeum of Emperor Claudius), sculptural groups found in situ, columns, and a road network — the cold, relatively calm waters of the bay have preserved organic and inorganic materials in good condition
- The **Parco Archeologico Sommerso di Baia** was established in 2002 — it is one of the few underwater archaeological parks in the world where the public can visit submerged ruins by glass-bottomed boat, snorkeling, or scuba diving
- The **Serapis Temple** (actually the macellum/market building) in nearby Pozzuoli contains columns with marine-bivalve borings at different heights — these **boring marks** provide a physical record of sea-level changes (and thus ground-level changes due to bradyseism) over the past 2,000 years and are a classic geological reference for understanding volcanic ground deformation

### 1.3 Heracleion/Thonis — Rediscovery of a Lost Port
- **Heracleion** (Egyptian name: Thonis), located in Abu Qir Bay ~6.5 km off the coast of Alexandria, Egypt, was a major port city from the 7th century BCE through the 8th century CE — mentioned in ancient Greek and Egyptian texts but its location was lost until its rediscovery by **Franck Goddio** and IEASM in 2000
- The city sank due to a combination of **soil liquefaction** (triggered by earthquakes in the geologically active Nile Delta), sediment compaction, and sea-level rise — geotechnical studies (Stanley et al. 2004) demonstrated that the waterlogged clay sediments of the delta are highly susceptible to liquefaction during seismic events
- Excavation has revealed: a **grand temple** of Amun-Gereb, a monumental **Ptolemaic-era canal**, over 64 ancient ships, 700 anchors, gold coins, bronze and stone statuary (including a 5.4-m granite statue of a pharaoh), and stelae with bilingual Egyptian-Greek inscriptions confirming the site's identification as Thonis-Heracleion

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Implications for Ancient Coastal Archaeology
- **Flemming (1998)**: estimated that hundreds of ancient coastal settlements across the Mediterranean are partially or fully submerged due to post-glacial sea-level rise (~120 m since the Last Glacial Maximum, ~2 m since 5000 BCE), tectonic activity, and volcanic subsidence — a significant portion of the ancient Mediterranean built environment exists underwater
- This has major implications for models of ancient trade, urbanization, and population: archaeological surveys that consider only terrestrial sites systematically undercount ancient settlements and misrepresent the density and distribution of coastal occupation
- Underwater survey technology (side-scan sonar, multibeam bathymetry, ROVs, AUVs, photogrammetry) has improved dramatically since the 2000s — enabling systematic survey of coastal zones rather than relying on chance discovery by divers

### 2.2 Alexandria's Royal Quarter and Pharos
- **Franck Goddio's** surveys of Alexandria's Eastern Harbor (since 1992) identified the submerged remains of the **Ptolemaic royal quarter** — including what may be the foundations of Cleopatra's palace complex and massive granite blocks from the **Pharos Lighthouse** (one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World, toppled by earthquakes in the 14th century CE)
- The identification of specific structures (e.g., distinguishing the Pharos foundations from those of the nearby Fort Qaitbay) remains debated — underwater conditions in Alexandria harbor (heavy sedimentation, modern construction, pollution) make definitive identification challenging
