## QUICK SUMMARY

The Saqqara Bird is a small carved sycamore-wood artifact (catalog #6347) housed in the Egyptian Museum, Cairo, dated to approximately 200 BCE (Ptolemaic period).
Discovered in 1898 during the excavation of the Pa-di-Imen tomb at Saqqara, the 14-cm-long object resembles a bird but possesses features — including a vertical tail fin and downward-angled wings — that physician and model aircraft enthusiast Khalil Messiha argued in 1969 represent a scale model of a monoplane glider.
Mainstream Egyptologists interpret the object as a ceremonial bird figurine, weathervane, or children's toy, noting that ancient Egyptians produced many stylized bird figurines and that the object lacks a horizontal tailplane necessary for stable flight.
Aerodynamic testing by multiple independent researchers has produced conflicting results, making the Saqqara Bird one of the most debated "out-of-place artifacts" in the forbidden archaeology literature.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Artifact existence and provenance

The artifact exists as a physical object in the Egyptian Museum, Cairo, registered under Special Register catalog number 6347.
It was discovered in 1898 during excavations at Saqqara, the vast necropolis of ancient Memphis, specifically from a tomb associated with Pa-di-Imen (Messiha, 1972; Czerny, 1997).
The artifact is carved from sycamore wood (*Ficus sycomorus*), a common material in ancient Egyptian craftsmanship.

### 1.2 Physical measurements

Length: approximately 14 cm (5.5 inches).
Wingspan: approximately 18 cm (7.1 inches).
Weight: approximately 39.12 grams.
The body is streamlined with a tapered nose and a rear section where the tail section attaches.
The wings are slightly downward-angled (negative dihedral) relative to the body axis (Messiha, 1972; Sanderson, 2006).

### 1.3 Dating context

The tomb contents and associated artifacts place the Saqqara Bird in the late Ptolemaic period, approximately 200 BCE, though some sources cite broader ranges of 200 BCE to the 3rd century BCE.
The artifact was stored among miscellaneous items and bird figurines in the Egyptian Museum for decades before attracting attention (Czerny, 1997).

### 1.4 Other ancient Egyptian bird figurines

The Egyptian Museum contains numerous wooden bird figures from various periods, many with dowel holes suggesting they were mounted on shrines, carried on poles, or used as weathervanes on boats.
Bird imagery, particularly the falcon (Horus), ibis (Thoth), and the *ba*-bird (soul bird), pervaded Egyptian religious art (Wilkinson, 1992).

## 2. CREDIBLE BUT DEBATED CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated)

### 2.1 Messiha's aerodynamic interpretation (1969–1972)

Egyptian physician Khalil Messiha, who was also an amateur model aircraft builder, examined the artifact and identified features he considered aerodynamically significant:
- The wings have an airfoil profile (curved upper surface, flat lower surface), generating lift.
- A vertical tail fin — unusual for bird figurines — would serve as a rudder.
- The body shape resembles a modern glider fuselage.
Messiha presented these claims at a 1969 symposium and published in 1972, leading the Egyptian Ministry of Culture to organize a special committee and brief exhibition at the Cairo Museum (Messiha, 1972).

### 2.2 Missing horizontal tailplane

The artifact as preserved lacks a horizontal tailplane (stabilizer), which is essential for stable longitudinal flight in conventional aircraft design.
Messiha hypothesized that a horizontal tailplane once existed but broke off, noting a slot or mark at the rear where one could have been attached.
Critics argue that no evidence of a separate tailplane has been found, and the slot may simply be damage or a mounting point for a stand (Czerny, 1997; Gregorie, 2002).

### 2.3 Simon Sanderson's Channel 4 experiments (2006)

Aeronautical engineer Simon Sanderson created a scale model five times larger than the original for a Channel 4 documentary.
Wind tunnel tests showed the shape did generate lift, but the model was unstable in pitch without the addition of a horizontal tailplane.
When Sanderson added a conjectural horizontal stabilizer, the model glided effectively at a ratio of approximately 4:1.
Critics noted that testing an enlarged replica with added components does not prove the original artifact had those features (Sanderson, 2006).

### 2.4 Martin Gregorie's replication tests (2002)

Model aircraft builder Martin Gregorie constructed an accurate balsa-wood replica matching the original's dimensions and mass distribution.
Gregorie found the replica would not glide in any configuration without adding a horizontal tailplane, concluding the artifact was not a functional glider design as found (Gregorie, 2002).

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Ancient Egyptian flight knowledge

Some alternative researchers argue the Saqqara Bird represents evidence that ancient Egyptians possessed aeronautic knowledge, possibly inherited from an earlier civilization or derived from systematic observation of birds.
This hypothesis has no supporting primary textual evidence from ancient Egypt — no hieroglyphic texts describe manned flight, flight theory, or glider construction (Childress, 1996).

### 3.2 Boomerang or throwing stick interpretation

Researchers have suggested the artifact might represent a throwing stick (similar to a boomerang), which the Egyptians used extensively for hunting birds in marshlands.
Ancient Egyptian tomb paintings (e.g., Nebamun) clearly depict throwing sticks in use, and the Saqqara Bird's wing profile could relate to this category of aerodynamic object.
However, the vertical tail fin and overall proportions do not match known Egyptian throwing sticks (Partridge, 1994).

### 3.3 Ceremonial or funerary purpose

The artifact may represent a *ba*-bird — an Egyptian soul-bird concept — placed in the tomb to symbolize the deceased's ability to fly in the afterlife.
Alternatively, it could be a votive offering to Horus or another avian deity.
The lack of painted features (eyes, feathers) on the surviving artifact makes symbolic interpretation difficult, though paint may have worn away over 2,200 years.

## 4. DUBIOUS OR FRINGE CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Full-scale ancient Egyptian aircraft

Claims that the Saqqara Bird proves ancient Egyptians built and operated full-scale aircraft or gliders lack any corroborating evidence — no full-scale craft, landing areas, flight manuals, or crash debris have ever been found.
This interpretation requires an extraordinary level of technological capability unsupported by the archaeological record (White, 2010).

### 4.2 Connection to Abydos "helicopter" hieroglyphs

Some fringe authors link the Saqqara Bird to the "helicopter" hieroglyphs at the Temple of Seti I in Abydos, claiming a pattern of flight technology evidence.
The Abydos glyphs are a well-documented example of palimpsest — where later carvings partially overlap earlier ones, creating accidental composite images — and do not depict helicopters or aircraft (Clagett, 1999).
