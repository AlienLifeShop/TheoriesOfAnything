## QUICK SUMMARY

Puma Punku (Pumapunku, "Door of the Puma") is a terraced platform mound at the pre-Columbian archaeological site of Tiwanaku in western Bolivia, approximately 72 km west of La Paz at 3,850 meters altitude.
The site is renowned for its precisely cut stone blocks — particularly the distinctive H-shaped blocks carved from red sandstone and larger andesite slabs — which demonstrate sophisticated engineering including interlocking joinery, I-shaped metal clamps, and modular design principles.
Dating to approximately 536–600 CE (based on radiocarbon dates from organic material at the site), Puma Punku represents some of the finest stone-cutting in the pre-Columbian Americas.
While alternative researchers frequently cite the site as evidence of lost advanced technology, academic archaeologists have documented plausible stone-working techniques using stone hammers, sand abrasion, and flat grinding surfaces, though debate continues regarding the full range of methods employed.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Site location and context

Puma Punku is located within the greater Tiwanaku archaeological complex in the Bolivian altiplano, approximately 1 km southwest of the Kalasasaya temple and Semi-Subterranean Court.
The site sits at approximately 3,850 meters (12,631 feet) elevation near the southern shore of Lake Titicaca.
Tiwanaku was the capital of a major pre-Columbian civilization (c. 300–1000 CE) that at its peak may have controlled a territory spanning parts of modern Bolivia, Peru, Chile, and Argentina (Kolata, 1993; Janusek, 2008).

### 1.2 Dating

Radiocarbon dates from organic material recovered from the lowest and deepest layers of the mound place initial construction of Puma Punku at approximately 536–600 CE.
The Tiwanaku civilization itself dates from approximately 300 to 1000 CE, with Puma Punku representing a later construction phase.
The site shows evidence of multiple construction phases and modifications over several centuries (Vranich, 2006).

### 1.3 The H-blocks

The distinctive H-shaped blocks are carved from red sandstone quarried from approximately 10 km away.
Each H-block measures approximately 2.5 m × 1.0 m × 0.6 m and is precisely cut with interlocking features — recessed channels, notches, and surface niches — designed to fit together in a modular fashion.
Over 100 H-block fragments have been cataloged at the site, though many are damaged from post-abandonment stone robbing and seismic activity (Protzen & Nair, 1997; Vranich, 2006).
After abandonment (~1000 CE), the site was extensively **looted for stone** — local communities, Inca builders, and Spanish colonial builders all recycled Pumapunku stone blocks, leaving the site in its current chaotic state.

### 1.4 Andesite megaliths

The largest stone blocks at Puma Punku are carved from andesite (a hard volcanic igneous rock) quarried from the Copacabana Peninsula on Lake Titicaca, approximately 90 km away.
The largest single andesite block weighs an estimated 131 metric tons (some sources estimate up to 144 tons).
Transport of these blocks across the altiplano and potentially across portions of the lake required substantial organized labor and engineering knowledge (Protzen & Nair, 1997).

### 1.5 I-shaped metal clamps

Tiwanaku builders used I-shaped clamps poured from molten metal (a copper-arsenic-nickel alloy or bronze) into carved recesses to lock adjacent stone blocks together.
Indentations for these clamps are visible on many blocks at both Puma Punku and the Akapana pyramid.
The clamps were poured in situ — liquid metal was cast directly into the stone channels — indicating on-site metallurgical capability at high altitude (Lechtman, 1996; Protzen & Nair, 1997).
Metallurgical analysis confirms the clamps are **copper-arsenic bronze** consistent with known Tiwanaku metallurgy; the clamps conform exactly to the irregular surfaces of the channels, confirming in-situ pouring (Lechtman and MacFarlane, 2005).
This pour-in-place clamping technique was used independently at multiple ancient sites worldwide — including ancient Greece, Rome, and India — suggesting convergent engineering solutions.

### 1.6 Measured precision

Protzen and Nair's systematic measurements (1997) documented:
- Flat surfaces ground to within 1–2 mm of true flatness over distances of several meters.
- Right angles accurate to within 1 degree.
- Matching cut depths on repeating elements (channels, niches) consistent to within several millimeters.
- Internal corners cut with crisp edges, not rounded as would be expected from purely abrasive methods.

### 1.7 Tool Marks and Manufacturing Evidence
- Detailed surface analysis of Pumapunku blocks reveals manufacturing signatures consistent with known pre-Columbian tools:
  - **Pecking marks**: consistent with stone hammers used for rough shaping
  - **Flat-ground surfaces**: consistent with grinding and polishing using flat stone tools and abrasive sand
  - **Internal right-angle channels**: produced by systematic grinding — no tool marks inconsistent with known pre-Columbian technology have been identified
- Protzen and Nair (2013) demonstrated experimentally that similar precision could be achieved using **flat stone plates** as reference surfaces combined with abrasive sand polishing

## 2. CREDIBLE BUT DEBATED CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated)

### 2.1 Construction techniques

Protzen and Nair demonstrated through experimental archaeology that ancient Tiwanaku stone workers could achieve much of the observed precision using:
- Hard hammerstone percussion for rough shaping (diorite or quartzite hammerstones found at the site).
- Flat stone grinding surfaces for achieving flatness.
- Sand abrasion for finishing and polishing.
Despite demonstrating plausibility, debate continues about whether these techniques alone can account for all observed features, particularly the crisp internal corners and the speed of production implied by the volume of cut stone (Protzen & Nair, 1997).

### 2.2 Modular design system

Vranich's detailed reconstruction studies (2006) demonstrated that the H-blocks and associated elements were part of a modular architectural system — prefabricated components designed to interlock like a three-dimensional puzzle.
This represents an unusual approach in pre-Columbian architecture, which normally relied on in-situ fitting.
The modular system implies advance planning, standardized measurements, and possibly a system of templates or measuring standards (Vranich, 2006).

### 2.3 Purpose and function

The original function of Puma Punku is debated:
- Kolata (1993): ceremonial platform and gateway complex for ritual processions.
- Vranich (2006): partially a port or dock facility related to a now-vanished extension of Lake Titicaca.
- Isbell (2004): elite administrative center.
The extensive destruction of the site by colonial-period stone robbing, seismic activity, and agricultural conversion has made definitive functional interpretation difficult.

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Unknown or lost stone-cutting technology

Researchers argue that the internal corners and precision of the H-blocks imply stone-cutting techniques not yet replicated through experimental archaeology — possibly involving metal tools that have not survived, or techniques no longer practiced.
No metal cutting tools have been recovered at Tiwanaku, and the Tiwanaku metallurgical tradition is primarily associated with bronze, gold, and silver ornamental work rather than heavy industrial tools (Lechtman, 1996).

### 3.2 Pre-Tiwanaku construction phase

Some alternative researchers and a few academics have proposed that Puma Punku may have an earlier construction phase predating the established radiocarbon dates, possibly extending into the first millennium BCE.
Current radiocarbon dating does not support this, but the dates are from organic material associated with the mound fill, not from the stone blocks themselves, leaving some interpretive latitude (Posnansky, 1945).

## 4. DUBIOUS OR FRINGE CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Machine-tool precision requiring modern technology

Claims that Puma Punku stones display "machine-tool precision" equivalent to modern CNC milling are exaggerated.
Protzen and Nair's actual measurements show impressive but achievable precision (1–2 mm tolerance) — comparable to other pre-industrial stone workmanship such as Inca polygonal masonry, Egyptian granite work, and medieval cathedral construction (Protzen & Nair, 1997).

### 4.2 Extraterrestrial or Atlantean construction

Claims that Puma Punku required extraterrestrial technology or was built by Atlantean survivors lack archaeological evidence.
The site fits within the well-documented trajectory of Tiwanaku civilization, which shows gradual development from early villages to monumental architecture over centuries (Janusek, 2008).

### 4.3 Extreme antiquity (15,000+ years)

Arthur Posnansky's early 20th-century astronomical dating of Tiwanaku to approximately 15,000 BCE has been decisively rejected by all modern dating methods.
Radiocarbon, ceramic seriation, and stratigraphic analysis consistently place the site within the first millennium CE (Kolata, 1993).
