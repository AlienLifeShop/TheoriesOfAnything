## QUICK SUMMARY

**Tiwanaku** (also spelled Tiahuanaco) — located at **3,850 meters** elevation on the Bolivian Altiplano, approximately 20 km southeast of Lake Titicaca — was the capital of one of the most significant pre-Columbian civilizations in South America. At its peak (c. **500–1000 CE**, the "Middle Horizon"), the Tiwanaku polity exerted cultural, economic, and possibly political influence across an estimated **600,000 km²**, encompassing portions of modern Bolivia, Peru, Chile, and Argentina. The city itself contained an estimated **10,000–40,000** residents (with a broader peri-urban and agricultural hinterland supporting additional populations) and featured monumental architecture that has generated both genuine archaeological admiration and alternative-history speculation. Key structures include: the **Akapana** — a partially artificial, cross-shaped stepped pyramid approximately 197 × 257 m at the base and originally ~17 m high, constructed from earth fill faced with precisely cut andesite and sandstone blocks, incorporating an elaborate internal drainage system that channeled rainwater through stone conduits from the summit through the structure's interior; **Kalasasaya** — a semi-subterranean ceremonial enclosure (~130 × 120 m) with standing stone pillars, containing the famous **Gateway of the Sun** (a single block of andesite, ~3 m high × 3.8 m wide, weighing ~10 tonnes, carved with the "Staff God" image often identified as Viracocha); **Pumapunku** (covered in M_2_05) — a T-shaped platform with the most precisely cut stone blocks at the site; and the **Semi-Subterranean Temple** (Templete Semisubterráneo) — a sunken courtyard with stone face sculptures embedded in its walls. Tiwanaku's engineering achievements include: long-distance stone transport (andesite from quarries ~90 km away at Copacabana Peninsula, transported across or around Lake Titicaca on reed boats or rafts — a remarkable logistical feat), precise stone cutting and fitting without mortar (using bronze and stone tools), and the development of **raised-field agriculture** (suka kollu) — elevated planting platforms surrounded by water channels that exploited thermal mass to protect crops from the Altiplano's severe frost, enabling the cultivation of potatoes, quinoa, and other crops at extreme altitude. The civilization's decline (c. 1000–1150 CE) coincided with a prolonged drought documented in ice-core and lake-sediment records — supporting an environmental-collapse hypothesis.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Chronology and Scope
- Tiwanaku's cultural sequence spans approximately **1500 BCE–1150 CE**: initial permanent settlement dates to the Late Formative (~1500–200 BCE); major monumental construction began in the **Early Intermediate/Urban Phase** (~300–500 CE); and the polity reached its greatest extent during the **Middle Horizon** (~500–1000 CE)
- At its height, Tiwanaku influence (identified through distinctive Tiwanaku ceramics, textiles, iconography, and architectural features) extended from southern Peru to northern Chile and northwestern Argentina — a territory comparable in scale to the later Inca Empire (though the nature of control — direct administration vs. cultural/economic influence — is debated)
- Population estimates for the city range from **10,000 to 40,000** (Kolata 1993), with Janusek (2008) favoring the lower range; the broader agricultural hinterland on the Altiplano supported additional tens of thousands
- **Radiocarbon dating**: over 100 dates from the site and surrounding settlements provide a secure chronological framework, though the timing of specific construction events within the monumental core remains debated

### 1.2 Monumental Architecture
- **Akapana**: the largest structure, a cross-shaped stepped pyramid with 7 terraces, originally rising ~17 m; constructed from a core of gravel and sand faced with precisely cut andesite blocks; the summit contained a sunken court with a sophisticated internal **drainage system** consisting of stone-lined channels that collected rainwater and channeled it through the structure's interior, exiting at the base — analysis by Kolata (1993) suggested this may have had ritual significance (water as a symbol of fertility)
- **Kalasasaya**: a raised platform enclosure (~130 × 120 m) with interior sunken areas, bounded by standing stone pillars (the tallest ~3.6 m); astronomical alignments with solstice sunrise/sunset positions have been proposed (Posnansky 1945) but remain debated — modern assessments suggest the alignments are approximate rather than precise
- **Gateway of the Sun**: carved from a single andesite block (~10 tonnes); the central figure (the "Staff God") holds staffs with condor heads and wears an elaborate headdress with solar rays and appendages terminating in puma and condor heads — this motif appears throughout Tiwanaku art and is interpreted as a major deity (often associated with Viracocha in later traditions)
- Stone-cutting techniques used **bronze chisels, stone hammers**, and **abrasive sand** — analysis of tool marks by Protzen & Nair (2013) demonstrated that the precision of Tiwanaku stonework (joins fitting within 1 mm) was achieved with these conventional tools

### 1.3 Raised-Field Agriculture (Suka Kollu)
- The Tiwanaku civilization developed an extensive system of **raised agricultural fields** on the Altiplano surrounding Lake Titicaca — elevated planting platforms (typically 5–15 m wide, 10–200 m long, raised 0.5–1.0 m above the surrounding surface) separated by water-filled channels
- The system exploited **thermal mass**: the water channels absorbed solar heat during the day and released it at night, reducing frost damage to crops by 2–4°C — crucial on the Altiplano, where frost is a constant risk even during the growing season
- The channels also provided irrigation, nutrient-rich sediment (from algae and aquatic organisms for fertilizer), and aquaculture (fish cultivation in channels) — a sophisticated integrated agricultural system
- **Kolata et al. (1996)**: experimental reconstruction of raised fields demonstrated **50–100% higher crop yields** compared to conventional Altiplano agriculture — these findings have informed modern development projects attempting to rehabilitate traditional raised-field systems for contemporary Andean communities

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Nature of Tiwanaku Political Organization
- Whether Tiwanaku was a **state** or a **complex chiefdom** is debated: Kolata argued for a state-level polity with centralized control over labor and territory; Janusek (2008) proposed a more decentralized model with autonomous corporate groups (ayllus) coordinated through ritual and economic ties rather than direct administration
- Tiwanaku's relationship with the contemporary **Wari** civilization (centered in Ayacucho, Peru) is also debated: the two polities shared some iconographic elements but appear to have been independent — whether they competed, cooperated, or simply coexisted remains unclear

### 2.2 Long-Distance Stone Transport
- Andesite blocks at Tiwanaku were quarried from sources on the **Copacabana Peninsula** (~90 km distant, across or around Lake Titicaca) — transport options include: reed boats (totora reed rafts documented ethnographically on Lake Titicaca can support multi-tonne loads), overland sledge transport, or a combination
- Sandstone blocks came from closer quarries (~10 km distant) at Cerro Kimsachata
- The logistical challenge of moving 10–130-tonne blocks (the largest stone at the site) at 3,850 m elevation, where human work capacity is reduced ~20% by hypoxia, represents a remarkable organizational achievement — though the techniques (ramps, levers, rollers, rafts) are within the capabilities of Bronze Age engineering

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Posnansky's Extreme Antiquity Claim
- **Arthur Posnansky** (1945, *Tihuanacu: The Cradle of American Man*): claimed that Tiwanaku was ~17,000 years old based on alleged astronomical alignments of the Kalasasaya — this date is contradicted by all radiocarbon evidence from the site and is not accepted by any modern archaeologist; Posnansky's astronomical calculations contained fundamental errors in methodology

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Non-Human or Lost-Civilization Builders
- **[UNSUPPORTED]** Claims that Tiwanaku was built by Atlanteans, extraterrestrials, or a pre-human advanced civilization are unsupported — the archaeological sequence shows continuous development from simple to complex architecture, tool marks are consistent with bronze and stone technology, and the cultural context (Andean agricultural societies with existing megalithic traditions) provides adequate explanation
