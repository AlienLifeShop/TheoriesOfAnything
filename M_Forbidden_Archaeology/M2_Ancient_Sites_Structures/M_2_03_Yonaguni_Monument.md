## QUICK SUMMARY

The Yonaguni Monument is a massive underwater rock formation located off the southern coast of Yonaguni Island, Japan's westernmost point in the Ryukyu archipelago.
Discovered in 1987 by dive instructor Kihachiro Aratake, the structure features step-like terraces, flat platforms, right-angled edges, and features interpreted as carved faces, pillars, and channels.
Marine geologist Masaaki Kimura (University of the Ryukyus) has championed the interpretation that it is a man-made monument submerged by post-glacial sea-level rise (~10,000+ BP), while geologist Robert Schoch (Boston University) argues it is a natural formation shaped by tectonic fracturing and erosion of well-bedded mudstone and sandstone, possibly with minor human modification.
The debate remains unresolved and exemplifies the broader tension between alternative archaeology and mainstream geology.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Physical existence and location

The Yonaguni Monument lies approximately 25 meters below sea level off the southern coast of Yonaguni Island (24°26′N, 123°01′E), the westernmost island in the Ryukyu chain, Okinawa Prefecture, Japan.
  The main structure measures approximately 50 m long × 20 m wide × 25 m tall.
  It sits on the continental shelf in an area of moderate tidal currents (Kimura, 2004).

### 1.2 Discovery (1987)

Dive tour operator Kihachiro Aratake discovered the formation in 1987 while searching for new hammerhead shark diving sites south of the island.
  His underwater photographs brought international attention to the structure and initiated the ongoing debate over its origin.
  The site has since become a popular diving destination (Kimura & Takaki, 2011).

### 1.3 Geological composition

The structure is composed of Lower Miocene Yaeyama Group sandstones and mudstones — well-bedded sedimentary rocks with pronounced jointing at regular intervals.
  These rock types are naturally predisposed to fracture into angular, step-like forms along bedding planes and orthogonal joint sets.
  The sedimentary layering creates the appearance of deliberate terracing (Schoch, 1999; Kimura, 2004).

### 1.4 Sea-level history

During the Last Glacial Maximum (~26,500–19,000 BP), global sea levels were approximately 120–130 m lower than present.
  The area around Yonaguni was dry land until approximately 10,000–8,000 BP, when post-glacial transgression submerged the current seafloor.
  If the structure were man-made, it would necessarily predate submersion (Lambeck et al., 2014).

### 1.5 Tectonic activity

The Ryukyu arc is seismically active, situated along the boundary between the Eurasian and Philippine Sea plates.
  Earthquakes, faulting, and tectonic uplift/subsidence have shaped the region's geology throughout the Quaternary.
  Active faulting could produce the sharp-edged fractures and displaced blocks observed at the site (Sibuet et al., 1998).

### 1.6 Natural analogues exist on land

Comparable angular, terraced rock formations produced by natural fracturing of well-bedded sandstone occur subaerially in the immediate region.
  These include the stepped rocky coastline of Yonaguni Island itself and formations on nearby Sanninudai.
  The terraced appearance is a product of differential erosion along bedding planes, not unique to the submarine structure (Schoch, 1999).

### 1.7 Submarine exploration documentation

Multiple diving expeditions by Japanese, American, and international teams have photographed and measured the structure from the 1990s to the present.
  Underwater video and photogrammetric surveys provide detailed surface documentation, though subsurface investigation remains limited.

### 1.8 Comparable natural formations worldwide

Geological formations with angular, stepped, "architectural" appearances occur naturally worldwide in well-jointed sedimentary rocks.
  Examples include the Giant's Causeway (Northern Ireland, basalt columns), the Bimini Road (Bahamas, beachrock slabs), and coastal rock platforms in Japan and Taiwan.
  These demonstrate that strongly geometric, "engineered-looking" rock formations are regular products of common geological processes.

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Kimura's man-made interpretation

Masaaki Kimura, professor of marine geology at the University of the Ryukyus, has argued since the 1990s that the structure shows evidence of deliberate carving:
  - Right-angled steps too regular for natural fracturing
  - A feature he interprets as a carved "face" with distinct features
  - Channel-like grooves that he identifies as drainage or ritual channels
  - What he describes as a "stadium" or amphitheater area
  - Apparent post holes and tool marks
  He dates potential human modification to ~10,000 BP based on sea-level chronology (Kimura, 2004; Kimura & Takaki, 2011).

### 2.2 Schoch's natural formation assessment

Robert Schoch, geologist at Boston University, conducted multiple dives at the site beginning in 1998.
  He concluded that all major features can be explained by natural geological processes:
  - Tectonic fracturing along orthogonal joint sets produces right-angle geometry
  - Differential erosion of bedding planes creates terracing
  - Biological erosion (boring organisms) shapes channels and holes
  He acknowledges the structure's visually striking appearance but attributes it to the unusual regularity of the local stratigraphy (Schoch, 1999; Schoch, 2003).

### 2.3 Possible human modification of natural features

A compromise position suggests that a natural rock formation may have been minimally modified by humans (quarrying, smoothing, carving) when it was above sea level.
  This interpretation is analogous to rock-cut monuments elsewhere (e.g., Petra, Sigiriya) that exploit natural geological features.
  The partially natural, partially modified scenario is difficult to test given the underwater context and absence of artifacts.

### 2.4 Jomon-period cultural context

If the structure were humanly modified, the relevant cultural context would be the Jomon period (~16,000–3,000 BP).
  Jomon culture produced the world's earliest pottery and sophisticated settlement patterns but no known monumental stone architecture in the Ryukyu Islands.
  The scale proposed for Yonaguni would be unprecedented for any known Jomon community and would predate Göbekli Tepe in monumental ambition (Pearson, 2013).

### 2.5 Absence of diagnostic artifacts

No stone tools, pottery, charcoal, or other unambiguous human artifacts have been recovered from the Yonaguni Monument or its immediate surroundings.
  The absence of artifact evidence weakens the case for human construction, though taphonomic processes (marine erosion, current action) could have removed or buried such evidence (Schoch, 2003).

### 2.6 Other submerged features in the area

Additional underwater formations have been reported near Yonaguni and other Ryukyu islands (Kerama, Aguni).
  Whether these represent a network of related structures or independent natural formations is unresolved.

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 "Turtle" and "face" features

Certain formations at the site have been interpreted as zoomorphic or anthropomorphic carvings — a "turtle" shape and a "face" with apparent eye sockets.
  These identifications rely heavily on pareidolia (human tendency to see meaningful patterns in random forms).
  No formal morphometric analysis comparing these features to known ancient carvings has been published.

### 3.2 Erosion rate calculations

Estimates of submarine erosion rates in the local sandstone/mudstone suggest that features as sharp as those observed at Yonaguni would become significantly rounded within 10,000–15,000 years of submersion.
  The relatively sharp edges observed may argue for either recent exposure by current scouring or a natural jointing origin that maintains angular geometry during erosion.

### 3.3 Connection to Ryukyu cosmology

Researchers have speculated that if the structure is man-made, it might connect to Ryukyuan religious traditions involving sacred stone formations (*utaki* worship sites).
  However, no Ryukyuan oral traditions specifically reference a submerged monument, and *utaki* traditions are documented only from later historical periods.

### 3.4 Pre-Jomon maritime civilization

Proposals that the monument represents evidence of a pre-Jomon maritime civilization challenge mainstream chronologies.
  The hypothesis requires that advanced stone-working existed in the western Pacific before ~10,000 BP — a claim without parallel evidence in the archaeological record.

### 3.5 Submerged road or causeway

Some divers have reported linear features on the seafloor extending from the main monument, interpreted as possible roadways or causeways.
  These have not been formally surveyed by geologists and may represent natural bedding-plane exposures or fault-aligned erosion channels.

### 3.6 Thermoluminescence dating potential

Ikeya (2004) proposed that thermoluminescence dating of the stone surface might distinguish between natural and human-modified surfaces.
  This approach has potential but has not been systematically applied to the monument.

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source)

### 4.1 Mu/Lemuria connection

The Yonaguni Monument has been claimed as a remnant of the mythical lost continent of Mu (Churchward, 1926) or Lemuria.
  These hypothetical continents are not supported by plate tectonics or any geological evidence.
  The Pacific Ocean floor is oceanic crust, not submerged continental land.

### 4.2 "Japanese Atlantis" as a global civilizational hub

Popular media descriptions of Yonaguni as "Japan's Atlantis" imply a sophisticated drowned civilization.
  No evidence supports large-scale urban settlement in the area at any period, and the Atlantis analogy is a journalistic invention.

### 4.3 Advanced machining evidence

Claims that the structure's angles and surfaces show evidence of power tools or advanced machining have no basis.
  The angles observed are consistent with natural joint-set geometry in sedimentary rocks, as demonstrated by surface analogues on Yonaguni Island itself.

### 4.4 Deliberate government cover-up

Assertions that the Japanese government suppresses research on Yonaguni to avoid rewriting history are contradicted by ongoing academic research, government cultural agency awareness, and the site's status as a popular tourist destination.
