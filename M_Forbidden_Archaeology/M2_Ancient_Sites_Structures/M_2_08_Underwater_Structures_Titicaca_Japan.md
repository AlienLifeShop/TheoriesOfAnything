## QUICK SUMMARY

Multiple significant underwater stone formations have been documented in two distant but thematically related regions: Lake Titicaca (Bolivia/Peru) and the waters surrounding the southern Japanese Ryukyu Islands.
In Lake Titicaca, systematic underwater archaeological surveys since 1966 — most notably by Belgian archaeologist Christophe Delaere (2013–present) and earlier by Johan Reinhard (1988) — have documented submerged Tiwanaku-era structures, offering vessels, and a stone terrace at depths of 5–12 meters near the Island of the Sun.
In Japan, beyond the well-known Yonaguni Monument (covered in M_2_03), additional submerged stepped-stone formations have been reported near the Kerama Islands and at Chatan on the Okinawan coast, ranging from probable natural formations to structures of ambiguous origin.
These sites collectively contribute to debates about post-glacial sea-level rise, lake-level fluctuation, and the possibility that coastal and lacustrine cultures built structures now submerged by changing water levels.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Lake Titicaca underwater offerings

The Khoa reef (Arrecife de Khoa), located between the Island of the Sun and Island of the Moon in Lake Titicaca, has yielded over 1,500 artifacts recovered through underwater excavation since the 1970s.
Artifacts include stone figurines, gold sheets, ceramic puma and llama figures, Spondylus shells (imported from the Pacific coast), and carved stone offering boxes (*tinka*).
These objects are associated with the Tiwanaku civilization (c. 500–1000 CE) and the Inca Empire (c. 1400–1533 CE) and represent deliberate ritual offerings deposited in the lake (Delaere & Capriles, 2020; Reinhard, 1992).

### 1.2 Delaere's systematic surveys (2013–present)

Belgian archaeologist Christophe Delaere conducted the first systematic photogrammetric underwater survey of the Khoa reef area using GPS-referenced dive grids.
His team documented:
- A stone terrace approximately 5 meters below the current lake surface.
- Ceramic fragments consistent with Tiwanaku Middle Horizon pottery.
- Organic remains (bone, shell) radiocarbon-dated to approximately 500–800 CE.
The findings were published in *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences* (PNAS) and represent the most rigorous underwater archaeological work at Titicaca to date (Delaere & Capriles, 2020).

### 1.3 Lake Titicaca level fluctuations

Lake Titicaca's water level has fluctuated significantly over the Holocene:
- During the Medieval Climate Anomaly (c. 1000–1300 CE), levels dropped as much as 5–12 meters below present.
- During the Little Ice Age (c. 1400–1850 CE), levels were generally higher.
- Paleoclimate records from sediment cores confirm multiple high-low cycles.
Structures now underwater could have been built during low-stand periods when they were above the waterline (Abbott et al., 1997; Baker et al., 2001).

### 1.4 Yonaguni Monument context (see M_2_03)

The Yonaguni underwater formation off the southernmost Ryukyu Island has been extensively documented since Kihachiro Aratake's 1987 discovery.
The main formation features step-like terraces, right-angle edges, and apparent channels. The debate between Masaaki Kimura (artificial construction) and Robert Schoch (natural sandstone fracturing) remains unresolved, with most geologists favoring natural origin (see M_2_03 for full coverage).

### 1.5 Kerama Island underwater formation

Circular stone arrangements have been reported on the seabed near the Kerama Islands (approximately 30 km west of Okinawa) at depths of 25–30 meters.
These formations consist of roughly circular arrangements of flat stones, described by divers but incompletely documented by professional archaeologists.
The formations' depth places them in the zone that would have been exposed during the Last Glacial Maximum (c. 20,000 years ago), when sea levels were approximately 120 meters lower (Kimura, 2004).

## 2. CREDIBLE BUT DEBATED CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated)

### 2.1 Tiwanaku-era stone terrace in Lake Titicaca

The stone terrace documented by Delaere at approximately 5 meters depth near the Island of the Sun shows cut stone features consistent with Tiwanaku architectural traditions.
The terrace could represent:
- A ritual platform built when lake levels were lower, subsequently inundated.
- A deliberately constructed underwater offering platform (built to be submerged).
- A natural rock shelf modified by human activity.
The association with datable ceramic and organic material supports Tiwanaku-era human activity, though the exact nature of the structure remains under investigation (Delaere & Capriles, 2020).

### 2.2 Chatan underwater formation (Okinawa)

Stepped and terraced rock formations have been reported in shallow waters off Chatan, Okinawa, including what appear to be carved steps and rectangular indentations.
Masaaki Kimura (University of the Ryukyus) has classified these as artificial structures, while other marine geologists note that Ryukyu limestone naturally forms stepped terraces through erosion along bedding planes and joint sets (Kimura, 2004; Schoch, 2007).

### 2.3 Deeper Titicaca structures

Reports of larger submerged structures at greater depths (20–30 meters) in Lake Titicaca have circulated since the 1960s, based on early sonar surveys and local oral traditions.
The Akakor Geographical Exploring expedition (2000) and earlier Cousteau dive operations (1966) reported stone walls and building foundations, but systematic documentation with modern standards remains incomplete (Reinhard, 1992).

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Lost lakeside city of Wanaku

Researchers have proposed that a major Tiwanaku settlement (sometimes called "Wanaku" or "Wanaca") extended into areas now under Lake Titicaca, based on the concentration of ritual offerings and stone features around the Island of the Sun.
While Tiwanaku was undeniably a major civilization with known cities (Tiwanaku itself, Lukurmata, Omo), the existence of a large submerged urban center remains unconfirmed by systematic excavation (Stanish, 2003).

### 3.2 Pre-Jōmon civilization in Ryukyu Islands

Kimura and some alternative researchers have proposed that the Ryukyu underwater formations represent evidence of a civilization predating the Jōmon period (before 14,000 BCE), destroyed by post-glacial sea-level rise.
No terrestrial archaeological evidence of such a civilization has been found in the Ryukyu Islands, and the Jōmon period itself — while producing some of the world's earliest pottery — did not include monumental stone architecture (Habu, 2004).

## 4. DUBIOUS OR FRINGE CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Titicaca as lost Atlantis location

Claims positioning Lake Titicaca as the site of Plato's Atlantis (based on altitude-adjusted readings of Plato's dimensions) lack any credible archaeological or textual support and require reinterpreting Plato's clearly Mediterranean/Atlantic geographical references.

### 4.2 Japanese formations as Mu/Lemuria

Identification of Japanese underwater formations as remnants of Mu or Lemuria — hypothetical lost continents proposed by 19th-century writers — has no geological or archaeological basis. Plate tectonics and ocean-floor drilling have conclusively ruled out the existence of sunken continental landmasses in the Pacific (Nunn, 2009).
