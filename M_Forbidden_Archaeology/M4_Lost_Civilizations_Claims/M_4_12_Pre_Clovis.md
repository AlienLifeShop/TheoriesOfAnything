## QUICK SUMMARY

For most of the 20th century, the "**Clovis First**" paradigm held that the first humans to enter the Americas were the bearers of the **Clovis culture** — characterized by distinctive fluted stone points — who arrived via the ice-free corridor between the Laurentide and Cordilleran ice sheets approximately **13,000-13,500 years ago**. Any site claiming earlier human presence was dismissed, often harshly, by the archaeological establishment. The collapse of the Clovis First model, one of the most significant paradigm shifts in American archaeology, occurred through the gradual accumulation of rigorously documented **pre-Clovis sites** that demonstrated human presence in the Americas before the Clovis horizon. The most important sites include: (1) **Monte Verde, Chile** (~14,500 cal BP) — accepted by a blue-ribbon panel of archaeologists in 1997 as the first definitively pre-Clovis site; (2) **Meadowcroft Rockshelter, Pennsylvania** (~16,000-19,000 cal BP) — excavated by James Adovasio from the 1970s, long contested but increasingly accepted; (3) **Paisley Caves, Oregon** (~14,300 cal BP) — with coprolites (fossilized feces) containing human DNA; (4) **Buttermilk Creek, Texas** (~15,500 cal BP) — with extensive lithic assemblages; (5) **White Sands, New Mexico** (~21,000-23,000 cal BP) — human footprints in now-hardened lakebed sediments, reported in 2021, the most controversial and potentially most significant; and (6) **Cerutti Mastodon, California** (~130,000 years BP) — fractured mastodon bones and cobbles claimed as evidence of human activity, extremely controversial and widely challenged. The compilation of evidence demonstrates that humans entered the Americas well before 13,000 years ago, probably by 15,000-16,000 years ago via a **Pacific coastal route**, with some evidence (White Sands, if confirmed) pushing arrival back to ~23,000 years ago during the Last Glacial Maximum.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 Monte Verde, Chile (~14,500 cal BP)
- **Dillehay, Tom D. (1989, 1997, *Monte Verde: A Late Pleistocene Settlement in Chile*)**: systematic excavation of a well-preserved open-air site in southern Chile:
  - **Artifacts**: butchered mastodon bone, wooden tools, cordage, seaweed, wild potato residues, stone tools of non-Clovis type
  - **Preservation**: waterlogged conditions preserved organic materials including wooden tent foundations, knotted cordage, and medicinal plant remains — unprecedented in Pleistocene-age American sites
  - **Dating**: multiple radiocarbon dates clustering at ~14,500 cal BP (~12,500 ¹⁴C years BP)
  - **Validation**: in January 1997, a panel of distinguished archaeologists (including former Clovis-First proponents) visited the site and unanimously accepted it as a **legitimate pre-Clovis occupation**. This was the decisive event in breaking the Clovis First paradigm

### 1.2 Paisley Caves, Oregon (~14,300 cal BP)
- **Jenkins, Dennis L., et al. (2012, *Science*)**: human coprolites (fossilized feces) from the Paisley Five Mile Point Caves:
  - Coprolites contain **human mitochondrial DNA** (haplogroups A2, B2) — unambiguously human
  - Radiocarbon dated to **~14,300 cal BP** — pre-Clovis
  - Associated with Western Stemmed Tradition (WST) stone points, which are technologically different from Clovis — indicating that Clovis was not the only (and not the earliest) lithic tradition in the Americas
  - The DNA evidence makes the human association essentially irrefutable

### 1.3 White Sands, New Mexico (~21,000-23,000 cal BP)
- **Bennett, Matthew R., et al. (2021, *Science*)**: human **footprints** preserved in the dried surface of ancient Lake Otero:
  - Multiple trackways of adults and children, clearly human based on foot morphology
  - Dated by radiocarbon analysis of **aquatic plant seeds** (*Ruppia cirrhosa*) in sediment layers above and below the footprint horizons: ~21,000-23,000 cal BP — **during the Last Glacial Maximum (LGM)**
  - **Controversy**: the dating relies on aquatic plant seeds, which can incorporate "old carbon" from dissolved carbonates in lake water, producing artificially old radiocarbon dates (the "freshwater reservoir effect"):
    - **Pigati et al. (2023, *Science*)**: responded with additional dating using **radiocarbon on terrestrial pollen** (not subject to freshwater reservoir effect) — confirming the ~21,000-23,000 cal BP age range
    - **OSL (optically stimulated luminescence)** dating of quartz grains in the footprint sediments also supports an LGM-age date
  - If confirmed, White Sands represents the **earliest direct evidence of humans in the Americas** — thousands of years earlier than most pre-Clovis sites

### 1.4 Buttermilk Creek, Texas (~15,500 cal BP)
- **Waters, Michael R., et al. (2011, *Science*)**: stratigraphically sealed stone tool assemblage at the Debra L. Friedkin site:
  - Over **15,000 artifacts** recovered from levels below Clovis-age deposits
  - OSL dating of the pre-Clovis level: ~15,500 cal BP
  - The lithic assemblage is technologically distinct from Clovis — suggesting that Clovis **developed from** an earlier, pre-existing lithic tradition in North America

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Meadowcroft Rockshelter, Pennsylvania (~16,000-19,000 cal BP)
- **Adovasio, J.M. (1977-present)**: excavated from 1973 onward, this rockshelter produced ~20,000 artifacts from deeply stratified deposits:
  - Lowest cultural levels (Stratum IIA) contain small blade tools and fibers, radiocarbon-dated to **~16,000-19,000 cal BP**
  - **Controversy**: critics (particularly Dincauze and Haynes) argued that the radiocarbon dates may be contaminated by **old carbon** from coal deposits in the area — potentially making the lowest levels appear older than they actually are
  - **Response**: Adovasio and colleagues have presented multiple lines of evidence against contamination, and the intermediate-aged levels (~14,000-15,000 cal BP) are now generally accepted
  - Meadowcroft is increasingly accepted as a legitimate pre-Clovis site, particularly in light of other confirmed pre-Clovis evidence

### 2.2 Coastal Migration Route
- The accumulation of pre-Clovis evidence supports a **Pacific coastal migration** route rather than the ice-free corridor:
  - Monte Verde (Chile, ~14,500 cal BP) and multiple coastal sites in western South and North America suggest that the earliest migrants followed the Pacific coast southward, using boats or coastal walking along now-submerged shorelines
  - The ice-free corridor between the Laurentide and Cordilleran ice sheets was not **biologically viable** (able to support human travel with sufficient food resources) until ~14,000-13,500 cal BP — too late for the earliest pre-Clovis sites

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Cerutti Mastodon, California (~130,000 years BP)
- **Holen, Steven R., et al. (2017, *Nature*)**: reported fractured mastodon bones and rounded cobble stones from a San Diego County site dated to **~130,000 years ago** by uranium-thorium dating:
  - The bone fracture patterns and cobble stone positions were interpreted as evidence of human activity — bones broken with hammerstones to extract marrow
  - **Criticism** (strong): most archaeologists reject this interpretation because: (1) no stone tools, cut marks, or other unambiguous cultural indicators were found; (2) bone breakage could result from natural processes (heavy machinery during suburban construction, geological compression); (3) the site was excavated during a highway project in the 1990s, not under ideal conditions; (4) a 130,000-year-old human presence in the Americas would require either an unknown hominin species or an unrecorded early migration — with no supporting evidence from genetics, other archaeological sites, or paleoanthropology
  - The claim remains highly controversial and is not accepted by the majority of archaeologists
