## QUICK SUMMARY

The **Voynich Manuscript** (Beinecke Rare Book and Manuscript Library, Yale University, catalog number **MS 408**) is a hand-written, lavishly illustrated codex of approximately **240 vellum pages** (c. 234 surviving, some missing) written in an **unknown script** that has never been deciphered despite over a century of effort by professional and amateur cryptanalysts, linguists, and historians. The manuscript was purchased by the Polish-American book dealer **Wilfrid Voynich** in 1912 from the Jesuit college at Villa Mondragone near Rome (the Jesuits had acquired it through Athanasius Kircher's correspondence network in the 17th century). **Radiocarbon dating** of the vellum (performed at the University of Arizona in 2009) placed the parchment's origin at **1404–1438 CE** (95% confidence), firmly in the early 15th century — an important constraint that eliminates attributions to Roger Bacon (13th century) and other earlier figures. The manuscript is divided into several sections based on illustration content: **botanical** (drawings of unidentified plants, ~113 species depicted), **astronomical/astrological** (circular diagrams with stars, zodiac symbols, and apparent celestial imagery), **biological/balneological** (drawings of small nude female figures in interconnected pools or tubes, some appearing to depict bathing or circulatory systems), **pharmaceutical** (drawings of plant parts alongside jars or vessels, possibly indicating herbal recipes), and **recipe/text** (pages of text without illustrations). The script consists of approximately **20–30 distinct glyphs** (depending on classification), written left-to-right with word spaces, and exhibits statistical properties consistent with natural language (Zipf's law conformance, consistent entropy profiles, word-length distributions) — this is the strongest evidence against the hoax hypothesis, as a medieval faker would have been unlikely to produce text with these statistical properties deliberately. Despite hundreds of claimed "decipherments," **none has been accepted** by the scholarly community: the manuscript remains the most famous unsolved puzzle in the history of cryptography.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Radiometric Data)

### 1.1 Physical Description and Provenance
- The manuscript is written on **calfskin vellum**, bound as a codex of approximately **23.5 × 16.2 cm** — some pages are foldout sheets up to six panels wide
- **Radiocarbon dating** (University of Arizona, 2009): four samples from different sections gave consistent dates of **1404–1438 CE** (95% confidence) — the ink has not been directly dated, but chemical analysis by McCrone Associates found iron-gall ink and mineral pigments (including blue azurite, green copper-rosinate, red mercuric sulfide) consistent with 15th-century European manuscript production
- Documented provenance: the earliest identified owner is **Georg Baresch** (Prague alchemist, early 17th century), who sent pages to Athanasius Kircher in Rome (1637 letter); the manuscript was later owned by Jan Marek Marci (rector of Charles University, Prague), who sent it to Kircher in 1665/66; it entered the Jesuit collection at Villa Mondragone; purchased by Voynich in 1912; donated to Yale's Beinecke Library in 1969
- The manuscript is freely available as a **digital facsimile** on the Beinecke Library website

### 1.2 Script and Statistical Properties
- The script uses approximately **20–30 distinct characters** (the exact number depends on whether certain forms are treated as variants of the same glyph or distinct glyphs) — common characters include "gallows" letters (tall, ornate glyphs), looped characters, and ligature-like combinations
- **Zipf's law**: the frequency distribution of "words" (delimited by spaces) follows Zipf's law (frequency ∝ 1/rank), a universal property of natural languages — this was noted by William Friedman's team in the 1940s–1950s
- **Entropy analysis** (Landini 2001; Amancio et al. 2013): the text has first-order and second-order entropy values consistent with natural languages (lower than random text, higher than simple substitution ciphers), and exhibits **long-range correlations** similar to natural language
- **Word-length distribution**: words in the Voynich text show a distinctive pattern with strong preferences for certain lengths (~5–8 characters), lower variance than typical European languages, and unusually high repetition rates within paragraphs
- These statistical properties are the strongest evidence that the text is **not random noise and not a simple hoax** — however, Gordon Rugg (2004) demonstrated that text with Zipf-like properties could be generated using a 16th-century cryptographic tool (a Cardan grille over a table of syllables)

### 1.3 Illustration Sections
- **Botanical section** (~113 plant drawings): most plants are unidentifiable with known species — some appear to be composites or imaginary; a few have been tentatively identified (e.g., sunflower, pepper) but these identifications are debated and some may represent Old World species
- **Astronomical/astrological section**: circular diagrams include recognizable zodiac symbols (Pisces, Aries, etc.) and concentric rings with stars — some diagrams resemble medieval astronomical/astrological charts
- **Biological section**: nude female figures (small scale) are depicted in interconnected pools or vessel-like structures connected by pipes — interpretations range from depictions of bathing (balneology, consistent with 15th-century spa culture) to highly stylized anatomical or alchemical diagrams

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Hypotheses on the Nature of the Text
- **Unknown natural language hypothesis**: the text may represent a real (possibly extinct or minority) language written in a unique script — candidates proposed include early Romance, early Turkish, Nahuatl, and others; none has yielded a convincing reading
- **Constructed language hypothesis** (Stolfi 1999): the text may represent an artificial/constructed language — the highly regular word structure (limited combinations of prefixes, roots, and suffixes) could reflect a systematic notation system (for medicine, alchemy, or botanical classification) rather than a natural language
- **Sophisticated cipher hypothesis**: the text may be a cipher of a known language (Latin, German, Italian) using a complex encryption method — however, standard cryptanalysis techniques (frequency analysis, known-plaintext attacks, Markov chain analysis) have failed to break it; if it is a cipher, the method is extremely effective for its era
- **Hoax hypothesis**: the manuscript may be a deliberate fabrication with no meaningful content — Rugg (2004) showed that Cardan grille methods could generate text with some Voynich-like properties; however, the cost of the vellum, quality of the illustration work, and statistical depth of the text make a pure hoax less likely

### 2.2 Origin and Authorship
- No author has been convincingly identified — Wilfrid Voynich initially attributed it to **Roger Bacon** (13th century), but radiocarbon dating eliminates this
- **Proposed candidates** include: Jacobus Sinapius (visible in an erased ownership signature under UV light), various 15th-century Italian or Central European scholars, and even Voynich himself (as a forger) — the forgery hypothesis is undermined by the radiocarbon date of the vellum (1404–1438, long before Voynich)

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Possible Geographic Origin
- Recent work (Tucker & Talbert 2013) proposed that some botanical illustrations match **New World plants** and that the manuscript may originate from colonial Mexico — this hypothesis is highly contested and requires the problematic assumption that the manuscript was written after 1492 despite the vellum dating to 1404–1438 (the vellum could theoretically have been stored unused for decades before use, but this adds a significant assumption)
