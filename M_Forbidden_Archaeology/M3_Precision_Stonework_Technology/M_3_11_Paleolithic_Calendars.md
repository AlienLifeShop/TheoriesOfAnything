## QUICK SUMMARY

In 1972, science journalist **Alexander Marshack** published *The Roots of Civilization*, arguing that series of marks engraved on Upper Paleolithic bone and antler artifacts — previously dismissed as random decorations or simple tallies — were in fact **lunar notation systems**: systematic records of the phases of the moon created by Ice Age humans as early as ~30,000-35,000 years ago. Marshack's primary evidence was the **Abri Blanchard bone plaque** (~32,000 years BP, Dordogne, France), a serpentine arrangement of 69 marks that he argued corresponded to approximately 2.25 lunar months, with changes in mark shape and depth tracking the waxing–waning–new moon cycle. He subsequently analyzed dozens of other Upper Paleolithic artifacts — including the **Ishango bone** (~20,000 years BP, Democratic Republic of Congo) — and found similar patterns of sequential marks that he interpreted as lunar or seasonal records. Marshack's hypothesis was revolutionary because it implied that Paleolithic humans possessed **time-factored cognitive abilities** — the capacity for systematic observation, recording, and tracking of natural cycles — far earlier than previously assumed. The hypothesis has been both influential and controversial. **Francesco d'Errico** (CNRS) challenged Marshack's methodology in the 1990s-2000s, using microscopic analysis to show that many marks on the same artifact were made with different tools at different times — consistent with accumulated tallying but not necessarily lunar tracking. **Michael Rappenglueck** supported the astronomical interpretation of some artifacts, including potential star-map interpretations of Lascaux cave paintings. The debate remains active: the strongest version of Marshack's claim (systematic lunar calendars) is considered unproven, while the weaker version (Paleolithic notational capacity) is now widely accepted.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 Upper Paleolithic Engraved Artifacts
- Hundreds of Upper Paleolithic bone, antler, and stone artifacts from Europe and Africa bear **sequences of incised marks** — lines, dots, notches, cupules:
  - **Abri Blanchard bone plaque** (~32,000 years BP, Aurignacian, Dordogne, France): a flat bone with a serpentine arrangement of 69 marks of varying shapes and depths
  - **Ishango bone** (~20,000 years BP, Lake Edward, DRC): a baboon fibula with three columns of grouped notches — groups include 11, 13, 17, 19 (primes) and various arrangements totaling 48 and 60
  - **La Marche antler** (~14,000 years BP, Vienne, France): antler with sequential marks
  - **Tai plaque** (~10,000 years BP, Drôme, France): bone plaque with grouped incisions
- These artifacts are real, well-provenance, and undisputed — the debate concerns their interpretation

### 1.2 Marks Were Deliberate and Sequential
- Microscopic analysis (both by Marshack and by his critics, especially d'Errico) confirms:
  - Marks were made **deliberately**, not accidentally — they are too regular and positioned to be incidental
  - On many artifacts, marks were made by **different tools at different times** — the microscopic trace evidence (incision width, depth, cross-section, direction) shows that marks were added to artifacts over **extended periods**, not all at once in a single decorative session
  - This temporal accumulation is consistent with a **recording or tallying function** — the artifacts were used to track something over time

### 1.3 Paleolithic Cognitive Capacity
- The existence of notational systems of any type in the Upper Paleolithic is now broadly accepted:
  - **D'Errico (1998, *Cambridge Archaeological Journal*)**: even while critiquing Marshack's specific lunar interpretation, d'Errico confirmed that the marks represent deliberate sequential recording — implying Paleolithic capacity for **external symbolic storage of information**
  - The capacity for notation, record-keeping, and sequential thinking is now considered part of the behavioral repertoire of anatomically modern humans from at least ~40,000 years ago

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Marshack's Lunar Hypothesis
- **Marshack, Alexander (1972, *The Roots of Civilization*)**: proposed that mark sequences on Paleolithic artifacts track **lunar phases**:
  - On the Abri Blanchard bone, the 69 marks correspond to ~2.25 synodic months (29.5 days × 2.25 ≈ 66.4 days) — close to the number of marks if one assumes some counting irregularity
  - Marshack claimed that changes in mark morphology (deeper/shallower, larger/smaller, differently shaped) corresponded to changes in the visible moon — full marks for full moon, smaller marks for crescent, gaps for new moon
  - He found similar patterns across multiple artifacts from different sites and periods
  - **Support**: the hypothesis is internally consistent — multiple artifacts show mark sequences divisible by ~29-30, and the multi-tool/multi-session accumulation is consistent with daily or periodic recording
  - **Weakness**: without an independent verification of what the marks represent, the correlation with lunar cycles could be coincidental — any sequence of ~30 marks will approximately match a lunar month by chance

### 2.2 D'Errico's Critique
- **D'Errico, Francesco (1989, 1998, 2001)**: performed rigorous microscopic reanalysis of several artifacts Marshack cited:
  - Confirmed sequential mark accumulation (multiple tools, multiple sessions)
  - Could **not** confirm that mark morphology systematically varied with lunar position — the variation in mark shape is better explained by changes in tool, hand position, and bone surface texture
  - Argued that the marks are consistent with **generic tallying** (counting animals, days, events) without necessarily being lunar calendars
  - Did not deny that lunar tracking was *possible* — only that it has not been *demonstrated* beyond reasonable doubt

### 2.3 Astronomical Interpretations of Cave Art
- **Rappenglueck, Michael (1999, 2004)**: proposed astronomical interpretations of specific cave paintings:
  - The dot pattern above the bull in the **Hall of the Bulls at Lascaux** (~17,000 years BP) may represent the **Pleiades** star cluster — the dot arrangement matches the cluster's visible configuration
  - A **pregnant horse figure** at Lascaux may represent a specific lunar or seasonal phase of the calendar
  - These interpretations are suggestive but not provable — cave art interpretation is inherently speculative

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Systematic Pre-Neolithic Calendars
- Whether Paleolithic notation systems reached the level of **systematic calendars** — tracking multiple cycles (lunar months, solar year, seasons) in an integrated system — remains unproven:
  - No artifact has been convincingly shown to track more than a few months' worth of events
  - True calendrical systems (coordinating lunar months with solar years) require sophisticated intercalation — not documented before the Neolithic/Bronze Age
  - Paleolithic people may have been capable of informal lunar tracking without formal calendrical systems

### 3.2 The Ishango Bone as Mathematical Knowledge
- Some interpretations of the Ishango bone suggest knowledge of **prime numbers** or **arithmetic operations** — the groupings (11, 13, 17, 19 on one column; numbers summing to 48 and 60 on others) are intriguing but may be coincidental given the small sample size

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Paleolithic People Had Advanced Mathematical Systems
- **[UNSUBSTANTIATED]** While notational capacity is confirmed, extrapolating from tally marks to advanced mathematics is unsupported by evidence. The marks are consistent with simple counting and tracking, not formal mathematical knowledge

### 4.2 All Paleolithic Mark Sequences Are Calendars
- **[OVERGENERALIZED]** Many bone markings may have purely decorative, structural (grip enhancement), or non-calendrical tallying functions. Applying a calendrical interpretation to all mark sequences is methodologically unsound
