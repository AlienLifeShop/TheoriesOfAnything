## QUICK SUMMARY

**Cultural evolutionary theory** — developed primarily by **Robert Boyd**, **Peter Richerson**, and **Joseph Henrich** — provides a rigorous, formally modeled framework for understanding how **cultural traits** (beliefs, practices, technologies, norms, institutions) are **transmitted, selected, and accumulated** across human populations. Rooted in the insight that culture constitutes a **second inheritance system** — operating alongside (and interacting with) genetic inheritance — this approach treats cultural variants as units that can be transmitted between individuals through **social learning** (imitation, teaching, language), subjected to selective forces (differential adoption, retention, or abandonment based on payoffs or biases), and accumulated over generations to produce **cumulative cultural evolution** — the ratchet-like buildup of complexity that distinguishes human from non-human culture. Boyd and Richerson's **dual inheritance theory** (*Culture and the Evolutionary Process*, 1985; *Not by Genes Alone*, 2005) formalized cultural transmission using population-genetics-style mathematical models — demonstrating how different **transmission biases** (conformist bias: copy the majority; prestige bias: copy successful or high-status individuals; content bias: prefer intrinsically attractive ideas; frequency-dependent bias) shape cultural dynamics in ways that are not reducible to individual rationality or genetic fitness. Henrich extended this framework to explain the evolution of **large-scale cooperation**, institutions, religions, and normative systems (*The Secret of Our Success*, 2016; *The WEIRDest People in the World*, 2020) — arguing that cumulative cultural evolution, not individual intelligence, is the primary engine of human adaptive success. Cultural evolutionary theory has been applied to understanding innovation and technological change, the spread of agriculture, the evolution of languages, the adoption of social norms and moral systems, the dynamics of religious movements, and the persistence of maladaptive cultural traits — offering a scientific framework that bridges evolutionary biology, anthropology, psychology, and archaeology.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Dual Inheritance Theory
- **Boyd and Richerson** (1985, 2005) formalized the idea that humans inherit information through **two channels**:
  1. **Genetic inheritance**: DNA transmitted from parents to offspring — slow, high-fidelity, biologically constrained
  2. **Cultural inheritance**: socially learned information transmitted between individuals (not necessarily parent to offspring) — fast, flexible, potentially one-to-many (broadcasting), and capable of cumulative increase in complexity
- The interaction between these systems — **gene-culture coevolution** — produces dynamics impossible to explain by either system alone:
  - **Lactase persistence**: the culturally transmitted practice of dairying created a selective environment favoring the genetic mutation for adult lactose digestion (Richerson et al. 2010)
  - **Sickle cell and agriculture**: forest-clearing agricultural practices in West Africa increased malaria exposure — creating the selective environment for the sickle cell trait
  - These examples demonstrate that **cultural practices create selective environments that shape genetic evolution** — and vice versa

### 1.2 Transmission Biases
- Cultural traits are not transmitted randomly — Boyd and Richerson identified key **biases** shaping who copies whom and what:
  - **Conformist bias (frequency-dependent)**: disproportionately adopt the most common variant in the population — produces cultural conservatism, regional uniformity, and boundary maintenance between groups ("when in Rome...")
  - **Prestige bias (model-based)**: preferentially copy individuals perceived as successful, skilled, or prestigious — can spread both adaptive and maladaptive traits (e.g., harmful status-signaling behaviors)
  - **Content bias (direct)**: prefer cultural variants that are intrinsically easier to learn, remember, or find appealing — partially explains cross-cultural universals in narrative structure, aesthetic preferences, and religious concepts
  - **Success bias**: copy individuals with demonstrably better outcomes — adaptive but cognitively demanding (requires evaluating payoffs)
  - These biases have been documented experimentally (Mesoudi 2009; Henrich and Gil-White 2001) and modeled mathematically — demonstrating their capacity to drive complex cultural dynamics

### 1.3 Cumulative Cultural Evolution
- **Cumulative culture** — the progressive, ratchet-like accumulation of modifications and improvements to cultural traits across generations — is a defining feature of human cognition:
  - No single individual could independently invent a kayak, a bow and arrow, a computer, or a legal system — these technologies emerge from the aggregation of small innovations over many generations
  - **Henrich (2016)**: argued that individual human intelligence is insufficient to explain human adaptive success — it is the **collective brain** (the population of interconnected, socially learning individuals) that drives cumulative cultural evolution
  - Experimental evidence: Caldwell and Millen (2008) demonstrated cumulative improvement in experimental tasks (paper airplane building, spaghetti tower construction) through chain transmission — each generation improving on the previous, exceeding individual innovation capacity

### 1.4 Mathematical Models
- Boyd and Richerson (1985) adapted **population genetics modeling** to cultural transmission:
  - Models track the **frequency** of cultural variants in a population over time — incorporating transmission biases, innovation rates, migration, and selection
  - Key results include: conformist transmission can maintain between-group variation even with moderate migration; prestige bias can spread traits faster than individual learning; small-group dynamics differ fundamentally from large-group dynamics
  - These models generate testable predictions about cultural diversity patterns, innovation rates, and the conditions favoring social over individual learning

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Cultural Group Selection
- Boyd, Richerson, and Henrich have argued for **cultural group selection** — the idea that groups with cultural traits that enhance cooperation and coordination outcompete groups with less cooperative norms:
  - Cultural traits (norms, institutions, punitive systems) can maintain between-group variation and within-group cooperation — creating the conditions for selection to operate at the group level
  - This is distinct from (and less controversial than) genetic group selection — because cultural transmission can maintain group-level variation more effectively than genetic transmission
  - The role of intergroup conflict, differential group extinction, and differential emigration in driving cultural evolution is supported by historical and ethnographic evidence but debated in its relative importance

### 2.2 WEIRD Psychology
- **Henrich, Heine, and Norenzayan (2010, *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*)** and Henrich (*The WEIRDest People in the World*, 2020) argued that Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, and Democratic (WEIRD) populations are **psychological outliers** — not the human default:
  - WEIRD populations exhibit unusually high individualism, analytic thinking, guilt-based moral reasoning, impersonal cooperation, and reduced kin-oriented social organization
  - These psychological patterns are argued to be products of **specific cultural evolutionary trajectories** — particularly the Western Church's medieval marriage regulations that dissolved extended kinship networks
  - The WEIRD thesis has been influential but debated — particularly regarding its causal mechanisms and the degree to which it characterizes non-WEIRD variation

### 2.3 Archaeological Applications
- Cultural evolutionary theory has been applied to archaeological problems:
  - **Innovation and technological change**: modeling rates of innovation as a function of population size and connectivity (Henrich 2004 — small, isolated populations lose complex technologies; larger, more connected populations innovate faster)
  - **Spread of agriculture**: modeling the Neolithic transition as cultural transmission (demic diffusion + cultural adoption) — integrating with archaeogenetic evidence
  - **Ceramic and lithic tradition evolution**: modeling the evolution of artifact styles as cultural lineages subject to transmission biases

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Quantifying Cultural Selection Pressures
- Developing methods to measure cultural selection pressures in the archaeological record — analogous to measuring natural selection in population genetics — is an active research goal but faces challenges in defining cultural "fitness," identifying transmission mechanisms, and controlling for drift and innovation

### 3.2 Cultural Phylogenetics
- Applying phylogenetic tree-building methods (borrowed from evolutionary biology) to cultural data (languages, artifact traditions, ritual practices) to reconstruct cultural "family trees" — is methodologically promising but faces challenges in accounting for horizontal transmission (borrowing/diffusion), reticulation, and convergent evolution that violate tree-model assumptions

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Culture Evolves Like Genes
- **[MISLEADING]** While cultural evolutionary theory uses mathematical tools borrowed from population genetics, Boyd, Richerson, and Henrich explicitly reject the claim that cultural evolution is **identical** to genetic evolution — cultural transmission involves blending, transformation, intentional modification, horizontal transfer (not just vertical), and many-to-many transmission patterns that have no genetic analog

### 4.2 Cultural Evolution Is Necessarily Progressive
- **[CONTRADICTED]** Cultural evolutionary theory does not assume that cultural change is directionally progressive — cultures can and do lose technologies, forget knowledge, and adopt maladaptive traits. The theory explicitly models conditions under which cultural loss, drift, and maladaptation occur
