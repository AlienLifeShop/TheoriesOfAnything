## QUICK SUMMARY

**Ethnoarchaeology** is the study of **living or recently documented societies** — their material culture, spatial organization, subsistence strategies, craft production, architecture, refuse disposal, and social practices — with the explicit goal of generating **interpretive models and analogies** for understanding the archaeological record. Founded on the premise that the relationship between human behavior and its material traces can be observed and documented in living contexts — and that these observations can inform archaeological interpretation — ethnoarchaeology bridges **ethnography and archaeology** to build what Lewis Binford called **"middle-range theory"**: the linking arguments that connect static archaeological remains to the dynamic behaviors that produced them. Key areas of ethnoarchaeological research include: **(1) site formation and discard behavior** — how refuse is generated, distributed, and managed in settlements (Schiffer 1972; O'Connell 1987); **(2) subsistence and mobility** — how hunter-gatherer, pastoral, and agricultural groups organize their movements, camp layouts, and food processing in relation to environmental conditions and social factors (Binford 1978, 1980; Yellen 1977); **(3) craft production** — how potters, knappers, metalworkers, and weavers organize their production, select raw materials, transmit knowledge, and distribute products (David and Kramer 2001); **(4) architecture and spatial organization** — how domestic and public spaces are constructed, used, maintained, and abandoned (Cameron and Tomka 1993); and **(5) symbolic and ritual behavior** — how material culture encodes social identity, status, ritual meaning, and cosmological beliefs. Ethnoarchaeology has been both enormously productive and heavily critiqued: productive because it generates testable models that would be impossible to derive from archaeological data alone; critiqued because analogical reasoning risks imposing present-day patterns onto fundamentally different past societies, and because the communities studied have often been marginalized peoples whose lives were documented without adequate consent, collaboration, or benefit.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Archaeological Record)

### 1.1 Middle-Range Theory and Actualistic Studies
- **Lewis Binford** (1978, 1980, 1983) articulated the theoretical foundation for ethnoarchaeology as the construction of **"middle-range theory"** — the set of linking arguments connecting observed material patterns to the behaviors that produced them:
  - Example: observing how Nunamiut (Inuit) hunters process caribou carcasses — which bones they transport to camp, which they leave at the kill site, how they process them for marrow — produces models for interpreting the fragmentary, transported bone assemblages found at Paleolithic sites
  - **Actualistic studies** — a broader category including both ethnoarchaeology and experimental archaeology — generate these interpretive frameworks through direct observation of cause-and-effect relationships between behavior and material traces

### 1.2 Discard Behavior and Site Formation
- Ethnoarchaeological studies of refuse disposal have produced influential models for interpreting archaeological site structures:
  - **Binford (1978)**: "The Nunamiut Ethnoarchaeology" — documented the spatial organization of Nunamiut camps, producing models of "drop zones" (where items are discarded at the point of use) and "toss zones" (where refuse is thrown from activity areas)
  - **O'Connell (1987, 1995)**: Hadza (Tanzania) camp studies — documented how camp size, occupation duration, and maintenance behavior affect the material signature of hunter-gatherer occupations
  - **Murray (1980)**: the concept of the **"Pompeii premise"** — the fallacy that archaeological floor assemblages represent snapshot "frozen moments" of activity, when in fact most deposits are cumulative palimpsests of repeated occupations, modified by cleaning, abandonment, and post-depositional processes

### 1.3 Subsistence and Mobility
- Ethnoarchaeological studies of foragers and pastoralists have documented:
  - **Residential vs. logistical mobility** (Binford 1980): "foragers" move their entire camp to resources (high residential mobility); "collectors" establish base camps and send task groups to distant resource patches (logistical mobility) — these strategies produce characteristically different archaeological signatures
  - **Camp structure**: Yellen (1977) documented !Kung San (Ju/'hoansi) camp layouts — ring-shaped arrangement of family hearths around a central communal space — influencing archaeological interpretation of similar patterns at African Middle Stone Age sites
  - **Pastoral nomadism**: ethnoarchaeological studies of Middle Eastern (Cribb 1991), East African (Lane 2013), and Central Asian pastoralists have documented the ephemeral material signature of mobile herding — challenging archaeologists to identify pastoralist occupation in the record

### 1.4 Craft Production
- **Ceramic ethnoarchaeology**: studies of living pottery traditions have documented:
  - Raw material selection, clay preparation, forming techniques (coiling, wheel-throwing, paddle-and-anvil), firing conditions, and post-firing treatments
  - The social organization of production: household-level, workshop, or specialist production — each producing different archaeological signatures (kiln types, waster distributions, tool kits)
  - **David and Kramer (2001)**: comprehensive survey of ethnoarchaeological studies across all craft domains
- **Lithic ethnoarchaeology**: studies of stone-tool-using communities (Australia, Papua New Guinea, Ethiopia) have documented:
  - Raw material procurement strategies, reduction sequences, tool-use contexts, and discard patterns — providing direct behavioral correlates for lithic archaeological assemblages

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Architecture and Abandonment
- Ethnoarchaeological studies of settlement abandonment have documented:
  - **De facto refuse**: the material left behind when a settlement is abandoned — dependent on the speed, cause, and circumstances of abandonment (planned vs. catastrophic; seasonal vs. permanent)
  - **Post-abandonment processes**: how abandoned structures decay, are scavenged for materials, colonized by animals, or reused — affecting what archaeologists eventually recover
  - **Cameron and Tomka (1993)**: systematic ethnoarchaeological study of abandonment processes — documenting that most abandoned settlements are **heavily stripped of usable materials** before final leaving, contrary to the "Pompeii premise"

### 2.2 The Limits of Analogy
- **Critiques of ethnoarchaeological analogy** are well-established:
  - **Uniformitarian assumption**: ethnoarchaeology assumes that relationships between behavior and material culture observed today are applicable to the past — but past societies may have had fundamentally different social organizations, cosmologies, environmental contexts, and technological repertoires
  - **Colonial context**: many ethnoarchaeological studies were conducted among indigenous peoples already transformed by colonialism, globalization, and market economies — raising questions about the applicability of observations to pre-contact or prehistoric contexts
  - **Ethics**: the power dynamics of external researchers studying marginalized communities — often without adequate consent, collaboration, or benefit-sharing — have been increasingly critiqued (see Gonzalez-Ruibal 2014)
  - **"Ethnographic present" fallacy**: treating living communities as "frozen in time" — static representatives of past lifeways — denies their historicity and agency

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Digital Ethnoarchaeology
- The application of **GIS, GPS tracking, time-motion analysis, and wearable sensors** to ethnoarchaeological fieldwork — quantitatively documenting movement patterns, activity durations, and spatial behavior — is a developing frontier that may produce more rigorous behavioral models

### 3.2 Collaborative and Indigenous-Led Models
- Emerging approaches prioritize **collaborative** and **community-based** ethnoarchaeological research — where indigenous communities participate as co-researchers and co-interpreters — potentially producing richer and more ethically grounded interpretive models. The efficacy and scalability of these approaches are still being evaluated

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Living Societies Are "Living Fossils"
- **[CONTRADICTED]** The notion that certain contemporary indigenous societies represent "earlier stages" of human cultural evolution — and can therefore be used as direct analogs for prehistoric behavior — is **scientifically and ethically discredited**. All living societies have their own complex histories, adaptations, and cultural dynamics — they are not frozen representatives of the human past

### 4.2 One Ethnographic Analog Is Sufficient
- **[MISLEADING]** Reliance on a single ethnographic case as the interpretive model for an archaeological context risks circular reasoning and confirmation bias. Best practice uses **multiple, independent analogs** and tests them against archaeological evidence — recognizing that no single modern case perfectly mirrors any past situation
