## QUICK SUMMARY

**Cargo cults** — the millenarian religious movements that emerged primarily in **Melanesia** (Papua New Guinea, Vanuatu, Solomon Islands, and other Pacific islands) during and after contact with Western industrial civilization, particularly during **World War II** — have been interpreted by anthropologists in diverse ways. The term "cargo cult" (from *kago*, Tok Pisin for manufactured goods) refers to movements in which indigenous peoples performed **rituals** (building symbolic airstrips, marching in formation, constructing mock radio equipment, carrying bamboo "rifles") intended to attract the return of Western manufactured goods ("cargo") which they believed were being withheld by colonial powers or were of spiritual origin. The most famous examples include the **John Frum movement** (Tanna, Vanuatu, from ~1940s) and the **Prince Philip movement** (also Tanna). While these movements are often presented as quaint examples of "primitive" misunderstanding, this analysis argues — following the work of **Peter Worsley** (1957), **Kenelm Burridge** (1960), and later anthropologists — that cargo cults are better understood as **rational responses to colonial disruption**, functioning as **revitalization movements** (in Anthony F.C. Wallace's framework) that display structural parallels with **ancient mystery schools**: initiatory structure, esoteric knowledge, prophetic leadership, ritual performance aimed at transformation, and the creation of new cosmological frameworks to make sense of radical social change.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 Historical Context
- Cargo cults emerged in the context of **colonial contact** and the massive disruption of traditional Melanesian social systems:
  - **Pre-contact Melanesian societies**: largely egalitarian (leadership earned through prestige, not inherited), with economies based on exchange networks (*kula*, *moka*), subsistence agriculture, and elaborate ceremonial life
  - **Colonial disruption**: European and later Japanese and American military forces arrived with vastly superior material goods — manufactured items whose production was invisible to indigenous observers (who never saw factories)
  - **World War II**: the massive American military buildup in the Pacific — with airstrips, supply drops, hospitals, Coca-Cola, canned food, trucks, and aircraft — was the catalytic event for many cargo movements. When the Americans left after the war, the cargo stopped

### 1.2 Major Cargo Cults
- **John Frum movement** (Tanna, Vanuatu, from ~1940s):
  - Centered on a prophetic figure named "John Frum" (possibly based on an American serviceman, or entirely mythical)
  - Believers expect John Frum to return, bringing cargo — particularly American manufactured goods
  - Adherents rejected Christian missions and colonial authority, returning to traditional customs (*kastom*)
  - **Still active**: the John Frum movement persists on Tanna to this day, with annual celebrations on February 15 ("John Frum Day")
- **Prince Philip movement** (Yaohnanen village, Tanna):
  - A subset of Tanna islanders who came to believe that **Prince Philip, Duke of Edinburgh** (1921-2021) was a divine being — a fulfillment of an ancient prophecy about the return of a pale-skinned son of the mountain god
  - Philip was aware of the movement and exchanged gifts and photographs with the community
- **Vailala Madness** (Papua, 1919): one of the earliest documented cargo movements — mass possession episodes, destruction of traditional sacred objects, and prophecies of ancestors returning in a ship laden with cargo

### 1.3 Peter Worsley's Analysis
- **Peter Worsley** (*The Trumpet Shall Sound*, 1957): the first comprehensive scholarly study of cargo cults:
  - Argued that cargo cults were **rational responses** to colonial oppression — not evidence of irrationality or "primitive mentality"
  - Interpreted them as **proto-political movements**: attempts by colonized peoples to understand, resist, and renegotiate their position in a colonial power structure
  - Emphasized the **material dimension**: cargo cults expressed a desire for economic equality and material justice, not merely spiritual delusion

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Structural Parallels with Mystery Schools
- The comparison between cargo cults and ancient mystery schools reveals significant structural parallels:
  - **Initiatory structure**: many cargo movements involved **graded access to knowledge** — not all community members were privy to the full cosmological narrative or the inner workings of the ritual program
  - **Prophetic leadership**: a charismatic leader (prophet) claims access to **hidden knowledge** about the true nature of reality — paralleling the *hierophant* of the Eleusinian Mysteries or the *mystagogue* of other ancient traditions
  - **Ritual performance**: the construction of mock airstrips, radio towers, and marching formations are not "imitation" but **ritual enactment** — designed to bring about a desired cosmic state through performative action (analogous to theurgic practice in Neoplatonic mystery traditions)
  - **New cosmological framework**: cargo cults create comprehensive **cosmological narratives** that explain why the current social order is unjust and how it will be overturned — paralleling the eschatological visions of mystery religions
  - **Concealment from outsiders**: cargo cult teachings were often kept secret from missionaries, colonial administrators, and uninitiated community members — functioning as esoteric knowledge

### 2.2 Revitalization Movement Theory
- **Anthony F.C. Wallace** (1956, "Revitalization Movements"): proposed a general framework for understanding rapid cultural change movements:
  - A **revitalization movement** is a deliberate, organized effort by members of a society to create a more satisfying culture
  - Occurs when a society is under severe stress (colonialism, economic disruption, epidemic disease) and existing cultural frameworks fail to provide adequate meaning or solutions
  - Stages: (1) Steady state disrupted → (2) Period of individual stress → (3) Period of cultural distortion → (4) Prophetic revelation → (5) Communication of new code → (6) Organization → (7) Adaptation → (8) New steady state or failure
  - Cargo cults, Ghost Dance (North America), Taiping Rebellion (China), and millenarian movements worldwide fit this pattern

### 2.3 Critique of the "Cargo Cult" Concept
- The very concept of "cargo cult" has been criticized:
  - **Lamont Lindstrom** (1993, *Cargo Cult: Strange Stories of Desire from Melanesia and Beyond*) argued the term is itself a **colonial construct** — imposed by outside observers to delegitimize indigenous political and religious movements
  - Different movements grouped under "cargo cult" have vastly different doctrines, practices, and social functions — the unifying label obscures this diversity
  - The term carries pejorative connotations of irrationality — obscuring the genuine political, economic, and spiritual logic of these movements
