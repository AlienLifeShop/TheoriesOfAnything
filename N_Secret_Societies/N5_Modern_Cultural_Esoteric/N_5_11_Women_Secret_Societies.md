## QUICK SUMMARY

Throughout history and across cultures, women have formed, led, and participated in **secret societies** and **initiatory organizations** that served as spaces of **female authority**, **knowledge transmission**, **spiritual practice**, and **social power** — often in contexts where formal political and religious authority was monopolized by men. This document examines three major examples: the **Sande** (also known as **Bundu**) society of **West Africa** (primarily Sierra Leone, Liberia, Guinea, and Ivory Coast) — one of the few secret societies in the world in which women have full control over masked ritual, serving as the primary institution for female initiation, education, and communal governance; the **Bori** spirit possession cult among the **Hausa** peoples of Nigeria and Niger — a predominantly female institution through which women excluded from Islamic formal religious practice accessed spiritual authority through ecstatic possession by spirits (*iskoki*); and the **Eleusinian priestesses** of ancient **Greece** — women who held high ritual offices in the Eleusinian Mysteries, the most sacred religious institution of the ancient Greek world, including the office of **Priestess of Demeter** (one of the most prestigious religious positions in Athens). These examples demonstrate that women's secret societies are not marginal exceptions but are **structural features** of human religious and social organization — providing counter-institutions through which women exercised authority, transmitted knowledge, and maintained spheres of action independent of male control.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 The Sande Society (West Africa)
- The **Sande** (called **Bundu** in some regions) is a women's secret society found among the **Mende**, **Temne**, **Vai**, **Gola**, **Kpelle**, and other peoples of Sierra Leone, Liberia, Guinea, and Ivory Coast:
  - Sande and the male **Poro** society together constitute the **dual governance system** of traditional Mende society — Poro governing male affairs, Sande governing female affairs
  - **Initiation**: girls enter the Sande "bush" (a secluded forest enclosure) for a period of weeks to months (historically longer, now often abbreviated) — undergoing instruction in: practical skills (farming, cooking, crafts), sexual and reproductive knowledge, social norms, history and tradition, herbalism and healing, music and dance
  - **Masquerade**: Sande is **unique** among documented African secret societies in that women own and perform masked rituals — the **Sowo** (also *Sowei* or *ndoli jowei*) mask, a smooth black helmet mask representing ideal female beauty and spiritual authority, is danced by senior Sande members. Sande is the **only known case worldwide** where women are the masqueraders in a traditional masked society
  - **Leadership**: the **Sowei** (senior leaders of the Sande society) hold significant political and social authority — mediating disputes, advising chiefs, and controlling access to the "bush" space
  - **Modern challenges**: Sande initiation traditionally included **female genital cutting** (FGC/FGM) — an element that has become the subject of intense international debate and legal prohibition in several countries, placing Sande institutions under significant external pressure

### 1.2 The Bori Cult (Hausa, West Africa)
- **Bori** is a spirit possession tradition practiced primarily among **Hausa** populations in Nigeria and Niger:
  - Bori centers on the belief in **iskoki** (spirits) — a pantheon of named spirits with specific characteristics, demands, and relationships to human practitioners
  - Predominantly **female**: while men participate, women constitute the majority of Bori practitioners and leaders — particularly women who are marginalized within the formal Islamic social structure (divorced women, barren women, women with disabilities, formerly enslaved women)
  - **Possession**: during Bori ceremonies, practitioners are possessed by specific spirits — entering altered states of consciousness in which the spirit speaks, dances, and demands offerings through the possessed person's body
  - **Social function**: Bori provides women with: (1) a **healing framework** (illnesses are diagnosed as spirit-related and treated through ritual), (2) **economic independence** (Bori practitioners earn income through healing and divination), (3) **alternative social status** (a woman of low social standing can become a respected Bori leader), and (4) **a counter-institution** to the formal Islamic religious establishment from which women are largely excluded
  - **Scholarly analysis**: **Fremont Besmer** (*Horses, Musicians and Gods: The Hausa Cult of Spirit Possession*, 1983) and **Adeline Masquelier** (*Prayer Has Spoiled Everything*, 2001) have documented Bori as a space of female agency within patriarchal society

### 1.3 Eleusinian Priestesses (Ancient Greece)
- Women held critical ritual roles in the **Eleusinian Mysteries** — the most important religious institution of ancient Athens:
  - **Priestess of Demeter**: the highest female religious office at Eleusis — hereditary within the **Eumolpid** and **Kerykes** families. The Priestess presided alongside the Hierophant in the inner ritual
  - **Priestess of Persephone** (*Kore*): officiated over the aspects of the mystery connected to the Underworld journey
  - **Hierophantides**: female initiatory officials who participated in the most sacred rites
  - **Panageis** ("all-holy women"): women consecrated to ritual offices within the sanctuary
  - Women participated in the Mysteries as **initiates** (*mystai*) on equal terms with men — one of the few Greek religious institutions that transcended gender barriers
  - The centrality of the **Demeter-Persephone myth** (mother-daughter bond, the seasonal cycle, agricultural fertility) meant that the Mysteries were fundamentally structured around **female experience** — even though the highest single office (Hierophant) was male

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Other Women's Secret Societies
- The Sande, Bori, and Eleusinian examples are not isolated — women's secret societies and initiatory organizations exist across cultures:
  - **Gelede** masquerade (Yoruba, Nigeria/Benin): honors "the mothers" (*awon iya wa*) — elderly women believed to possess extraordinary spiritual power. While men wear the masks, the entire institution pays tribute to female spiritual authority
  - **Thesmophoria** (ancient Greece): an all-female festival in honor of Demeter — men were strictly excluded. Women practiced rites that remain only partially understood due to the secrecy surrounding them
  - **Bona Dea** rites (ancient Rome): all-female religious rites from which men were excluded — the violation of these rites by **Publius Clodius Pulcher** (62 BCE) was a major political scandal
  - **Leopard Society** women's branches: in some West African contexts, women had their own parallel organizations within broader secret society structures
  - **Amazonian women's ritual**: among some indigenous Amazonian peoples, women have ritual roles and knowledge systems that are kept secret from men

### 2.2 Women's Societies as Counter-Institutions
- Sociological analysis reveals a common function across women's secret societies:
  - They provide **alternative authority structures** in societies where formal political and religious power is held by men
  - They create **protected spaces** for women's knowledge transmission — particularly knowledge related to sexuality, reproduction, healing, and child-rearing
  - They serve as **collective bargaining institutions** — Sande leaders, for example, can collectively sanction men who violate women's rights
  - They offer **alternative status systems** — allowing women who are marginalized in the male-dominated hierarchy to achieve respect and authority within the women's organization

### 2.3 The Problem of Evidence
- Scholarly documentation of women's secret societies faces distinctive challenges:
  - **Secrecy**: the same secrecy that makes these societies socially powerful makes them difficult to study — much ritual knowledge is genuinely hidden from outsiders (including male researchers)
  - **Male bias in scholarship**: colonial-era ethnography was overwhelmingly conducted by male researchers who were denied access to women's religious practices — resulting in systematic underrepresentation
  - **Oral tradition**: women's secret knowledge is often transmitted orally and ritually rather than through written texts — making it vulnerable to disruption and invisible to text-focused scholarship
  - Recent scholarship (particularly by female anthropologists like **Sylvia Boone**, **Ruth Phillips**, and **Adeline Masquelier**) has begun to correct this imbalance

### 3.1 Deep Antiquity of Women's Initiatory Traditions
- Scholars have proposed that women's initiation rites may be among the **oldest** human ritual traditions:
  - Evidence from Upper Paleolithic cave art (e.g., vulvar symbols, female figurines — the "Venus" figurines) has been interpreted as evidence of female-centered ritual
  - However, reconstructing the ritual practices of prehistoric societies from material evidence alone is inherently speculative

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Women's Secret Societies Were Merely Subordinate to Men's
- **[INCORRECT]** The Sande society, for example, exercises genuine political authority alongside (not subordinate to) the male Poro society. Women's societies in many cultures represent **independent** power structures, not subsidiaries of male organizations

### 4.2 A Universal Matriarchal Religion Preceded Patriarchy
- **[UNSUBSTANTIATED]** While women's ritual roles were clearly important in many ancient and traditional societies, the claim of a universal prehistoric "matriarchal religion" (popularized by Marija Gimbutas and taken up by some feminist spirituality movements) is not supported by the archaeological evidence — which shows a much more diverse and complex picture
