## QUICK SUMMARY

The **Bohemian Club** is an exclusive **all-male private club** founded in **1872** in **San Francisco, California**, originally for journalists, artists, and musicians ("bohemians"), which over the following decades transformed into one of the most elite social organizations in the United States, with a membership that has included virtually every **Republican president** since Calvin Coolidge, along with leading **corporate executives**, **military leaders**, **media moguls**, and cultural figures. The club's annual two-week summer retreat — the **Bohemian Grove** encampment at a **2,700-acre** redwood forest near **Monte Rio, Sonoma County** — is one of the most scrutinized elite gatherings in the world, featuring the infamous **"Cremation of Care"** ceremony (a theatrical ritual involving the symbolic burning of "worldly cares" before a large owl effigy), "Lakeside Talks" (off-the-record speeches by world leaders on policy topics), and camp-based entertainment. Sociologists — particularly **G. William Domhoff** (*The Bohemian Grove and Other Retreats*, 1974) and **Peter Martin Phillips** (1994) — have studied the Grove as a case study in **elite social cohesion**: a space where the ruling class builds solidarity, develops consensus on policy, and reinforces class identity through shared ritual, recreation, and camaraderie outside the formal structures of government and business.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Established)

### 1.1 History
- Founded **1872** in San Francisco by journalists and artists:
  - Original members included **Henry George** (economist), **Ambrose Bierce** (author), and **Jack London** (writer)
  - Originally a social club for literary and artistic bohemians — the motto was (and remains) "**Weaving Spiders Come Not Here**" (from *A Midsummer Night's Dream*), meaning business deals should not be conducted at club gatherings
  - By the early 20th century, wealthy businessmen began dominating the membership, displacing the original artistic character — though artists and academics continue to participate as "talent" members
- Membership (~2,700 as of recent estimates) includes:
  - At least 5 former US Presidents (Herbert Hoover, Dwight Eisenhower, Richard Nixon, Ronald Reagan, George H.W. Bush — with additional presidents attending as guests)
  - Major corporate CEOs (from companies including Bechtel, Bank of America, Pacific Gas & Electric, and others)
  - University presidents, military generals, media executives

### 1.2 The Bohemian Grove
- The **annual summer encampment** runs for approximately two weeks in late July:
  - Located in a **2,700-acre** old-growth redwood grove along the Russian River near Monte Rio, California
  - Approximately **2,000-3,000 men** attend (members and guests)
  - The Grove is divided into approximately **118 camps** — each with a distinct character and membership (e.g., "Mandalay," "Cave Man," "Isle of Aves," "Hill Billies")
  - Activities include: informal socializing, theatrical performances (the "Grove Play" and "Low Jinks"), musical performances, drinking, swimming, and the "Lakeside Talks"

### 1.3 Cremation of Care Ceremony
- The **opening ceremony** of the Grove encampment:
  - Performed on the first Saturday night of the encampment
  - A theatrical ritual in which a figure representing "Dull Care" (worldly worries, anxiety, ambition) is symbolically burned before a **40-foot concrete owl** statue at the lakeside
  - The ceremony includes: a procession of robed figures carrying torches, musical accompaniment, a dramatic script with roles for "Priest," "Voice of the Owl," and other characters
  - The owl is the **symbol of the Bohemian Club** (representing wisdom/Athena)
  - The ceremony is explicitly presented as **theatrical symbolism** — not religious worship

### 1.4 Domhoff's Sociological Analysis
- **G. William Domhoff** (*The Bohemian Grove and Other Retreats*, 1974; updated in *Who Rules America?* series):
  - The Grove functions as a space for **elite social cohesion** — where members of the ruling class build personal relationships, develop shared worldviews, and create the social bonds that enable coordinated action in government and business
  - The "Lakeside Talks" serve as **informal policy planning** — off-the-record presentations on national and international issues
  - Historically significant: in **September 1942**, a meeting at the Grove brought together **Ernest O. Lawrence** (Nobel laureate, inventor of the cyclotron), **J. Robert Oppenheimer**, **Arthur Compton**, and other key physicists to discuss the feasibility of an atomic bomb — this meeting is credited as a crucial planning session for what became the **Manhattan Project**, before it had formal government authorization (Rhodes, *The Making of the Atomic Bomb*, 1986; Bird & Sherwin, *American Prometheus*, 2005)

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 The Power Elite Framework
- The Bohemian Club illustrates C. Wright Mills' concept of the **"power elite"** (1956):
  - Mills argued that American society was governed by a small, interconnected group of leaders in the military, corporate, and political sectors — who moved between these institutions and shared a common social background
  - The Bohemian Grove is precisely the kind of informal social space where this interconnection is maintained and reproduced
  - The exclusion of women, minorities (historically), and non-elite individuals reinforces the **class**, **gender**, and **racial** boundaries of the power elite

### 2.2 Policy Formation
- Multiple scholars have documented the role of elite social gatherings in informal policy formation:
  - The Grove was where Ronald Reagan and Richard Nixon reportedly negotiated their respective presidential candidacies
  - However, the motto "Weaving Spiders Come Not Here" is nominally enforced — direct business transactions violate Grove etiquette
  - The distinction between "informal relationship building" and "direct policy planning" is extremely blurred in practice

### 2.3 Gender and Power
- The Bohemian Club's **all-male** membership policy has been challenged:
  - In 1978, the California Supreme Court declined to force the club to admit women
  - The all-male character of the Grove has been analyzed as a space for **elite masculine bonding** — a reinforcement of patriarchal power networks through shared recreation, ritual, and exclusion of women
