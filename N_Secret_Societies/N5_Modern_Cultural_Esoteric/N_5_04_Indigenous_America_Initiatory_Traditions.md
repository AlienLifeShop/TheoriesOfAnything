## QUICK SUMMARY

The **indigenous peoples of the Americas** developed an extraordinary diversity of **secret initiatory societies** — ceremonial organizations with restricted membership, graded initiation, guarded esoteric knowledge, and defined ritual responsibilities — that functioned as essential structures of **social, religious, political, and medical governance** across the continents for millennia before European contact. Unlike Western secret societies (which typically exist as marginal or counter-cultural organizations within larger societies), many indigenous American secret societies were **integral to governance** — the keepers of essential ceremonial knowledge without which the community believed its survival, fertility, and cosmic order would fail. The **Pueblo peoples** of the American Southwest (Hopi, Zuni, Keresan, Tewan, and Towa groups) operated perhaps the most elaborate systems of ceremonial societies: each Pueblo village was organized around multiple **secret societies** (medicine societies, hunting societies, clown societies, warrior societies, kachina societies) — each responsible for specific ceremonies at designated points in the agricultural and ceremonial calendar; membership was by clan membership, curing initiation, or invitation; and the **kiva** (underground or semi-subterranean ceremonial chamber) served as the sacred, restricted space for ritual performance. Among the **Ojibwe** (Anishinaabe) and neighboring Great Lakes nations, the **Midewiwin** (Grand Medicine Society) was a multi-degree initiatory society dedicated to healing, medicine, and the oral transmission of sacred knowledge — initiates progressed through typically **four to eight degrees** (accounts vary), each requiring sponsorship, gift-giving, ceremonial instruction, and the receipt of a **medicine bundle** (*mide bag*) containing sacred objects; the Midewiwin's knowledge was recorded on **birch bark scrolls** (*wiigwaasabak*) using pictographic symbols — a unique system of sacred writing that preserved ritual instructions, origin narratives, and medicinal knowledge. The **Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) False Face Society** — a **healing society** whose members wore carved wooden masks representing forest spirit beings — conducted ritual door-to-door healing ceremonies during specific seasons; membership was restricted to individuals who had been healed by the society or who dreamed of False Faces; the masks themselves were considered sacred and powerful — non-initiates were forbidden from handling them. These examples represent a fraction of the hundreds of distinct ceremonial society traditions documented across the Americas — from the **Hopi Snake Society** and **Kachina cults** of the Southwest, to the **Sun Dance** pledgers of the Plains, to the **Cannibal Society** (*Hamatsa*) of the Kwakwaka'wakw of the Pacific Northwest. The study of these traditions raises profound ethical issues: most indigenous ceremonial societies explicitly forbid the revelation of their secrets to outsiders, and ethnographic documentation of "secret" ceremonies (much of it conducted during the 19th and early 20th centuries without community consent) is itself a form of **cultural intrusion** — a tension between academic knowledge and indigenous sovereignty that remains unresolved.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Ethnographic Documentation)

### 1.1 Pueblo Ceremonial Societies
- **Ortiz (1969, *The Tewa World*)**: documented the dual organizational structure of Tewa Pueblo society — divided into Summer and Winter moieties (*People of the Summer* and *People of the Winter*), each with its own ceremonial leaders (*Made People* — initiated members of specific societies), secular leaders, and ritual calendar; the *Made People* controlled agricultural and healing ceremonies essential to community survival
- **Hopi kachina societies**: the **kachinas** (*katsinam*) — spirit beings representing ancestral and nature forces — are impersonated by initiated men wearing elaborate masks and costumes during ceremonies from approximately the winter solstice through mid-July; the kachina initiation (for boys at approximately age 10) involves the revelation that the masked figures are community members, not actual spirits — a "secret" whose maintenance is central to Hopi religious life
- **Zuni**: organized around six kiva groups (each associated with a cardinal direction), plus numerous specialized societies (e.g., the Bow Priesthood — a warrior society; the Rain Priesthood; various medicine societies) — ethnographic documentation by Matilda Coxe Stevenson (1904, *The Zuñi Indians*) and Frank Hamilton Cushing provides detailed but ethically problematic accounts

### 1.2 Midewiwin — Grand Medicine Society
- **Hoffman (1891, *The Mide'wiwin or "Grand Medicine Society" of the Ojibwa*)**: the earliest comprehensive ethnographic account — described a multi-degree society devoted to healing, with initiation ceremonies involving:
  - Sponsorship by existing members
  - Instruction in medicinal plants, ritual procedures, and origin narratives
  - Ceremonial "shooting" (symbolic killing and revival of the initiate using a *migis* — sacred white shell)
  - Receipt of a medicine bundle and the right to practice healing
- **Birch bark scrolls**: Midewiwin knowledge was recorded in pictographic form on birch bark — these scrolls depicted ritual procedures, migration narratives, and medicinal information; they constitute one of the few indigenous American writing systems (alongside Maya glyphs and various pictographic traditions) and are held as sacred by the Midewiwin — many remain in community hands, while others were collected by museums (their return is increasingly demanded under repatriation principles)
- The Midewiwin has experienced a significant **revival** in the 20th–21st centuries as part of broader indigenous cultural revitalization movements

### 1.3 Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) Societies
- **The False Face Society**: masks are carved from living basswood trees (the carver begins the work on a living tree, then completes the mask after removing the wood) — the masks are considered alive and are fed tobacco and corn mush; healing ceremonies involve visiting the sick person's home, burning tobacco, and performing dances
- **Haudenosaunee ceremonial societies** include: the False Face Society, **Husk Face Society** (wearing braided cornhusk masks — associated with agricultural ceremonies), **Bear Society** and **Otter Society** (medicine societies using specific songs and dances for healing), and the **Little Water Society** (considered the most powerful and secretive of Haudenosaunee medicine societies)
- **Ethics of study**: in 1995, the Haudenosaunee Grand Council formally requested that museums remove False Face masks from public display — many museums (including the National Museum of the American Indian) have complied; this represents an important precedent in the tension between ethnographic documentation and indigenous sacred rights

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Plains Societies and Age-Graded Systems
- Many Plains nations (Blackfoot, Crow, Cheyenne, Arapaho) organized their male populations into **age-graded military and ceremonial societies** — men progressed through successive societies as they aged, each with its own songs, dances, regalia, and battlefield obligations
- The **Cheyenne Contraries** (members of the Contrary Society, *hohnúhka*): warriors who did everything backwards (saying yes when they meant no, walking backwards, bathing in dirt) — this represents a tradition of **sacred clown** or **heyoka** behavior found across many indigenous American cultures, in which ritual inversion (deliberate violation of social norms) served as a form of spiritual power and social commentary

### 2.2 Northwest Coast Winter Ceremonies
- The **Kwakwaka'wakw (Kwakiutl)**: the **Hamatsa** (Cannibal Society) ceremony was the most prestigious of the winter ceremonial performances — involving the initiation of a young man who was "possessed" by the Cannibal Spirit (*Baxwbakwalanuksiwe'* — "the cannibal at the north end of the world"), performed wild and dramatic behavior, and was gradually "tamed" and reintegrated into human society through an elaborate multi-day ceremonial sequence involving masked dances (particularly the famous **crooked beak mask** and **Huxwhukw** crane mask)
- Boas's extensive documentation (1897, 1966) of these ceremonies is the primary ethnographic source — but it was conducted during a period when the Canadian government had criminalized the potlatch and associated ceremonies (Potlatch Ban, 1884–1951), making Kwakwaka'wakw participation itself an act of resistance
