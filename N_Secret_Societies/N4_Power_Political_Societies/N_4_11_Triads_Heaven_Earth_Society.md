## QUICK SUMMARY

The **Tiandihui** (天地會, "**Heaven and Earth Society**"), also known as the **Hongmen** (洪門, "Vast Gate"), the **Three Harmonies Society** (三合會, *Sanhehui* — the origin of the English term "**Triad**"), and by numerous other names, is one of the world's oldest and most extensive **secret society traditions** — originating in **southern China** during the **Qing dynasty** (1644–1912) and spreading throughout the Chinese diaspora in Southeast Asia, the Americas, Europe, and Australasia. The Tiandihui's **founding mythology** (an elaborate origin legend enacted in initiation rituals) centers on the **Shaolin monastery**: according to this legend, five monks survived the destruction of Shaolin by the Manchu Qing emperor, swore an oath of brotherhood to overthrow the Qing and restore the **Ming dynasty** (*fǎn Qīng fù Míng* — 反清復明, "Oppose Qing, Restore Ming"), and founded the society as a vehicle of Chinese (Han) resistance against Manchu rule. The **historical origins** are less romantic: scholarly analysis (**Murray 1994, *The Origins of the Tiandihui*; Ownby 1996, *Brotherhoods and Secret Societies in Early and Mid-Qing China*) suggests the Tiandihui emerged in **Fujian province** in the mid-**18th century** (c. 1761–1770s) as a **mutual aid brotherhood** among marginalized men — laborers, merchants, drifters, and the underemployed — who formed sworn brotherhoods for economic cooperation, physical protection, and social identity in an era of rapid population growth, geographic mobility, and economic uncertainty. The anti-Manchu political dimension was likely a **later addition** (grafted onto the already-existing brotherhood structure) rather than the founding purpose. The Tiandihui's **initiation rituals** — documented from captured ritual manuals (翻弄 — seized by Qing officials) and ethnographic observation — are among the most elaborate of any secret society: they include the recitation of an **origin narrative** (the Five Ancestors legend), the passage through a symbolic **"City of Willows"** (representing the journey from profane to sacred space), the taking of **36 oaths** (specifying mutual obligations: protect fellow members, keep society secrets, share resources, avenge wrongs — violation was punishable by death), blood drinking, incense burning, and the assumption of a **new identity** within the society's hierarchy (headed by a **Dragon Head**, *Lung Tau*; an **Incense Master**, *Heung Chu*, who conducts rituals; a **Vanguard**, *Sin Fung*; and numbered ranks: 489, 438, 432, 415, 49 — these numbers are derived from numerological codes embedded in the founding myth). During the 19th and early 20th centuries, Triad societies played significant roles in: the **Taiping Rebellion** (1850–1864, the deadliest civil war in history — the Small Swords Society, a Triad affiliate, briefly captured Shanghai in 1853), **overseas Chinese communities** (Tiandihui/Hongmen lodges served as mutual aid societies, labor brokerages, dispute mediators, and sometimes violent power brokers in Southeast Asian tin mines, Californian gold fields, and urban Chinatowns worldwide), the **anti-Qing revolution** (Sun Yat-sen was a Hongmen member; Triad networks supported the 1911 Revolution), and — in the 20th century — the transformation of some branches into **organized crime syndicates** (drug trafficking, extortion, gambling, prostitution) in Hong Kong, Macau, Taiwan, and the global diaspora.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Historical and Archival Sources)

### 1.1 Historical Origins
- **Murray (1994, *The Origins of the Tiandihui*)**: based on Qing dynasty judicial archives (confessions, interrogation records, seized documents — the most reliable primary sources, as they were recorded under oath and cross-examined) — established that the Tiandihui was founded in **Fujian province** in the 1760s–1770s; the earliest documented leader was a Buddhist monk named **Ti Xi** (提喜, also known as Zheng Kai); the initial membership was overwhelmingly composed of rural laborers, itinerant merchants, and displaced men seeking mutual protection
- **Ownby (1996)**: argued that the Tiandihui emerged from a longer tradition of Chinese sworn brotherhoods (*jieyi xiongdi* — "sworn brothers," modeled on the legendary oath of the Peach Garden from *Romance of the Three Kingdoms*) — the organization of fictive kinship groups for mutual aid was common in southern China, and the Tiandihui ritualized and formalized this practice
- **Anti-Manchu ideology**: the slogan *fǎn Qīng fù Míng* became central to the society's identity, but archival evidence suggests it was adopted as a unifying ideology particularly from the late 18th century onward — some early Tiandihui groups appear to have been primarily concerned with local mutual aid rather than dynastic restoration

### 1.2 Ritual and Organization
- **Initiation rituals**: documented in seized ritual manuals (called "treasured scrolls" or *baojuan*) and Qing judicial records — the ceremony typically lasted several hours and included:
  - Recitation of the **origin legend** (Five Ancestors, Shaolin destruction, Ming loyalty)
  - Passage through a symbolic landscape: gates, bridges, and the "**City of Willows**" (representing the society's ideal of a just domain)
  - Drinking of a mixture of wine and blood (from a rooster and from the initiates' own fingers — creating a blood bond)
  - Swearing the **36 oaths** (e.g., "If I betray my sworn brothers, let me be struck by five thunderbolts"; "If I seduce a brother's wife, let me die beneath ten thousand swords")
  - Learning recognition signs: specific hand signals, tea-cup arrangements (how a cup is held or placed conveys coded messages), verbal phrases, and numerological codes
- **Numerical codes** (well-documented in 20th-century Triad studies): e.g., Incense Master = 438 (4 × 3 × 8 = 96; a referent to the founding mythology); foot soldiers = 49 (4 × 9 = 36, the number of oaths)

### 1.3 Overseas Chinese Diaspora
- **Trocki (1990, *Opium and Empire*)**: documented the role of Tiandihui/Hongmen societies in controlling labor, opium distribution, and conflict resolution in 19th-century Southeast Asian Chinese communities — in Malaya, Triad kongsi (companies) managed tin mines, organized labor, dispensed justice, and sometimes waged war against each other and colonial authorities
- In the United States, Chinese tongs (堂 — "halls," many affiliated with Hongmen lodges) served both as community organizations and, in some cases, as vehicles for gambling, prostitution, and inter-tong violence (the "Tong Wars" of late 19th/early 20th century San Francisco and New York)

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Sun Yat-sen and the 1911 Revolution
- **Sun Yat-sen** (1866–1925): documented Hongmen member who was initiated the Zhigongtang (致公堂, the American Hongmen branch) during his fundraising trips to the United States; Hongmen networks provided: financial support, organizational infrastructure, and recruits for Sun's revolutionary movement — the overlap between anti-Qing secret societies and early Republican revolutionaries is extensive
- After the 1911 Revolution, many Hongmen lodges expected rewards and legitimacy under the Republic — some evolved into fraternal associations or political organizations (the Chinese Freemasons — Zhigongtang — became a legal fraternal society); others continued as underground networks

### 2.2 Transformation into Organized Crime
- The transition from political-fraternal organization to criminal enterprise was gradual and incomplete — not all Hongmen lodges became criminal organizations; many remain legal fraternal bodies (particularly in the diaspora)
- In **Hong Kong** (1945–1997): Triad societies (14K, Sun Yee On, Wo Shing Wo, among others) became major organized crime groups controlling drug trafficking, extortion, gambling, prostitution, and infiltrating legitimate businesses and the entertainment industry — the colonial government's Anti-Triad Bureau and post-handover law enforcement have significantly suppressed Triad power but have not eliminated it

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Ancient Origins at Shaolin
- The founding legend's claim of roots in the Shaolin monastery and the Five Ancestors dates the society's spiritual origins to the Kangxi or Yongzheng periods (late 17th–early 18th century) — while this narrative served as a powerful legitimating myth, no historical evidence confirms that the Tiandihui's actual organizational origins predate the 1760s

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 All Chinese Are Triad Members
- **[UNSUPPORTED]** Stereotypical claims that Chinese communities are universally controlled by Triads — Triad membership has always been a minority of the Chinese population; the vast majority of Chinese diaspora communities function through legal institutions, family networks, and legitimate business associations
