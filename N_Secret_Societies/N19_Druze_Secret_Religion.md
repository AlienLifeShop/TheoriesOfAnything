## QUICK SUMMARY

The Druze are a distinct ethno-religious community of approximately 1-2 million people concentrated in Lebanon, Syria, Israel, and Jordan, whose faith crystallized in the early 11th century during the Fatimid Caliphate in Cairo. Emerging from an Ismaili Shia theological milieu, the Druze venerate the Fatimid Caliph al-Hakim bi-Amr Allah (r. 996-1021) as the ultimate manifestation of divine unity (Tawhid) and follow a closed religious system that prohibits conversion in or out, guards its sacred scriptures (the *Rasa'il al-Hikma*, "Epistles of Wisdom") from outsiders, and teaches the transmigration of souls (taqammus). The community is divided between the *'uqqal* (the knowledgeable/initiated, also called *ajawid*) who have access to doctrine, and the *juhhal* (the uninitiated) who do not. This combination of theological secrecy, endogamous marriage practices, and geographic concentration has made the Druze one of the most distinctive and least understood religious communities in the world.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Historical Record)

### 1.1 Historical Origins — The Fatimid Context
- The Druze faith emerged in **Cairo** during the reign of the sixth Fatimid Caliph, **al-Hakim bi-Amr Allah** (985-1021 CE), who ruled Egypt, North Africa, and parts of the Levant.
- The Fatimid dynasty was **Ismaili Shia**, adhering to a tradition of esoteric interpretation (*batin*) in which each outward (*zahir*) religious law conceals an inner meaning accessible only to initiates.
- In **1017 CE**, the Persian-Turkic missionary **Hamza ibn Ali ibn Ahmad** publicly proclaimed al-Hakim as the embodiment of divine unity — not merely an imam but the **cosmic intellect** itself made manifest. This proclamation marks the formal beginning of the Druze movement.
- The name "Druze" derives from **Muhammad al-Darazi**, an early missionary who also preached al-Hakim's divinity but was later condemned by Hamza as a heretic. The community itself prefers the name **Muwahhidun** ("Unitarians" or "People of Tawhid").
- Al-Hakim disappeared during a nighttime walk on the Muqattam hills near Cairo on **February 13, 1021**. His body was never found. Druze theology holds that he did not die but entered **occultation** (*ghayba*) and will return at the end of time.
- The Druze *da'wa* (mission/preaching) was officially **closed in 1043 CE** by the last missionary leader, Baha al-Din al-Muqtana. After this date, no new members could be accepted — a restriction that has been maintained for nearly a millennium.

### 1.2 The Rasa'il al-Hikma (Epistles of Wisdom)
- The sacred scripture of the Druze consists of **111 epistles** (*rasa'il*), composed primarily by Hamza ibn Ali and Baha al-Din al-Muqtana between 1017 and 1043 CE.
- These letters, collectively called the **Kitab al-Hikma** ("Book of Wisdom"), articulate Druze theology, cosmology, and ethics in a dense, allusive style drawing on Neoplatonic, Ismaili, and Quranic vocabulary.
- Access to the Hikma is strictly **restricted to the 'uqqal** — initiated members who have demonstrated commitment to the faith's ethical precepts. Uninitiated Druze and non-Druze are not permitted to read the texts.
- Manuscript copies are carefully guarded; major critical editions were published by Western scholars only in the 20th century (Seybold 1902, De Smet 2007).

### 1.3 Community Structure — 'Uqqal and Juhhal
- The Druze community is divided into two classes:
  - **'Uqqal** (singular: *'aqil*) — "the knowledgeable," initiated members who have undertaken to live by strict ethical rules, attend the *khalwa* (prayer meeting), study the Hikma, and observe dietary and behavioral restrictions. Also called **ajawid** ("the good ones").
  - **Juhhal** (singular: *jahil*) — "the uninitiated," the majority of Druze who participate in communal identity and social obligations but do not have access to doctrinal secrets.
- Female initiates (**ajawid women**) have equal religious status to male ajawid — a notably egalitarian feature.
- The **khalwa** is held on Thursday evenings in a modest, unadorned prayer house (distinct from mosques or churches), where the 'uqqal gather for prayer, discussion, and study.

### 1.4 Endogamy and Demographic Stability
- Druze practice strict **endogamy** — marriage outside the community is strongly discouraged and historically rare.
- Population estimates: approximately **800,000-1,000,000** in Lebanon (the largest concentration), **700,000-800,000** in Syria (mainly Jabal al-Druze/Suwayda), **140,000-150,000** in Israel, and smaller communities in Jordan and diaspora.
- The closed community model has produced a **genetically distinct population** that has been extensively studied in population genetics (see Section 2.3).

### 1.5 Seven Moral Precepts
- The Druze faith prescribes **seven duties** for the faithful:
  1. **Truthfulness** (sidq al-lisan) — honesty in speech
  2. **Protection of brethren** (hifz al-ikhwan) — mutual support within the community
  3. **Renunciation of other religions** — exclusive spiritual loyalty
  4. **Dissociation from unbelief** (al-bara'a min al-iblis) — rejection of the devil and false worship
  5. **Acknowledgment of divine unity** (al-tawhid) — in all ages and manifestations
  6. **Acceptance of God's acts** (al-rida) — contentment with divine will
  7. **Submission to God's will** (al-taslim) — resignation in adversity
- These precepts emphasize **ethical monotheism** and community solidarity rather than complex ritual practice — the Druze have no dietary laws comparable to halal or kosher, no fasting month equivalent to Ramadan (though individual asceticism is practiced by the 'uqqal), and no pilgrimage obligation to distant sites.

### 1.6 Historical Role and Military Traditions
- The Druze have played a military role disproportionate to their numbers throughout Levantine history: the **Ma'an dynasty** (Fakhr al-Din II, 1572-1635) established a semi-autonomous emirate in Mount Lebanon; the **Jumblatt** and **Arslan** families have remained politically powerful into the 21st century.
- In Israel, Druze men serve in the **Israel Defense Forces** under a 1956 agreement — the only Arab-speaking community with mandatory conscription — a decision that has generated both loyalty and internal debate.
- During the Lebanese Civil War (1975-1990) and the Syrian Civil War (2011-present), Druze communities navigated complex alliances to preserve their mountain strongholds, often successfully maintaining neutrality or strategic local defense.

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Scholarly Consensus / Strong Inference)

### 2.1 Core Theological Concepts
- **Tawhid** (divine unity): the central Druze concept, emphasizing the absolute oneness and transcendence of God, who is beyond all attributes, descriptions, and human comprehension.
- **Five Cosmic Principles** (Hudud): Druze cosmology identifies five emanations from the divine:
  1. **al-'Aql** (Universal Mind/Intellect) — identified with Hamza ibn Ali
  2. **al-Nafs** (Universal Soul) — identified with Isma'il al-Tamimi
  3. **al-Kalima** (The Word) — identified with Muhammad al-Wahid
  4. **al-Sabiq** (The Precedent) — identified with Salama ibn 'Abd al-Wahhab
  5. **al-Tali** (The Follower) — identified with Baha al-Din al-Muqtana
- These five cosmic principles are understood as successive emanations through which the transcendent God creates and sustains the universe — a framework heavily indebted to **Neoplatonic** emanation theory (Plotinus, Proclus) as filtered through Ismaili philosophy.

### 2.2 Taqammus — The Transmigration of Souls
- Druze believe in **taqammus** (literally "putting on a shirt"): the soul transmigrates instantaneously at death into a new human body being born at that moment.
- Key features distinguishing Druze reincarnation from Hindu/Buddhist models:
  - Souls transmigrate **only between humans** — never into animals or plants.
  - Reincarnation is **instantaneous** — there is no intermediate state, purgatory, or *bardo*.
  - A **Druze soul** always reincarnates as Druze; there is no mixing across religious boundaries.
  - Some Druze, especially children, report **past-life memories** (*natiq*), which have been studied by Ian Stevenson and Erlendur Haraldsson.
- The fixed number of Druze souls was allegedly determined at the closing of the da'wa in 1043; population growth is explained by the return of souls from hidden or dispersed communities.

### 2.3 Genetic Distinctiveness
- Population genetics studies (Zalloua et al. 2008, Haber et al. 2013) confirm that **Druze populations show reduced genetic diversity** and distinctive allele frequencies consistent with centuries of endogamy.
- Y-chromosome and mitochondrial DNA analyses place Druze within the broader **Levantine genetic landscape** but with notable founder effects and genetic drift.
- The Druze have become a **model population** for genetic studies of isolated communities and Mendelian disease prevalence.
- Specific genetic conditions occur at elevated frequency in Druze populations due to **founder effects**: certain forms of deafness, congenital adrenal hyperplasia, and other Mendelian disorders have been mapped using Druze family pedigrees.
- Despite endogamy, the Druze show **heterogeneous origins**: autosomal analyses reveal Near Eastern, East Mediterranean, and minor sub-Saharan African ancestral components, suggesting the founding population was itself diverse before the closure of conversion.

### 2.4 Worship Practice and Sacred Calendar
- Druze worship is centered on **Thursday evening gatherings** (*majlis*) held in a *khalwa* (prayer hall, architecturally austere, often in remote locations). The *ʿuqqal* attend regularly; the *juhhal* are not prohibited but generally do not participate.
- No call to prayer, no set liturgy, no sermons in the conventional sense: the *majlis* involves readings from the Rasa'il al-Hikma, silent contemplation, and discussion led by senior *ʿuqqal*.
- The principal Druze holiday is **Ziyarat al-Nabi Shu'ayb** (Pilgrimage to the Prophet Jethro), held annually in April at the shrine near Hittin, Israel. This is both a religious and social gathering, reuniting Druze from Israel, Lebanon, Syria, and the diaspora.
- The Druze also observe **ʿId al-Adha** (Festival of Sacrifice), but not Ramadan, the two Eids of mainstream Islam, or any Christian or Jewish holidays.

### 2.5 Jethro / Shu'ayb Veneration
- The Druze revere the biblical figure **Jethro** (Arabic: Shu'ayb), the father-in-law of Moses, as their spiritual ancestor and chief prophet.
- The shrine of Nabi Shu'ayb near Tiberias (Hittin, Israel) is the most important Druze pilgrimage site, with an annual festival in April.
- Jethro's role as a **priest-mediator** who advised Moses and recognized God's supremacy before Israel did (Exodus 18) resonates with the Druze self-understanding as bearers of an ancient monotheistic wisdom predating Judaism, Christianity, and Islam.
- Other venerated prophets in the Druze tradition include **Adam, Noah, Abraham, Moses, Jesus, and Muhammad** — each understood as a successive manifestation of divine unity, with al-Hakim as the final and most perfect manifestation.
- The Druze maintain sacred sites across the Levant, including **maqams** (shrines) dedicated to prophets and saints, often located on hilltops — a practice some scholars connect to ancient Canaanite high-place (*bamah*) traditions.

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Plausible but Unproven)

### 3.1 Pre-Islamic Roots
- Some scholars (e.g., Bryer 1975, Abu-Izzeddin 1984) have speculated that Druze theology preserves elements of **pre-Islamic religions** of the Levant — particularly Phoenician, Hurrian, or even Hittite traditions — filtered through centuries of Islamic, Neoplatonic, and Gnostic synthesis.
- The Druze reverence for specific hilltop sites, certain trees, and seasonal festivals has been interpreted as echoing ancient Canaanite *high place* worship, though direct evidence is lacking.
- The five **cosmic emanations** (Mind, Soul, Word, Preceder, Follower) bear structural similarities to both **Neoplatonic hypostases** (One, Nous, Psyche, Nature) and to the **Ismaili cosmological hierarchy** developed by Hamid al-Din al-Kirmani (d. ~1021), suggesting multiple philosophical tributaries feeding into Druze theology.

### 3.2 Connection to Gnostic Dualism
- Certain features of Druze theology — the emanation cosmology, the distinction between an unknowable God and a manifest cosmic intellect, the emphasis on hidden knowledge — parallel **Gnostic** and **Cathar** themes (→ N17).
- However, Druze theology is fundamentally **monist** (Tawhid), not dualist; the apparent duality arises from levels of emanation, not from competing deities.

### 3.3 al-Hakim's Policies and Madness
- Al-Hakim's reign included erratic and contradictory policies: persecution of Christians and Jews (destruction of the Church of the Holy Sepulchre, 1009), followed by reversals; banning certain foods; nocturnal wanderings. Whether these reflect **madness** (as hostile sources claim), **mystical practice**, or deliberate political strategy remains debated.

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — Unsupported / Fringe)

### 4.1 Star Worship or Paganism
- Medieval and modern polemicists have accused the Druze of secretly worshipping stars, animals, or the golden calf. These accusations appear in hostile Sunni heresiographies and have **no basis** in Druze scriptures or practice.

### 4.2 Ritual Orgies
- The accusation of ritual sexual misconduct (*ibahiyya*) — a standard slander applied to heterodox groups throughout Islamic history — has been thoroughly debunked by scholars who have had access to Druze communities and texts.

### 4.3 Connection to European Freemasonry
- Occasional claims linking the Druze to Freemasonry (supposedly via Crusader contact) have no historical foundation. The similarity of having initiated and uninitiated classes is a common feature of many religious traditions.
