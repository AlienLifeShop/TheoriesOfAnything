## QUICK SUMMARY

**Deontological ethics** (from Greek *deon*, "duty") holds that the morality of an action depends on whether it conforms to a **rule or duty**, not on its consequences. The most influential deontologist is **Immanuel Kant** (1724–1804), who argued that morality is grounded in **pure practical reason** — the moral law is not discovered through experience but is a requirement of rational agency itself. Kant's central concept is the **categorical imperative** — an unconditional moral command binding on all rational beings. He formulated it in several ways: (1) the **universalizability formula** ("Act only according to that maxim whereby you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law") — a maxim passes the moral test if it can be universalized without contradiction; (2) the **humanity formula** ("Act so that you treat humanity, whether in your own person or in that of another, always as an end and never merely as a means") — persons have **dignity** (Würde), not price, and must never be used as mere instruments; (3) the **kingdom of ends formula** — act as a legislating member of a community of rational agents who respect each other's autonomy. For Kant, only actions done **from duty** (aus Pflicht) — not merely in conformity with duty but motivated by respect for the moral law — have genuine moral worth. The **good will** is the only thing good without qualification. **W. D. Ross** (1930) modified deontology with **prima facie duties** — duties that hold unless overridden by a stronger duty (fidelity, reparation, gratitude, justice, beneficence, self-improvement, non-maleficence). Contemporary Kantian ethics includes **Christine Korsgaard's** constructivism (moral obligations derive from the structure of practical reason and reflective endorsement), **Onora O'Neill's** application of the universalizability test to institutional contexts, and **T. M. Scanlon's** contractualism ("what principles could no one reasonably reject?"). Major objections include **rigidity** (Kant's claim that lying is always wrong, even to a murderer at the door), the **motivation problem** (acting from duty seems cold and alienating), and **conflicting duties** (what to do when duties clash).

## 1. WHAT IS DEONTOLOGICAL ETHICS?

### 1.1 Core Features

- **Agent-centered constraints:** Some actions are wrong regardless of how good their consequences might be — you may not murder one to save five, torture an innocent person to prevent a catastrophe, or lie even if lying would produce better outcomes
- **Moral rules are binding categorically:** Not "if you want X, do Y" (hypothetical imperative) but "do Y, period" (categorical imperative) — moral obligations are unconditional
- **Right over Good:** Deontology gives priority to the **right** (what we are obligated to do) over the **good** (what produces the best state of affairs); the right constrains the pursuit of the good

### 1.2 Deontology vs. Consequentialism

| Feature | Deontological Ethics | Consequentialism |
|---------|---------------------|------------------|
| What determines rightness? | Conformity to duty/rules | Outcomes/consequences |
| Can good ends justify bad means? | No — absolute constraints | Yes — if net outcomes are positive |
| Key question | "What is my duty?" | "What produces the best outcome?" |
| Treatment of persons | Always as ends, never mere means | May sacrifice some for greater good |
| Paradigm case | Kant | Bentham, Mill |

## 2. KANT'S MORAL PHILOSOPHY — FOUNDATIONS

### 2.1 The Good Will and Duty

- *Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals* (1785): "There is nothing it is possible to think of anywhere in the world, or indeed anything at all outside it, that can be held to be good without limitation, excepting only a **good will**"
- Intelligence, courage, wealth, even happiness — all can be misused; only the good will is intrinsically good; the good will is the will that acts **from duty** (aus Pflicht), not from inclination
- **The shopkeeper example:** A shopkeeper who gives correct change because he fears losing customers acts in conformity with duty but not from duty; one who gives correct change out of respect for honesty — even when cheating would be profitable — acts from duty; only the latter has moral worth

### 2.2 Reason as the Source of Morality

- Kant argued that moral principles cannot be derived from experience (empirical observation tells us what IS, not what OUGHT to be) nor from human nature (moral law must apply to ALL rational beings, not just humans); morality must be grounded in **pure practical reason** — the same reason that enables us to think logically also legislates moral requirements
- The moral law is not imposed from outside (divine command, social convention) but is self-legislated — rational agents impose the moral law upon themselves through their own reason; this is **autonomy** (self-legislation), the fundamental moral capacity

## 3. THE CATEGORICAL IMPERATIVE

### 3.1 Hypothetical vs. Categorical Imperatives

- **Hypothetical imperative:** "If you want X, then do Y" — conditional on your desires (e.g., "If you want to pass the exam, study"); binds only those who share the relevant desire
- **Categorical imperative:** "Do Y" — unconditional; binds ALL rational agents regardless of desires or circumstances; this is the form of the moral law

### 3.2 Three Formulations

**Formula of Universal Law (FUL):**
> "Act only according to that maxim whereby you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law of nature."

- **The universalizability test:** Before acting, formulate your maxim ("I will do X in circumstance Y to achieve Z"); imagine this maxim as a universal law — everyone in that circumstance acts the same way; if the universalized maxim involves a **contradiction in conception** (the universal law would make the action logically impossible — e.g., universal false promising would destroy the institution of promising) or a **contradiction in will** (you cannot rationally will the universal law — e.g., you cannot rationally will that no one ever helps anyone, since you might need help), then the action is morally impermissible

**Formula of Humanity (FH):**
> "Act so that you treat humanity, whether in your own person or in that of another, always as an end and never merely as a means."

- Persons are rational agents with **dignity** (Würde) — an unconditional, incomparable worth; dignity is distinguished from **price** (Preis) — things with price can be exchanged for equivalents; persons cannot; to use someone merely as a means is to treat them as a tool for your purposes without respecting their rational agency
- "Merely as a means" — Kant does NOT prohibit all instrumental relationships (using a taxi driver to get somewhere); he prohibits treating persons SOLELY as instruments — without their rational consent or regard for their own ends

**Formula of the Kingdom of Ends (FKE):**
> "Act according to maxims of a universally legislating member of a merely possible kingdom of ends."

- A **kingdom of ends** is an ideal community of rational agents, each treating every other as an end in themselves and co-legislating moral principles; morality is what rational agents would agree to as members of such a community; this anticipates contractualist approaches (Rawls, Scanlon)

## 4. AUTONOMY, DIGNITY, AND THE KINGDOM OF ENDS

### 4.1 Autonomy as the Foundation

- **Autonomy** (self-legislation): The capacity of rational agents to bind themselves to the moral law through their own reason — not obeying external authority but recognizing moral requirements as self-imposed; this is the ground of human dignity
- Autonomy requires **freedom** — Kant argued that morality presupposes free will (P_1_04); we must regard ourselves as free even if the phenomenal world is deterministic; this connects to Kant's broader philosophical system (the distinction between phenomena and noumena from the *Critique of Pure Reason*)

### 4.2 Dignity and Rights

- Kantian ethics provides a powerful foundation for **human rights** — the inherent dignity of rational agents is unconditional and cannot be overridden by any calculus of consequences; this underlies modern human rights discourse, the Geneva Conventions, and medical ethics principles like informed consent
- **Kant on punishment:** Even punishment must respect dignity — retributive justice (proportional punishment for crime) respects the criminal as a rational agent who chose to act; deterrence-only theories treat criminals as mere means to social ends

## 5. KANT ON LYING, DUTIES, AND APPLICATIONS

### 5.1 The Absolute Prohibition on Lying

- Kant's most controversial application: In "On a Supposed Right to Lie from Philanthropy" (1797), Kant argued that lying is ALWAYS wrong — even lying to a murderer who asks whether your friend is hiding in your house; the duty of truthfulness admits no exceptions
- **Justification:** If you lie, you take responsibility for the consequences of your lie; if you tell the truth, the bad outcome is the murderer's responsibility, not yours; furthermore, universal lying would destroy communication itself — a performative contradiction
- This extreme position has been criticized even by sympathetic Kantians — Korsgaard and others argue Kant's reasoning here is flawed and that his own principles could justify deception in extreme cases

### 5.2 Perfect and Imperfect Duties

- **Perfect duties** (strict, admitting no exception): Duties to refrain — no lying, no false promising, no suicide, no using others merely as means; violation is always wrong
- **Imperfect duties** (wide, allowing latitude in execution): Duties to pursue — develop your talents, help others in need; you must adopt these as ongoing maxims but have discretion in when and how; you need not help everyone at every moment, but you must make beneficence part of your practical orientation

## 6. W. D. ROSS — PRIMA FACIE DUTIES

### 6.1 The Right and the Good (1930)

- **W. D. Ross** rejected both utilitarianism (consequences aren't all that matters) and strict Kantianism (duties cannot be absolute — they sometimes conflict); proposed **prima facie duties** — duties that are binding unless overridden by a stronger duty in a particular situation:
  1. **Fidelity** — keep promises
  2. **Reparation** — make amends for past wrongs
  3. **Gratitude** — return kindness
  4. **Justice** — distribute benefits and burdens fairly
  5. **Beneficence** — improve the condition of others
  6. **Self-improvement** — develop one's own virtue and knowledge
  7. **Non-maleficence** — do not harm others (generally stronger than beneficence)
- When duties conflict, the agent must exercise **judgment** (not follow a formula) to determine which duty is stronger in the particular case; Ross acknowledged this introduces an element of moral intuition and does not provide an algorithm

## 7. CONTEMPORARY KANTIAN ETHICS

### 7.1 Korsgaard — Sources of Normativity (1996)

- **Christine Korsgaard**: Moral obligations derive from the **structure of practical reason** and our need for **practical identity** — we need to act on principles we can endorse on reflection; when you reflect on your motivations, you must be able to endorse them as a self-legislating agent; morality is the condition of rational agency itself
- Korsgaard's **constructivism**: Moral truths are not discovered but constructed through the activity of practical reason; the categorical imperative is the procedural principle by which rational agents generate specific moral obligations

### 7.2 Scanlon — Contractualism (1998)

- **T. M. Scanlon** (*What We Owe to Each Other*, 1998): An action is wrong if it would be disallowed by any set of principles that no one could **reasonably reject** as a basis for general agreement; this is NOT about maximizing welfare (utilitarian) but about finding principles each person can accept as a free and equal participant
- Scanlon's contractualism is Kantian in spirit (respecting persons as rational agents, seeking universalizable principles) but differs from Kant in being explicitly interpersonal rather than derived from individual practical reason

### 7.3 O'Neill — Kantian Constructivism Applied

- **Onora O'Neill**: Applied the universalizability test to questions of justice, poverty, and institutional design; argued that Kant's ethics is not merely individual morality but provides principles for just institutions; emphasized the practical applicability of Kantian reasoning against charges of excessive abstraction

## 8. COUNTER-ARGUMENTS AND CRITICAL ASSESSMENT

### 8.1 The Rigidity Objection
- **Objection:** Kantian absolutism produces absurd results — you must tell the truth to the murderer, never lie, never break a promise; real moral life requires sensitivity to circumstances
- **Response:** Many contemporary Kantians (Korsgaard, Herman, O'Neill) argue that Kant himself is misinterpreted — the universalizability test requires careful attention to the maxim's description; a maxim of "lying to protect an innocent life from a murderer" may be universalizable; Ross's prima facie framework resolves conflicts without absolutism

### 8.2 The Motivation Objection
- **Objection (Williams, Stocker):** Acting from duty seems cold and impersonal — if your friend visits you in the hospital and says "I came because it's my duty," not "I came because I care about you," that's alienating; Kantian morality seems to require suppressing natural affection
- **Response:** Kant distinguishes acting from duty from acting merely from inclination; he does NOT require the absence of feeling — only that duty, not feeling alone, is the determining ground of action; one can act from duty AND with affection simultaneously; Baron and Herman have developed sympathetic accounts of Kantian moral motivation

### 8.3 The Empty Formalism Objection
- **Objection (Hegel, Mill):** The categorical imperative is merely formal — it says "be consistent" but provides no content; you can universalize almost any maxim if you describe it specifically enough; it generates no determinate obligations
- **Response:** The universalizability test is not merely about logical consistency but about whether you can rationally WILL the maxim as universal law — this includes what you can will given human needs, vulnerabilities, and the social conditions of action; O'Neill has shown the test has real moral content

### 8.4 The Problem of Conflicting Duties
- **Objection:** When duties conflict (keep a promise vs. save a life), strict Kantianism provides no resolution — it insists all duties are absolute
- **Response:** Ross's prima facie duties (§6) are the standard deontological resolution; Kantian constructivists argue the categorical imperative itself can resolve conflicts through careful maxim formulation
