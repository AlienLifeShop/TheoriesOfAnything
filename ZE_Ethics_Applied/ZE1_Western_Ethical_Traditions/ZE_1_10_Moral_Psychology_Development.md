## QUICK SUMMARY

**Moral psychology** investigates how humans actually make moral judgments, develop moral capacities, and experience moral emotions — bridging empirical research and philosophical ethics. **Developmental approaches**: Jean Piaget (*The Moral Judgment of the Child*, 1932) observed children progressing from "heteronomous morality" (rules as fixed, authority-based) to "autonomous morality" (rules as negotiable, justice-based); **Lawrence Kohlberg** (1958–1987) extended this into a six-stage theory of moral development: **Pre-conventional** (Stage 1: obedience/punishment; Stage 2: self-interest/exchange), **Conventional** (Stage 3: interpersonal conformity; Stage 4: law-and-order), **Post-conventional** (Stage 5: social contract/rights; Stage 6: universal ethical principles). Kohlberg's theory was criticized by **Carol Gilligan** (*In a Different Voice*, 1982) for gender bias — Kohlberg's studies focused on males and valued abstract justice reasoning over care-based reasoning; Gilligan proposed an "ethic of care" that valued relationships, responsiveness, and contextual judgment, more commonly (though not exclusively) expressed by women. Subsequent research (Walker, 1984, 2006) found smaller gender differences than Gilligan claimed, but the care-justice distinction proved philosophically productive. **Jonathan Haidt's Social Intuitionist Model** (*The Righteous Mind*, 2012) argued that moral judgments are primarily intuitive, not rational — "moral reasoning" is largely post-hoc rationalization of gut reactions. His **Moral Foundations Theory** identifies six universal moral "taste receptors": **Care/Harm**, **Fairness/Cheating**, **Loyalty/Betrayal**, **Authority/Subversion**, **Sanctity/Degradation**, and **Liberty/Oppression** — political liberals emphasize Care and Fairness, while conservatives weight all six more equally. The **trolley problem** (Philippa Foot, 1967; Judith Jarvis Thomson, 1985) — would you divert a trolley to kill one person instead of five? would you push a large man onto the tracks? — became a major tool for studying moral cognition; Joshua Greene's fMRI studies (2001) showed that personal moral dilemmas (pushing) engage emotional brain regions (ventromedial prefrontal cortex, amygdala) more than impersonal ones (switching), supporting **dual-process theory**: moral judgment involves both fast, emotional intuitions (System 1) and slower deliberative reasoning (System 2). **Psychopathy** (estimated 1% prevalence in the general population, higher in prison populations) provides a natural experiment: psychopathic individuals can identify moral rules but lack empathic emotional responses, suggesting that moral understanding without moral emotion is insufficient for moral behavior (Blair, 2007).

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Scholarly Consensus)

### 1.1 Kohlberg's Stage Theory
- Kohlberg's longitudinal studies (begun 1958, published through the 1980s) documented directional moral development across cultures — individuals progress through stages in the same sequence (though not all reach higher stages); the theory is supported by cross-cultural research (Snarey, 1985 — review of 27 countries) but criticized for Western bias, emphasis on justice to the exclusion of care, and difficulty confirming the existence of Stage 6

### 1.2 Dual-Process Moral Cognition
- Greene et al.'s fMRI studies (2001, 2004) demonstrated that personal moral dilemmas (pushing someone to their death) activate emotional processing regions (vmPFC, amygdala) significantly more than impersonal dilemmas (pulling a switch), supporting the view that distinct cognitive systems contribute to moral judgment — replicated in multiple studies, though the interpretation remains debated

### 1.3 Psychopathy and Moral Emotions
- Research consistently shows that individuals with psychopathic traits (measured by Hare's PCL-R) demonstrate intact ability to distinguish moral from conventional transgressions but show reduced empathic arousal, guilt, and fear (Blair, 2007; Kiehl, 2006) — supporting the role of emotion in moral functioning

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Moral Foundations Theory
- Haidt's MFT has generated substantial empirical support (large-scale surveys, cross-cultural data from MoralFoundations.org) and clarified liberal-conservative moral differences, but critics challenge its foundations: the number of foundations is contested (Suhler & Churchland, 2011), the theory may confuse moral content with moral processing, and the political application oversimplifies complex ideological landscapes

### 2.2 Gilligan's Care Ethics
- Gilligan's claim of a distinct female moral voice emphasizing care over justice was groundbreaking but empirically contested: meta-analyses (Jaffee & Hyde, 2000) found only small gender differences in care vs. justice orientation; the philosophical contribution (ethics of care as a distinct moral framework) has proven more durable than the empirical gender claims

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Moral Enhancement
- Some philosophers (Ingmar Persson & Julian Savulescu, *Unfit for the Future*, 2012) argue that pharmacological or genetic moral enhancement (boosting empathy, reducing bias) may be necessary to address global threats like climate change and nuclear weapons — this remains highly speculative and raises profound concerns about autonomy and authenticity

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Moral Nativism (Strong Version)
- **[DEBUNKED]** The strong claim that morality is entirely innate (a "moral organ" analogous to Chomsky's language faculty, proposed by Marc Hauser, *Moral Minds*, 2006) was undermined by Hauser's scientific misconduct findings (Harvard investigation, 2010) and by evidence that moral norms show substantial cultural variation beyond what a fixed moral grammar would predict; a weaker nativist position (innate moral dispositions shaped by culture) remains defensible

### Counter-Arguments
- Haidt's intuitionism may overstate the case against moral reasoning — while intuitions often come first, deliberative reasoning can revise intuitions over time (e.g., changing attitudes toward slavery, women's rights, LGBTQ+ rights reflect reasoned moral progress, not merely shifting intuitions)
- Moral development theories may reflect culturally specific trajectories rather than universal patterns — Kohlberg's "highest" stages align suspiciously with Western liberal individualism
- The trolley problem and similar thought experiments may have limited ecological validity — real moral decisions involve context, relationships, uncertainty, and time pressure that artificial scenarios strip away
