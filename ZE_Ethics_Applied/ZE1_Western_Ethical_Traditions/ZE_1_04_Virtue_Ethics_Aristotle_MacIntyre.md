## QUICK SUMMARY

**Virtue ethics** is the ethical tradition that focuses not on rules for action (deontology — ZE_1_06) or on consequences (utilitarianism — ZE_1_05) but on **character**: What kind of person should I be? What human excellences (virtues) enable a flourishing life? The tradition originates with **Aristotle** (*Nicomachean Ethics*, c. 340 BCE), who argued that the goal of human life is **eudaimonia** (flourishing, well-being, living well); eudaimonia is achieved through the exercise of the **virtues** (aretai — excellences of character) — courage, temperance, justice, generosity, truthfulness, proper ambition, and above all **phronesis** (practical wisdom, the capacity to discern the right action in particular circumstances). Each virtue is a **mean between two extremes** — excess and deficiency (courage is the mean between cowardice and recklessness; generosity between stinginess and prodigality). Virtues are acquired through **habituation** — by repeatedly performing virtuous actions, one develops virtuous dispositions; moral education is therefore central. After centuries of dominance in ancient and medieval ethics, virtue ethics was eclipsed by Kantian deontology and utilitarianism in the modern period. The revival began with **Elizabeth Anscombe's** "Modern Moral Philosophy" (1958), which argued that modern ethics' focus on moral rules and obligations is incoherent without the theological framework (divine lawgiver) that originally grounded it — and that ethics should return to Aristotelian virtue and human flourishing. **Alasdair MacIntyre's** *After Virtue* (1981) provided the landmark philosophical argument: modern moral discourse is in a state of "grave disorder" — we use moral vocabulary (rights, duties, utility) stripped from the traditions that gave it meaning; the only way to recover coherent moral thought is to return to the Aristotelian tradition of virtues, practices, and narrative. Contemporary virtue ethicists include **Philippa Foot**, **Rosalind Hursthouse**, **Julia Annas**, and **Martha Nussbaum** (whose capabilities approach applies virtue-ethics insights to global justice and development).

## 1. WHAT IS VIRTUE ETHICS?

### 1.1 The Three Approaches to Ethics

| Approach | Central Question | Focus | Key Figure |
|----------|-----------------|-------|-----------|
| **Virtue ethics** | "What kind of person should I be?" | Character, virtues, flourishing | Aristotle |
| **Deontology** | "What rules should I follow?" | Duty, rights, moral law | Kant (ZE_1_06) |
| **Consequentialism** | "What produces the best outcomes?" | Consequences, utility, welfare | Mill (ZE_1_05) |

- Virtue ethics is **agent-centered** rather than act-centered — it asks about the character of the person acting, not primarily about the rightness of individual acts; a virtuous person does the right thing because they have developed the right character — they perceive, feel, and respond appropriately as a matter of settled disposition

### 1.2 Why Virtue?

- Virtue ethicists argue that rule-based and consequence-based theories miss the lived reality of moral life: real ethical decision-making requires **judgment, perception, emotional responsiveness, and practical wisdom** — not just rule-following or calculation; moral rules underdetermine action (they don't tell you what to do in complex particular circumstances); consequences are uncertain and often incommensurable
- Moral development matters: becoming a good person is not about learning a set of rules but about forming one's character through practice, mentorship, and habituation — analogous to becoming skilled at a craft or art

## 2. ARISTOTLE'S VIRTUE THEORY

### 2.1 Eudaimonia — The Human Good

- **Eudaimonia** (often translated "happiness" but better: "flourishing," "living well," "doing well"): the highest human good — that for the sake of which everything else is done; eudaimonia is NOT a subjective feeling (pleasure, satisfaction) but an **objective condition** — a life of activity in accordance with the virtues
- The **function argument** (*Nicomachean Ethics* I.7): Every living thing has a characteristic function (ergon); the eye's function is sight, the knife's function is cutting; what is the function of a human being? Living in accordance with **reason** — the capacity that distinguishes humans from other animals; excellence (virtue) in the exercise of this function constitutes eudaimonia
- Eudaimonia is an **activity**, not a state — "living well and doing well"; it requires a complete life (not just moments of virtue) and certain external goods (health, resources, friends, luck); virtue is necessary but (Aristotle thought) not strictly sufficient for eudaimonia

### 2.2 The Doctrine of the Mean

- Each moral virtue is a disposition to feel and act in a **mean** between two vices — one of excess and one of deficiency; "virtue is a mean between two vices, the one involving excess, the other deficiency" (*NE* II.6)
- Examples:

| Deficiency (Vice) | Mean (Virtue) | Excess (Vice) |
|-------------------|---------------|---------------|
| Cowardice | **Courage** | Recklessness |
| Insensibility | **Temperance** | Profligacy |
| Stinginess | **Generosity** | Prodigality |
| Self-deprecation | **Truthfulness** | Boastfulness |
| Humorlessness | **Wit** | Buffoonery |
| Quarrelsomeness | **Friendliness** | Obsequiousness |

- The mean is NOT a mathematical midpoint but a **relative mean** — what counts as the virtuous response varies with the agent, the situation, and the circumstances; determining the mean requires **phronesis** (practical wisdom)

### 2.3 Phronesis — Practical Wisdom

- **Phronesis** (practical wisdom, prudence): the master virtue — the intellectual capacity to perceive what is required in particular situations and to deliberate well about means to good ends; it unifies all the moral virtues: one cannot be truly courageous without practical wisdom (otherwise one cannot distinguish courage from recklessness), and one cannot have practical wisdom without the moral virtues (biased emotional responses distort practical judgment)
- Phronesis is NOT a set of rules — it is the acquired capacity for **perceptual judgment** in particular situations; each situation is unique; the practically wise person sees what the situation requires (what Aristotle calls "perception") and responds appropriately
- **Habituation:** Virtues are acquired by practice — "We become just by doing just acts, temperate by doing temperate acts, brave by doing brave acts" (*NE* II.1); moral education begins with habituation (doing the right things even before understanding why) and matures into reflective virtue (doing the right things with understanding and appropriate feeling)

## 3. THE VIRTUES — A CATALOGUE

### 3.1 Aristotle's List

- Aristotle discusses ~12 virtues in the *Nicomachean Ethics*: courage, temperance, generosity, magnificence, magnanimity, proper ambition, patience, truthfulness, wit, friendliness, modesty, and justice; **justice** is treated as the most important social virtue ("the whole of virtue in relation to others")
- This list reflects Aristotle's aristocratic social context — "magnanimity" (megalopsychia, "great-souledness") is the virtue of the great man who knows his own worth; this has been criticized as reflecting class bias

### 3.2 Other Virtue Traditions

- **Stoic virtues (P_4_05):** Wisdom, courage, justice, temperance — the four cardinal virtues; the Stoics held that virtue is SUFFICIENT for eudaimonia (unlike Aristotle, who required external goods)
- **Christian virtues:** Medieval thinkers (Aquinas) added the three theological virtues — faith, hope, and charity — to the four cardinal virtues; the resulting seven virtues reframed Aristotelian ethics in a Christian context
- **Confucian virtues (P_4_07):** Ren (humaneness, benevolence), yi (righteousness), li (ritual propriety), zhi (wisdom), xin (trustworthiness) — a virtue-ethical tradition developed independently in China

## 4. THE ECLIPSE AND REVIVAL OF VIRTUE ETHICS

### 4.1 The Eclipse

- From the 17th century onward, modern moral philosophy (Kant, Mill, Bentham) shifted focus from character to action — from "What kind of person should I be?" to "What should I DO?"; this reflected: secularization (moral rules without a deity), the rise of political liberalism (focus on individual rights), and the Enlightenment demand for universal, rational moral principles
- Virtue ethics survived in Thomistic/Catholic ethics and in some British moral philosophy (the "moral sense" tradition of Hume, Hutcheson, Adam Smith) but was marginalized in mainstream ethical theory

### 4.2 Elizabeth Anscombe's Challenge

- **"Modern Moral Philosophy" (1958):** One of the most influential papers in 20th-century ethics; Anscombe argued: (1) Modern moral vocabulary (moral "ought," obligation, duty) derives from a divine law conception of ethics — God as moral legislator; without this framework, these concepts are empty shells; (2) Both Kantian ethics and utilitarianism are fundamentally confused — Kant's categorical imperative is formal and empty; utilitarianism licenses monstrous acts if they maximize utility; (3) Ethics should return to the Aristotelian framework — virtues, character, human flourishing — abandoning the obsession with rules
- Anscombe's paper launched the **virtue ethics revival** of the late 20th century

## 5. ALASDAIR MACINTYRE — AFTER VIRTUE

### 5.1 The Disquieting Suggestion

- *After Virtue: A Study in Moral Theory* (1981): Opens with a thought experiment — imagine a catastrophe that destroys scientific knowledge; fragments are later recovered and used without understanding of the framework that gave them meaning; MacIntyre argues this is the ACTUAL condition of modern moral discourse — we use moral terms (justice, rights, duty, utility) ripped from the traditions (Aristotelian, Christian, Kantian, utilitarian) that gave them coherence; the result is interminable moral disagreement — each side deploys fragments of incompatible traditions against each other, with no rational resolution possible

### 5.2 Practices, Narrative, and Tradition

- MacIntyre redefines virtue through three concepts:
  - **Practice:** A coherent, complex, socially established cooperative human activity through which goods internal to that activity are realized (e.g., chess, farming, architecture, scientific inquiry, medicine); virtues are the qualities needed to achieve the internal goods of practices (courage, honesty, justice)
  - **Narrative:** Human life is a story with beginning, middle, and end; virtues are the qualities that enable a person to seek the good of their life AS A WHOLE — not just as a series of disconnected episodes; "I can only answer the question 'What am I to do?' if I can answer the prior question 'Of what story or stories do I find myself a part?'"
  - **Tradition:** Virtues and practices exist within living traditions — ongoing arguments about goods and standards that extend across generations; tradition provides the context for understanding what the virtues are and what counts as flourishing

### 5.3 Nietzsche or Aristotle?

- MacIntyre presents the central choice of modern ethics as: **Nietzsche or Aristotle** — either we accept that moral discourse is nothing but expressions of will and preference (Nietzsche) or we recover the Aristotelian tradition of rational moral inquiry grounded in a conception of human nature and the human good; MacIntyre chooses Aristotle (later converting to Catholicism and incorporating Thomistic elements)

## 6. CONTEMPORARY VIRTUE ETHICS

### 6.1 Philippa Foot and Rosalind Hursthouse

- **Philippa Foot** (*Natural Goodness*, 2001): Grounded virtue in natural-historical judgments — just as a good oak tree has deep roots and produces acorns, a good human being has certain characteristics (practical rationality, sociality, strength of will); moral goodness is a form of natural goodness — excellence in the characteristic way of being human
- **Rosalind Hursthouse** (*On Virtue Ethics*, 1999): Developed virtue ethics as a comprehensive normative theory rival to deontology and consequentialism; provided the **v-rule** account: "An action is right iff it is what a virtuous agent would characteristically do in the circumstances"; addressed objections about action-guidance, moral dilemmas, and the plurality of virtues

### 6.2 Martha Nussbaum and the Capabilities Approach

- **Martha Nussbaum** (*Women and Human Development*, 2000): Applied Aristotelian virtue ethics to global justice through the **capabilities approach** — a list of central human capabilities (life, bodily health, bodily integrity, senses and imagination, emotions, practical reason, affiliation, play, control over environment, etc.) that every political order should secure for its citizens; this connects virtue ethics to political philosophy and development economics

## 7. COUNTER-ARGUMENTS AND CRITICAL ASSESSMENT

### 7.1 Action-Guidance Objection
- **Objection:** Virtue ethics does not provide clear rules for action — "Do what the virtuous person would do" is unhelpfully circular; how do I know what the virtuous person would do?
- **Response (Hursthouse):** Virtue ethics provides as much action-guidance as its rivals — "Do what maximizes utility" and "Act only on universalizable maxims" are also difficult to apply in practice; virtue ethics provides v-rules ("Be honest," "Be courageous," "Be just") that are as clear as Kantian duties

### 7.2 Situationism Challenge
- **Objection (Doris, Harman):** Social psychology shows that behavior is determined more by situations than by character traits — the Milgram experiment, Stanford prison experiment, and bystander effect suggest that stable virtuous character traits may not exist; virtue ethics assumes a psychology that is empirically unsupported
- **Response:** The situationist evidence shows that *many* people lack robust virtues — which is exactly what virtue ethics predicts, since developing virtue requires long habituation, good moral education, and supportive social conditions; the claim is not that everyone is virtuous but that virtue is the ideal toward which moral development should aim

### 7.3 Relativism Objection
- **Objection:** Virtue lists are culturally variable — Aristotle's virtues reflect Greek aristocratic values; MacIntyre's practices are historically specific; there is no universal list of virtues
- **Response (Nussbaum, Foot):** Some virtues are near-universal (courage, honesty, justice) because they address universal features of human life (danger, communication, social cooperation); cultural variation in the specific expression of virtues does not entail that virtue itself is culturally relative
