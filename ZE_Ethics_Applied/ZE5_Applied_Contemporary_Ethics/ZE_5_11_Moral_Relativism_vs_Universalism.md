## QUICK SUMMARY

The debate between **moral relativism** and **moral universalism** is among the most fundamental in ethics. **Relativism** holds that moral judgments are valid only relative to a cultural, historical, or individual framework — there are no trans-cultural moral truths. **Universalism** holds that at least some moral principles (prohibitions on murder, torture, slavery) are valid for all humans regardless of culture. The debate has profound practical consequences: if relativism is correct, then external criticism of practices like female genital cutting, caste-based discrimination, or honor killing may constitute cultural imperialism; if universalism is correct, then moral criticism across cultural boundaries is not only permissible but obligatory. **Gilbert Harman** (*The Nature of Morality*, 1977) defended a sophisticated moral relativism grounded in the observation that different societies operate under different implicit moral agreements. **David Wong** (*Natural Moralities*, 2006) developed **pluralistic relativism** — there are multiple adequate moralities, but not all moralities are adequate (they must meet minimum conditions of social cooperation). On the universalist side, **Martha Nussbaum** (*Women and Human Development*, 2000) argued that the **capabilities approach** provides a cross-cultural standard of human flourishing that avoids both dogmatic universalism and anything-goes relativism. **John Rawls** (*The Law of Peoples*, 1999) sought a "political" universalism — a set of minimum human rights principles that all "decent" societies could accept regardless of their comprehensive doctrines. The question is not merely academic: every human rights appeal, every international intervention, every cross-cultural moral judgment presupposes an answer to the relativism–universalism question.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Experimentally Confirmed)

### 1.1 Forms of Moral Relativism
- Moral relativism takes several distinct forms, which must not be conflated:
  - **Descriptive moral relativism**: the empirical observation that different societies hold different moral beliefs — uncontroversial as a sociological fact (Westermarck, 1906; Sumner, 1906). This is not itself a moral claim
  - **Metaethical relativism**: the philosophical thesis that moral judgments are true or false only relative to a framework (culture, individual, or social agreement) — there are no framework-independent moral truths. **Harman** (1975, 1977) defended this as "moral relativism" — morality is a form of implicit social agreement, and moral judgments make sense only within the agreement
  - **Normative relativism**: the prescriptive claim that one **ought** not to judge other cultures' moral practices by one's own standards. This is the most controversial form — and arguably self-undermining, since it makes a universal moral claim ("you ought not to judge") that contradicts its own relativism
  - **Wong** (*Natural Moralities*, 2006): argued for **pluralistic relativism** — there are genuinely different but adequate moralities because moral systems serve social functions (cooperation, conflict resolution) that can be fulfilled in multiple ways. However, not all moralities are adequate — those that fail basic social functionality are genuinely deficient

### 1.2 Forms of Moral Universalism
- **Moral realism**: there are objective moral facts, analogous to physical facts, that hold independently of human beliefs (Shafer-Landau, *Moral Realism*, 2003). Moral progress consists in better approximating these facts
- **Kantian universalism**: moral principles must be universalizable — the Categorical Imperative demands that maxims be testable as universal law. Culture is irrelevant to the validity of moral principles because moral reasoning operates at the level of rational agency, not cultural membership
- **Natural law**: moral truths are grounded in human nature and accessible to reason. Aquinas held that the fundamental principles of natural law — "do good, avoid evil" — are known to all humans regardless of culture. Modern natural law theorists (Finnis, *Natural Law and Natural Rights*, 1980) refine this argument
- **Capabilities approach** (Nussbaum, 2000): identifies ten **central human capabilities** (life, bodily health, bodily integrity, senses/imagination/thought, emotions, practical reason, affiliation, other species, play, control over environment) that all societies must protect as a minimum condition of justice. This provides a substantive universal standard while allowing different societies to realize these capabilities in culturally specific ways
- **Constructivism** (Rawls, Scanlon): moral principles are those that would be chosen or accepted by rational agents under appropriate conditions (the original position, the contractualist test). This generates universal principles without relying on moral "facts" — the universality comes from the structure of practical reason

### 1.3 The Cross-Cultural Evidence
- **Anthropological data**: genuine moral diversity exists — practices that are morally required in some cultures are prohibited in others (e.g., polygamy, filial piety, honor-based violence, communal vs. individual property)
- **But also**: remarkable convergence on basic moral principles — prohibitions on unprovoked killing, theft, and promise-breaking appear in virtually every recorded culture (Brown, *Human Universals*, 1991). The specific forms vary enormously, but the underlying moral categories are strikingly similar
- **Psychological evidence**: moral foundations theory (Haidt and Joseph, 2004) identifies six cross-cultural moral foundations: care/harm, fairness/cheating, loyalty/betrayal, authority/subversion, sanctity/degradation, liberty/oppression. All cultures employ these foundations but **weight them differently** — which accounts for both moral universality and moral diversity

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Supported by Multiple Scholars / Strong Circumstantial Evidence)

### 2.1 The Human Rights Question
- Human rights represent the most consequential universalist claim:
  - **Universal Declaration of Human Rights** (1948): asserts rights that apply to "all members of the human family" regardless of culture, nation, or religion
  - **Relativist critique**: the UDHR was drafted primarily by Western powers and reflects Western liberal values — individualism, autonomy, property rights. It has been criticized as cultural imperialism by Asian values proponents (Lee Kuan Yew), Islamic scholars, and postcolonial theorists
  - **Universalist response** (Donnelly, *Universal Human Rights in Theory and Practice*, 2013): while the specific formulation is modern and Western-influenced, the underlying values — dignity, freedom from torture, right to livelihood — are not culturally bounded. People in all cultures value their own lives, security, and dignity
  - **Pragmatic middle ground** (An-Na'im, 2002): advocates for cross-cultural dialogue that builds consensus on human rights norms through internal cultural reform rather than external imposition

### 2.2 Hard Cases
- Practices that test the limits of both relativism and universalism:
  - **Female genital cutting** (FGC): practiced in parts of Africa (28+ countries), the Middle East, and Southeast Asia. Relativists argue that outsider condemnation is cultural imperialism; universalists argue that the practice causes irreversible harm and violates bodily integrity regardless of cultural context. **Nussbaum** (1999) argued forcefully that FGC is a human rights violation — cultural context does not transform harm into benefit
  - **Caste-based discrimination**: deeply embedded in South Asian social systems, defended by some cultural traditionalists as integral to social order. Universalists condemn it as a violation of human equality. **Ambedkar** (1891–1956) — himself a Dalit — rejected caste from within the tradition, demonstrating that internal critique is possible and does not require external imposition
  - **Honor killing**: defended by some cultural practitioners as necessary for social order; condemned by human rights organizations as murder. The tension between cultural authority and individual rights is acute

### 2.3 Error Theory and Moral Anti-Realism
- **Mackie** (*Ethics: Inventing Right and Wrong*, 1977): argued for **error theory** — all moral judgments are false because there are no objective moral facts. Morality is a human invention, not a discovery. This is not relativism (which says moral judgments are true relative to a framework) but nihilism (which says they are never true)
- **Implications**: if Mackie is right, both relativism and universalism are mistaken — there is nothing for moral judgments to be relative to, and nothing for universal principles to accurately describe. Most ethicists find this position too radical — it undermines the entire enterprise of moral reasoning

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Limited Evidence / Emerging Hypotheses)

### 3.1 Moral Convergence
- Some theorists argue that humanity is gradually converging on universal moral norms:
  - **Moral progress** (Kitcher, *The Ethical Project*, 2011): human moral history shows a pattern of expanding moral circles — from kin to tribe to nation to humanity — and deepening moral sensitivity (abolition of slavery, women's suffrage, LGBTQ+ rights)
  - Whether this convergence reflects discovery of objective moral truths or merely contingent cultural dynamics remains philosophically contested

### 3.2 Evolutionary Ethics
- Evolutionary approaches (Joyce, *The Evolution of Morality*, 2006; Street, 2006) suggest that moral intuitions are products of natural selection — shaped by survival pressures rather than moral truth
  - If our moral intuitions are evolutionary adaptations, their truth or falsity becomes uncertain — they may be useful fictions rather than reliable guides to moral reality. This is the "evolutionary debunking argument" against moral realism

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — Fringe / Not Supported by Evidence)

### 4.1 All Morality Is Equally Valid
- Extreme relativism — the claim that every cultural moral system is equally valid — is self-undermining and practically untenable:
  - It cannot condemn practices like slavery, genocide, or torture even when perpetrated within a culture that endorses them
  - It cannot account for moral progress within cultures — if all systems are equally valid, then reform (abolition, suffrage, civil rights) is not improvement but merely change
  - Wong (2006) argues persuasively that constrained relativism — acknowledging multiple adequate moralities while maintaining minimum standards — is the most defensible position

### 4.2 Western Ethics Is the Only Universal Ethics
- The claim that Western liberal values represent the unique universal moral framework is also problematic:
  - It ignores the rich moral traditions of Confucianism, Buddhism, Ubuntu, Indigenous ethics, and other non-Western systems that arrive at universal moral principles through different philosophical paths
  - Genuine universalism must be dialogical — achieved through cross-cultural engagement, not unilateral assertion

## COUNTER-ARGUMENTS

- **Relativism vs. universalism**: **Gilbert Harman** and **David Wong** defend forms of moral relativism or pluralism — arguing that moral truths are relative to cultural frameworks or that multiple incompatible but internally coherent moral systems can coexist. **Martha Nussbaum** and **Thomas Scanlon** argue for universalism grounded in shared human capabilities or rational contractualism, defending cross-cultural moral standards against relativist challenge
- **Human rights and cultural imperialism**: The claim that international human rights norms represent Western cultural imperialism imposed on non-Western societies is contested — **Jack Donnelly** and others respond that human rights have been adopted and adapted by diverse cultures and are not inherently Western, while critics argue the genealogy and enforcement mechanisms remain power-laden
