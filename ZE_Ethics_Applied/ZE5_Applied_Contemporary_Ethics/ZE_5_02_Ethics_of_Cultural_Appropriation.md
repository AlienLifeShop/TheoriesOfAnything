## QUICK SUMMARY

**Cultural appropriation** — the adoption of elements (dress, music, cuisine, religious symbols, hairstyles, language) from one culture by members of another, typically from a marginalized or minority culture by members of a dominant culture — has become one of the most contested moral concepts of the 21st century. At its core, the debate asks: **When does cultural exchange become cultural extraction?** And what distinguishes genuine appreciation from exploitative borrowing? **James O. Young** (*Cultural Appropriation and the Arts*, 2008) provided the leading philosophical analysis, distinguishing **object appropriation** (taking physical artifacts), **content appropriation** (using stories, themes, styles), **subject appropriation** (representing another culture's experience), and **style appropriation** (adopting artistic or aesthetic forms). Young argued that not all cultural appropriation is wrong — cultures have always borrowed from each other, and such exchange is often valuable — but that appropriation becomes morally problematic when it involves **misrepresentation**, **exploitation of asymmetric power relations**, or **harm to the source culture**. Critics include **Erich Hatala Matthes** (2016), who argued that the concept is often applied too broadly, and **Loretta Todd** (Indigenous filmmaker), who insisted that the issue is not merely about borrowing but about who has the **right to tell certain stories** and who profits from them. The debate intersects with colonial history, race relations, intellectual property, artistic freedom, and the politics of identity.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Experimentally Confirmed)

### 1.1 Defining Cultural Appropriation
- **Young** (2008) developed the most precise philosophical taxonomy:
  - **Object appropriation**: taking possession of tangible cultural objects — museum acquisition of Indigenous artifacts, removal of cultural relics during colonialism (the Elgin Marbles, Benin Bronzes). Moral issues: original ownership, consent, repatriation obligations
  - **Content appropriation**: using another culture's stories, knowledge, or sacred elements — retelling Indigenous myths in novels, using traditional medicine knowledge without acknowledgment. Moral issues: misrepresentation, decontextualization, exploitation of sacred material
  - **Subject appropriation**: outsiders representing another culture's experience — white novelists writing from a Black perspective, Western directors making films about non-Western cultures. Moral issues: authenticity, voice, stereotyping, who has standing to speak
  - **Style appropriation**: adopting the aesthetic forms of another culture — jazz influences in classical music, African textile patterns in fashion, yoga studios in Western cities. Moral issues: credit, commodification, stripping context
- Not all instances of these types are morally problematic: the ethical assessment depends on context, power relations, credit/acknowledgment, and impact on the source culture

### 1.2 Power Asymmetry and Colonial Context
- The moral weight of cultural appropriation is inseparable from **power dynamics** and **colonial history**:
  - When a **dominant culture** borrows from a **marginalized culture**, the power asymmetry transforms what might otherwise be neutral cultural exchange into potential exploitation. The dominant culture profits (economically, socially, aesthetically) while the source culture may receive no credit, compensation, or respect
  - **Historical pattern**: colonial powers systematically extracted cultural artifacts, knowledge, and practices from colonized peoples while simultaneously suppressing those same cultures (banning languages, religions, art forms). Contemporary appropriation can reproduce this pattern even without colonial intent
  - **Double standard**: elements that are punished or stigmatized when practiced by marginalized peoples (dreadlocks, Indigenous ceremonies, Black musical styles) may be celebrated and commodified when adopted by dominant-culture members — a dynamic the philosopher **C. Thi Nguyen** has described as "aesthetic injustice"

### 1.3 Music and Appropriation
- The history of popular music provides some of the most extensively documented cases:
  - **Rock and roll**: emerged from African American blues, gospel, and rhythm and blues traditions. White artists (Pat Boone, Elvis Presley, The Rolling Stones) brought Black musical forms to white audiences, often with greater commercial success than the Black originators. Whether this constitutes appreciation, homage, or exploitation remains debated
  - **World music**: the category itself (coined by Western record labels in the 1980s) has been criticized as homogenizing diverse non-Western musical traditions for Western consumption (Feld, 2000)
  - **Musical borrowing**: is not inherently wrong — all musical traditions develop through exchange. The moral question concerns credit, compensation, and whether the borrowing distorts or decontextualizes the source material

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Supported by Multiple Scholars / Strong Circumstantial Evidence)

### 2.1 Sacred vs. Secular Elements
- The appropriation of **sacred** cultural elements raises distinct moral concerns:
  - Native American war bonnets, sacred pipe ceremonies, sweat lodges, and vision quests have been adopted by non-Indigenous people — often commercially (New Age workshops, "shamanic retreats"). Indigenous communities have consistently objected that these practices are sacred, require cultural context and earned authority, and are trivialized by casual adoption
  - **Aboriginal Australian** sacred designs, restricted to initiated men of specific clans, have appeared on tourist souvenirs and commercial products — violating both cultural protocol and spiritual significance
  - Matthes (2016) argues that the harm of sacred element appropriation is particularly clear because it involves not just borrowing but **violation of specifically expressed cultural constraints** on who may use certain practices or symbols

### 2.2 The Positive Case for Cultural Exchange
- Not all cross-cultural borrowing is appropriation, and scholars warn against overextending the concept:
  - **Kwame Anthony Appiah** (*Cosmopolitanism*, 2006): cultures are not hermetically sealed containers — they have always borrowed, hybridized, and evolved through contact. Attempts to enforce cultural purity are historically inaccurate and potentially authoritarian
  - **Young** (2008) himself argues that much cultural borrowing is morally neutral or positively valuable: jazz incorporates European harmonic language; sushi has been transformed by non-Japanese chefs; yoga has been adapted worldwide. The question is whether the borrowing occurs within a framework of respect, credit, and reciprocity
  - The distinction between **appreciation** (learning from another culture with respect, acknowledgment, and humility) and **appropriation** (extracting elements for personal gain without credit, context, or consent) is crucial but often difficult to draw in practice

### 2.3 Intellectual Property and Traditional Knowledge
- Cultural appropriation intersects with **intellectual property** law:
  - Western IP law protects individual creators for limited periods; traditional cultural expressions developed collectively over generations fall outside this framework
  - The **WIPO Intergovernmental Committee** on Intellectual Property and Genetic Resources, Traditional Knowledge and Folklore has been negotiating (since 2000) an international legal instrument for the protection of traditional cultural expressions — but agreement has been elusive
  - **Biopiracy**: the patenting of traditional medicinal knowledge by pharmaceutical companies (neem tree, turmeric, ayahuasca) represents a particularly egregious form of cultural extraction — profiting from knowledge developed by communities over centuries without consent or benefit-sharing

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Limited Evidence / Emerging Hypotheses)

### 3.1 Digital Appropriation
- Social media has created new dynamics of cultural appropriation:
  - Viral adoption of culturally specific dances (TikTok), slang (African American Vernacular English), fashion, and food — often without credit to originators
  - The speed, scale, and anonymity of digital sharing may accelerate appropriation and make attribution more difficult — but also offers marginalized communities tools to call out appropriation and control their own narratives
  - Whether digital appropriation is qualitatively different from pre-digital appropriation, or merely faster, remains debated

### 3.2 Cultural Appropriation in Cuisine
- The globalization of food raises complex questions:
  - Is a non-Japanese sushi restaurant cultural appropriation? What about a non-Korean kimchi producer? A non-Mexican taco chain?
  - Scholars (Ray, 2016) argue that food appropriation becomes morally problematic primarily when it involves (a) economic extraction (large corporations profiting from cuisines associated with immigrant labor), (b) misrepresentation (claiming authenticity while distorting), or (c) erasure of the source culture's contribution

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — Fringe / Not Supported by Evidence)

### 4.1 All Cultural Borrowing Is Appropriation
- The maximalist position — that any use of any element of another culture is appropriation and therefore wrong — is not supported by serious scholarship:
  - This position would make virtually all cultural activity impermissible, since all cultures are products of historical exchange
  - It contradicts the empirical reality of how cultures actually develop: through contact, borrowing, hybridization, and mutual influence

### 4.2 Cultural Appropriation Is Never a Legitimate Concern
- The opposite extreme — that "cultural appropriation" is an invented concept with no moral force — dismisses legitimate grievances:
  - Real harms (economic exploitation, misrepresentation, desecration of sacred elements, reinforcement of colonial power dynamics) are well-documented and morally significant
  - Dismissing all concerns about cultural appropriation ignores the structural inequalities that distinguish exploitative extraction from reciprocal exchange

## COUNTER-ARGUMENTS

- **Cosmopolitan exchange vs. power critique**: **Kwame Anthony Appiah** and other cosmopolitans argue that cultural borrowing and exchange have been the engines of cultural creativity throughout history, and that policing cultural boundaries produces a counterproductive cultural isolationism. Critics respond that the concept of cultural appropriation is specifically about power-asymmetric borrowing — dominant groups extracting value from marginalized cultures without reciprocity, credit, or understanding
- **Scope of the concept**: **James O. Young** (*Cultural Appropriation and the Arts*, 2008) distinguished multiple types of appropriation (subject, content, motif, style) and argued that not all are morally objectionable — while **Zoe Todd** and indigenous scholars emphasize that sacred knowledge and ceremonial practices involve categories where any uncontextualized appropriation is harmful
