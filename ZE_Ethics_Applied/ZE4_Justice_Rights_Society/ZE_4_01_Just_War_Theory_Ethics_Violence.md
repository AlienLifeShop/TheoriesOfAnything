## QUICK SUMMARY

**Just war theory** — the ethical framework for evaluating when the use of military force is morally justified and how it may be conducted — has roots in classical antiquity (Cicero, Augustine) and medieval theology (Aquinas), and remains central to international humanitarian law and military ethics. The theory comprises three components: **jus ad bellum** (the right to go to war — criteria for justified resort to force), **jus in bello** (justice in war — rules governing conduct during hostilities), and increasingly, **jus post bellum** (justice after war — obligations during occupation and peacebuilding). **Jus ad bellum** criteria include: **just cause** (typically self-defense against armed aggression, or defense of others — the UN Charter, Article 51, recognizes individual and collective self-defense), **legitimate authority** (war must be declared by proper authority, not private individuals), **right intention** (aim to restore just peace, not conquest or revenge), **proportionality** (expected benefits must outweigh expected harms), **reasonable chance of success** (futile wars are unjustified regardless of cause), and **last resort** (all peaceful alternatives must be exhausted). **Jus in bello** principles include: **discrimination/distinction** (combatants must distinguish between military targets and civilians — the principle of civilian immunity), **proportionality** (military actions must not cause civilian harm excessive relative to military advantage), and **military necessity** (force must be limited to what is necessary to achieve legitimate objectives). These principles are codified in the **Geneva Conventions** (1864, revised 1949) and **Additional Protocols** (1977), which constitute the core of **international humanitarian law (IHL)**. **Challenges** to just war theory include: **pacifism** (absolute moral opposition to all war — Quaker, Jain, and some Buddhist traditions hold that violence is never justified), **realism** (the view that moral categories are inapplicable to interstate relations — states act from interest, not morality), **terrorism and asymmetric warfare** (non-state actors who deliberately target civilians challenge traditional combatant/civilian distinctions), **autonomous weapons** (see ZD_2_06 — machines making lethal decisions), **nuclear deterrence** (threatening massive civilian casualties to prevent war), and **humanitarian intervention** (military force to prevent genocide or mass atrocities — justified under "Responsibility to Protect" doctrine but contested as potential cover for power politics).

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Scholarly Consensus)

### 1.1 Augustinian and Thomistic Foundations
- Augustine (4th–5th century CE) argued that war could be justified to restore peace and punish wrongdoing, provided it was waged by legitimate authority with right intention; Aquinas (13th century) systematized the criteria (legitimate authority, just cause, right intention) in *Summa Theologica* II-II, Q.40 — these criteria remain the foundation of just war theory

### 1.2 Geneva Conventions as IHL Core
- The four Geneva Conventions of 1949 (ratified by all 196 states — universal ratification unique in international law) establish legal protections for wounded soldiers, prisoners of war, and civilians in armed conflict; the Additional Protocols (1977) expanded civilian protections and defined combatant status

### 1.3 Principle of Distinction
- The distinction between combatants and civilians is the foundational rule of IHL — deliberately targeting civilians constitutes a war crime under the Rome Statute (ICC); this principle is challenged by insurgencies, terrorism, and urban warfare where combatant/civilian boundaries blur

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Responsibility to Protect (R2P)
- The R2P doctrine (adopted at 2005 UN World Summit) holds that sovereignty implies responsibility — when states fail to protect populations from genocide, war crimes, ethnic cleansing, or crimes against humanity, the international community has a responsibility to act, including military intervention as a last resort; application has been selective and contested (Libya 2011 vs. Syria)

### 2.2 Nuclear Deterrence Ethics
- Nuclear deterrence — preventing war by threatening unacceptable retaliation — is ethically paradoxical: it requires credible willingness to commit what would be the greatest crime in history (mass civilian killing); Michael Walzer called it "supreme emergency" reasoning; the Catholic Church (Pope Francis, 2019) has shifted from conditional acceptance to calling nuclear possession itself immoral

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Autonomous Weapons and Jus in Bello
- Whether autonomous weapons systems can comply with jus in bello principles (distinction, proportionality, military necessity) is actively debated — proponents argue AI may reduce civilian casualties through precision; opponents argue machines cannot exercise the contextual moral judgment required by IHL

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 War as Inherently Ennobling
- **[DEBUNKED]** Romantic and fascist glorification of war as morally ennobling, character-building, or necessary for social vitality — promoted by figures from Heraclitus to Marinetti — is contradicted by overwhelming evidence of war's destructive psychological effects (PTSD, moral injury) and the disproportionate suffering of civilians

### Counter-Arguments
- Just war theory may serve as post hoc rationalization — leaders invoke just war language to legitimize wars driven by economic or strategic interests rather than genuine moral concerns
- Absolute pacifism faces the challenge of mass atrocity — if military force is never justified, how should atrocities like genocide be prevented when diplomatic means fail?
