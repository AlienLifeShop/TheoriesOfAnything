## QUICK SUMMARY

The ethics of memory examines moral obligations related to remembering, forgetting, and representing the past — who has the right to decide what is remembered, how it is commemorated, and what is allowed to be forgotten. **Avishai Margalit** (*The Ethics of Memory*, 2002) distinguished between "thick" relations (family, community — where remembering is an ethical duty) and "thin" relations (strangers, humanity at large — where the duty to remember operates differently). **Paul Ricoeur** (*Memory, History, Forgetting*, 2004) provided a comprehensive philosophical treatment of memory as simultaneously personal capacity, historical method, and political instrument — concluding with a reflection on "just memory" as the fragile balance between the pathologies of excess memory (obsessive commemoration) and excess forgetting (willful erasure). Historical practices of deliberate memory destruction include Roman **damnatio memoriae** (official erasure of a person's name and image from public record — applied to emperors Nero, Domitian, and Commodus), the **Stalinist** erasure of "unpersons" from photographs and records, the **destruction of Palmyra** by ISIS (2015) as ideological memory-assault, and ongoing debates about **Confederate monuments** in the US (over 1,700 Confederate symbols identified by SPLC, 2019; hundreds removed since 2017). The field connects memory ethics to transitional justice: truth commissions, memorials, archives, and reparations are all mechanisms for establishing a "just memory" after atrocity.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Historical Record / Peer-Reviewed)

### 1.1 Damnatio Memoriae in Roman Practice
- The Roman Senate could decree *damnatio memoriae* — the systematic destruction of inscriptions, statues, portraits, and references to a disgraced person, most commonly emperors after their overthrow
- Documented cases include: Domitian (after assassination in 96 CE — his name chiseled from inscriptions empire-wide), Geta (murdered by Caracalla in 211 CE — face removed from the Severan Tondo panel painting, now in the Berlin Museum)
- The practice reveals a sophisticated understanding of memory as political infrastructure — destroying the record was understood as a form of punishment extending beyond death

### 1.2 Maurice Halbwachs and Collective Memory
- Halbwachs (*Les cadres sociaux de la mémoire*, 1925; *La mémoire collective*, posthumous 1950) established that memory is fundamentally social: individuals remember within "social frameworks" (family, religion, class), and collective memories are actively constructed and maintained by groups
- He distinguished between individual memory (autobiographical), collective memory (shared group narratives), and historical memory (formalized knowledge) — all three are shaped by present concerns
- Halbwachs died in Buchenwald concentration camp (1945), giving his theoretical work on memory tragic personal resonance

### 1.3 Confederate Monument Debates
- The SPLC (2019) identified 1,747 Confederate symbols in US public spaces — including 780 monuments, 103 public schools named after Confederate leaders, and 10 military bases
- Most Confederate monuments were erected not during the Civil War but during two later periods: 1900–1920 (coinciding with Jim Crow legislation and the "Lost Cause" narrative) and 1950s–60s (coinciding with the Civil Rights Movement) — suggesting commemorative rather than historical purposes
- The removal debate intensified after the 2017 Charlottesville rally; by 2023, approximately 200+ monuments had been removed or relocated

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Ricoeur's "Just Memory"
- Ricoeur (2004) identified two pathological extremes: "too much memory" (repetitive, traumatic fixation that blocks healing — as in cycles of ethnic revenge) and "not enough memory" (complicit forgetting that enables injustice to continue unchallenged)
- "Just memory" (*juste mémoire*) is the difficult balance: remembering enough to prevent repetition and honor victims, while allowing enough forgetting to enable social reconciliation
- Whether Ricoeur's framework can operate at collective scale or only describes individual moral psychology is debated

### 2.2 Archives as Ethical Instruments
- Jacques Derrida (*Archive Fever*, 1995) argued that the archive is not a neutral repository but an instrument of power — what is archived (and what is destroyed or never collected) determines what can be remembered
- Transitional justice archives (e.g., the Stasi Records Agency in Germany, managing 111 kilometers of files; the US National Security Archive's FOIA collection) represent deliberate decisions about which state secrets should become public memory

### 3.1 Right to Be Forgotten in the Digital Age
- The EU's "right to be forgotten" (established in *Google Spain v. AEPD*, CJEU 2014; codified in GDPR Article 17) asserts that individuals can request removal of personal data from search results — creating a conflict between privacy rights (the right to not be defined by past mistakes) and public interest in information access
- Whether digital forgetting is technically achievable or merely produces a false sense of erasure remains debated

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 History Is Objective and Memory Is Merely Subjective
- **[CONTESTED]** The sharp history/memory distinction (history = scientific, objective; memory = personal, subjective) is undermined by historiography's own constructive processes — Pierre Nora (*Les lieux de mémoire*, 1984–1992) showed that historical monuments, archives, and commemorations are themselves "sites of memory" that shape rather than merely record the past

## COUNTER-ARGUMENTS

- **Memory politics**: **Paul Ricoeur** (*Memory, History, Forgetting*, 2004) identified the paradox of "too much memory" (instrumentalized commemoration that perpetuates resentment) and "too little memory" (willful forgetting that enables repetition) — navigating between these poles is a central challenge in transitional justice and post-conflict societies
- **Monument removal debate**: Whether Confederate monuments (and other commemorations of contested historical figures) should be removed as symbols of white supremacy or preserved as historical artifacts is a live public debate — historians, activists, and civic leaders hold divergent views on whether the primary function of public monuments is commemoration (implying endorsement) or historical education
