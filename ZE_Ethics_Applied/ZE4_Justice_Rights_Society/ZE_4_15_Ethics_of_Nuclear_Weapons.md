## QUICK SUMMARY

The **ethics of nuclear weapons** constitutes one of the most consequential moral questions of the modern era: **Can the threat to annihilate millions of civilians ever be morally justified?** Since the atomic bombings of **Hiroshima** (August 6, 1945; ~80,000 immediate deaths, ~140,000 total by year's end) and **Nagasaki** (August 9, 1945; ~40,000 immediate deaths, ~70,000 total), humanity has lived under the specter of nuclear annihilation. The dominant Cold War strategic doctrine — **mutually assured destruction (MAD)** — held that nuclear war was prevented by the certainty that both superpowers would be annihilated in any exchange, making initiation irrational. But MAD raises profound moral problems: it requires the **conditional intention** to kill millions of civilians; it treats entire populations as hostages; and its stability depends on rationality assumptions that may be unwarranted. **Michael Walzer** (*Just and Unjust Wars*, 1977) argued that nuclear deterrence occupies a moral gray zone — it may prevent catastrophic war but rests on an immoral threat. The **Catholic Church** has evolved from conditional acceptance of deterrence (1983 pastoral letter) to Pope Francis's declaration (2017) that both the use and possession of nuclear weapons are morally condemnable. The **International Campaign to Abolish Nuclear Weapons (ICAN)** won the 2017 Nobel Peace Prize for its work leading to the Treaty on the Prohibition of Nuclear Weapons (TPNW, 2021). With ~12,500 nuclear warheads remaining worldwide (SIPRI, 2023) and the **Doomsday Clock** at 90 seconds to midnight (2023, the closest ever), these moral questions remain urgent.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Experimentally Confirmed)

### 1.1 Hiroshima and Nagasaki: The Moral Reckoning
- The atomic bombings of 1945 remain the only wartime use of nuclear weapons:
  - **Hiroshima**: "Little Boy" (uranium-235), 15-kiloton yield, destroyed ~5 square miles; ~80,000 killed instantly, ~60,000 more by year's end from burns, radiation, and injuries. Survivors (*hibakusha*) suffered elevated rates of cancer, birth defects, and psychological trauma for decades
  - **Nagasaki**: "Fat Man" (plutonium-239), 21-kiloton yield; hilly terrain limited destruction compared to Hiroshima, but ~40,000 immediate deaths, ~30,000 additional by year's end
  - **Moral justification debate**: the official US justification was that the bombings saved lives by avoiding a land invasion of Japan (estimated 500,000+ Allied casualties). Critics (Alperovitz, *The Decision to Use the Atomic Bomb*, 1995) argue Japan was already near surrender, the bombings were primarily a demonstration for the Soviet Union, and alternatives (demonstration, naval blockade, modified surrender terms) were not seriously pursued
  - **Just war analysis** (Walzer, 1977; Anscombe, 1961): the bombings violated the **principle of distinction** (targeting civilians is prohibited) and **proportionality** (the harm inflicted exceeded any military necessity). G.E.M. Anscombe — one of the few philosophers to publicly oppose the bombings at the time — argued they constituted murder of the innocent

### 1.2 Deterrence Theory and MAD
- **Nuclear deterrence** posits that the threat of massive retaliation prevents nuclear attack:
  - **Mutually Assured Destruction (MAD)**: formalized by US Secretary of Defense Robert McNamara in the 1960s — both superpowers maintain second-strike capability sufficient to destroy the other, making a first strike suicidal. Nuclear war is thus "rationally" prevented
  - **Moral problem of conditional intentions**: deterrence works only if the threat to retaliate is credible — meaning decision-makers must genuinely intend to launch retaliatory strikes killing millions. But can one morally intend to do what one cannot morally do? This is the **paradox of deterrence** (Kavka, 1978; Lewis, 1984)
  - **Fragility of deterrence**: deterrence assumes rational decision-making, accurate information, functional command and control, and absence of accidental launch. Historical near-misses (Stanislav Petrov, 1983; Able Archer exercise, 1983; Hawaiʻi false missile alert, 2018) demonstrate that deterrence's stability is more precarious than its proponents suggest (Sagan, *The Limits of Safety*, 1993)

### 1.3 Just War Theory Applied
- Traditional **just war** criteria applied to nuclear weapons:
  - **Jus ad bellum** (right to go to war): a nuclear first strike is virtually impossible to justify — no threat is proportionate to the devastation of nuclear war
  - **Jus in bello** (conduct in war): nuclear weapons cannot satisfy the principles of **distinction** (they inevitably kill massive numbers of civilians) or **proportionality** (their destruction exceeds any military objective). Even "tactical" nuclear weapons produce fallout, fire, and radiation affecting civilian populations
  - **Walzer** (*Just and Unjust Wars*, 1977): argued that nuclear deterrence represents a "supreme emergency" exception — in extremis (facing civilizational destruction), the threat to use nuclear weapons may be reluctantly justified, but the actual use almost never can be. This exception has been criticized as undermining the absolute prohibition on targeting civilians

### 1.4 Current Arsenal and Risk
- **SIPRI** (Stockholm International Peace Research Institute, 2023): approximately 12,512 nuclear warheads worldwide — Russia (~6,257), US (~5,550), China (~350), France (~290), UK (~225), Pakistan (~170), India (~164), Israel (~90, undeclared), North Korea (~40–50)
- **Doomsday Clock** (Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists): set at **90 seconds to midnight** in 2023 — the closest to catastrophe since the Clock's creation in 1947 — citing nuclear risk from the Russia-Ukraine war, climate change, and disruptive technologies
- Modern nuclear weapons are vastly more powerful than the Hiroshima/Nagasaki bombs: a single US Trident submarine carries ~20 warheads of ~100–475 kilotons each — one submarine can destroy every major city in a medium-sized country

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Supported by Multiple Scholars / Strong Circumstantial Evidence)

### 2.1 Nuclear Winter
- **Nuclear winter hypothesis** (Turco, Toon, Ackerman, Pollack, Sagan — "TTAPS," 1983; updated by Robock & Toon, 2012):
  - A large-scale nuclear exchange would inject massive quantities of soot into the upper atmosphere, blocking sunlight and producing global cooling of 5–10°C lasting years
  - Agricultural collapse, mass starvation, and ecosystem devastation would kill billions — vastly more than the direct blast and radiation casualties
  - Modern modeling (Robock et al., 2007; Toon et al., 2019) confirms that even a "limited" regional nuclear war (e.g., India-Pakistan, ~100 Hiroshima-sized weapons) could produce significant global cooling and famine affecting billions
  - Nuclear winter strengthens the ethical case against nuclear weapons: the consequences extend far beyond the combatant states, constituting an attack on all of humanity and the global ecosystem

### 2.2 Abolition Arguments
- The case for complete nuclear disarmament:
  - **George P. Shultz, William Perry, Henry Kissinger, and Sam Nunn** (2007, 2008 *Wall Street Journal* op-eds): four former US national security leaders argued that deterrence is becoming increasingly unreliable and that working toward a world free of nuclear weapons is both necessary and achievable
  - **Treaty on the Prohibition of Nuclear Weapons (TPNW)**: adopted 2017, entered into force 2021 — prohibits the development, testing, production, stockpiling, use, and threat of use of nuclear weapons. 93 signatories, 70 ratifications (as of 2024). All nine nuclear-armed states and most NATO members have not signed
  - **ICAN** arguments: nuclear weapons are inherently indiscriminate, their humanitarian consequences are unacceptable, and their continued existence poses an existential threat. The prohibition norm established by the TPNW follows the model of chemical weapons (CWC, 1997) and biological weapons (BWC, 1975) bans

### 2.3 Religious Perspectives
- Major religious traditions have addressed nuclear weapons ethics:
  - **Catholic Church**: The US Catholic Bishops' pastoral letter *The Challenge of Peace* (1983) conditionally accepted deterrence as a "strictly conditioned moral acceptance" — permitted only as a step toward disarmament. **Pope Francis** (Nagasaki, 2019; *Fratelli Tutti*, 2020) explicitly condemned both use and possession: "The use of atomic energy for purposes of war is today, more than ever, a crime"
  - **World Council of Churches**: declared nuclear weapons incompatible with Christian faith (1983, Vancouver)
  - **Islamic scholars**: have generally opposed nuclear weapons as incompatible with Islamic prohibitions on killing civilians and environmental destruction, though some argue for nuclear capability as defensive deterrence against nuclear-armed adversaries

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Limited Evidence / Emerging Hypotheses)

### 3.1 Nuclear Weapons as Existential Risk
- **Existential risk** analysis (Bostrom, 2002; Ord, *The Precipice*, 2020) frames nuclear war as a potential civilizational extinction event:
  - Toby Ord estimated a ~1% probability of existential catastrophe from nuclear weapons in the next century — lower than some other existential risks (AI, engineered pandemics) but still unacceptably high
  - Whether nuclear war could literally cause human extinction (as opposed to civilizational collapse followed by recovery) depends on uncertain nuclear winter modeling and the resilience of remnant populations

### 3.2 Minimal Deterrence
- Scholars propose **minimal deterrence** as a middle path between abolition and current arsenals:
  - Maintaining a small number of survivable weapons (~100–200) to deter nuclear attack while dramatically reducing arsenals and alert levels
  - This preserves deterrence (arguably) while reducing accident risk, arms race instability, and the severity of potential nuclear war. Whether minimal deterrence is stable or inevitably leads back to arms competition remains debated (Glaser, 1998)

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — Fringe / Not Supported by Evidence)

### 4.1 Nuclear Weapons Have Made War Obsolete
- The "nuclear peace" thesis — that nuclear weapons have made great-power war impossible and are therefore a force for good — oversimplifies:
  - Nuclear deterrence has not prevented proxy wars, conventional conflicts, or limited wars between nuclear-armed states (India-Pakistan, 1999 Kargil). The "long peace" among great powers since 1945 has multiple causes beyond nuclear weapons (Gaddis, 1986; Mueller, 1989)
  - The risk of accidental, unauthorized, or irrational use means nuclear weapons are not a guarantee of peace but a wager — one that could catastrophically fail

### 4.2 Nuclear Weapons Can Be Used Surgically
- Claims that modern nuclear weapons can be used in a "limited" or "tactical" manner without escalating to general nuclear war:
  - Virtually all war games and simulations demonstrate **escalation dynamics**: once nuclear weapons are used, the pressure to escalate to strategic exchange is enormous. There are no historical precedents for controlled nuclear war (Bracken, 2012; Ellsberg, *The Doomsday Machine*, 2017)
  - Even "small" nuclear weapons produce fallout, electromagnetic pulse, and political consequences that make controlled use illusory

## COUNTER-ARGUMENTS

- **Deterrence paradox**: **Gregory Kavka** identified the paradox of deterrence — nuclear deterrence requires the credible intention to perform an act (nuclear retaliation) that would be morally impermissible to actually carry out. Whether it is morally possible to intend something one believes would be wrong to do is a philosophical puzzle with no consensus resolution
- **Hiroshima revisionism**: Whether the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki were militarily necessary or morally justified is contested — the official justification (preventing a costly invasion) has been challenged by **Gar Alperovitz** (*The Decision to Use the Atomic Bomb*, 1995), who argued that Japan was already seeking surrender and that the bombs were primarily used to intimidate the Soviet Union. **Michael Walzer** (*Just and Unjust Wars*, 1977) treats the bombings as a "supreme emergency" exception while acknowledging their moral gravity
