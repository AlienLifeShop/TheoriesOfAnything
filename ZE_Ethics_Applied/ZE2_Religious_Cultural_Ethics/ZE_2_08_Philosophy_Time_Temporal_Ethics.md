## QUICK SUMMARY

The philosophy of time and temporal ethics investigates how our understanding of time's nature shapes moral obligations. McTaggart's 1908 argument that time is unreal introduced the distinction between A-series (past/present/future as objective features) and B-series (earlier/later relations only), which remains foundational. The metaphysical debate between **presentism** (only the present exists), **eternalism** (all times are equally real — the "block universe"), and the **growing block** theory (past and present exist, the future does not) has direct ethical implications: if future people already exist (eternalism), our obligations to them differ categorically from if they do not yet exist (presentism). Heidegger's analysis of *Sein-zum-Tode* (being-toward-death) argues that authentic existence requires confronting temporal finitude. Practical temporal ethics includes: **intergenerational justice** (what do we owe future generations?), **temporal discounting** (should future suffering count less than present suffering?), and **deep time ethics** (moral obligations across geological timescales, as in nuclear waste storage requiring 10,000+ year containment).

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Philosophical Consensus)

### 1.1 McTaggart's Paradox
- J.M.E. McTaggart ("The Unreality of Time," *Mind*, 1908) argued that the A-series (past/present/future) involves a contradiction: every event is past, present, AND future, and attempts to resolve this generate an infinite regress
- The argument established the A-series/B-series distinction that structures all subsequent philosophy of time
- While most philosophers reject McTaggart's conclusion that time is unreal, his conceptual framework remains indispensable

### 1.2 Temporal Discounting in Economics and Ethics
- Standard economic practice discounts future costs and benefits by 3–7% annually (US Office of Management and Budget uses 3% and 7%)
- At 5% discount rate, $1 million in damages 100 years from now has a present value of only ~$7,600 — meaning catastrophic future harms are treated as nearly worthless
- Philosophers (Parfit, *Reasons and Persons*, 1984; Broome, 1994) argue that pure time preference — valuing future welfare less simply because it is future — is ethically indefensible

### 1.3 Heidegger on Temporality
- In *Being and Time* (1927), Heidegger argued that Dasein (human existence) is fundamentally temporal — we exist as a "thrown projection," already in a situation (past), actively engaged (present), and oriented toward possibilities (future)
- "Being-toward-death" (*Sein-zum-Tode*) is not morbid preoccupation but the condition for authentic existence — acknowledging finitude gives urgency and meaning to choices

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Eternalism and Ethical Implications
- If the block universe of special relativity is correct (all spacetime events are equally real), then future persons exist in the same sense as past persons — this strengthens obligations to future generations (Callender, 2017)
- However, the inference from physics to ethics is contested: the block universe may be an artifact of the mathematical formalism rather than a metaphysical truth about reality

### 2.2 Obligations to Future Generations
- The non-identity problem (Parfit, 1984): policies that affect which specific people will exist cannot harm those people, since they would not exist under alternative policies — yet surely some futures are better than others
- Rawls (*A Theory of Justice*, 1971) proposed a "just savings principle" — each generation owes the next enough to maintain just institutions — but acknowledged difficulty in specifying the rate

### 2.3 Deep Time Ethics and Nuclear Waste
- The US EPA requires nuclear waste repositories (e.g., Yucca Mountain) to demonstrate safety for 1 million years — the longest regulatory timeframe in existence
- Designing warning systems for deep geological repositories raises unprecedented semiotic challenges: how do you communicate danger to cultures that may not share any of our languages, symbols, or concepts? (Sebeok, 1984; WIPP Expert Panel)

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Consciousness and the Specious Present
- William James coined "specious present" (1890) — the felt duration of the present moment (estimated 2–3 seconds in psychological studies)
- Whether the specious present has metaphysical significance (a window into the true nature of time) or is merely a feature of neural processing remains unresolved

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Time Is Purely Subjective
- **[CONTESTED]** The claim that time is entirely a construct of consciousness (no objective temporal order exists) is contradicted by the thermodynamic arrow of time, radioactive decay, and cosmological expansion — while the *experience* of time may be subjective, physical processes exhibit objective temporal asymmetries

---

## COUNTER-ARGUMENTS

- **Temporal discounting**: Whether future well-being should be given less moral weight than present well-being is disputed — most economists use positive discount rates (reflecting time preference and uncertainty), while **Derek Parfit** and **John Broome** argued that pure time preference is irrational and morally unjustifiable. The discount rate debate has enormous practical consequences for climate policy (Stern-Nordhaus debate)
- **Non-identity problem**: **Parfit's** non-identity problem (*Reasons and Persons*, 1984) shows that future people's existence depends on present policy choices, making it impossible for them to be "harmed" by those choices in the standard counterfactual sense — yet environmental degradation seems clearly wrong. No proposed solution has achieved consensus
