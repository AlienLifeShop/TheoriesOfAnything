## QUICK SUMMARY

**Buddhist ethics** (*sila*) forms one of the three pillars of the Buddhist path (alongside meditation [*samadhi*] and wisdom [*prajna*]) and is grounded in the **Four Noble Truths** (suffering exists; it arises from craving; it can be ended; the Eightfold Path is the way) and the principle of **dependent origination** (*pratityasamutpada* — all phenomena arise interdependently, so actions have consequences that ripple through the web of relationships). The **Five Precepts** (*panca-sila*) constitute the foundational ethical code for lay Buddhists: (1) refrain from taking life (*panatipata veramani*), (2) refrain from taking what is not given, (3) refrain from sexual misconduct, (4) refrain from false speech, (5) refrain from intoxicants that cloud the mind. **Ahimsa** (non-harm/nonviolence) is the first and most fundamental precept, shared with **Jainism** (where ahimsa is the supreme ethical principle — Jain monks carry brooms to sweep insects from their path and wear mouth-cloths to avoid inhaling small creatures, and strict Jain ethics prohibits all forms of violence including toward microorganisms) and **Hinduism** (where ahimsa appears in the *Mahabharata*, *Yoga Sutras*, and was central to Gandhi's political philosophy of *satyagraha*). **Key concepts**: **Karma** — ethical actions generate consequences that shape future experience (not fatalism, but moral causation — one's present choices determine future conditions); **Compassion** (*karuna*) and **loving-kindness** (*metta*) — the affective dimensions of Buddhist ethics, cultivated through meditation practices (metta bhavana — extending benevolence systematically to self, loved ones, neutral persons, enemies, and all beings); the **Bodhisattva ideal** (Mahayana Buddhism) — the vow to defer one's own liberation until all sentient beings are freed from suffering, creating an ethics of universal compassion. **Engaged Buddhism** (term coined by Thich Nhat Hanh, 1960s) applies Buddhist ethics to social and political issues: Hanh's anti-war activism during the Vietnam War, the Dalai Lama's advocacy for Tibetan autonomy through nonviolence (Nobel Peace Prize, 1989), and Sulak Sivaraksa's social activism in Thailand. **Debates** include whether Buddhist ethics is consequentialist (actions judged by karmic outcomes), virtue-based (cultivating wholesome states), or deontological (precepts as rules); whether killing is ever justified in Buddhist ethics (the Jataka tales include a story of the Buddha killing a bandit to save 500 lives — the "skillful means" argument); and the tension between monastic renunciation and lay engagement with the world.

## 1. VERIFIED CLAIMS (Tier 1 — Peer-Reviewed / Scholarly Consensus)

### 1.1 Five Precepts and Ahimsa
- The Five Precepts are attested across all Buddhist traditions (Theravada, Mahayana, Vajrayana) and in the earliest Pali Canon texts (e.g., *Sigalovada Sutta*, *Dhammapada*); ahimsa as the first precept reflects a pan-Indian ethical principle shared with Jainism (which predates Buddhism) and Hindu traditions

### 1.2 Dependent Origination as Ethical Foundation
- *Pratityasamutpada* (dependent origination) — "when this exists, that comes to be; with the arising of this, that arises" — is recognized by scholars as the metaphysical basis of Buddhist ethics: because all beings are interconnected, harming others harms oneself and the collective; Keown (2005) argues this grounds a "Buddhist naturalism"

### 1.3 Engaged Buddhism
- Thich Nhat Hanh (1926–2022) articulated Engaged Buddhism through his Order of Interbeing (1966) and Fourteen Mindfulness Trainings, linking inner practice to social justice; his anti-war activism during the Vietnam War (nominated for the Nobel Peace Prize by Martin Luther King Jr., 1967) represents the most influential modern integration of Buddhist ethics and political engagement

## 2. CREDIBLE CLAIMS (Tier 2 — Academic / Debated but Supported)

### 2.1 Classification of Buddhist Ethics
- Scholars disagree on how to categorize Buddhist ethics within Western frameworks: Keown (2001) argues for a virtue-ethics interpretation (focus on character development), Goodman (2009) defends a consequentialist reading (actions judged by reduction of suffering), while Hallisey (1996) and others argue Western categories distort Buddhist moral thought

### 2.2 Buddhist Vegetarianism
- Whether Buddhist ethics requires vegetarianism is debated across traditions: the Pali Canon records the Buddha eating meat offered to him (provided the animal was not killed specifically for him); Mahayana sutras (e.g., *Lankavatara Sutra*) prohibit meat-eating; contemporary practice varies by tradition and region — Tibetan Buddhists traditionally ate meat by necessity, while many East Asian Buddhist monasteries are strictly vegetarian

## 3. SPECULATIVE CLAIMS (Tier 3 — Possible but Unverified)

### 3.1 Buddhist Ethics and Environmental Thought
- Scholars and practitioners (Joanna Macy, Stephanie Kaza) propose that Buddhist interdependence naturally supports deep ecology and environmental ethics — but traditional Buddhist texts focus primarily on sentient beings rather than ecosystems, and the environmental interpretation may be a modern construction

## 4. DUBIOUS CLAIMS (Tier 4 — No Credible Source / Contradicted by Evidence)

### 4.1 Buddhism as Entirely Pacifist
- **[DEBUNKED]** The claim that Buddhism is inherently and universally pacifist is contradicted by historical evidence: Buddhist justifications for violence exist (Sri Lankan chronicles glorifying warfare, Japanese Zen support for militarism during WWII — documented by Brian Victoria, *Zen at War*, 1997, and the Myanmar Buddhist nationalist 969 Movement), demonstrating that Buddhist ethics, like all religious ethics, can be selectively interpreted to support violence

### Counter-Arguments
- Ahimsa as an absolute principle faces difficult cases: should one refrain from killing to prevent genocide? The Mahayana "skillful means" (*upaya*) allows apparent ethical violations when motivated by compassion — but this exception risks justifying any violence as compassionate
- Karma as a moral framework can function as victim-blaming — interpreting suffering as the result of past bad karma can justify indifference to present injustice
- Buddhist ethics may be too focused on individual mental states (reducing craving, cultivating equanimity) to address structural injustice — Engaged Buddhism represents an attempt to correct this limitation
